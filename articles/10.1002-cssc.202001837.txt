Several material properties such as intensity ratio of the Raman spectrum, lateral size of HC crystallite (<i>L</i><sub>a</sub>), and interlayer distance (<i>d</i><sub>002</sub>) have been discussed as factors affecting anode performance. However, these factors do not reflect the bulk property of the Na<sup>+</sup> intercalation reaction directly, since Raman analysis has high surface sensitivity and <i>L</i><sub>a</sub> and <i>d</i><sub>002</sub> provide only one‐dimensional crystalline information. Herein, it was proposed that the crystallite interlayer area (<i>A</i><sub>i</sub>) defined using <i>L</i><sub>a</sub>, <i>d</i><sub>002</sub>, and stacking height (<i>L</i><sub>c</sub>) governs Na<sup>+</sup> intercalation behavior of various HCs. It was revealed that various wood‐derived HCs exhibited the similar total capacity of approximately 250 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, whereas the Na<sup>+</sup> intercalation capacity (<i>C</i><sub>i</sub>) was proportional to <i>A</i><sub>i</sub> with the correlation coefficient of <i>R</i><sup>2</sup>=0.94. The evaluation factor of <i>A</i><sub>i</sub> was also adaptable to previous reports and strongly correlated with their <i>C</i><sub>i</sub>, indicating that <i>A</i><sub>i</sub> is more widely adaptable than the conventional evaluation methods.</p></abstract><abstract type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p><b>Interesting interlayer</b>: Factors affecting hard carbon performances as Na‐ion battery anodes are still controversial. In this report, using hard carbons derived from six wood species, it was found for the first time that the total interlayer area of crystallite (<i>A</i><sub>i</sub>) is proportionally correlated with the intercalation capacity. <i>A</i><sub>i</sub> also shows strong correlations in previous reports, indicating that <i>A</i><sub>i</sub> is a more critical factor than conventional ones.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18645631:media:cssc202001837:cssc202001837-toc-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/cssc202001837-toc-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="cssc202001837-sec-0001" numbered="no"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Reducing carbon dioxide emissions has been one of the biggest challenges over the past few decades and continues to be one till now. In the 2015 Paris Agreement, 187 countries set numerical targets for carbon dioxide emissions. However, to achieve this goal, that is, to limit the temperature increase from pre‐industrial times to less than 2 °C, an additional 20 gigatons of carbon dioxide needs to be reduced.<link href="#cssc202001837-bib-0001"><sup>[1]</sup></link> Specifically, energy‐related carbon dioxide emissions, which account for a large proportion of total greenhouse gas emission, must be reduced. Energy storage devices play an important role in utilizing renewable energy. Although there are various types of batteries, lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs) have gained attention because of their high energy density and stable cycle performance. However, LIBs contain valuable metals such as lithium and cobalt, impeding their cost‐effectiveness and recyclability. Therefore, there is a demand for inexpensive and high‐performing next‐generation batteries, which has motivated intensive research on ion batteries from sodium, potassium, manganese, and zinc, as well as organic batteries.<link href="#cssc202001837-bib-0002 #cssc202001837-bib-0003 #cssc202001837-bib-0004 #cssc202001837-bib-0005 #cssc202001837-bib-0006"><sup>[2–6]</sup></link> Sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs), in particular, have potential as the next‐generation storage device because sodium has chemical properties similar to lithium, is approximately 1000 times more abundant, and requires the price of a cathode active material around 50 times lower than LIBs.<link href="#cssc202001837-bib-0007"><sup>[7]</sup></link> In addition, although most lithium reserves are limited to a small region in South America, sodium is globally available (e. g., at high concentrations in sea water), making it an almost unlimited resource.<link href="#cssc202001837-bib-0007 #cssc202001837-bib-0008"><sup>[7,8]</sup></link> However, SIBs face several challenges, one of which is the type of anode material suitable for these batteries. In general, natural and artificial graphite are employed as the anode material in ion batteries. However, they cannot be used in SIBs because their interlayer distance is too small to allow intercalation of sodium ions into graphite. Hard carbons (HCs) are the most promising alternative to graphite because of their larger interlayer distance.<link href="#cssc202001837-bib-0009 #cssc202001837-bib-0010 #cssc202001837-bib-0011"><sup>[9–11]</sup></link> So far, various types of HCs have been proposed as SIB anodes; they can be divided into two groups: bottom‐up and top‐down HCs. Bottom‐up HCs are prepared by carbonizing monomeric or oligomeric isolated saccharide, such as HCs prepared by carbonizing glucose or sucrose at around 1000 °C, exhibit discharge capacities of 300 or 280–361 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively.<link href="#cssc202001837-bib-0012 #cssc202001837-bib-0013 #cssc202001837-bib-0014 #cssc202001837-bib-0015 #cssc202001837-bib-0016 #cssc202001837-bib-0017"><sup>[12–17]</sup></link> Top‐down HCs are produced by carbonizing polymeric natural biomass; for example, HCs obtained by carbonizing banana peels and peanut shells as precursors exhibit discharge capacities of 355 and 315 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively.<link href="#cssc202001837-bib-0018 #cssc202001837-bib-0019"><sup>[18,19]</sup></link> Wood‐biomass‐derived HCs have also attracted attention in recent years because of their low cost, abundance, and comparable performance to other HCs, asdepicted in Table <link href="#cssc202001837-tbl-0001">1</link> (≈300 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>).<link href="#cssc202001837-bib-0020 #cssc202001837-bib-0021 #cssc202001837-bib-0022 #cssc202001837-bib-0023"><sup>[20–23]</sup></link> Nonetheless, optimal HCs as SIB anodes have not yet been reported because the factors governing plateau capacity have not been elucidated for the achievement of high HC capacity. So far, the intensity ratio (<i>I</i><sub>D1</sub>/<i>I</i><sub>G</sub> and <i>I</i><sub>G</sub>/<i>I</i><sub>D3</sub>) of the Raman spectrum, interlayer distance of graphite nanocrystallite (<i>d</i><sub>002</sub>), lateral size of crystallite (<i>L</i><sub>a</sub>), and micropore volume have been proposed as factors.<link href="#cssc202001837-bib-0020 #cssc202001837-bib-0024 #cssc202001837-bib-0025 #cssc202001837-bib-0026"><sup>[20,24–26]</sup></link> Due to the high surface sensitivity of Raman analysis, the intensity ratio (<i>I</i><sub>D</sub>/<i>I</i><sub>G</sub>) is strongly correlated with the slope capacity derived from the adsorption and desorption of Na ions onto the carbon surface (correlation coefficient <i>R</i><sup>2</sup>=0.90).<link href="#cssc202001837-bib-0027"><sup>[27]</sup></link> The plateau capacity (derived from the intercalation reaction of Na ions into bulk HCs) cannot be evaluated by Raman analysis because of its inability to analyze the bulk properties. Despite this reasoning, it was reported that <i>I</i><sub>G</sub>/<i>I</i><sub>D3</sub> of the Raman spectrum is correlated with the plateau capacity (when <i>R</i><sup>2</sup> is 0.76–0.99).<link href="#cssc202001837-bib-0020 #cssc202001837-bib-0025"><sup>[20,25]</sup></link> Although some studies explain that this strong correlation is attributed to the D3 band derived from amorphous carbon and the <i>I</i><sub>G</sub>/<i>I</i><sub>D3</sub> corresponding to the graphitization degree, this rationale is applicable only to the measurement scope of Raman analysis, that is, the surface of the carbon materials. In addition, the origin of the D3 band of carbon materials is not clearly explained.<link href="#cssc202001837-bib-0020"><sup>[20]</sup></link> Therefore, we believe that Raman analysis is unsuitable for the assessment of plateau capacity. On the other hand, X‐ray diffraction (XRD) analysis can evaluate bulk crystal properties, which affect the intercalation capacity; several studies have investigated the correlation between XRD‐derived indices [<i>d</i><sub>002</sub>, <i>L</i><sub>a</sub>, stacking height (<i>L</i><sub>c</sub>), etc.] and the plateau capacity. However, these indices only capture one‐dimensional crystalline information, leading to a strong correlation among <i>L</i><sub>a</sub>, <i>L</i><sub>c</sub>, and plateau capacity reported in some papers, but no correlation to other indices.<link href="#cssc202001837-bib-0020 #cssc202001837-bib-0024 #cssc202001837-bib-0025 #cssc202001837-bib-0026 #cssc202001837-bib-0028 #cssc202001837-bib-0029 #cssc202001837-bib-0030 #cssc202001837-bib-0031"><sup>[20,24–26,28–31]</sup></link> In summary, many material properties have been proposed as factors governing the capacity of HC serving as the SIB anode; however, a widely applicable indicator for many reports has not been found yet. Furthermore, the linear relationship between the aforementioned indices and plateau capacity has not been explained. Thus, it is necessary to determine the material properties of HCs that affect the plateau capacity for their wide‐scale applicability as SIB anodes.</p><tabular xml:id="cssc202001837-tbl-0001" pRights="unknown" eRights="yes" copyright="Wiley‐VCH Verlag GmbH &amp; Co. Although there are various theories proposed, the most common understanding is that the slope region derives from the adsorption and desorption of Na ions onto the carbon surface, as well as from the reversible deposition of Na metal in pores, which originates from intercalation reactions of Na ions into graphite layers.<link href="#cssc202001837-bib-0026"><sup>[26]</sup></link> To gain deeper insights, the slope and plateau capacities were calculated by dividing the profiles with 0.15 V vs. Na/Na<sup>+</sup>, as a boundary (Table <link href="#cssc202001837-tbl-0002">2</link>). From the viewpoint of energy density, increasing plateau capacity is required to improve the SIB anode performance. Therefore, it is necessary to know which HC material property affects the plateau capacity. In the following sections, the material properties of HCs were measured. xml:id="cssc202001837-sec-0004" numbered="no"><title type="main">Raman analysis</title><p>The Raman spectra of HCs are shown in Figure <link href="#cssc202001837-fig-0003">3</link>. Regardless of the species, G bands (≈1600 cm<sup>−1</sup>) and D bands (D1≈1350 cm<sup>−1</sup>, D3≈1500 cm<sup>−1</sup>, and D4≈1200 cm<sup>−1</sup>) were observed. G bands originate from the vibrations of carbon atoms in the graphite sheet plane, while the D bands are considered to originate from the disorder or intracrystalline defects in the graphite crystal structure. However, the origins of these D bands are controversial, and several plausible interpretations have been proposed. For example, it has been argued that the D1 band (≈1350 cm<sup>−1</sup>) originates from the vibrations of carbon atoms at defects in the graphite crystal and at the edges of graphite sheets, while the D3 band (≈1500 cm<sup>−1</sup>) is related to the short‐range sp3 carbon vibration of amorphous carbon, and the D4 band (≈1200 cm<sup>−1</sup>) is caused by the disordered graphitic lattice.<link href="#cssc202001837-bib-0020 #cssc202001837-bib-0025"><sup>[20,25]</sup></link> The obtained Raman spectra was divided into four bands: G, D1, D3, D4; and each peak intensity ratio (<i>I</i><sub>D1</sub>/<i>I</i><sub>G</sub> and <i>I</i><sub>G</sub>/<i>I</i><sub>D3</sub>) was calculated as shown in Table <link href="#cssc202001837-tbl-0003">3</link>. The <i>I</i><sub>D1</sub>/<i>I</i><sub>G</sub> values of HCs were close to 1, regardless of the species except for Japanese oak, suggesting that Japanese oak may possess more structural defects than the other HCs. On the other hand, the <i>I</i><sub>G</sub>/<i>I</i><sub>D3</sub> values ranged from 1.60 to 2.34. The <i>I</i><sub>G</sub>/<i>I</i><sub>D3</sub> values of Japanese Cedar, Magnolia, and Larch were higher than the other three species, suggesting a higher degree of graphitization.</p><figure xml:id="cssc202001837-fig-0003"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18645631:media:cssc202001837:cssc202001837-fig-0003"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/cssc202001837-fig-0003-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Raman spectra for each HC.</p></caption></figure><tabular xml:id="cssc202001837-tbl-0003" pRights="unknown" eRights="yes" copyright="Wiley‐VCH Verlag GmbH &amp; Co. The correlation coefficients (<i>R</i><sup>2</sup>) between the intensity ratio (<i>I</i><sub>D1</sub>/<i>I</i><sub>G</sub> and <i>I</i><sub>G</sub>/<i>I</i><sub>D3</sub>) and slope capacity were 0.03 and 0.42, respectively, indicating the absence of correlation (Figure <link href="#cssc202001837-fig-0006">6 a,b</link>). According to Xiao et al., slope capacity is derived from the adsorption and desorption of Na ions onto the carbon surface and defects and the metal deposition into the micropores.<link href="#cssc202001837-bib-0026"><sup>[26]</sup></link> Therefore, slope capacity cannot be evaluated by <i>I</i><sub>D1</sub>/<i>I</i><sub>G</sub> and <i>I</i><sub>G</sub>/<i>I</i><sub>D3</sub>, which reflect the surface crystallinity of carbon materials. Thus, we analyzed the correlation between the slope capacity and BET surface area or the total pore volume. Relatively high correlation coefficients of 0.88 for BET surface area and 0.69 for the total pore volume were observed, indicating a strong correlation between the slope capacity and BET surface area or the total pore volume (Figure <link href="#cssc202001837-fig-0006">6 c,d</link>). These results suggest that the BET surface area and total pore volume are plausible indices for slope capacity evaluation, which is believed to originate from the Na ion adsorption onto carbon surfaces as well as the Na ion precipitation and the presence of defects in pores. Considering the principle of the slope capacity, it is necessary to combine both BET surface area and total pore volume when evaluating the actual slope capacity.</p><figure xml:id="cssc202001837-fig-0006"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18645631:media:cssc202001837:cssc202001837-fig-0006"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/cssc202001837-fig-0006-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Correlation between the slope capacity and (a) <i>I</i><sub>D1</sub>/<i>I</i><sub>G</sub>, (b) <i>I</i><sub>G</sub>/<i>I</i><sub>D3</sub> of Raman spectrum, (c) BET surface area, and (d) total pore volume.</p></caption></figure></section><section xml:id="cssc202001837-sec-0009" numbered="no"><title type="main">Plateau capacity</title><p>Figure <link href="#cssc202001837-fig-0007">7 a,b</link> shows the correlation between the plateau capacity and the <i>I</i><sub>D1</sub>/<i>I</i><sub>G</sub> and <i>I</i><sub>G</sub>/<i>I</i><sub>D3</sub> of the Raman spectra. The correlation coefficients <i>R</i><sup>2</sup> were 0.30 and 0.03, respectively, indicating no correlation was found. While strong correlations were found in some reports, there were other reports where no correlation was found.<link href="#cssc202001837-bib-0020 #cssc202001837-bib-0025 #cssc202001837-bib-0028 #cssc202001837-bib-0029 #cssc202001837-bib-0030 #cssc202001837-bib-0031"><sup>[20,25,28–31]</sup></link> A potential explanation for the reports showing strong correlations is the assumption that the crystal structure of the surface and bulk parts is similar. We believe that this is because it is impossible to discuss bulk crystal properties (necessary for the discussion of Na ion intercalation) by Raman analysis, which provides surface crystal information. Therefore, to evaluate plateau capacity, Raman analysis cannot be used for HC‐based anode materials.</p><figure xml:id="cssc202001837-fig-0007"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18645631:media:cssc202001837:cssc202001837-fig-0007"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/cssc202001837-fig-0007-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Correlation between the plateau capacity and (a) <i>I</i><sub>D1</sub>/<i>I</i><sub>G</sub>, (b) <i>I</i><sub>G</sub>/<i>I</i><sub>D3</sub> of Raman spectrum, (c) <i>d</i><sub>002</sub>, and (d) <i>A</i><sub>i</sub>.</p></caption></figure><p>As shown in Figure <link href="#cssc202001837-fig-0007">7 c</link>, there was no correlation between <i>d</i><sub>002</sub> and the plateau capacity (<i>R</i><sup>2</sup>=0.00). This is because <i>d</i><sub>002</sub> only captures one‐dimensional crystalline information and does not take into account other crystal information (e. g., crystallite size, number of layers, etc.) that can affect the intercalation reaction. Therefore, it is suggested that <i>d</i><sub>002</sub> alone is unsuitable for assessing plateau capacity. So far, while it has been reported that <i>L</i><sub>a</sub> is proportional to plateau capacity, there has been no discussion about the relationship between <i>L</i><sub>c</sub> and plateau capacity.<link href="#cssc202001837-bib-0020 #cssc202001837-bib-0025"><sup>[20,25]</sup></link> Since the plateau capacity is essentially derived from the intercalation of Na ions to graphite nanocrystallite, it is necessary to consider the <i>L</i><sub>c</sub> (in other words, the stacking height of graphite nanocrystallite). Therefore, we calculated <i>A</i><sub>i</sub> from the structural properties obtained from XRD analysis (<i>L</i><sub>a</sub>, <i>L</i><sub>c</sub>, and <i>d</i><sub>002</sub>), and investigated the correlation between <i>A</i><sub>i</sub> and the plateau capacity. As shown in Figure <link href="#cssc202001837-fig-0007">7 d</link>, <i>A</i><sub>i</sub> and the plateau capacity were found to be strongly correlated (<i>R</i><sup>2</sup>=0.94). This is because the amount of Na ion intercalation increases with a higher <i>A</i><sub>i</sub>, increasing the plateau capacity as a result. Thus, <i>A</i><sub>i</sub> can be used as a plausible indicator to accurately evaluate the plateau capacity. The reason why <i>L</i><sub>a</sub> has been highly correlated in previous reports is because it has a large effect on <i>A</i><sub>i</sub>, as can be understood from Equation (4) (<i>A</i><sub>i</sub> has <i>L</i><sub>a</sub><sup>2</sup> as a factor).</p></section><section xml:id="cssc202001837-sec-0010" numbered="no"><title type="main">Usefulness of <i>A</i><sub>i</sub></title><p>To confirm the usefulness of <i>A</i><sub>i</sub> we extracted data from previous reports in which both the structure properties (<i>L</i><sub>a</sub> and <i>L</i><sub>c</sub>, <i>d</i><sub>002</sub>) and plateau capacity were reported and applied to <i>A</i><sub>i</sub>.<link href="#cssc202001837-bib-0020 #cssc202001837-bib-0025 #cssc202001837-bib-0028 #cssc202001837-bib-0029 #cssc202001837-bib-0030 #cssc202001837-bib-0031"><sup>[20,25,28–31]</sup></link> The results are shown in Figure <link href="#cssc202001837-fig-0008">8</link>. In five of the six reports, the correlation coefficient <i>R</i><sup>2</sup> is higher than 0.8, suggesting the usefulness of <i>A</i><sub>i</sub>. However, a strong correlation was not found in the report by Zhang et al. (<i>R</i><sup>2</sup>=0.51).<link href="#cssc202001837-bib-0031"><sup>[31]</sup></link> In addition, HCs prepared at temperatures above 1500 °C did not to show a strong correlation.<link href="#cssc202001837-bib-0025 #cssc202001837-bib-0029 #cssc202001837-bib-0031"><sup>[25,29,31]</sup></link> It was previously reported that HCs prepared at temperatures above 1500 °C have smaller interlayer distances than HCs prepared at lower temperatures. The total discharge capacities of these HCs were 204–268 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, as high as those of the previous reports, supporting the potential of such HCs as anode materials for sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs). In addition, the crystallite interlayer area (<i>A</i><sub>i</sub>) was proposed as a new material index for HCs, and a strong correlation was found between <i>A</i><sub>i</sub> and the plateau capacity (<i>R</i><sup>2</sup>=0.94). Furthermore, when <i>A</i><sub>i</sub> was adapted to previous reports, a similar strong correlation was found (<i>R</i><sup>2</sup>&gt;0.8). It is suggested that <i>A</i><sub>i</sub>, which indicates a multi‐dimensional view of the crystallite, has a wider range of applicability than the conventional one‐dimensional indices, such as the intensity ratio (<i>I</i><sub>G</sub>/<i>I</i><sub>D3</sub>) of Raman spectrum, interlayer distance (<i>d</i><sub>002</sub>), and lateral size (<i>L</i><sub>a</sub>) of the crystallite, thus serving as a superior evaluation index. This finding suggests that the plateau capacity of HCs as SIB anodes can be enhanced by increasing <i>A</i><sub>i</sub> simultaneously with <i>L</i><sub>a</sub> and stacking height <i>L</i><sub>c</sub>, while keeping the interlayer distance large enough for Na ion reinsertion.</p></section><section xml:id="cssc202001837-sec-0012" numbered="no"><title type="main">Experimental Section</title><section xml:id="cssc202001837-sec-0013" numbered="no"><title type="main">Preparation of HCs</title><p>Six types of wood‐derived HCs (willow, Japanese cedar, Japanese oak, magnolia, bamboo, and larch), produced in a stone oven using our previously reported process, were pulverized with a hammer and mashed in a mortar for 10 min.<link href="#cssc202001837-bib-0035"><sup>[35]</sup></link> To remove inorganic impurities such as metal salts, the mashed HCs (≈3 g) were placed in a 2 <sc>m</sc> HCl solution (300 mL) and stirred at 80 °C for 24 h. The HCs were then washed thoroughly with pure water and collected by filtration [Omnipore 1.0 μm polytetrafluoroethylene (PTFE) Membrane, 47 mm] before drying in a vacuum oven at 70 °C for at least 6 h. To remove organic impurities, the HCs (3 g) were soaked in acetone (500 mL) and washed by sonication for 1 h. The resulting HCs were collected and dried in the same manner as the HCl wash.</p></section><section xml:id="cssc202001837-sec-0014" numbered="no"><title type="main">Preparation of electrode and coin cell assembly</title><p>Polyvinylidene difluoride (PDVF; 28.57 mg) was added to <i>N</i>‐Methyl‐pyrrolidone (NMP; 250 μL), and the mixture was blended for 10 min. Active material (100.00 mg) and acetylene black (AB; 14.29 mg) were added to the resulting solution. The mixture was then blended for 15 min. The obtained black slurry was pasted onto a copper foil with a thickness of approximately 48 μm, making the thickness of the paste portion 100 μm, and then dried at 80 °C for at least 6 h. The dried electrode was hollowed out with a 10Øpunch and dried again in a vacuum at 120 °C for another 6 h, before moving into a glove box. The battery was assembled using a CR2032 coin cell. The electrolyte was 1 <sc>m</sc> NaPF<sub>6</sub> in propylene carbonate (PC), the separator was glass fiber (GA‐55, ADVANTEC), and the counter electrode was 12 Ø sodium metal.</p></section><section xml:id="cssc202001837-sec-0015" numbered="no"><title type="main">Electrochemical measurements</title><p>Potentiostat (VMP3, Biologic) was used to conduct electrochemical measurements. The sodium ion storage capacity was tested at a constant current density of 20 mA per g of active material in the voltage window of 0.001 to 2 V vs. Na/Na<sup>+</sup>. The charge and discharge profiles were divided into two regions: slope and plateau, with 0.15 V vs. Na/Na<sup>+</sup> as a boundary.</p></section><section xml:id="cssc202001837-sec-0016" numbered="no"><title type="main">Characterization</title><p>The Raman spectra of HCs was measured with a Raman spectrometer (XploRA, HORIBA Scientific). The laser wavelength and the laser power were 532 nm and in the range of 2.0–2.5 mW, respectively. The measurement time was 50 s, integrated three times. The XRD spectra of HCs was measured by XRD equipment (D8 ADVANCE, BRUKER). The specific surface area and total pore volume was measured using an N<sub>2</sub> adsorption/desorption analyzer (QUADRASORB evo, Quantachrome Instruments). The specific surface area was calculated from the adsorption points in the range of 0.1–0.3 relative pressure using the BET theory. The total surface area was measured several times and the average value used as the BET specific surface area. The total pore volume was calculated from the amount of N<sub>2</sub> adsorption at a relative pressure of <i>P</i>/<i>P</i><sub>0</sub>=0.999.</p></section></section><section xml:id="cssc202001837-sec-0017" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>This study was financially supported by the Program for Creation of Interdisciplinary Research from the Frontier Research Institute for Interdisciplinary Sciences, Tohoku University.
