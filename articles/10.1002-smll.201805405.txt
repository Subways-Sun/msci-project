type="main">Abstract</title><p>Molybdenum disulfide (MoS<sub>2</sub>), a 2D‐layered compound, is regarded as a promising anode for sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) due to its attractive theoretical capacity and low cost. The main challenges associated with MoS<sub>2</sub> are the low rate capability suffering from the sluggish kinetics of Na<sup>+</sup> intercalation and the poor cycling stability owning to the stack of MoS<sub>2</sub> sheets. In this work, a unique architecture of bundled defect‐rich MoS<sub>2</sub> (BD‐MoS<sub>2</sub>) that consists of MoS<sub>2</sub> with large vacancies bundled by ultrathin MoO<sub>3</sub> is achieved via a facile quenching process. When employed as anode for a SIB, the BD‐MoS<sub>2</sub> electrode exhibits an ultrafast charge/discharge due to the pseudocapacitive‐controlled Na<sup>+</sup> storage mechanism in it. Further experimental and theoretical calculations show that Na<sup>+</sup> is able to cross the MoS<sub>2</sub> layer by vacancies, not only limited to diffusion along the layer, thus realizing a 3D Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion with faster kinetics. Meanwhile, the bundling architecture reduces the stack of sheets with a superior cycle life illustrating the highly reversible capacities of 350 and 272 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 2 and 5 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 1000 cycles.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="smll201805405-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p><b>Bundled defect‐rich MoS<sub>2</sub></b> is achieved by quenching MoS<sub>2</sub> sheet. Na<sup>+</sup> can cross MoS<sub>2</sub> layers by vacancies and is not limited to diffusion along the layer, realizing 3D diffusion for high rate capability. The bundled architecture reduces the stack of sheets with a superior cycle life, illustrating the capacities of 350 and 272 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 2 and 5 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 1000 cycles.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll201805405:smll201805405-gra-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll201805405-gra-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body xml:id="smll201805405-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="no"><section xml:id="smll201805405-sec-0010" type="opening"><p>Rechargeable sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) have shown great potential as grid‐scale applications for the rise of renewable solar and wind power due to the abundant nature of sodium resource (2.36%) and the suitable redox potential (−2.71 V vs SHE).<link href="#smll201805405-bib-0001"/> However, the larger ionic radius of Na<sup>+</sup> (1.06 Å) compared with that of Li<sup>+</sup> (0.76 Å) limits the structural variability and choice of electrode materials for hold Na<sup>+</sup>.<link href="#smll201805405-bib-0002"/> Recently, 2D‐layered metal dichalcogenides (TMDs), such as MoS<sub>2</sub>,<link href="#smll201805405-bib-0003"/> SnS<sub>2</sub>,<link href="#smll201805405-bib-0004"/> VS<sub>2</sub>,<link href="#smll201805405-bib-0005"/> and WS<sub>2</sub>,<link href="#smll201805405-bib-0006"/> have been widely researched for promising SIB anode because their suitable interlayer spacing is able to host Na<sup>+</sup>. Among them, MoS<sub>2</sub> has attracted more attention due to its high theoretical capacity (670 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>) and low cost.<link href="#smll201805405-bib-0007 #smll201805405-bib-0008"/> Such a high capacity obtains from intercalation and conversion reaction during discharge.<link href="#smll201805405-bib-0009"/> Na<sup>+</sup> first inserts into MoS<sub>2</sub> interlayer with the voltage mathvariant="normal">Mo</mi></mrow></math></displayedItem></p><p>In a general sodiation process, intercalation with a solid‐state diffusion of Na<sup>+</sup> exhibits a sluggish kinetics.<link href="#smll201805405-bib-0011 #smll201805405-bib-0012"/> Particularly at high current density, Na<sup>+</sup> is difficult to insert into MoS<sub>2</sub> resulting in poor rate capability.<link href="#smll201805405-bib-0003"/> A common strategy in improving the process is nanostructuring MoS<sub>2</sub>, for instance, preparing MoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheet,<link href="#smll201805405-bib-0013"/> MoS<sub>2</sub> nanowall,<link href="#smll201805405-bib-0014"/> and MoS<sub>2</sub> quantum dot,<link href="#smll201805405-bib-0015"/> based on the nanosize with shorter intercalation path. Another strategy is the expansion of MoS<sub>2</sub> layer spacing to decrease the Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion barrier, including inserting exotic materials in the interlayer,<link href="#smll201805405-bib-0016"/> chemical or physical exfoliating,<link href="#smll201805405-bib-0017"/> and controlled synthesis of MoS<sub>2</sub> with few layers due to the fewer layers with larger spacing.<link href="#smll201805405-bib-0012"/> Even so, Na<sup>+</sup> is limited to enter MoS<sub>2</sub> from the edge of sheets and diffuse along the layer, which determines a 2D Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion process. On the other hand, MoS<sub>2</sub> suffers from intrinsic stack of sheets with an inferior cycle reversibility.<link href="#smll201805405-bib-0007 #smll201805405-bib-0018"/> The nanostructured MoS<sub>2</sub> is more serious. To date, combining with carbon nanomaterial as support is still the most effective method to stabilize MoS<sub>2</sub>.<link href="#smll201805405-bib-0019"/> But in most cases, carbon nanomaterial with large surface area and volume increases side reactions and lowers energy density.<link href="#smll201805405-bib-0020"/> Therefore, how to break through the limitation of 2D materials to achieve rapid Na<sup>+</sup> insertion/extraction and electrochemical reversibility remains a challenge.</p><p>We propose a unique architecture of bundled defect‐rich MoS<sub>2</sub> (BD‐MoS<sub>2</sub>) that consists of MoS<sub>2</sub> with large vacancies bundled by ultrathin MoO<sub>3</sub> to meet the above challenge. When employed the BD‐MoS<sub>2</sub> as anode for SIB, Na<sup>+</sup> can insert into the MoS<sub>2</sub> and diffuse between the layers through the vacancies instead of only along MoS<sub>2</sub> layer to realize 3D Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion for faster kinetics. Meanwhile, the bundled architecture reduces the stack of MoS<sub>2</sub> sheets to remain integrated electrode structure. Consequently, the BD‐MoS<sub>2</sub> electrode achieves high rate capability, superior cycle life, and high energy density.</p><p>The BD‐MoS<sub>2</sub> was prepared by quenching MoS<sub>2</sub> sheet. The method is inspired by the steel quenching process that is a common surface treatment technique for metallic materials. The as‐prepared MoS<sub>2</sub> sheet was heated to critical temperature of oxidation in the air atmosphere (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll201805405-fig-0001"><b>1</b></link>a, Figure S1 in the Supporting Information). The thermal motion of the atoms is intense in the heated MoS<sub>2</sub>. Some Mo<sup>4+</sup> depart from equilibrium position and are oxidized to Mo<sup>6+</sup>, forming consecutive vacancy defects and lattice mismatch. Then, it was cooled sharply to maintain the instantaneous state. The phase of the product was confirmed by X‐ray diffraction (XRD) (Figure <link href="#smll201805405-fig-0001"/>b). The rough XRD pattern of the BD‐MoS<sub>2</sub> suggests the defective lattice. It shows four strong peaks at the 14.3, 33.1°, 39.6°, and 58.5°, indexing to the 2H‐MoS<sub>2</sub> (PDF 37–1492).<link href="#smll201805405-bib-0021"/> The other pronounced peaks at 12.7° and 25.8° indicate the oxidization of the MoS<sub>2</sub> to α‐MoO<sub>3</sub> (PDF 47–1320)<link href="#smll201805405-bib-0022"/><displayedItem xml:id="smll201805405-disp-0003" type="mathematics" numbered="yes"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" display="block" altimg="urn:x-wiley:dummy:media:smll201805405:smll201805405-math-0003" wiley:location="graphic/smll201805405-math-0003.png"><mrow><mn>2</mn><msub><mrow><mi mathvariant="normal">MoS</mi></mrow><mn>2</mn></msub><mo>+</mo><mn>7</mn><msub><mi mathvariant="normal">O</mi><mn>2</mn></msub><mo>=</mo><mn>2</mn><msub><mrow><mi mathvariant="normal">MoO</mi></mrow><mn>3</mn></msub><mo>+</mo><mn>4</mn><msub><mrow><mi mathvariant="normal">SO</mi></mrow><mn>2</mn></msub></mrow></math></displayedItem></p><figure xml:id="smll201805405-fig-0001"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll201805405:smll201805405-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll201805405-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) Schematic diagram of quenching MoS<sub>2</sub>. The red and blue represent the heating and cooling processes, respectively. b) XRD patterns of the BD‐MoS<sub>2</sub>, as‐prepared MoS<sub>2</sub>, and MoO<sub>3</sub>. c) SEM images of the BD‐MoS<sub>2</sub>. d) Low‐ and e–h) high‐resolution TEM images of the BD‐MoS<sub>2</sub>. Inset of (g) is atomic model for the vacancies to show the microstructure clearly.</caption></figure><p>Based on the thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) results of the 8.9% weight loss in BD‐MoS<sub>2</sub> composites, the MoO<sub>3</sub> loading ratio is determined to be 11 wt% (Figure S2, Supporting Information). X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) was employed to detect the elements' chemical status on the surface of BD‐MoS<sub>2</sub> (Figure S3, Supporting Information). The high‐resolution spectra of Mo 3d show two different chemical states. One state is close to the Mo<sup>4+</sup>, corresponding binding energy at 228.5 and 231.8 eV.<link href="#smll201805405-bib-0007"/> The other state with higher energy of 233.1 and 235.3 eV attributes to Mo<sup>6+</sup>.</p><p>Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) shows the morphology of BD‐MoS<sub>2</sub> as uniformly distributed particle with size about 100 nm (Figure <link href="#smll201805405-fig-0001"/>c), while the as‐prepared MoS<sub>2</sub> presents sheet morphology (Figure S4, Supporting Information). The evolution of morphology is attributed to the oxidation of the sheet edge. The microstructures of BD‐MoS<sub>2</sub> are further characterized by transmission electron microscopy (TEM) (Figure <link href="#smll201805405-fig-0001"/>d‐h). From high‐resolution TEM, a poorly crystallized thin layer on the product is identified as MoO<sub>3</sub> (Figure <link href="#smll201805405-fig-0001"/>e, Figure S5 in the Supporting Information). It is worth noting that there are more MoO<sub>3</sub> on the edge of MoS<sub>2</sub> than surface, which possibly attributed to the atoms exposed at the edge with a lower activation energy and easier to oxidize. The MoO<sub>3</sub> plays a role in bundling MoS<sub>2</sub>. When tested as an electrode, such an architecture with a decreased surface energy reduces the stack of sheets enabling a long cycle life.<link href="#smll201805405-bib-0023"/> A typical edge view of MoS<sub>2</sub> with clear lattice fringes is measured to be 0.64 nm assigned to (002) plane of 2H‐MoS<sub>2</sub>.<link href="#smll201805405-bib-0013"/> Notably, the BD‐MoS<sub>2</sub> presents a broken lattice, while the as‐prepared MoS<sub>2</sub> shows a nearly defect‐free crystal (Figure <link href="#smll201805405-fig-0001"/>f, Figure S6 in the Supporting Information). As shown in Figure <link href="#smll201805405-fig-0001"/>g–h, the continuous distributed vacancies of Mo and S atoms are several or even a dozen, which provide the possibility for Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion in it.<link href="#smll201805405-bib-0024"/> From another perspective, the architecture is similar to the tied MoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets, decreasing the most surface area and side reactions instead of an individual nanosheet.<link href="#smll201805405-bib-0025"/></p><p>The electrochemical properties of the products were tested by galvanostatic charge/discharge with the votage of 0.01–3 V. The voltage profiles of the BD‐MoS<sub>2</sub> and as‐prepared MoS<sub>2</sub> electrodes were recorded at 0.5 A g<sup>−1</sup>. In the deep initial discharge, both exhibit similar capacity (564 vs 552 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>) and two distinct discharge platforms (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll201805405-fig-0002"><b>2</b></link>a). It is worth noting that the BD‐MoS<sub>2</sub> electrode shows the higher potential of discharge platform than the as‐prepared MoS<sub>2</sub> electrode, which indicates a better ion diffusion kinetics in BD‐MoS<sub>2</sub> electrode.<link href="#smll201805405-bib-0011"/> The detailed voltage profiles and its differential curves of the BD‐MoS<sub>2</sub>, as‐prepared MoS<sub>2</sub>, and MoO<sub>3</sub> electrodes were provided in Figure S7 (Supporting Information) to in‐depth analysis of the electrochemical process. From differential curves, the BD‐MoS<sub>2</sub> electrode with two pronounced peaks at 0.84 and 0.70 V during the initial discharge contributes the stepwise intercalation of Na<sup>+</sup> into the interlayer of MoS<sub>2</sub> (Figure S7a, Supporting Information).<link href="#smll201805405-bib-0010"/> Followed by a sharp peak at 0.08 V is attributed to the conversion of MoS<sub>2</sub>. In subsequent cycling, two weak peaks appear at 0.21 and 0.03 V, neither is observed in the as‐prepared MoS<sub>2</sub> electrode, while they are close to the peak potential of deep sodiation of MoO<sub>3</sub> (Figure S7b,c, Supporting Information). Thus, these two peaks are attributed to the Na<sup>+</sup> storage in the oxide layer of the BD‐MoS<sub>2</sub>. Furthermore, the ex situ XRD demonstrates Na<sup>+</sup> storage in the BD‐MoS<sub>2</sub> electrode with a reversible conversion process (Figure S8, Supporting Information).</p><figure xml:id="smll201805405-fig-0002"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll201805405:smll201805405-fig-0002" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll201805405-fig-0002-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Comparison of a) charge/discharge profile, b) cycle performance, and c) rate capacity for the BD‐MoS<sub>2</sub> and as‐prepared MoS<sub>2</sub> electrodes. d) Rate corresponding typical discharge curves and its average potential (inset). e) Long‐term cycling for the BD‐MoS<sub>2</sub> electrode at 2 and 5 A g<sup>−1</sup>.</caption></figure><p>The BD‐MoS<sub>2</sub> electrode maintains an integrated structure after cycling, enabling a long cycle life (Figure S9, Supporting Information).<link href="#smll201805405-bib-0023"/> It can deliver a capacity of 354 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.5 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 100 cycles with an average coulombic efficiency (CE) of 98.5% (Figure <link href="#smll201805405-fig-0002"/>b). We can see that from the 10th to 50th cycle, the capacity of the BD‐MoS<sub>2</sub> electrode increases to a peak of 380 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, indicating a cycling‐induced activation behavior occurred.<link href="#smll201805405-bib-0012"/> By contrast, the as‐prepared MoS<sub>2</sub> electrode only retains 45 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 100 cycles. In the first 50 cycles, its CE fluctuates significantly due to structural adjustment. Finally, the as‐prepared MoS<sub>2</sub> electrode presents a destroyed morphology of needle‐like clusters (Figure S10, Supporting Information). The stability of BD‐MoS<sub>2</sub> electrode is attributed to the bundling architecture, which effectively reduces the stack of MoS<sub>2</sub>. To confirm the effect of MoO<sub>3</sub>, BD‐MoS<sub>2</sub> is treated by 0.5 mol L<sup>−1</sup> NaOH to dissolved MoO<sub>3</sub>. Consequently, the electrode removed MoO<sub>3</sub> shows a severe decay of capacity in the first 100 cycles (Figure S11, Supporting Information). Besides, the MoO<sub>3</sub> electrode as a reference exhibits a low capacity of 81 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> because the inherent property of MoO<sub>3</sub> limits the storage of Na<sup>+</sup> (Figure S12, Supporting Information). Thus, the capacity of BD‐MoS<sub>2</sub> electrode mainly contributes to MoS<sub>2</sub>. Figure <link href="#smll201805405-fig-0002"/>c depicts the promising rate performance of the BD‐MoS<sub>2</sub> electrode. It delivers the rate‐capacities of 411, 380, 345, 319, 279, and 262 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at the current densities of 0.1, 0.2, 0.5, 1, 2, and 5 A g<sup>−1</sup>. When the current density turns back to 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>, the capacity can be immediately recovered to 430 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, revealing that the electrodes can tolerate ultrafast charge/discharge process.<link href="#smll201805405-bib-0003"/> Impressively, when the current density increases from 2 to 5 A g<sup>−1</sup>, the decreased capacity is only 6.1%. The capacity of the as‐prepared MoS<sub>2</sub> electrode is comparable to BD‐MoS<sub>2</sub> at low current density of 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>, while its capacity decays rapidly with the doubling current density. From the detailed discharge curves, the BD‐MoS<sub>2</sub> electrode is changed slightly, and exhibits higher average discharge potential with the increase of current density, which indicate a faster kinetics of Na<sup>+</sup> storage (Figure <link href="#smll201805405-fig-0002"/>d). Naturally, the BD‐MoS<sub>2</sub> electrode achieves an excellent recycling capability at high current density of 2 A g<sup>−1</sup>, illustrating a high specific capacity of 350 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 1000 cycles (Figure <link href="#smll201805405-fig-0002"/>e). Even cycled at 5 A g<sup>−1</sup>, it retains the capacity of 272 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 1000 cycles with the average CE up to 99.6%. In other words, the BD‐MoS<sub>2</sub> electrode delivers a highly reversible capacity of 272 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> in just about 3 min (Figure S13, Supporting Information). The BD‐MoS<sub>2</sub> electrode with such high energy density and cyclic stability is among the more promising reported by comparing with MoS<sub>2</sub>‐based electrodes in literatures (Figure S14 and Table S1, Supporting Information).<link href="#smll201805405-bib-0011 #smll201805405-bib-0012 #smll201805405-bib-0019 #smll201805405-bib-0026"/></p><p>Sweep voltammetry and electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) technologies were applied to investigate Na<sup>+</sup> storage behavior in BD‐MoS<sub>2</sub> electrode. Based on previous researches, Na<sup>+</sup> storage mechanism of nanostructured particles always contains faradaic and non‐faradaic processes.<link href="#smll201805405-bib-0012 #smll201805405-bib-0027"/> The faradaic process exists both in the bulk as a battery process and on the surface as pseudocapacitance, and the latter is mainly found in the double layer capacitors. It is well known from Equation <link href="#smll201805405-disp-0004"/><displayedItem xml:id="smll201805405-disp-0004" type="mathematics" numbered="yes"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" display="block" altimg="urn:x-wiley:dummy:media:smll201805405:smll201805405-math-0004" wiley:location="graphic/smll201805405-math-0004.png"><mrow><mi>I</mi><mo>=</mo><mi>a</mi><msup><mi>v</mi><mi>b</mi></msup></mrow></math></displayedItem>where <i>I</i> is the current, <i>a</i> is a constant, <i>v</i> is the scan rate, and <i>b</i> is interrelated with the solvated storage mechanism. The <i>b</i>‐value is closer to 0.5, the system is mainly controlled by diffusion, while the <i>b</i>‐value is closer to 1, and the system is mainly pseudocapacitive‐controlled process. The sweep voltammetry curves of the BD‐MoS<sub>2</sub> electrode present analogous shapes and four redox peaks at various scanning rates range from 0.2 to 0.8 mV s<sup>−1</sup> (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll201805405-fig-0003"><b>3</b></link>a). The logarithm of the peak current density is proportional to the logarithm of the sweep rate, and the <i>b</i>‐values of the four redox peaks can be acquired as 1.02, 0.92, 0.94, 0.80 separately (Figure <link href="#smll201805405-fig-0003"/>b). Thus, the pseudocapacitive‐controlled Na<sup>+</sup> storage mechanism is more dominant in the BD‐MoS<sub>2</sub> electrode, which determines a fast kinetics of Na<sup>+</sup> storage (compared to the as‐prepared MoS<sub>2</sub> electrode; Figure S15a,b, Supporting Information).</p><figure xml:id="smll201805405-fig-0003"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll201805405:smll201805405-fig-0003" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll201805405-fig-0003-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) Sweep voltammetry curves and b) corresponding logarithm peak current versus logarithm scan rate plots (inset: calculated <i>b</i>‐values). c) Nyquist plots of the BD‐MoS<sub>2</sub> and as‐prepared MoS<sub>2</sub> electrodes (inset: equivalent circuit and typical Randles‐circuit values). d) Zre as a function of ω<sup>−1/2</sup> in the semi‐infinite region for the calculation of the Warburg coefficient (σ<sub>w</sub>).</caption></figure><p>Figure <link href="#smll201805405-fig-0003"/>c shows the EIS of the BD‐MoS<sub>2</sub> and as‐prepared MoS<sub>2</sub> electrodes after five cycles. The medium frequency region is assigned to the charge‐transfer resistance (<i>R</i><sub>ct</sub>) between active substance and electrolyte, and it correlates with the Fermi level and free‐electron concentration in MoS<sub>2</sub>.<link href="#smll201805405-bib-0028"/> Thus, the lower <i>R</i><sub>ct</sub> in the BD‐MoS<sub>2</sub> than as‐prepared MoS<sub>2</sub> electrode (7.1 Ω vs 28.4 Ω) indicates faster charge transfer. From Figure <link href="#smll201805405-fig-0003"/>d, the ω<sup>−1/2</sup> against <i>Z</i><sub>re</sub> with linear relation are drawn to estimate Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion coefficient.<link href="#smll201805405-bib-0029"/> The lower slope of σ<sub>w</sub> suggests a better Na<sup>+</sup> storage kinetics in BD‐MoS<sub>2</sub> electrode based on the diffusion formula (Figure S15, Supporting Information). After 100 cycles, the BD‐MoS<sub>2</sub> electrode still has a smaller impedance, while the impedance of the as‐prepared MoS<sub>2</sub> electrode significantly increases due to destroyed diffusion path of Na<sup>+</sup> (Figure S15c,d, Supporting Information).<link href="#smll201805405-bib-0023"/></p><p>Sodium‐ion storage in BD‐MoS<sub>2</sub> electrode with a faster kinetics can further explain by ex situ TEM and first principles theory to analyze the diffusion path and energy barrier of Na<sup>+</sup>.<link href="#smll201805405-bib-0011 #smll201805405-bib-0030"/> We recorded high‐resolution TEM images of the BD‐MoS<sub>2</sub> electrode discharged to 0.65 V, and the voltage is in the process of Na<sup>+</sup> intercalation into MoS<sub>2</sub>. From <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll201805405-fig-0004"><b>4</b></link>a,b, the MoS<sub>2</sub> layers are more expanded than the original lattice fringes. It is worth noting that the spacing is expanded to 0.89 nm in the defective lattice, while the spacing of indefective lattice is 0.68 nm, suggesting Na<sup>+</sup> is easier to diffuse into the interlayer with vacancies. Previous work shows that when Na<sup>+</sup> inserts into interlayer of 2H‐MoS<sub>2</sub>, a phase transition occurs from 2H to 1T (Figure <link href="#smll201805405-fig-0004"/>c).<link href="#smll201805405-bib-0031"/> From high‐resolution TEM along (001) plane, the phase of 1T‐MoS<sub>2</sub> around the vacancies was observed, which further confirming Na<sup>+</sup> can insert into MoS<sub>2</sub> interlayer by vacancies (Figure <link href="#smll201805405-fig-0004"/>d). After the repeating charge/discharge, the large vacancies can still be observed (Figure S16, Supporting Information).</p><figure xml:id="smll201805405-fig-0004"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll201805405:smll201805405-fig-0004" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll201805405-fig-0004-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a,b) High‐resolution TEM images of the BD‐MoS<sub>2</sub> electrode after discharge to 0.65 V. c) Schematic of the phase transition from 2H‐MoS<sub>2</sub> to 1T‐MoS<sub>2</sub> during intercalation process. d) High‐resolution TEM images and fast‐Fourier‐transform pattern selecting from it.</caption></figure><p>Based on the density functional theory (DFT), the diffusion barrier of Na<sup>+</sup> in MoS<sub>2</sub> is simulated. In order to simplify the calculation, we considered monolayer MoS<sub>2</sub> including three models of nondefective MoS<sub>2</sub>, defective MoS<sub>2</sub> with a vacancy, and defective MoS<sub>2</sub> with two vacancies (Figures S17 and S18, Supporting Information).<link href="#smll201805405-bib-0032"/> The calculated energy barrier of Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion between two adjacent Mo adsorption sites along the nondefective MoS<sub>2</sub> plane is 0.084 eV, while Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion across the nondefective MoS<sub>2</sub> plane should overcome an extremely high activation energy barrier of 14.31 eV, indicating Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion across the nondefective MoS<sub>2</sub> plane is absolutely forbidden (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll201805405-fig-0005"><b>5</b></link>a, Figure S19 in the Supporting Information). Therefore, Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion in the indefective MoS<sub>2</sub> materials is two‐dimensional. For the defective MoS<sub>2</sub> with one vacancy, the activation energy barriers of Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion across the (002) plan along the 5↔6 and 5↔7 are 1.470 and 1.478 eV (Figure <link href="#smll201805405-fig-0005"/>b, Figure S20 in the Supporting Information). The activation energy barriers of ≈1.470 eV for Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion across the defective MoS<sub>2</sub> plane are much lower than indefective MoS<sub>2</sub> monolayer, suggesting Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion across the defective MoS<sub>2</sub> plane is much more likely. The activation energy barriers for Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion across the defective MoS<sub>2</sub> with two vacancies are located at the range of 0.291–0.661 eV, which are much smaller than those with the one vacancy defective MoS<sub>2</sub> monolayer (Figure <link href="#smll201805405-fig-0005"/>c, Figure S21 in the Supporting Information). We believe that if the vacancy holes are larger, the activation energy barrier of Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion across the defective MoS<sub>2</sub> plane is lower. Thus, Na<sup>+</sup> can achieves a 3D diffusion in the defective MoS<sub>2</sub>.</p><figure xml:id="smll201805405-fig-0005"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll201805405:smll201805405-fig-0005" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll201805405-fig-0005-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>The pathways of the lowest Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion barrier across a) the indefective MoS<sub>2</sub> monolayer, b) defective MoS<sub>2</sub> with one vacancy, and c) two vacancies and their corresponding potential energy curves. The purple, yellow, and green balls represent Mo, S, and Na, respectively.</caption></figure><p>In conclusion, the bundled defect‐rich MoS<sub>2</sub> has been prepared by a scalable quenching process. When employed the BD‐MoS<sub>2</sub> as working electrode for SIB, a pseudocapacitive‐controlled Na<sup>+</sup> storage mechanism determines fast kinetics with a high rate capability. Further ex situ TEM and DFT calculation demonstrate that Na<sup>+</sup> can insert into interlayer of MoS<sub>2</sub> and diffuse between the layers through the vacancies rather than only limited to along the (002) plane. Thus, Na<sup>+</sup> intercalation in BD‐MoS<sub>2</sub> electrode is a 3D diffusion process, which implies shorter and more paths for fast kinetics. It is impressive that at high current densities from 2 to 5 A g<sup>−1</sup>, the capacity decay is only 6.1%. Moreover, the bundled architecture with decreased surface energy prevents the stack of MoS<sub>2</sub> sheets enabling a superior cycle life over 1000 cycles to be a promising anode for SIB. This work also suggests an approach to enhanced rate for TMDs that are not limited to MoS<sub>2</sub>.</p></section><section xml:id="smll201805405-sec-0020" type="acknowledgments"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>This work was proudly supported by the Shaanxi Natural Science Foundation of China No. 2017JM5006; Academic Team funding No. XSD1445; Provincial Key Academic Leaders Scientific Research Foundation No. BJ15‐01; the State Key Laboratory of Solidification Processing in NWPU No. SKLSP201617, and the Natural Science Advance Research Foundation of Shaanxi University of Science and Technology No. 2016QNBJ‐08.</p></section><section xml:id="smll201805405-sec-0030" type="conflictOfInterest"><title type="main">Conflict of Interest</title><p>The authors declare no conflict of interest.</p></section><bibliography xml:id="smll201805405-bibl-0001" style="numbered"><bib xml:id="smll201805405-bib-0001"><citation type="journal" xml:id="smll201805405-cit-0001">a) <author><givenNames>D.</givenNames> <familyName>Bruce</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>K.</givenNames> <familyName>Haresh</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>T.</givenNames> <familyName>Jean‐Marie</familyName></author>, <journalTitle>Science</journalTitle> <pubYear year="2011">2011</pubYear>, <vol>334</vol>, <pageFirst>928</pageFirst>;</citation><citation type="journal" xml:id="smll201805405-cit-0002">b) <author><givenNames>S.
