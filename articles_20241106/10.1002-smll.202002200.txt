information.</caption></supportingInfoItem></supportingInformation><abstractGroup><abstract type="main" xml:lang="en"><title type="main">Abstract</title><p>Embedding the functional nanostructures into a lightweight nanocarbon framework is very promising for developing high performance advanced electrodes for rechargeable batteries. Here, to realize workable capacity, core–shell (FeSe<sub>2</sub>/C) nanostructures are embedded into carbon nanotube (CNT) framework via a facile wet‐chemistry approach accompanied by thermally induced selenization. The CNT framework offers 3D continuous routes for electronic/ionic transfer, while macropores provide adequate space for high mass loading of FeSe<sub>2</sub>/C. However, the carbon shell not only creates a solid electronic link among CNTs and FeSe<sub>2</sub> but also improves the diffusivity of sodium ions into FeSe<sub>2</sub>, as well as acts as a buffer cushion to accommodate the volume variations. These unique structural features of CNT/FeSe<sub>2</sub>/C make it an excellent host for sodium storage with a capacity retention of 546 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> even after 100 cycles at 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. Moreover, areal and volumetric capacities of 5.06 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup> and 158 mAh cm<sup>−3</sup> are also achieved at high mass loading 16.9 mg cm<sup>−2</sup>, respectively. The high performance of multi‐benefited engineered structure makes it a potential candidate for secondary ion batteries, while its easy synthesis makes it extendable to further complex structures with other morphologies (such as nanorods, nanowires, etc.) to meet the high energy demands.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="smll202002200-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>A free standing electrode (CNT/FeSe<sub>2</sub>/C) with double cage protection is presented for sodium ion battery (SIB). The high porosity in the CNT/FeSe<sub>2</sub>/C structure results in densified framework upon compaction while dual carbon protection buffers the volume changes upon battery cycling. These features make CNT/FeSe<sub>2</sub>/C anode for practical application with high gravimetric, areal, and volumetric capacities along with good cyclic stability.<blockFixed xml:id="smll202002200-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="smll202002200-sec-0010"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Sodium ion battery (SIB) is considered a good alternative to the lithium ion battery (LIB) in terms of its similar charge storage mechanism but at a lower cost (estimated cost reduction of 4.3%)<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202002200-bib-0001"><sup>1</sup></link><sup>]</sup> due to the natural abundance of Na (20 ppm for Li and 23 600 ppm for Na).<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202002200-bib-0002 #smll202002200-bib-0003"><sup>2–3</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, many factors such as the small charge storage capacity, low mass loading, and sluggish kinetics of Na ion in current electrode materials limit its practical applications.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202002200-bib-0004 #smll202002200-bib-0005 #smll202002200-bib-0006"><sup>4–6</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Various anode chemistries have been explored, which are classified into three main groups based on their charge storage mechanism, that is, intercalation‐type (e.g., graphite, layered materials), alloy‐type (e.g., Sn, Si, Ge), and conversion‐type (e.g., Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>, FeS<sub>2</sub>, MoS<sub>2</sub>).<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202002200-bib-0007 #smll202002200-bib-0008"><sup>7–8</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Unfortunately, limited space in intercalation materials and huge volume changes in alloying hosts put them behind the most favorable conversion chemistry due to their large capacity and low mechanical strains. Therefore, facile approaches need to be developed for designing new electrode materials based on conversion‐type chemistries which are appropriate to realize the storage ability of SIBs comparable to that of LIBs.</p><p>Recently, transition metal selenides (TMSe) are found highly suitable for sodium storage due to their large theoretical capacities (&gt;450 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>) and comparably high conductivities.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202002200-bib-0009 #smll202002200-bib-0010"><sup>9–10</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Moreover, TMSe did not suffer from the poly‐sulfide anion shuttle effect that is normally associated with sulfide type anodes.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202002200-bib-0011"><sup>11</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Taking an example of TMSe, FeSe<sub>2</sub> is highly attractive as an anode for SIBs due to a large abundance of Fe, low toxicity, environment benignity, good chemical stability, selenium‐rich structure and high theoretical capacity (501.5 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>).<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202002200-bib-0012"><sup>12</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, FeSe<sub>2</sub> also possesses low intrinsic conductivity and suffers from large volume variations. These issues become crucial as high mass loading is required for commercial applications; therefore, to have control over structural pulverization and formation of unstable and inhomogeneous solid electrolyte interface (SEI) film, better designing of electrode materials are needed.</p><p>Until now, a lot of efforts have been devoted to overcoming these issues by developing various compositions of FeSe<sub>2−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i> in different morphologies, moreover, blended with various materials.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202002200-bib-0013 #smll202002200-bib-0014 #smll202002200-bib-0015 #smll202002200-bib-0016"><sup>13–16</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, higher surface area increases the chances for agglomeration of these nanostructured materials as well as causes intensive side reactions during the sodiation/de‐sodiation process.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202002200-bib-0017"><sup>17</sup></link><sup>]</sup> An inner conductive coating over the surface is a well‐accepted approach to alleviate this issue, which is favorable in preventing side reactions, reducing inter‐particle resistance and agglomeration.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202002200-bib-0018 #smll202002200-bib-0019"><sup>18–19</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, blending of this conductive powder with binder to construct an electrode reduces the overall conductivity of electrode, thus abolishing the benefit of additional coating. Moreover, such designing of electrode materials limits the mass loadings to very low value (≤1.5 mg cm<sup>−2</sup>), resulting in low areal and volumetric capacities.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202002200-bib-0020"><sup>20</sup></link><sup>]</sup> To circumvent these issues, impeding of various nanostructures (nanowires, nanosheets, or nanoparticles) inside the 3D carbon matrix is an effective approach.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202002200-bib-0021 #smll202002200-bib-0022 #smll202002200-bib-0023"><sup>21–23</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, the direct incorporation of active materials into the carbon matrix may cause a detachment during the electrochemical process and result in agglomeration, hence capacity fading. Thus, a strong pining point needs to develop like individual carbon cage for each nanostructure inside the 3D carbon matrix, may resolve all issues, and result in free‐standing ready to use electrodes.</p><p>Although, layered transition metal chalcogenides (TMCs) have been deposited on carbon nanotube (CNT)<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202002200-bib-0024"><sup>24</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and carbon nanofiber (CNF),<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202002200-bib-0025"><sup>25</sup></link><sup>]</sup> but uniform deposition of highly crystalline nanoparticles (NPs) of non‐layered TMCs on 3D porous CNT without any aggregation is a big challenge due to several factors: (i) poor penetration of various precursors (Fe and Se precursors) solutions into the 3D CNT framework (ii) weak physical bonding of active material with CNT, and (iii) active material must possess adequate surface and adsorption energies to attach on the solid substrate. Besides, previously carbon coating on simple systems or layered materials was carried out which is easy,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202002200-bib-0024 #smll202002200-bib-0025"><sup>24–25</sup></link><sup>]</sup> but to realize it on complex foam especially coated with particles as NPs coating provides variable nucleation site therefore homogenous coating of amorphous carbon is difficult.</p><p>Herein, an easy and stepwise methodology is developed to realize a cage‐inside‐a‐cage to overcome most of the challenges associated with SIBs, while active material is decorated in the core cage through a simple method. A double cage protection and a free‐standing electrode is fabricated in three steps: (i) hydrothermal grafting of Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> onto the CNT framework, (ii) thermal‐induced selenized conversion of Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> into FeSe<sub>2,</sub> and (iii) coating of an amorphous carbon layer over FeSe<sub>2</sub> NPs and spreading onto individual CNTs by carbonization (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll202002200-fig-0001"><b>1</b></link>). (iv) the thickness of amorphous shell was kept as low as possible so it allows faster ionic flow and provides sufficient electronic conductivity. Moreover, the incorporation of FeSe<sub>2</sub>/C into the CNT does not affect the structural integrity of the hybrid thus, retaining the ability to greatly improve the Na<sup>+</sup> ion storage. High porosity between the individual CNTs offers large mass loadings while the intimate contact between the carbon core and FeSe<sub>2</sub> shell controls the SEI film and buffers the volume expansion/contraction. The highly compressible and 3D porous CNT/FeSe<sub>2</sub>/C hybrid could be densified to obtain ultra‐high areal mass loadings up to 16.9 mg cm<sup>−2</sup> corresponding to areal capacity of 5.06 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup>. It therefore highlights the benefits of easy structural manipulation to develop high performing electrode materials for future energy storage systems.</p><figure xml:id="smll202002200-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll202002200:smll202002200-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll202002200-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Fabrication process of CNT/FeSe<sub>2</sub>/C.</caption></figure></section><section xml:id="smll202002200-sec-0020"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p>To obtain 3D porous CNT/FeSe<sub>2</sub>/C hybrid, initially, Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> NPs are impulsively grown and grafted on the individual CNT (CNT/Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>) via a hydrothermal process, where CNTs are present in the form of a 3D framework. Afterward, a thermal‐induced selenization was carried out to convert solid Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> NPs to porous FeSe<sub>2</sub> NPs at the surface of CNTs, so that the larger ions can easily move in and out of NPs. At high temperature, that is, 500 °C, Se powder reacted with incoming H<sub>2</sub> gas to produce H<sub>2</sub>Se gas (Equation (<link href="#smll202002200-disp-0001">1</link>)), which is a strong reducing agent and spontaneously transformed the Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> NPs into FeSe<sub>2</sub> NPs via Kirkendall diffusion effect (Equation (<link href="#smll202002200-disp-0002">2</link>)).<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202002200-bib-0026"><sup>26</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In the end, an amorphous carbon layer is encapsulated on the CNT/FeSe<sub>2</sub> simply by the glucose carbonization process. Further, three composites with different amorphous carbon contents of 0 wt% (CNT/FeSe<sub>2</sub>), 8.0 wt% (CNT/FeSe<sub>2</sub>/C‐1) and 17.6 wt% (CNT/FeSe<sub>2</sub>/C‐2) were fabricated at optimized conditions to investigate the influence of amorphous carbon coating on anode performance by controlling the concentration of glucose in the precursor solution. The SEM image of CNTs/Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>, confirm the fine growth of Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> NPs on the surface of CNTs with an average size of ≈50 nm (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll202002200-fig-0002"><b>2</b>a</link>). It is also observed that Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> NPs are homogeneously distributed throughout the 3D CNTs network. The flexible and mechanically robust nature of CNTs helped in the thermal conversion of Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> to FeSe<sub>2</sub> without adversely impacting on the framework structure (Figure <link href="#smll202002200-fig-0002">2b</link>). Figure <link href="#smll202002200-fig-0002">2c</link> reveals that carbon is uniformly coated on each FeSe<sub>2</sub> NPs as well as individual CNT to result in highly conductive and dually protected structure. The transmission electron microscope (TEM) results show that a porous structure is obtained with a little effect on the particle size after converting Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> (≈50 nm) into FeSe<sub>2</sub> (≈80 nm) due to the ripening of NPs under thermal selenization process (Figure <link href="#smll202002200-fig-0002">2d,e</link>). A TEM image of CNT/FeSe<sub>2</sub>/C indicates that FeSe<sub>2</sub> NPs are strongly attached to the CNT as evident from Figure <link href="#smll202002200-fig-0002">2f</link>, having an ≈2.8 nm thick amorphous carbon coat (Figure <link href="#smll202002200-fig-0002">2g,h</link>). A d‐spacing of 3.37 Å was observed in the high resolution TEM (HRTEM) image of CNT/FeSe<sub>2</sub>/C that corresponds to the (120) lattice plane of FeSe<sub>2</sub> (Figure <link href="#smll202002200-fig-0002">2g</link>). The crystal structure of the hybrid frameworks at each stage during the preparation of CNT/FeSe<sub>2</sub>/C was analyzed by X‐ray diffraction (XRD). Figure S2, Supporting Information shows the XRD patterns of the CNT/Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> and is indexed well to the maghemite‐C phase of Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> (JCPDS # 39–1346), revealing crystalline growth of Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> NPs. The XRD patterns of CNT/FeSe<sub>2</sub> and CNT/FeSe<sub>2</sub>/C are shown in Figure <link href="#smll202002200-fig-0002">2i</link>. All characteristic peaks in both hybrids are well consistent with the orthorhombic phase of FeSe<sub>2</sub> (JCPDS # 82–0269) with preferential growth along (120) plane. In addition to the characteristic peaks of orthorhombic FeSe<sub>2</sub>, only one peak at 27.3° that corresponds to the (002) plane of the CNT is seen, delineating high crystallinity of FeSe<sub>2</sub> and purity of the composites. Further, we have calculated the rate capability of the CNT/FeSe<sub>2</sub>/C anode at the various areal mass loading ranging from 6.32 to 16.9 mg cm<sup>−2</sup> (Figure <link href="#smll202002200-fig-0005">5e</link>). Specifically, at very high areal mass loading of 16.9 mg cm<sup>−2</sup>, when the specific current density gradually ramped up, areal capacities of 6.11, 5.04, 3.55, 2.58, and 1.33 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup> were reversibly measured at 100, 200, 400, 600, and 800 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively, where reduction in capacity is a common trend due to higher current density. However, when the specific current density reverted to 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, excellent areal capacity retention of 5.26 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup> was achieved demonstrating excellent rate performance of the electrode. Such good rate performance at ultra‐high mass loading is very important for practical applications such as in high energy and power density batteries.</p><p>In addition, volumetric capacity was calculated based on the density of the electrodes according to this formula: ρ = <i>m</i>/<i>A</i> × <i>h</i>, where <i>m</i>, ρ, <i>A</i>, and <i>h</i> are the mass, density, base area, and thickness of the framework, respectively. We obtained volumetric capacities of 158 mAh cm<sup>−3</sup> for the most densified and the thickest framework corresponding to a density of 528 mg cm<sup>−3</sup> (Figure S10, Supporting information). The high areal and volumetric capacities are attributed to dual protective carbon network and densified framework.</p><p>To understand the better electrochemical properties in CNT/FeSe<sub>2</sub>/C compared with CNT/FeSe<sub>2</sub>, CV curves at different scan rates are performed (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll202002200-fig-0006"><b>6</b>a,b</link>). It reveals the same shape during reduction and oxidation processes, but a peak shift is observed as the scan rate increases, showing small polarization at higher scan rates. According to previous reports,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202002200-bib-0012 #smll202002200-bib-0039 #smll202002200-bib-0040"><sup>12,39,40</sup></link><sup>]</sup> the peak current (<i>i</i>) and scan rate (<i>v</i>) are obeyed by the Equation <i>i</i> = <i>av<sup>b</sup></i>. The value of <i>b</i> is important to determine the electrochemical behavior (i.e., if <i>b</i> ≈ 0.5, then the charge storage behavior is dominated by an ionic diffusion controlled process and if <i>b</i> ≈ 1.0, it represents a capacitive process).<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202002200-bib-0040"><sup>40</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The <i>b</i> value for different peaks can be attained by the slope of the log(<i>i</i>) versus log(<i>v</i>) graph (Figure <link href="#smll202002200-fig-0006">6c</link>, Figure S11a, Supporting Information). Specifically, the contribution of the capacitive and ionic diffusion at a given potential can be computed via the relationship <i>i</i>(<i>V</i>) = <i>k</i><sub>1</sub><i>v</i> + <i>k</i><sub>2</sub><i>v</i><sup>1/2</sup>, where <i>k</i><sub>1</sub><i>v</i> and <i>k</i><sub>2</sub><i>v</i><sup>1/2</sup> are respectively, the capacitive and ionic diffusion contributions. The <i>k</i><sub>1</sub> and <i>k</i><sub>2</sub> values can be determined by the slope and intercept of <i>v</i><sup>1/2</sup> verses <i>iv<sup>−</sup></i><sup>1/2</sup> plot (Figure <link href="#smll202002200-fig-0006">6d</link>, Figure S11b, Supporting Information). Under the scan rate of 0.8 mV s<sup>−1</sup>, the separations of capacitive and ionic diffusion contribution are presented in Figure <link href="#smll202002200-fig-0006">6e</link> and Figure S11c, Supporting Information, and the share of the capacitive contribution in the total charge storage of the CNT/FeSe<sub>2</sub>/C and CNT/FeSe<sub>2</sub> is 82% and 78%, respectively, indicating a higher rate capability in CNT/FeSe<sub>2</sub>/C than CNT/FeSe<sub>2</sub>. Moreover, as expected, with an increasing scan rate, the capacitive contribution increases gradually for both electrodes (Figure <link href="#smll202002200-fig-0006">6f</link>, Figure S11d, Supporting Information).</p><figure xml:id="smll202002200-fig-0006"><label>6</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll202002200:smll202002200-fig-0006" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll202002200-fig-0006-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Quantitative pseudo‐capacitive analysis: a,b) CV profiles at various scan rates for CNT/FeSe<sub>2</sub>/C, and CNT/FeSe<sub>2</sub>, respectively c) Relationship between Logarithm scan rate and Logarithm redox peak currents of CNT/FeSe<sub>2</sub>/C, d) Plots of (scan rate)<sup>1/2</sup> verses <i>i</i> (scan rate)<sup>−1/2</sup> used to compute constants <i>k</i><sub>1</sub> and <i>k</i><sub>2</sub> at different potentials e) Capacitive contribution at 0.8 mV s<sup>−1</sup> scan rate. f) Percentage of capacitive contribution at various scan rates.</caption></figure></section><section xml:id="smll202002200-sec-0030"><title type="main">Conclusions</title><p>In summary, a cage‐on‐cage approach is developed for SIB electrode materials made of the porous CNT/FeSe<sub>2</sub>/C framework. A simple three‐step approach is designed, where initially Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> NPs were decorated into the CNT framework by a simple hydrothermal process, which then successfully converted into FeSe<sub>2</sub> by thermal‐induced selenization route and finally, an amorphous carbon coat is constructed over each FeSe<sub>2</sub> NPs and individual CNT. When used as an anode for SIB, the CNT/FeSe<sub>2</sub>/C delivered a reversible gravimetric capacity of 546 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup> after 100 cycles. Even at a high current density of 500 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, a discharge capacity of 423 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> is maintained. In addition to the sustained gravimetric performance, an areal capacity of 5.06 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup> was achieved at an ultra‐high mass loading (16.9 mg cm<sup>−2</sup>). The overall high performance and good stability, including gravimetric capacity, high material loading, and areal capacity are attributed to synergistic effects of densely packed structure and dual conductive carbon network.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202002200-sec-0040"><title type="main">Experimental Section</title><section xml:id="smll202002200-sec-0050" numbered="no"><section xml:id="smll202002200-sec-0060" numbered="no"><section xml:id="smll202002200-sec-0070" numbered="no"><title type="main">Fabrication of CNT/Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub></title><p>All the reagents and chemicals were analytical grade and used without further purification. First, the CNT framework was synthesized by a CVD method using a precursor solution containing 1,2‐dichlorobenzene as a carbon source and ferrocene as a catalyst reported by Gui et al.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202002200-bib-0041"><sup>41</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Second, 0.4 <sc>m</sc> Fe(NO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>3</sub>·9H<sub>2</sub>O and urea were dissolved in Dimethylformamide (DMF) by magnetic stirring at room temperature. Then a piece of already fabricated CNT framework (40 mg) in square shape was immersed into the above solution for 15 min and then transferred into an autoclave for the execution of hydrothermal reaction at 150 °C for 25 h. After complete reaction, the hybrid framework was washed many times with deionized (DI) water and ethanol and subsequently, freeze‐dried for further use.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202002200-sec-0080" numbered="no"><title type="main">Fabrication of CNT/FeSe<sub>2</sub></title><p>The CNT/FeSe<sub>2</sub> was prepared by the selenization of CNT/Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> into the furnace in a reduced atmosphere. Specifically, the CNT/Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> framework and Se powder with the ratio (1:3) were placed in the opposite corner of a glass tube of length 15 cm. This glass tube containing the hybrid framework and Se powder was placed at the center of the furnace tube and selenization was executed at 500 °C for 4 h. under the environment of H<sub>2</sub>/Ar (15 vol% of H<sub>2</sub>) gas at a ramping rate of 10 °C. After selenization, the tube furnace was naturally cooled down to room temperature and got the product CNT/FeSe<sub>2</sub>.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202002200-sec-0090" numbered="no"><title type="main">Fabrication of CNT/FeSe<sub>2</sub>/C Framework</title><p>The carbon was coated onto the CNT/FeSe<sub>2</sub> framework by a simple carbonization approach. Typically, the as‐prepared CNT/FeSe<sub>2</sub> framework was dipped into 0.1 <sc>m</sc> and 0.2 <sc>m</sc> aqueous glucose solution (40 mL) in the autoclave to synthesize CNT/FeSe<sub>2</sub>/C‐1 and CNT/FeSe<sub>2</sub>/C‐2, respectively. The autoclave was placed in the electric oven for the hydrothermal reaction at 180 °C for 10 h. The glucose coated CNT/FeSe<sub>2</sub> was washed several times with DI water and ethanol and then freeze‐dried. Last of all, the glucose coated CNT/FeSe<sub>2</sub> hybrid framework was converted into carbon coated CNT/FeSe<sub>2</sub> framework by an annealing process at 450 °C for 2 h. in an Ar environment.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202002200-sec-0100" numbered="no"><title type="main">Materials Characterizations</title><p>The structural and morphological characterizations of all the frameworks were carried out using SEM (NOVA SEM) and TEM (FEI Tacnai T20). The crystalline phase information was observed by a powder X‐ray diffractometer (Bruker D8) using Cu K<sub>α</sub> (1.5406 °A) radiation. Raman spectra were recorded via a Raman microscope (Renishaw) equipped with laser light of 532 nm wavelength for excitation. The XPS measurements were calculated on Kratos Axis Ultra monochromatized Al K<sub>α</sub> radiation to study the chemical valence state and surface element composition and chemical valence of the hybrid. The carbon content in each hybrid framework was determined by TGA in the air with a heating rate of 10 °C min<sup>−1</sup>. The specific surface area and pore size distribution were determined using the N<sub>2</sub> sorption test in the Quantachrome autosorb‐IQ gas adsorption analyzer at 77 K.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202002200-sec-0110" numbered="no"><title type="main">Electrochemical Testing</title><p>The battery performance was evaluated using a coin cell CR 2032 battery. The coin cells were manufactured in the Ar glove box with hybrid frameworks as the working electrode, and sodium foil as the reference/counter electrode. The working electrode was used as a free‐standing electrode without any passive components (binders, conductive agent, and current collectors). 1.0 <sc>m</sc> NaClO<sub>4</sub> in propylene carbonate (PC) = 100 vol% with 5% ethylene carbonate (EC) was used as the electrolyte, and the glass fiber used as the separator. The galvanostatic charge/discharge tests were performed using the LAND CT 2001A analyzer in the voltage range of 0.01–3.0 V. The CV and electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) measurements were carried out on an electrochemical work station (CHI660E).</p></section></section></section></section><section xml:id="smll202002200-sec-0120" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>This work was financially supported by the Key Area R&amp;D Program of Guangdong Province (2018B030327001, 2018B010109009) and the Natural Science Foundation of China (51732005, 51772008, 51672007, and 11974023).
