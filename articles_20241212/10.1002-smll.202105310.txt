type="main">Abstract</title><p>The enhancement of the structural stability of conversion‐based metal sulfides at high current densities remains a major challenge in realizing the practical application of sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs). The instability of metal sulfides is caused by the large volume variation and sluggish reaction kinetics upon sodiation/desodiation. To overcome this, herein, a heterostructured nanocube anode composed of CuS/FeS<sub>2</sub> embedded in nitrogen‐doped carbon (CuS/FeS<sub>2</sub>@NC) is synthesized. Size‐ and shape‐controlled porous carbon nanocubes containing metallic nanoparticles are synthesized by the two‐step pyrolysis of a bimetallic Prussian blue analog (PBA) precursor. The simple sulfurization‐induced formation of highly conductive CuS along with FeS<sub>2</sub> facilitates sodium‐ion diffusion and enhances the redox reversibility upon cycling. The mesoporous carbon structure provides excellent electrolyte impregnation, efficient charge transport pathways, and good volume expansion buffering. The CuS/FeS<sub>2</sub>@NC nanocube anode exhibits excellent sodium storage characteristics including high desodiation capacity (608 mAh g<sup>–1</sup> at 0.2 A g<sup>–1</sup>), remarkable long‐term cycle life (99.1% capacity retention after 300 cycles at 5 A g<sup>–1</sup>), and good rate capability up to 5 A g<sup>–1</sup>. The simple, facile synthetic route combined with the rational design of bimetallic PBA nanostructures can be widely utilized in the development of conversion‐based metal sulfides and other high‐capacity anode materials for high‐performance SIBs.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="smll202105310-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>A facile, effective nanoconfinement of intermixed CuS/FeS<sub>2</sub> in nitrogen‐doped carbon derived from bimetallic Prussian blue analogs (PBAs) enhances the structural stability and facilitates sodium‐ion diffusion by preventing particle aggregation and metal dissolution. These nanocubes demonstrate superior sodium‐ion storage performance and the synthetic approach based on bimetallic PBAs will promote the development of various heterostructured materials for energy storage applications.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll202105310:smll202105310-gra-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll202105310-gra-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body xml:id="smll202105310-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="smll202105310-sec-0010"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Recently, several next‐generation batteries have been developed with high energy/power density, cost effectiveness, and scale‐up feasibility, to meet the requirements of medium‐ to large‐scale applications. Among these batteries, sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) are considered one of the most promising alternatives to conventional lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs), particularly in terms of cost, because sodium‐containing resources are earth abundant and inexpensive.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202105310-bib-0001 #smll202105310-bib-0002 #smll202105310-bib-0003 #smll202105310-bib-0004"><sup>1–4</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, the ionic radius of sodium is larger than that of lithium (Na<sup>+</sup>: 1.02 Å versus Li<sup>+</sup>: 0.76 Å); therefore, SIBs suffer from slow reaction kinetics and cannot utilize graphite‐based anode materials currently used in LIBs.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202105310-bib-0005 #smll202105310-bib-0006 #smll202105310-bib-0007 #smll202105310-bib-0008"><sup>5–8</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In this regard, numerous attempts have been made to develop alternative anode materials and realize their practical implementation in SIBs.</p><p>Various metal sulfides have been gained attention as conversion‐based anode materials owing to their superior specific capacities, redox reversibility, and reaction kinetics compared to their oxide counterparts.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202105310-bib-0005 #smll202105310-bib-0009 #smll202105310-bib-0010 #smll202105310-bib-0011 #smll202105310-bib-0012"><sup>5,9–12</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Most metal sulfide anodes undergo reversible conversion reactions with sodium ions, as expressed in Equation (<link href="#smll202105310-disp-0001">1</link>):<displayedItem xml:id="smll202105310-disp-0001" type="mathematics"><label>1</label><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" display="block" altimg="urn:x-wiley:dummy:media:smll202105310:smll202105310-math-0001" wiley:location="graphic/smll202105310-math-0001.png"><mrow><mtable><mtr><mtd><mrow><msub><mi mathvariant="normal">M</mi><mi>y</mi></msub><msub><mi mathvariant="normal">S</mi><mi>x</mi></msub><mo>+</mo><mn>2</mn><mi>x</mi><msup><mtext>Na</mtext><mo>+</mo></msup><mo>→</mo><mi>x</mi><msub><mtext>Na</mtext><mn>2</mn></msub><mi mathvariant="normal">S</mi><mo>+</mo><mi>y</mi><mi mathvariant="normal">M</mi></mrow></mtd></mtr></mtable></mrow></math></displayedItem>where M denotes a transition metal, such as Cu, Fe, Ni, Mo, and Ti. According to Equation (<link href="#smll202105310-disp-0001">1</link>), one mole of sulfur in metal sulfides (M<i><sub>y</sub></i>S<i><sub>x</sub></i>) undergoes electrochemical reactions with two moles of sodium ions, thereby making the theoretical capacity of metal sulfides considerably higher than that of intercalation‐based anode materials.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202105310-bib-0013"><sup>13</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Among the diverse types of metal sulfides, FeS<sub>2</sub> has several advantages as an anode material, such as its low cost, nontoxicity, and high theoretical capacity of 894 mAh g<sup>–1</sup>.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202105310-bib-0014 #smll202105310-bib-0015 #smll202105310-bib-0016 #smll202105310-bib-0017 #smll202105310-bib-0018"><sup>14–18</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Despite these advantages, it suffers from large volume variation (expansion and contraction), which causes severe capacity fading owing to the pulverization of active materials and the structural failure of the electrode over repeated cycling.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202105310-bib-0019"><sup>19</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In addition, FeS<sub>2</sub> exhibits poor rate performance and redox reversibility owing to its intrinsically low electrical conductivity.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202105310-bib-0020"><sup>20</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>Previous efforts to improve the electrochemical characteristics of FeS<sub>2</sub> have mainly focused on designing nanostructured composites with bifunctional carbon‐based materials that offer high conductivity and serve as a structure‐reinforcing buffer matrix against significant volume variation upon cycling.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202105310-bib-0021 #smll202105310-bib-0022"><sup>21,22</sup></link><sup>]</sup> For example, Chen et al. demonstrated that an FeS<sub>2</sub>/carbon nanotube neural network exhibited enhanced cycling stability with a reversible capacity of 394 mAh g<sup>–1</sup> after 400 cycles at 200 mA g<sup>–1</sup>.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202105310-bib-0014"><sup>14</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Yin et al. prepared FeS<sub>2</sub>@porous octahedral carbon derived from a metal‐organic framework, which showed an excellent rate capability of up to 5000 mA g<sup>–1</sup> and good cycle performance.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202105310-bib-0015"><sup>15</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Another promising and efficient strategy to attain superior electrochemical performance is the construction of heterostructured architectures.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202105310-bib-0023 #smll202105310-bib-0024 #smll202105310-bib-0025 #smll202105310-bib-0026 #smll202105310-bib-0027 #smll202105310-bib-0028 #smll202105310-bib-0029"><sup>23–29</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The realization of a heterogeneous structure composed of different constituents is known to provide rapid sodium‐ion diffusion channels and improved structural stability. Accordingly, several heterostructured binary metal sulfide anode materials, including Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>/FeS<sub>2</sub>, Ni<sub>3</sub>S<sub>2</sub>/MoS<sub>2</sub>, ZnS/SnS<sub>2</sub>, and Cu<sub>2</sub>S/MoS<sub>2</sub>, have demonstrated outstanding sodium‐ion storage performance.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202105310-bib-0023 #smll202105310-bib-0024 #smll202105310-bib-0027 #smll202105310-bib-0029"><sup>23,24,27,29</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, the fabrication of such heterostructures generally requires complex and multistep preparation processes, which impedes large‐scale synthesis and increases production costs.</p><p>Prussian blue analogs (PBAs) are coordination compounds comprising various types of metal ions and organic ligands.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202105310-bib-0030 #smll202105310-bib-0031"><sup>30,31</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The simple pyrolysis of PBAs in an inert atmosphere provides an inexpensive, scalable method for preparing metal‐containing carbon‐based materials, which can further be used as effective precursors for heterostructured metal sulfide materials with easily tunable morphologies and varied compositions. Although several PBA‐derived binary Mn/Fe‐ and Ni/Fe‐based metal sulfides have been investigated to improve the electrochemical performance of FeS<sub>2</sub> anodes in SIBs,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202105310-bib-0032 #smll202105310-bib-0033"><sup>32,33</sup></link><sup>]</sup> to the best of our knowledge, there have been no studies on the heterogeneous combination of FeS<sub>2</sub> and CuS, which provides high conductivity (10<sup>–3</sup> S cm<sup>–1</sup>) and enhanced sodiation/desodiation reversibility.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202105310-bib-0034 #smll202105310-bib-0035"><sup>34,35</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Inspired by the aforementioned strategy based on the versatile properties of bimetallic PBAs, herein, we designed and prepared heterostructured CuS/FeS<sub>2</sub> coupled with porous nitrogen‐doped carbon (denoted as CuS/FeS<sub>2</sub>@NC) from a bimetallic Cu/Fe‐PBA precursor. Size‐ and shape‐controlled Cu/Fe‐PBAs were synthesized for the first time via an easily scalable co‐precipitation technique using oxalic acid as a chelating agent. Subsequently, facile, simple annealing, and sulfurization were carried out, and the obtained CuS/FeS<sub>2</sub>@NC composite maintained the submicron‐sized, nanocube‐shaped characteristics of the Cu/Fe‐PBA precursor. The obtained composite possesses several properties which are beneficial in an anode material: i) a nitrogen‐doped porous carbon structure can offer improved ionic/electronic transport and act as a durable mechanical buffer against the large volume expansion of active materials and ii) the introduction of highly conductive CuS accelerates the sodium‐ion diffusion rate and electron transfer kinetics. Hence, the rationally designed CuS/FeS<sub>2</sub>@NC electrode provides superior sodium storage performance with a high capacity and long‐term cycling stability even at a high current density, making it a promising anode material for high‐performance SIBs.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202105310-sec-0020"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p>The preparation process of nanocube‐shaped CuS/FeS<sub>2</sub>@NC is illustrated in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll202105310-fig-0001"><b>1</b></link>. The Cu/Fe‐PBA precursor was synthesized via a simple, scalable co‐precipitation method at room temperature using oxalic acid as a chelating agent. The brown Cu/Fe‐PBA precipitates were analyzed by field‐emission scanning electron microscopy (FE‐SEM) and X‐ray diffraction (XRD) to verify the effects of oxalic acid on the synthesis of the materials. The Cu/Fe‐PBA precursor prepared without oxalic acid exhibited spherical particles with an average diameter of ≈50 nm (Figure <link href="#smll202105310-supitem-0001">S1a</link>, Supporting Information), whereas the precursor synthesized using oxalic acid had a cubic morphology with particle sizes in the range of 500–600 nm (Figure <link href="#smll202105310-supitem-0001">S1b</link>, Supporting Information). In the latter case, the chelating agent (oxalic acid) reduced the rate of the precipitation reaction between Cu<sup>2+</sup> and [Fe(CN)<sub>6</sub>]<sup>3–</sup>, thereby increasing the particle size. This result is similar to those of previous studies using sodium citrate.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202105310-bib-0036 #smll202105310-bib-0037"><sup>36,37</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Furthermore, the XRD patterns (Figure <link href="#smll202105310-supitem-0001">S1c</link>, Supporting Information) reveal that the reduction in the co‐precipitation rate improved the crystallinity of the Cu/Fe‐PBA precursor, which improved the dispersion of metallic nanoparticles (Cu and Fe) within the pyrolyzed porous carbon matrix during the subsequent thermal decomposition. The energy dispersive X‐ray spectroscopy (EDS) mapping images (Figure <link href="#smll202105310-supitem-0001">S2</link>, Supporting Information) clearly show the homogeneous distribution of elemental Cu, Fe, C, and N throughout the particle, which confirms the formation of the bimetallic Cu/Fe‐PBA precursor. The as‐prepared Cu/Fe‐PBA precursor was subjected to a two‐step heat treatment involving carbonization and sulfurization under an argon atmosphere to obtain the final heterostructured CuS/FeS<sub>2</sub>@NC nanocubes.</p><figure xml:id="smll202105310-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll202105310:smll202105310-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll202105310-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Schematic of preparation process of CuS/FeS<sub>2</sub>@NC nanocubes.</caption></figure><p>The particle size and morphology of the as‐prepared heterostructured composite (CuS/FeS<sub>2</sub>@NC) were observed by FE‐SEM analysis. The FE‐SEM image of CuS/FeS<sub>2</sub>@NC shows a nanocube‐like morphology with relatively uniform particle sizes ranging from ≈500–600 nm (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll202105310-fig-0002"><b>2</b>a</link>), which are comparable to those of the control sample (FeS<sub>2</sub>@NC) derived from the Fe/Fe‐PBA precursor (Figure <link href="#smll202105310-supitem-0001">S3a,b</link>, Supporting Information). Interestingly, the size and shape of the CuS/FeS<sub>2</sub>@NC are essentially identical to those of the Cu/Fe‐PBA precursor (Figure <link href="#smll202105310-supitem-0001">S1b</link>, Supporting Information), even after the sequential carbonization and sulfurization processes. Although the surface of the CuS/FeS<sub>2</sub>@NC nanocubes became rough and wrinkled, the resultant nanocubes with a controlled morphology were expected to achieve high mass loading of the active materials, thereby improving the energy density of SIBs.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202105310-bib-0038 #smll202105310-bib-0039"><sup>38,39</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The detailed internal microstructure and elemental distribution of the CuS/FeS<sub>2</sub>@NC nanocubes were investigated by transmission electron microscopy (TEM) and corresponding EDS mapping techniques. In contrast to the Cu/Fe‐PBAs, which were nonporous (Figure <link href="#smll202105310-supitem-0001">S2</link>, Supporting Information), the CuS/FeS<sub>2</sub>@NC nanocubes generated by the thermal decomposition of Cu/Fe‐PBA crystalline frameworks had a porous structure (Figure <link href="#smll202105310-fig-0002">2b</link>). The abundant pores in the particles offer facile ionic transport pathways by increasing the electrolyte/electrode contact area, thereby improving the rate capability.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202105310-bib-0040"><sup>40</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Moreover, metal sulfide nanoclusters formed inside the porous carbon frameworks (Figure <link href="#smll202105310-fig-0002">2c</link>). The high‐resolution TEM images show the lattice fringes with two different interplanar spacings of ≈0.24 and ≈0.27 nm, corresponding to the (210) plane of FeS<sub>2</sub> and the (006) plane of CuS, respectively (Figure <link href="#smll202105310-supitem-0001">S4</link>, Supporting Information). The well‐dispersed composite structure can inhibit the aggregation of active materials during repeated cycling, thereby improving long‐term cycling stability.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202105310-bib-0041"><sup>41</sup></link><sup>]</sup> EDS mapping images reveal the uniform distribution of the Cu, Fe, and S elements (Figure <link href="#smll202105310-fig-0002">2d</link>), while the overlapping signals of elemental C and N indicate the presence of heterostructured metal sulfides (CuS and FeS<sub>2</sub>) encapsulated by nitrogen‐doped carbon. It is noteworthy that the highly conductive porous nitrogen‐doped carbon not only promotes electrolyte impregnation but also facilitates electron transfer upon sodiation/desodiation, which enables fast charging and discharging.</p><figure xml:id="smll202105310-fig-0002"><label>2</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll202105310:smll202105310-fig-0002" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll202105310-fig-0002-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) FE‐SEM, b) bright‐field TEM, c) dark‐field TEM, and d) corresponding EDS mapping images of CuS/FeS<sub>2</sub>@NC (green: Cu, red: Fe, cyan: S, blue: C, and violet: N).</caption></figure><p>XRD patterns were obtained to identify the crystal structures of the heterostructured metal sulfide nanocubes. Owing to the use of the Fe/Fe‐PBA precursor, the FeS<sub>2</sub>@NC nanocubes exhibited only the diffraction peaks of pure FeS<sub>2</sub> (PDF# 96‐154‐4892) after sulfurization (Figure <link href="#smll202105310-supitem-0001">S3c,d</link>, Supporting Information). However, all the diffraction peaks of the CuS/FeS<sub>2</sub>@NC nanocubes match well with those of hexagonal CuS (PDF# 96‐900‐0524) and cubic FeS<sub>2</sub> (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll202105310-fig-0003"><b>3</b>a</link>), indicating the in situ formation of both stoichiometric CuS and FeS<sub>2</sub> without any impurities during the sulfurization process. Owing to the high conductivity of CuS,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202105310-bib-0034 #smll202105310-bib-0042"><sup>34,42</sup></link><sup>]</sup> the combination of CuS and FeS<sub>2</sub> is expected to improve the conductivity of the active materials and result in rapid sodium‐ion insertion/extraction behavior. X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) measurements were performed to further establish the chemical status of the CuS/FeS<sub>2</sub>@NC nanocubes, and the obtained binding energy spectra in the Cu 2p, Fe 2p, and S 2p regions are presented in Figure <link href="#smll202105310-fig-0003">3b–d</link>. The CuS/FeS<sub>2</sub>@NC nanocube retains the original particle size (500–600 nm) and cubic morphology with well‐dispersed metal sulfide nanoparticles (Figure <link href="#smll202105310-supitem-0001">S14</link>, Supporting Information). Conversely, although the carbon framework of FeS<sub>2</sub>@NC nanocube retains its original size and shape to a certain degree, elemental Fe is extracted from the nanocube and forms agglomerated FeS<sub>2</sub> particles outside the carbon framework, thereby reducing the reversibility of the sodiation/desodiation reaction (Figure <link href="#smll202105310-supitem-0001">S15</link>, Supporting Information). This observation further confirms that the effective nanoconfinement of interspersed heterogeneous metal sulfides within the porous carbon framework improves the structural stability and electrochemical reactivity of active materials, and prevents unfavorable metal dissolution and particle aggregation during cycling.</p><figure xml:id="smll202105310-fig-0006"><label>6</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll202105310:smll202105310-fig-0006" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" nanocubes.</caption></figure><p>The underlying reasons for the outstanding sodium storage performance of CuS/FeS<sub>2</sub>@NC are schematically illustrated in Figure <link href="#smll202105310-fig-0006">6d</link> and are summarized as follows: i) The close contact between the sulfide‐based nanoclusters and nitrogen‐doped carbon matrix not only induces rapid redox reactions of active materials by facilitating electron transfer but also prevents particle agglomeration. ii) The mesoporous carbon structure provides excellent electrolyte impregnation and sufficient volume‐variation‐buffering spaces, which are advantageous for facile charge transfer kinetics and high structural stability of active materials. iii) The realization of heterogeneous binary metal sulfides (CuS and FeS<sub>2</sub>) enables stepwise redox reactions and rapid sodium‐ion diffusion, thereby reducing the voltage hysteresis between the sodiation and desodiation processes of the electrode and suppressing the pulverization of the active materials by minimizing the mechanical stress caused by volume expansion.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202105310-sec-0030"><title type="main">Conclusions</title><p>To address the major hurdles in the practical application of high‐capacity metal sulfide anode materials, such as slow electrochemical kinetics and large volume variation upon sodiation/desodiation, heterostructured CuS/FeS<sub>2</sub>@NC nanocubes were synthesized for the first time via the two‐step annealing process of bimetallic Cu/Fe‐PBAs followed by a sulfurization process. Combined morphological and structural characterization revealed that the CuS/FeS<sub>2</sub>@NC nanocubes are composed of heterogeneous binary metal sulfide (CuS and FeS<sub>2</sub>) nanoclusters uniformly distributed throughout a nitrogen‐doped mesoporous carbon framework. The unique nanoarchitecture with porous carbon encapsulation not only improves the mechanical integrity of the electrode owing to the abundant volume accommodation sites but also ensures rapid electron transfer through the connection network between the nitrogen‐doped carbon matrix and metal sulfide particles. Furthermore, the effective nanoconfinement of intermixed metal sulfides enhances the structural stability of the CuS/FeS<sub>2</sub>@NC nanocubes by preventing particle aggregation and metal dissolution upon repeated cycling. Owing to these advantages, the heterostructured CuS/FeS<sub>2</sub>@NC nanocube anode retains a high reversible capacity of 537 mAh g<sup>–1</sup> even after 300 cycles at a high current density of 5.0 A g<sup>–1</sup> with a superior rate capability. The excellent sodium storage performance of the CuS/FeS<sub>2</sub>@NC nanocubes is attributed to the combination of the surface capacitive charge storage provided by nitrogen‐doped mesoporous carbon and the presence of highly conductive CuS in the heterostructured nanocubes; this combination enables rapid diffusion‐controlled sodium‐ion storage. The simple and scalable synthetic approach using a composition‐ and property‐tunable bimetallic PBA precursor described herein can potentially facilitate the design and construction of various heterostructured metal sulfide materials for high‐performance SIBs and other energy storage applications.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202105310-sec-0040"><title type="main">Experimental Section</title><section xml:id="smll202105310-sec-0050" numbered="no"><section xml:id="smll202105310-sec-0060" numbered="no"><section xml:id="smll202105310-sec-0070" numbered="no"><title type="main">Synthesis of Cu/Fe‐PBAs and Cu/Fe‐Based Nanocubes</title><p>Bimetallic Cu/Fe‐PBAs were synthesized using a co‐precipitation method at room temperature. Solution A was prepared by dissolving copper(II) sulfate (3 mmol) and oxalic acid (1.8 g) in deionized water (50 mL). Potassium hexacyanoferrate (K<sub>3</sub>[Fe(CN)<sub>6</sub>], 2 mmol) and oxalic acid (1.8 g) were dissolved in deionized water (50 mL) to obtain solution B. Solutions A and B were separately stirred for 1 h before mixing. Then, solution A was quickly added to solution B, and the mixed solution was vigorously stirred for 6 h. A brown precipitate (Cu/Fe‐PBA) was obtained, which was centrifuged and washed with HCl, deionized water, and ethanol several times, followed by drying overnight in a convection oven at 60 °C. The precipitate was carbonized via continuous two‐step pyrolysis under an argon atmosphere to obtain Cu/Fe‐based nanocubes. First, the as‐prepared powder was annealed at 200 °C for 2 h at a ramp rate of 1 °C min<sup>–1</sup>. Subsequently, the temperature was increased to 700 °C at a ramp rate of 5 °C min<sup>–1</sup> and the thermal treatment was continued for 5 min to accomplish the carbonization process. Finally, the carbonized nanocubes were naturally cooled and collected.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202105310-sec-0080" numbered="no"><title type="main">Synthesis of CuS/FeS<sub>2</sub>@NC and FeS<sub>2</sub>@NC Nanocubes</title><p>The obtained Cu/Fe‐based nanocubes and elemental sulfur were placed in an alumina boat separately at a weight ratio of 1:3, and the boat was placed in a tube furnace for sulfurization. The sulfurization process was performed at 300 °C for 3 h under an argon atmosphere. After natural cooling, the final heterostructured nanocubes consisting of CuS/FeS<sub>2</sub> particles dispersed in porous nitrogen‐doped carbon were obtained. For comparison, FeS<sub>2</sub>@NC nanocubes were also synthesized via a hydrothermal method using an Fe/Fe‐PBA precursor instead of Cu/Fe‐PBA. To prepare the Fe/Fe‐PBA precursor, 4 mmol of K<sub>3</sub>[Fe(CN)<sub>6</sub>] was added to an HCl solution (0.1 <sc>m</sc>, 100 mL), and the mixture was stirred until a clear solution was obtained. The solution was then heated at 80 °C for 12 h in an autoclave. The carbonization process was carried out under the same conditions, but the subsequent sulfurization was carried out at 450 °C for 3 h to ensure the formation of phase‐pure FeS<sub>2</sub> without any remaining undesirable Fe–C bonds.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202105310-sec-0090" numbered="no"><title type="main">Material Characterization</title><p>The particle size and surface morphology of the CuS/FeS<sub>2</sub>@NC and FeS<sub>2</sub>@NC nanocubes were observed by FE‐SEM (Inspect F50). The detailed microstructure and elemental distribution of the samples were analyzed by TEM (Talos F200X, FEI) and EDS at an accelerating voltage of 200 kV. XRD patterns were obtained using a Rigaku MiniFlex II with Cu <i>Kα</i> (λ = 1.5418 Å) radiation. The chemical states of each element were determined by XPS using a PHI5000 VersaProbe (ULVAC‐PHI Inc., Japan). Raman analysis was carried out using an Invia Raman microspectrometer (Renishaw Inc., UK) with a 523 nm laser beam. The carbon content was estimated by TGA (SDT Q 600) and elemental analysis (Thermo Scientific FLASH 2000). The BET specific surface area and pore size distribution were obtained from nitrogen adsorption/desorption isotherms measured using an ASAP2010 instrument (Micromeritics, USA) to confirm the porous characteristics of the sample.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202105310-sec-0100" numbered="no"><title type="main">Electrochemical Measurements</title><p>The working electrode was fabricated by mixing slurries containing 80 wt% active materials, a 10 wt% conductive agent (Super P), and a 10 wt% poly(acrylic acid) binder. The blended slurry was cast on a Cu foil current collector using a doctor blade and then dried at 80 °C in a vacuum oven overnight. The electrodes were cut into disks (diameter of 1.2 cm) with the typical areal mass loading of 1.5–1.8 mg cm<sup>−2</sup>. Next, 2032 coin‐type half‐cells were then assembled in an argon‐filled glove box inside a dry room. The electrolyte consisted of sodium hexafluorophosphate (NaPF<sub>6</sub>, 1 <sc>m</sc>, Sigma Aldrich) salt dissolved in diethylene glycol dimethyl ether (DEGDME, Sigma Aldrich). A glass fiber (Whatman, UK) and sodium metal (Sigma Aldrich) were employed as the separator and counter/reference electrode, respectively. Galvanostatic charge–discharge measurements were performed at 25 °C in the voltage range of 0.01–2.7 V (versus Na/Na<sup>+</sup>) using an automated battery testing system (Maccor Series 4000, USA). CV curves were obtained using a VSP‐300 potentiostat (Biologic, France) at various scan rates ranging from 1.0 to 10 mV s<sup>–1</sup>. EIS measurement was performed in the frequency range of 10 mHz to 1 MHz by applying a perturbation signal of 5 mV.</p></section></section></section></section><section xml:id="smll202105310-sec-0110" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>This work was supported by the Korea Institute of Science and Technology (KIST) Institutional Program (Project No. 2E30992).
