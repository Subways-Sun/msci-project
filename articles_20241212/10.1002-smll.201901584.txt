type="main" xml:lang="en"><title type="main">Abstract</title><p>Herein, 1D free‐standing and binder‐free hierarchically branched TiO<sub>2</sub>/C nanofibers (denoted as BT/C NFs) based on an in situ fabrication method as an anode for sodium‐ion batteries are reported. The in situ fabrication endows this material with large surface area and strong structural stability, providing this material with abundant active sites and smooth channels for fast ion transportation. As a result, BT/C NFs with the character of free‐standing membranes are directly used as binder‐free anode for sodium‐ion batteries, delivering a capacity of 284 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at a current density of 200 mA g<sup>−1</sup> after 1000 cycles. Even at a high current density of 2000 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, the reversible capacity can still achieve as high as 204 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>. By means of kinetic analysis, it is demonstrated that the remarkable surface pseudocapacitive behavior is also a major factor to achieve excellent performance. The rationally designed structure coupled with the inherent pseudocapacitive behavior gives this material potential for sodium‐ion batteries.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="smll201901584-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p><b>Free‐standing hierarchically branched TiO<sub>2</sub>/C nanofibers</b> are in situ fabricated as an anode for sodium‐ion batteries. The large surface area, flexible feature, and pseudocapacitive sodium storage behavior provide this material with abundant active sites, smooth channels for fast ions transportation, strong structural stability, and therefore excellent electrochemical performance when directly used as a binder‐free anode for sodium‐ion batteries.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll201901584:smll201901584-gra-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll201901584-gra-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body xml:id="smll201901584-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="smll201901584-sec-0010"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) have been extensively considered as ideal cheap candidate energy storage devices owing to their low price as well as abundant sodium source. However, the much larger ionic radius of Na<sup>+</sup> compared to Li<sup>+</sup> affords slower ion transport, sluggish reaction kinetics, and larger volume changes during the Na<sup>+</sup> insertion/extraction, which cause the limited sodium storage and rate capability.<link href="#smll201901584-bib-0001 #smll201901584-bib-0002 #smll201901584-bib-0003"/> In addition, the commercial graphic anodes for lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs) are not capable of storing sodium ions.<link href="#smll201901584-bib-0004 #smll201901584-bib-0005"/> Therefore, it drives the exploring of new materials for high‐performance SIBs. Transitional metal oxide nanomaterials have drawn considerable attention as anode materials for SIBs owing to their distinct reaction mechanism, abundant active sites, and short diffusion pathways.<link href="#smll201901584-bib-0006"/> Among them, titanium dioxide (TiO<sub>2</sub>) has been extensively studied in recent years due to its natural abundance, low cost, environment friendliness, attractive theoretical specific capacity of 335 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>, and superior structure's stability during the charge/discharge processes.<link href="#smll201901584-bib-0007 #smll201901584-bib-0008"/> Noticeably, the anatase TiO<sub>2</sub> has displayed excellent electrochemically activity for Na<sup>+</sup> storage, attributing to its favorable 2D diffusion pathways and proper interstitial sites for Na<sup>+</sup> accommodation.<link href="#smll201901584-bib-0009 #smll201901584-bib-0010 #smll201901584-bib-0011 #smll201901584-bib-0012 #smll201901584-bib-0013"/> However, the practical application of TiO<sub>2</sub>‐based anode is retarded by the intrinsic low electric conductivity and sluggish diffusion of sodium ions. It is reported that 1D nanostructured materials could afford efficient electron transport along one direction.<link href="#smll201901584-bib-0014 #smll201901584-bib-0015"/> Combination with carbon is also known as a promising strategy to improve the electrochemical performance of materials in various systems.<link href="#smll201901584-bib-0012 #smll201901584-bib-0013 #smll201901584-bib-0016 #smll201901584-bib-0017 #smll201901584-bib-0018"/> Carbon can stabilize the nanomaterial and provide an interconnected network for electron transport, and the voids in the carbon can allow permeation of the electrolyte for fast Na<sup>+</sup> transport.<link href="#smll201901584-bib-0019 #smll201901584-bib-0020"/> To date, a variety of carbon coating techniques are established based on the postprocessing composition with carbon source, which can solve the problem of the poor electronic conductivity. However, it will also introduce dispensable charge transfer resistance into the interface between coated carbon and active materials. On the other hand, the in situ synthesis has been proven to be a promising method to avoid this problem. Wang et al. demonstrated a facile one‐pot strategy to synthesize branched TiO<sub>2</sub> architectures,<link href="#smll201901584-bib-0021"/> the reversible specific capacity, and rate performance was remarkably enhanced for LIBs. Previously, our group fabricated branched TiO<sub>2</sub> nanofibers via an in situ preparation method, which also possessed excellent Li<sup>+</sup> storage performance.<link href="#smll201901584-bib-0022 #smll201901584-bib-0023"/></p><p>Compared to the diffusion‐controlled process in conventional sodium storage materials, pseudocapacitive charge storage has the advantage of alleviating fundamental kinetic barriers by limiting the reactions occurring at the surface instead of solid‐state diffusion to facilitate a high charging rate for high power.<link href="#smll201901584-bib-0024 #smll201901584-bib-0025 #smll201901584-bib-0026 #smll201901584-bib-0027 #smll201901584-bib-0028 #smll201901584-bib-0029 #smll201901584-bib-0030"/> Therefore, enhancing the pseudocapacitive contribution is an efficient approach to improving the cycling stability and rate performance of anode materials.<link href="#smll201901584-bib-0031"/> It is reported that nanosized materials tend to exhibit outstanding pseudocapacitive behavior.<link href="#smll201901584-bib-0030"/> Thus, rational design of electrode materials with nanoscale is an elegant strategy to enhance the performance of pseudocapacitance. The large surface area can also increase the contribution of pseudocapacitance; thus, it will greatly enhance the rate capability.<link href="#smll201901584-bib-0030 #smll201901584-bib-0032 #smll201901584-bib-0033"/> In addition, hierarchical materials especially those in situ formed during the synthesis process can facilitate mass transport, fast ion, and electron transfer owing to the high structural strength that can provide smooth migration channels for electrons and ions, leading to high storage.<link href="#smll201901584-bib-0021 #smll201901584-bib-0022 #smll201901584-bib-0023 #smll201901584-bib-0031"/> In order to further improve energy density of SIBs, it is ideal to fabricate flexible and free‐standing electrode with nanostructures, in which all of the materials could participate in sodium storage. This feature can not only significantly simplify the cell packing by eliminating inactive ingredients, but also further improve both volumetric energy and power density for SIBs, consequently ensuring improved overall performance.<link href="#smll201901584-bib-0034 #smll201901584-bib-0035 #smll201901584-bib-0036"/></p><p>Based on the abovementioned strategies, in order to improve the sodium storage performance of TiO<sub>2</sub>, it would be wise to prepare 1D free‐standing and binder‐free TiO<sub>2</sub>/carbon nanofibers with large surface area and high structural stability. In this study, we report a rational design of free‐standing branched TiO<sub>2</sub>/carbon nanofibers (denoted as BT/C NFs) that were in situ generated via electrospinning and an alkali hydrothermal treatment method (<b>Scheme</b> <link href="#smll201901584-fig-0008"/>a,b). The as‐prepared BT/C NFs are well performed with 1D structure, large surface area, and flexible feature which are beneficial for ion and electron transfer and pseudocapacitive contribution (Scheme <link href="#smll201901584-fig-0001"/>c). The in situ fabrication endows the materials with strong structural stability, and carbon can enhance electrical conductivity of the BT/C NFs electrode and suppress the agglomeration of TiO<sub>2</sub> nanoparticles. As expected, the purposely designed BT/C NFs' electrode exhibited much higher capacity and excellent rate performance compared with original TiO<sub>2</sub>/carbon nanofibers (denoted as T/C NFs) when directly used as binder‐free anode for SIBs.</p><figure xml:id="smll201901584-fig-0008"><title type="figureName">Scheme</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll201901584:smll201901584-fig-0008" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll201901584-fig-0008-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) Schematic illustration of the synthesis process of the BT/C NFs. b) Digital images of free‐standing BT/C NFs. c) Illustration of transport paths of Na<sup>+</sup> ions and electrons in the BT/C NFs.</caption></figure></section><section xml:id="smll201901584-sec-0020"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p>The T/C NFs exhibited uniformly continuous 1D nanostructures with a relatively smooth surface (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll201901584-fig-0001"/>a,b). Interestingly, the sample BT/C NFs showed a branched structure (Figure <link href="#smll201901584-fig-0001"/>c,d) after the alkali hydrothermal treatment, where numerous short branches were well in situ grown with a remarkably high density covered the entire surface of the pristine T/C NFs. It is noted that the average diameter of BT/C NFs (≈800 nm) was larger than those of T/C NFs (≈500 nm), contributing to the stripping and regrowth of TiO<sub>2</sub> during the alkali hydrothermal process. Figure S1 (Supporting Information) records the composition changes of samples during the alkali etching process. As presented in Figure S1a (Supporting Information), the energy dispersive X‐ray spectrometry (EDS) investigation verified that the sample T/C NFs only consisted of C, Ti, and O. After alkali hydrothermal treatment, additional Na<sup>+</sup> ions appeared (Figure S1b, Supporting Information), and it could be completely removed by the ion‐exchange treatment (Figure S1c,d, Supporting Information).</p><figure xml:id="smll201901584-fig-0001"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll201901584:smll201901584-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll201901584-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>SEM images of a,b) sample T/C NFs and c,d) BT/C NFs with different magnifications, respectively.</caption></figure><p>Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) was used to further investigate the detail morphology of as‐prepared BT/C NFs (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll201901584-fig-0002"/>). From Figure <link href="#smll201901584-fig-0002"/>a, it could be observed that the surface of BT/C NFs was rough, with many branches randomly growing to the trunk, which was in well consistent with scanning electron microscope (SEM) images. According to the high‐resolution TEM (HRTEM) image in Figure <link href="#smll201901584-fig-0002"/>b, the lattice fringes with spacing was measured to be 0.24 nm, corresponding to the (103) crystal plane of anatase TiO<sub>2</sub>. The detailed structure collected from the interface between the branch and the trunk is shown in Figure S2 (Supporting Information). It could be seen clearly that the branch rooted into the trunk and a pore was left in the trunk due to the exfoliation of TiO<sub>2</sub> on the surface (the yellow line marking area). The trunk remained a good crystallinity, and the branch also showed good crystallinity due to the second calcination after alkali hydrothermal treatment. The amorphous regions around the lattice fringes reflected the disordered carbon phase, and the lattice of graphitic carbon can also be observed clearly (Figure S2, Supporting Information). This feature can not only reinforce the connection between the branches and the trunk, but also provide smooth migration channels for electrons, and ions for the branches were in situ grown on the trunks.<link href="#smll201901584-bib-0023"/> The elemental mapping analysis of a single BT/C NF in Figure <link href="#smll201901584-fig-0002"/>c further certified the successful preparation of branched TiO<sub>2</sub>/C nanofibers. Element Ti and O were distributed in the whole nanofibers while element C was only located at the trunk region, illustrating that the branches were only composed with TiO<sub>2</sub>. This feature was in favor of the contact with electrolyte for TiO<sub>2</sub> and guaranteed the well electrical conductivity of the BT/C NFs' electrode at the same time.</p><figure xml:id="smll201901584-fig-0002"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll201901584:smll201901584-fig-0002" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll201901584-fig-0002-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) TEM, b) HRTEM, and c) elemental mapping images of the sample BT/C NFs.</caption></figure><p>The crystal phases of the T/C NFs and BT/C NFs were defined by X‐ray diffraction (XRD). As shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll201901584-fig-0003"/>a, both samples exhibited the identical phase structure of anatase TiO<sub>2</sub>. The major diffraction peaks observed at 2θ = 25.32°, 37.75°, 48.01°, 53.90°, 55.07°, 62.72°, 68.77°, 70.14°, and 75.05° belonged to the (101), (004), (200), (105), (211), (204), (116), (220), and (215) planes of anatase TiO<sub>2</sub> (JCPDS No. 21‐1272), respectively. A broad peak at around 26° indicated the existence of amorphous carbon,<link href="#smll201901584-bib-0037 #smll201901584-bib-0038"/> as shown in Figure S3 (Supporting Information). There were no other diffraction peaks being detected, suggesting the high purity of the as‐synthesized TiO<sub>2</sub> crystallites. Comparing with T/C NFs, the BT/C NFs sample showed better crystallization degree. This can be partly attributed to the re‐calcination process at high temperature and the extremely large surface area of BT/C NFs (Figure <link href="#smll201901584-fig-0003"/>c) which could promote the process of crystallization.<link href="#smll201901584-bib-0039"/> Moreover, the particle size of branched TiO<sub>2</sub> became larger due to the exposure of them after the alkali hydrothermal treatment which is different from the sample T/C NFs with TiO<sub>2</sub> nanoparticles being embedded in carbon matrix, which caused the sharpen peak.<link href="#smll201901584-bib-0040"/> The high crystallinity of BT/C NFs was beneficial for the promotion of the sodium storage performance for anatase TiO<sub>2</sub> that can supply effective paths and more accommodation sites for Na<sup>+</sup>.<link href="#smll201901584-bib-0009 #smll201901584-bib-0041 #smll201901584-bib-0042"/></p><figure xml:id="smll201901584-fig-0003"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll201901584:smll201901584-fig-0003" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll201901584-fig-0003-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) XRD patterns. b) TGA curves under air atmosphere. c) N<sub>2</sub> adsorption–desorption isotherm; and d) pore size distribution of T/C NFs and BT/C NFs.</caption></figure><p>In order to identify the carbon contents in the samples, thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) measurements were conducted. As shown in Figure <link href="#smll201901584-fig-0003"/>b, there were two steps of weight loss in the TGA curve. The weight loss before 300 °C mainly belonged to the evaporation of adsorbed water.<link href="#smll201901584-bib-0043 #smll201901584-bib-0044"/> The sloping part between 300 and 550 °C was assigned to the combustion of carbon. The content of carbon in the BT/C NFs was estimated to be ≈48.8%, which was slightly smaller than that of T/C NFs (≈50.0%). This can be attributed to the little amount of carbon loss on the surface of nanofibers during the acid and alkali treatment process. Figure <link href="#smll201901584-fig-0003"/>c,d demonstrates the N<sub>2</sub> adsorption–desorption isotherms and the corresponding pore size distribution of the samples. As shown in Figure <link href="#smll201901584-fig-0003"/>c, both samples showed type IV isotherm, and the hysteresis loop indicated typical characteristic of mesoporous structures. The pore size distribution plots obtained from the desorption branch of the isotherm confirmed that BT/C NFs had well‐defined micropores while T/C NFs had micro‐ and mesopores. Therefore, the distribution of the pore size was much more centralized for BT/C NFs (Figure <link href="#smll201901584-fig-0003"/>d). The unique porous features were conducive to fasting ionic diffusion and mass transport; thus, efficient electrochemical reactions were favored. The specific surface area was further calculated by the Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) analysis. The specific surface area of BT/C NFs was 506.7 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup>, which was ≈50 times larger than that of T/C NFs (10.7 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup>). The specific surface area of BT/C NFs was also much larger than that of the similar TiO<sub>2</sub> branched architecture (20.41 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup>) obtained via epitaxy growth.<link href="#smll201901584-bib-0045"/> Studies revealed that the increase of surface area could also facilitate fast electrons' acceptance and ions' diffusion through the branches and ensure sufficient contact between electrolyte and active materials.<link href="#smll201901584-bib-0046"/> Thus, the high specific surface area of BT/C NFs would be beneficial for applications associated with surface structure by supplying more reaction sites, such as pseudocapacitive energy storage.<link href="#smll201901584-bib-0028 #smll201901584-bib-0047"/></p><p>To provide further investigation, Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy (FT‐IR) spectra and Raman spectra of the T/C NFs and BT/C NFs were conducted. From the FT‐IR spectra in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll201901584-fig-0004"/>a, both samples showed CO and CO stretch. The CO vibrations were well retained while CO vibration was a little shifted from 1588.4 to 1596 cm<sup>−1</sup> after alkali hydrothermal treatment. In sample T/C NFs, TiO vibration was located at 583 cm<sup>−1</sup> while in sample BT/C NFs, it is shifted to 504.7 cm<sup>−1</sup>, indicating the longer bond lengths. This was due to the exposure of TiO<sub>2</sub> nanoparticles of nanofibers during the acid and alkali hydrothermal treatment process, which will enhance the amount of the exposure atoms, therefore, large the distance between atoms on the surface. This could cause the small bond force constant and result in the longer bond lengths.<link href="#smll201901584-bib-0048"/> A new shoulder peak at 741 cm<sup>−1</sup> which was NH bending vibration appeared in sample BT/C NFs due to the concentrated HNO<sub>3</sub> treatment before the alkali hydrothermal treatment.<link href="#smll201901584-bib-0049"/> The broad band between 3300 and 3700 cm<sup>−1</sup> might be mainly due to the adsorbed moisture, which confirmed TGA measurements in Figure <link href="#smll201901584-fig-0003"/>b.<link href="#smll201901584-bib-0050"/></p><figure xml:id="smll201901584-fig-0004"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll201901584:smll201901584-fig-0004" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll201901584-fig-0004-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) FT‐IR and b) Raman spectra of the T/C NFs and BT/C NFs.</caption></figure><p>Figure <link href="#smll201901584-fig-0004"/>b shows the Raman spectra of the two samples. Figure S4 (Supporting Information) shows the morphology of BT/C NFs without being acid treatment before hydrothermal treatment. It could be observed that the fibers remained in their original morphologies with relatively smooth surface. This result indicated that the acid treatment process of T/C NFs was indispensable for the formation of the branched morphologies. Then the effect of acid treatment for T/C NFs was investigated by detecting the FT‐IR spectrum. As shown in Figure S5 (Supporting Information), all of the characteristic peaks in the FT‐IR spectrum were the same except for the peak located at 1715 cm<sup>−1</sup> which was assigned to COOH. As shown in Figure S6 (Supporting Information), the contact angle between T/C NFs and water was 141°. However, after acid treatment, the contact angle reduced to nearly 0°, indicating that the fiber after acid treatment can be soaked by water completely. Moreover, acid treatment could partially etched the microstructure of the fiber surface,<link href="#smll201901584-bib-0063 #smll201901584-bib-0064"/> resulting in the exposure of TiO<sub>2</sub> by spalling of some amorphous carbon particles,<link href="#smll201901584-bib-0065"/> which was beneficial for the alkali hydrothermal treatment. Figure S7 (Supporting Information) shows the morphologies obtained at different hydrothermal treatment times at 120 °C. It could be seen that only a few short nanofibers dispersed on the surface of T/C NFs at 0.5 h. With the increase of time, more and more branches were formed and when the time was increased to 2 h, the branched morphologies were well obtained. The same phenomenon was also observed in the different temperature experiments, as shown in Figure S8 (Supporting Information). These results indicated that the hydrothermal time and temperature were very important factors for the formation of well‐branched morphologies.</p><p>According to the above experiments, a possible formation mechanism of the BT/C NFs could be proposed as follows: the acid treatment process enhanced the hydrophily and spalling of some carbon on the surface of the fibers which was in favor of the contact between T/C NFs and the alkali solution, and simultaneously lets TiO<sub>2</sub> on the surface or near‐surface to expose to the alkali solution. It was noticeable that the obtained BT/C NFs exhibited a higher rate performance than most of the other carbon and TiO<sub>2</sub> composite anode materials, making it one of the promising candidates for SIBs.</p></section><section xml:id="smll201901584-sec-0030"><title type="main">Conclusion</title><p>In summary, free‐standing membranes of hierarchically branched TiO<sub>2</sub>/C nanofibers have been prepared through a facile in situ synthesis route including an electrospinning followed by an alkali hydrothermal treatment. The in situ growth of the free‐standing branches leaves the materials with strong structures' stability and reduced ion transport resistance, which is beneficial for the mechanical properties and fast ion transportation. The large surface area supplies a higher number of reaction sites, and the nanofibers offer 1D charge transportation directions and short ionic transport lengths. Thus, the branched TiO<sub>2</sub>/C nanofibers' electrode delivers a stable capacity of 283.5 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at a current density of 200 mA g<sup>−1</sup> after 1000 cycles and can still achieve 204.1 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> even when the current density is raised to a very high rate of 2000 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. Such excellent electrochemical performance can be ascribed to the special design and inherent pseudocapacitive behavior of the materials. Therefore, the free‐standing hierarchically branched TiO<sub>2</sub>/C nanofibers can be used as promising anode materials for SIBs and some other flexible electronic devices.</p></section><section xml:id="smll201901584-sec-0040"><title type="main">Experimental Section</title><p><i>Reagents and Materials</i>: All the chemicals were of analytical grade and were used as received. Tetrabutyl titanate (Ti(OC<sub>4</sub>H<sub>9</sub>)<sub>4</sub>), methanol (CH<sub>3</sub>OH), acetic acid (CH<sub>3</sub>COOH), nitric acid (HNO<sub>3</sub>), hydrochloric acid, and sodium hydrate (NaOH) were purchased from Sinopharm Chemical Reagent Co., Ltd. PVP (<i>M<sub>W</sub></i> = 1 300 000) was purchased from BASF chemical company in Germany.</p><p><i>Preparation of TiO<sub>2</sub>/C Nanofibers</i>: About 2.5 g of PVP and 2.5 g of Ti(OC<sub>4</sub>H<sub>9</sub>)<sub>4</sub> were dissolved in a mixture of CH<sub>3</sub>OH and CH<sub>3</sub>COOH (weight ratio 4:1). Then the solution was stirred for about 6 h at room temperature to form the electrospinning precursor. A 10 mL precursor was transferred into a 20 mL syringe with a 22 G stainless steel needle connected to a high‐voltage supply (BGG Bmei Co., Ltd.). A high voltage of 12 kV was applied between the needle tip and aluminum collector. The flow rate and tip‐collector distance were set as 1.6 mL h<sup>−1</sup> and 12 cm, respectively. Finally, the as‐obtained fibers were first stabilized at 250 °C in air for 2 h, and then carbonized in a tube furnace at 700 °C in N<sub>2</sub> atmosphere for 3 h with a heating rate of 10 °C min<sup>−1</sup>. The as‐prepared flexible TiO<sub>2</sub>/C nanofibers were denoted as T/C NFs.</p><p><i>Preparation of Branched TiO<sub>2</sub>/C Nanofibers</i>: After being treated with concentrated HNO<sub>3</sub> at 60 °C for 2 h, T/C NFs were transferred into a 100 mL Teflon‐lined stainless steel autoclave. Then the NaOH solution (10 mol L<sup>−1</sup>, 50 mL) was added into the autoclave that was kept at 120 °C for 2 h. After the autoclave was cooled down to room temperature, the obtained samples were washed thoroughly with water and ethanol, followed by the immersion in dilute hydrochloric acid (0.1 mol L<sup>−1</sup>, 50 mL) for 2 h and dried at 60 °C for 12 h. Finally, the dried samples were further annealed in a tube furnace at 700 °C for 2 h in N<sub>2</sub> atmosphere. The obtained sample was still flexible (Scheme <link href="#smll201901584-fig-0001"/>b) and was denoted as BT/C NFs.</p><p><i>Material Characterization</i>: Structures were characterized by XRD with an X'pert MPDPro (PANalytical Co.) by using Cu Kα radiation at 40 kV and 40 mA. Morphologies and elemental compositions of the samples were analyzed through a JEOL 7800F field emission SEM equipped with homologous EDS and G2F30 field emission TEM at an acceleration voltage of 300 kV. TGA was used to determine the content of carbon in the samples. The degree of graphitization in the samples was studied by Raman spectroscopy. Nitrogen adsorption–desorption isotherms were measured with an ASAP 2020 instrument. XPS was carried out on an Axis Ultra, Kratos (UK) at monochromatic Al Kα radiation (150 W, 15 kV, and 1486.6 eV).</p><p><i>Electrochemical Measurements</i>: The flexible T/C NFs and BT/C NFs were cut into disks and used as the working electrodes (≈1 mg) directly to assemble batteries with CR2025 coin cells with Na foil as counter and reference electrodes. The full cells were assembled using Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>/C as the counter electrode. Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>/C powder was prepared via a sol–gel approach. Simply, NH<sub>4</sub>VO<sub>3</sub>, NaH<sub>2</sub>PO<sub>4</sub>, and citric acid were mixed in aqueous solution in a molar ratio of 2:3:2. The solution was held at 80 °C with stirring. PVP was dissolved in deionized water with a concentration of 10 wt%. The PVP solution was poured to the former solution and stirred to slowly evaporate water until it was transformed into a gel. The gel was sintered at 150 °C for 4 h and then at 850 °C for 8 h under N<sub>2</sub> flow to obtain the final material. The electrolyte was 1 <sc>m</sc> NaClO<sub>4</sub> in a mixture of ethylene carbonate (EC), dimethyl carbonate (DMC), and ethyl methyl carbonate (EMC) (1:1:1 by volume) with 2.0% fluoroethylene carbonatite (FEC). Glass fiber (GF/D, Whatman) was used as the separator. Cell assembly was conducted in an Ar‐filled glove box with oxygen and moisture level below 0.1 ppm. Prior to assembly of full cells, the BT/C NFs' anode was precycled in a half cell for three cycles at 50 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. The cell was dismantled in a glove box and then taken out for further usage in full cells. Galvanostatic charge/discharge tests were conducted at a voltage range of 0.01–3.0 V on a LANHE battery tester. CV measurements were conducted at a scan rate of 0.5 mV s<sup>−1</sup> and EIS measurements were performed in the frequency range from 100 kHz to 0.01 Hz on a Chenhua CHI660D electrochemical workstation.</p></section><section xml:id="smll201901584-sec-0050" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>This work was supported by Natural science fund of Jiangsu Province (BK20170416), Natural Science Foundation of China (Grant No. 51803164), Natural Science Basic Research Plan in Shaanxi Province of China (2017JM2022), and China Postdoctoral Science Foundation (2018M643635).
