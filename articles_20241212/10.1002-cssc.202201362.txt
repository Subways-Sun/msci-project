Based on this dilute hybrid electrolyte and configured by bimetallic Prussian blue analogue (Ni<sub>2</sub>ZnHCF) cathode and 3,4,9,10‐perylenetetracarboxylic diimide (PTCDI) anode, the full cell demonstrated surprising cycle performance with a specific capacity of 60 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> (&gt;800 cycles) and achieved prominent performance at low temperature. Glycerol effectively expanded the electrochemical stability window of the hybrid electrolyte to 2.7 V from formation of strong hydrogen bonds with water molecules and realized the operation of the cell at low temperature, delivering a stable reversible capacity of 40 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at −10 °C. The hybrid electrolyte of glycerol‐water provides a new alternative in development of low‐cost, long‐lifespan, and low‐temperature ASIBs and other aqueous battery systems.</p></abstract><abstract type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p><b>Baby, it's cold outside</b>: A diluted glycerol‐water hybrid electrolyte with expanding electrochemical stability window of 2.7 V and freezing points below −80 °C is proposed. The introduction of glycerol enables aqueous sodium‐ion batteries with superior cycle stability and low‐temperature performance.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18645631:media:cssc202201362:cssc202201362-toc-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/cssc202201362-toc-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta><noteGroup xml:id="cssc202201362-ntgp-0004"><note xml:id="cssc202201362-note-1001"><label>+</label><p>These authors contributed equally to this work.</p></note></noteGroup></header><body sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="cssc202201362-sec-0001" numbered="no"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Aqueous sodium‐ion batteries (ASIBs) have been regarded as a promising candidate for large‐scale energy storage due to their high safety and low cost.<link href="#cssc202201362-bib-0001"><sup>[1]</sup></link> However, ASIBs are restricted by the narrow electrochemical stability window (ESW) and freezing of common aqueous electrolytes at low temperature. Several methods have been used to enlarge ESW of aqueous electrolytes, such as water‐in‐salt (WiS) electrolyte, hybrid electrolyte, and so on.<link href="#cssc202201362-bib-0004"><sup>[2]</sup></link> Suo <i>et al</i>. first reported that 9.26 <sc>m</sc> sodium trifluoromethane sulfonate electrolyte enlarged the ESW up to 2.5 V, and Kühnel <i>et al</i>. employed sodium bis(fluorosulfonyl)imide electrolyte with ultrahigh concentration (37 <sc>m</sc>), accomplishing a wide ESW of 2.6 V.<link href="#cssc202201362-bib-0008"><sup>[3]</sup></link> Indeed, WiS electrolytes are an ideal approach to broaden the ESW, but there are several disadvantages of these electrolytes, such as high costs and too acidic or alkaline environment, which could severely restrict their practical applications.<link href="#cssc202201362-bib-0011"><sup>[4]</sup></link> Recently, modification of the solvation‐sheath structure of charge carriers by applying particular dipole molecule has been reported. Molecules with amide or hydroxy groups could effectively expand the ESW (over 2.5 V) through modifying the solvation‐sheath of cations.<link href="#cssc202201362-bib-0012"><sup>[5]</sup></link> But excessively large proportion of dipole molecules in electrolytes results in a lower ionic conductivity of aqueous electrolyte. This issue of insufficient ionic conductivity and electrolytes freezing could lead the battery to sacrifice their absolute majority capacity and power, especially at low temperature.<link href="#cssc202201362-bib-0013"><sup>[6]</sup></link> Hybrid electrolyte plays an increasingly crucial role in enhancement of the low‐temperature performance of aqueous batteries. Chua <i>et al</i>. reported ethanol and water hybrid electrolyte with 1 <sc>m</sc> sodium acetate could cycle at 0 °C and exhibited excellent cycling stability.<link href="#cssc202201362-bib-0014"><sup>[7]</sup></link> Dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO) was applied to low‐temperature aqueous battery at −50 °C, which damaged the hydrogen bond network within water molecules and prevented hydrogen bond grids from forming in ice crystals.<link href="#cssc202201362-bib-0015"><sup>[8]</sup></link> However, despite the remarkable performance at low temperatures, excessive ethanol and DMSO constitute inevitable safety risks, and a green and safe electrolyte with wide ESW and low freezing point is urgently demanded, aiming to provide a novel method for the development of low‐temperature aqueous electrolytes.</p><p>In this study, we construct a dilute hybrid electrolyte with wide ESW (≈2.7 V) and lower freezing point (under −80 °C), that is, 1 <sc>m</sc> NaNO<sub>3</sub> in a mixture of glycerol (Gly) and deionized water (Di). In this dilute hybrid electrolyte system, glycerol plays an effective role in damaging the hydrogen bond network between water molecules and allowing an expanded ESW. Based on this dilute hybrid electrolyte and configured by Ni<sub>2</sub>ZnHCF cathode and 3,4,9,10‐perylenetetracarboxylic diimide (PTCDI) anode, a full cell displays a capacity of 60 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 800 cycles (at 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup>) and provides a capacity of 40 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup> at −10 °C, corresponding to 80 % capacity retention of that at 25 °C.</p></section><section xml:id="cssc202201362-sec-0002" numbered="no"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p>Hybrid solvent of glycerol‐water was prepared by mixing glycerol and water in different mass ratios, and 1 <sc>m</sc> NaNO<sub>3</sub> was dissolved in different co‐solvent of glycerol‐water to construct hybrid electrolytes. For convenience, 1 <sc>m</sc> NaNO<sub>3</sub> glycerol‐water hybrid electrolytes of the various glycerol and water mass ratios used are denoted as <i>x</i> : <i>y</i> Gly–Di. Specifically, the mass fractions of glycerol in 0 : 1 Gly–Di, 1 : 2 Gly–Di, 2 : 1 Gly–Di, and 9 : 1 Gly–Di hybrid electrolytes are 0, 33.3, 66.7, and 90 %, respectively. Linear sweep voltammetry (LSV) was employed to evaluate the ESW. As shown in Figure <link href="#cssc202201362-fig-0001">1</link>a, compared with the dilute 1 <sc>m</sc> NaNO<sub>3</sub> aqueous electrolyte (1.8 V), the hybrid electrolyte of 2 : 1 Gly–Di demonstrates an expanded ESW of 2.7 V, and the super‐concentrated 9 : 1 Gly–Di expands over 3 V. The expanded ESW of the 1 <sc>m</sc> NaNO<sub>3</sub> hybrid electrolytes indicates that the addition of glycerol could indeed restrain the decomposition of water at a dilute concentration of NaNO<sub>3</sub>. Upon cathodic/anodic scan, the H<sub>2</sub>/O<sub>2</sub> evolution is noticeably suppressed with increasing the mass fraction of glycerol due to decreased activity of water (Figure <link href="#cssc202201362-fig-0001">1</link>a). By comparing with WiS electrolytes and others hybrid electrolytes (Table S1), diluted Gly‐water hybrid electrolyte reaches a similar level in terms of ESW, ionic conductivity, and density. For matching with the electrolyte to configure full cells, two electrodes with suitable redox potentials were selected. Figure <link href="#cssc202201362-fig-0001">1</link>b,c shows the cyclic voltammetry (CV) results of the Ni<sub>2</sub>ZnHCF and PTCDI electrode in 2 : 1 Gly–Di in the first three cycles at a scan rate of 0.5 mV s<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. Except for the first anodic scan, Ni<sub>2</sub>ZnHCF cathode displays a pair of redox peaks around 0.52/0.40 V, and these redox peaks in 2nd and 3rd scans overlap well, suggesting its good reversibility in this electrolyte. In the CV curves of PTCDI anode, two reversible redox peak couples around −0.11/−0.58 and −0.76/−1.05 V are observed. The reduction peak at −0.77 V (in the 1st cycle) shifts to −0.58 V (in the 2<sup>nd</sup> and 3<sup>rd</sup> cycles), indicating higher reversibility.<link href="#cssc202201362-bib-0016"><sup>[9]</sup></link> In addition, all peaks after the 1<sup>st</sup> cycle also overlap well, indicating its reversible sodium de/insertion process.</p><figure xml:id="cssc202201362-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18645631:media:cssc202201362:cssc202201362-fig-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/cssc202201362-fig-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>(a) LSV curves (1 mV s<sup>−1</sup>) in a three‐electrode system. CV curves (0.5 mV s<sup>−1</sup>) of (b) Ni<sub>2</sub>ZnHCF and PTCDI and (c) in 2 : 1 Gly–Di electrolyte.</p></caption></figure><p>Raman spectroscopy, Fourier‐transform infrared (FTIR) spectroscopy, and <sup>1</sup>H nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) spectroscopy were employed to evaluate the strength of hydrogen bond in Gly–Di hybrid electrolytes. As observed from the Raman spectra (Figure <link href="#cssc202201362-fig-0002">2</link>a) of Gly–Di hybrid electrolytes with different mass fractions, the symmetric O−H stretching and asymmetric O−H stretching vibrations of glycerol and water contribute to the broad characteristic peaks from 3150 to 3500 cm<sup>−1</sup> because of the abundant hydrogen bonds among the water molecules, and a shoulder peak at around 3615 cm<sup>−1</sup> is considered as the free O−H of water molecules.<link href="#cssc202201362-bib-0014 #cssc202201362-bib-0017"><sup>[7,10]</sup></link> As the mass fraction of glycerol increases, the amplitudes of O−H stretching bands and the shoulder peak reduce and even completely disappear. Reduction of the amplitudes indicates the addition of glycerol weakens hydrogen bond interactions among water molecules. In addition, as shown in Figure <link href="#cssc202201362-fig-0002">2</link>b, the formation of hydrogen bond between glycerol and water is also illustrated by the red‐shift toward the lower wavenumber of the stretching modes of C−H. FTIR spectra of all as‐prepared electrolytes are displayed in Figure <link href="#cssc202201362-fig-0002">2</link>c; the general information on the hydrogen bond could not differentiate specific contributions from the O−H modes of glycerol and water because of the overlap of that between glycerol and water in FTIR spectra. Therefore, the H−O−H bend mode around 1650 cm<sup>−1</sup> has been employed to inspect the contribution from water since this band does not overlap with any modes of glycerol. Formation of more linear hydrogen bonding, in which water molecules are involved, is indicated by blue‐shift behavior of H−O−H bend mode as the glycerol concentration increases.<link href="#cssc202201362-bib-0018"><sup>[11]</sup></link></p><figure xml:id="cssc202201362-fig-0002"><label>2</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18645631:media:cssc202201362:cssc202201362-fig-0002"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/cssc202201362-fig-0002-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>(a,b) Raman spectra, (c) FTIR spectra, (d) <sup>1</sup>H NMR spectra, (e) ionic conductivity (room temperature), and (f) DSC curves of the hybrid electrolytes with different mass fractions of glycerol.</p></caption></figure><p>Figure <link href="#cssc202201362-fig-0002">2</link>d shows the <sup>1</sup>H NMR chemical shifts of hybrid electrolytes. Hydrogen bond formation between glycerol and water is demonstrated by the significant shift of the peaks with the addition of glycerol.<link href="#cssc202201362-bib-0019"><sup>[12]</sup></link> The ionic conductivity of hybrid electrolytes was measured, as shown in Figure <link href="#cssc202201362-fig-0002">2</link>e. With the increase of glycerol concentration, the ionic conductivity decreases obviously compared with dilute 1 <sc>m</sc> NaNO<sub>3</sub> aqueous electrolyte (≈72.1 mS cm<sup>−1</sup>). 9 : 1 Gly–Di exhibits poor ionic conductivity (≈0.8 mS cm<sup>−1</sup>) because of its high viscosity. Fortunately, 2 : 1 Gly–Di exhibits an ionic conductivity of around 8.0 mS cm<sup>−1</sup> for practical application in aqueous batteries. The freezing point of the hybrid electrolytes was determined by differential scanning calorimetry (DSC). As shown in Figure <link href="#cssc202201362-fig-0002">2</link>f, no exothermic or endothermic peaks appear in the DSC data of hybrid electrolytes (2 : 1 Gly–Di and 9 : 1 Gly–Di), indicating that the freezing points of 2 : 1 Gly–Di and 9 : 1 Gly–Di are below −80 °C. The addition of glycerol damages the hydrogen bond network among water molecules and prevents the formation of ice crystals, explaining the decreased freezing point of hybrid electrolyte. The optical photographs shown in Figure S1 display 2 : 1 Gly–Di and 9 : 1 Gly–Di are still liquid at −20 °C, which is consistent with the DSC data. Remarkably, these hybrid electrolytes are still non‐flammable as demonstrated in Figure S2. Thus, 2 : 1 Gly–Di and 9 : 1 Gly–Di achieve the lower freezing point, making them suitable candidates for low‐temperature applications.</p><p>After detailed characterizations of the hybrid electrolytes, electrochemical performance tests were performed. We evaluated the efficacy of as‐prepared hybrid electrolytes by choosing Ni<sub>2</sub>ZnHCF and PTCDI as the cathode and anode materials. The PTCDI compound was used as purchased, and the Ni<sub>2</sub>ZnHCF powder was fabricated by the method described in the Experimental Section. Scanning electron microscope (SEM) images (Figure S3) and powder X‐ray diffraction (XRD) patterns (Figure S4) are in good agreement with previous report.<link href="#cssc202201362-bib-0020"><sup>[13]</sup></link> The galvanostatic charge‐discharge tests of PTCDI and Ni<sub>2</sub>ZnHCF in half‐cells are given in Figure <link href="#cssc202201362-fig-0003">3</link>a,c, respectively. Compared with cycling in dilute 1 <sc>m</sc> NaNO<sub>3</sub> aqueous electrolyte (Figure S5), Ni<sub>2</sub>ZnHCF displays a rather stable capacity of 55 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup> in 2 : 1 Gly–Di (Figure <link href="#cssc202201362-fig-0003">3</link>b). The improved cycle stability and coulombic efficiency are attributed to the addition of glycerol. However, excessive glycerol reduces the ionic conductivity of the hybrid electrolytes, limiting the capacity of Ni<sub>2</sub>ZnHCF half‐cell cycled in 9 : 1 Gly–Di (Figure S6). The charge‐discharge tests of PTCDI half‐cell demonstrate a stable sodiation and desodiation capacity of 60 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#cssc202201362-fig-0003">3</link>d). The electrochemical performances at 25 and −10 °C of full cells configured by Ni<sub>2</sub>ZnHCF cathode and PTCDI anode are shown in Figure <link href="#cssc202201362-fig-0004">4</link>a,c. The full cell cycled in 2 : 1 Gly–Di at 25 °C could maintain a capacity of 57 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 800 cycles without obvious capacity loss. Continuous capacity increase within 50 cycles is attributed to the poor electron transfer ability of PTCDI anode.<link href="#cssc202201362-bib-0021"><sup>[14]</sup></link> By contrast, as shown in Figure S7, electrolyte decomposition is clearly observed in 0 : 1 Gly–Di and 1 : 2 Gly–Di, leading to large irreversibility and low coulombic efficiency, which evidences the contribution of glycerol to suppression of decomposition of electrolyte. The cycle performance of full cell employing 9 : 1 Gly–Di is shown in Figure S8, the insufficient ionic conductivity makes its low stable capacity of 40 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>. The rate performance of Ni<sub>2</sub>ZnHCF/PTCDI full cell employing 2 : 1 Gly–Di and 9 : 1 Gly–Di at various current densities is further compared in Figure <link href="#cssc202201362-fig-0004">4</link>b. Ni<sub>2</sub>ZnHCF/PTCDI full cell employing 2 : 1 Gly–Di exhibits a much better rate capability.</p><figure xml:id="cssc202201362-fig-0003"><label>3</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18645631:media:cssc202201362:cssc202201362-fig-0003"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/cssc202201362-fig-0003-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Half‐cell performances of Ni<sub>2</sub>ZnHCF cathode (a,b) and PTCDI anode (c,d) in 2 : 1 Gly–Di electrolyte.</p></caption></figure><figure xml:id="cssc202201362-fig-0004"><label>4</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18645631:media:cssc202201362:cssc202201362-fig-0004"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/cssc202201362-fig-0004-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Full‐cell performances. (a) Charge – discharge curves (25 and −10 °C), (b) rate performance (25 °C), and (c) cycle performance (25 and −10 °C).</p></caption></figure><p>A high capacity of 40 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> is obtained as the current density increases to 3 A g<sup>−1</sup>, which is much higher than that of full cells employing 9 : 1 Gly–Di. The better rate capability of full cell employing 2 : 1 Gly–Di may contribute to its excellent ionic conductivity. Notably, the Gly–Di hybrid electrolyte could be applied in a wide temperature range. As demonstrated in Figure <link href="#cssc202201362-fig-0004">4</link>a and Figure S9, the Ni<sub>2</sub>ZnHCF/PTCDI full cells employing 2 : 1 Gly–Di and 9 : 1 Gly–Di are cycled between 0.1 and 2.0 V at −10 °C, the charge/discharge curves of the Ni<sub>2</sub>ZnHCF/PTCDI full cell obtained at −10 °C indicate that the battery is working properly at low temperature. The Ni<sub>2</sub>ZnHCF/PTCDI full cell provides a capacity of 40 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup> at −10 °C, corresponding to 80 % capacity retention of that at 25 °C. It could be clearly detected from Figure <link href="#cssc202201362-fig-0004">4</link>c that the Ni<sub>2</sub>ZnHCF/PTCDI full cell exhibits excellent cycle performance at −10 °C, which is much better than that of full cells employing 9 : 1 Gly–Di.</p></section><section xml:id="cssc202201362-sec-0003" numbered="no"><title type="main">Conclusion</title><p>We reported a green, low‐cost, and diluted glycerol (Gly)‐water hybrid electrolyte system. The introduction of glycerol suppresses the activity of water protons, benefiting from the formation of hydrogen bond interaction. Gly‐water hybrid electrolyte enables aqueous sodium‐ion batteries with superior cycle stability and low‐temperature performance. Overall, our study not only provides a novel strategy for the development of low‐temperature aqueous electrolytes but also illustrates a new practical electrolyte system for aqueous batteries in future application.</p></section><section xml:id="cssc202201362-sec-0004" numbered="no"><title type="main">Experimental Section</title><section xml:id="cssc202201362-sec-0005" numbered="no"><title type="main">Materials</title><p>Ni<sub>2</sub>ZnHCF was synthesized by a previously reported simple co‐precipitation method. The typical synthesis process of Ni<sub>2</sub>ZnHCF is described as follows. 50 mL solution containing 0.02 <sc>m</sc> ZnSO<sub>4</sub> ⋅ 7H<sub>2</sub>O and 50 mL solution containing 0.04 <sc>m</sc> NiCl<sub>2</sub> ⋅ 6H<sub>2</sub>O were added into a 100 mL solution dissolving 0.03 <sc>m</sc> K<sub>3</sub>Fe(CN)<sub>6</sub> drop by drop under stirring at 60 °C for 2 h. After complete reaction, the obtained solution was aged for several hours until the precipitates were collected by centrifugation and washed with water and pure ethanol for 3 times, respectively. The washed solid products were dried at 70 °C for over 12 h. The organic solid of PTCDI was a commercially available pigment and was purchased from Macklin.</p></section><section xml:id="cssc202201362-sec-0006" numbered="no"><title type="main">Electrolytes</title><p>The electrolyte was prepared by dissolving appropriate amounts of NaNO<sub>3</sub> in different co‐solvent amounts of glycerol and deionized water at room temperature.</p></section><section xml:id="cssc202201362-sec-0007" numbered="no"><title type="main">Characterization of materials</title><p>The morphology was observed by using field‐emission SEM (Tescan MIRA3 FEGESEM). CV was also carried out on Ni<sub>2</sub>ZnHCF and PTCDI electrodes by using the same three‐electrode setup. The Ni<sub>2</sub>ZnHCF and PTCDI electrodes were prepared by doctor‐blade casting slurries with 70 wt % active material, 20 wt % Super P, and 10 wt % polyvinylidene fluoride (PVDF) on SS mesh. <i>N</i>‐Methyl‐2‐pyrrolidone was used as the solvent for the slurries. After drying at 60 °C under high vacuum for 12 h, electrodes with a diameter of 12 mm were punched. The charge – discharge curves of Ni<sub>2</sub>ZnHCF and PTCDI half cells were tested by coin cells, by using 1 <sc>m</sc> NaNO<sub>3</sub> aqueous solution at different concentrations of glycerol as electrolyte, respectively. The counter electrodes were oversized self‐standing activated carbon electrodes composed of 70 wt % activated carbon (Coconut shell charcoal), 20 wt % Super P, and 10 wt % polytetrafluoroethylene. For the full cells, the weight ratio of Ni<sub>2</sub>ZnHCF and PTCDI was 2 : 1, and the specific capacity was calculated based on the PTCDI weight. The average active material mass loading of Ni<sub>2</sub>ZnHCF and PTCDI was approximately 1.6 and 0.8 mg cm<sup>−2</sup>, respectively.
