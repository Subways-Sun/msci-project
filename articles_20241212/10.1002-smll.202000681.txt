Information</caption></supportingInfoItem></supportingInformation><abstractGroup><abstract type="main" xml:lang="en"><title type="main">Abstract</title><p>SnO<sub>2</sub> has been considered as a promising anode material for lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs) and sodium ion batteries (SIBs), but challenging as well for the low‐reversible conversion reaction and coulombic efficiency. To address these issues, herein, SnO<sub>2</sub> quantum dots (≈5 nm) embedded in porous N‐doped carbon matrix (SnO<sub>2</sub>/NC) are developed via a hydrothermal step combined with a self‐polymerization process at room temperature. The ultrasmall size in quantum dots can greatly shorten the ion diffusion distance and lower the internal strain, improving the conversion reaction efficiency and coulombic efficiency. The rich mesopores/micropores and highly conductive N‐doped carbon matrix can further enhance the overall conductivity and buffer effect of the composite. As a result, the optimized SnO<sub>2</sub>/NC‐2 composite for LIBs exhibits a high coulombic efficiency of 72.9%, a high discharge capacity of 1255.2 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 100 cycles and a long life‐span with a capacity of 753 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 1500 cycles at 1 A g<sup>−1</sup>. The SnO<sub>2</sub>/NC‐2 composite also displays excellent performance for SIBs, delivering a superior discharge capacity of 212.6 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 1 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 3000 cycles. These excellent results can be of visible significance for the size effect of the uniform quantum dots.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="smll202000681-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>SnO<sub>2</sub> quantum dots (≈5 nm) embedded in porous N‐doped carbon matrix (SnO<sub>2</sub>/NC) are developed via a hydrothermal step combined with a self‐polymerization process at room temperature. xml:id="smll202000681-sec-0010"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>During the past decade, the application fields of lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs) have gradually expanded from portable electronic devices to electric vehicles, which also mean that LIBs are developed to the direction of high energy/power density, high safety and long cycle life.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000681-bib-0001 #smll202000681-bib-0002 #smll202000681-bib-0003 #smll202000681-bib-0004 #smll202000681-bib-0005"><sup>1–5</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Meanwhile, sodium ion batteries (SIBs) as a potential power supply have also attracted considerable attention for large‐scale stationary energy storage in virtue of the natural abundance, low cost, and wide distribution compared with LIBs.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000681-bib-0006 #smll202000681-bib-0007 #smll202000681-bib-0008 #smll202000681-bib-0009"><sup>6–9</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, the broadly used graphite anode for LIBs cannot fully satisfy the ever‐growing energy density requirement due to its low theoretical intercalation capacity (≈372 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>) and potential safety hazard.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000681-bib-0010"><sup>10</sup></link><sup>]</sup> It is noteworthy that graphite is not suitable for sodium storage owing to the larger radius of sodium ion than that of lithium ion, which make sodium ion difficult to intercalate into lattice planes of graphite.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000681-bib-0011"><sup>11</sup></link><sup>]</sup> What is more serious is that the large ionic radius of sodium ion can also lead to the sluggish diffusion kinetics and poor structural stability during repeated charge/discharge processes.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000681-bib-0012"><sup>12</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Therefore, developing advanced anodes with improved electrochemical properties, such as higher reversible capacity, longer cycle life, and better rate capability is imperative for next‐generation LIBs and SIBs. Recently, various kinds of materials have been explored as anodes for LIBs and SIBs, including disordered carbonaceous materials,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000681-bib-0013 #smll202000681-bib-0014"><sup>13,14</sup></link><sup>]</sup> intercalation type materials (TiO<sub>2</sub>, Li<sub>4</sub>Ti<sub>5</sub>O<sub>12</sub>, NaTiO<sub>2</sub>),<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000681-bib-0015 #smll202000681-bib-0016 #smll202000681-bib-0017"><sup>15–17</sup></link><sup>]</sup> conversion reaction materials (Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>, Co<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>, NiO),<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000681-bib-0018 #smll202000681-bib-0019 #smll202000681-bib-0020"><sup>18–20</sup></link><sup>]</sup> alloying reaction materials (Si, P, Ge, Sn, Sb)<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000681-bib-0021"><sup>21</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and conversion‐alloying type materials (SnO<sub>2</sub>, Sb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>, Sb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>, Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>, SnS, SnS<sub>2</sub>).<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000681-bib-0022 #smll202000681-bib-0023 #smll202000681-bib-0024 #smll202000681-bib-0025 #smll202000681-bib-0026 #smll202000681-bib-0027"><sup>22–27</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>Among these anode materials, SnO<sub>2</sub> has gained great concern and is deemed as one of the promising anode material for both LIBs and SIBs due to its environmental benignity, appropriate lithium/sodium insertion potential and high theoretical capacity (1494 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> for lithium storage and 1378 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> for sodium storage). Nevertheless, several severe challenges have impeded its further practical application: 1) huge volume expansion (260% for Li<sub>22</sub>Sn<sub>5</sub> and 420% for Na<sub>15</sub>Sn<sub>4</sub>) causes serious pulverization and exfoliation of the electrode active material during the repeated insertion/extraction processes;<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000681-bib-0028"><sup>28</sup></link><sup>]</sup> 2) low initial coulombic efficiency (CE) derived from the low‐reversible conversion reaction and unstable solid electrolyte interface (SEI) layer; 3) poor intrinsic conductivity results in inferior rate performance.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000681-bib-0029"><sup>29</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>In order to address the aforementioned issues, several strategies have been explored and investigated. Thereinto, rationally reduction of grain size and construction of various nanostructures is considered as an effective method, consisting of nanoparticles,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000681-bib-0030"><sup>30</sup></link><sup>]</sup> nanotubes,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000681-bib-0031"><sup>31</sup></link><sup>]</sup> nanowires,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000681-bib-0032 #smll202000681-bib-0033"><sup>32,33</sup></link><sup>]</sup> nanosheets,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000681-bib-0034"><sup>34</sup></link><sup>]</sup> nanoboxes,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000681-bib-0035"><sup>35</sup></link><sup>]</sup> hollow nanostructures,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000681-bib-0036 #smll202000681-bib-0037 #smll202000681-bib-0038"><sup>36–38</sup></link><sup>]</sup> all of which can significantly enhance the lithium/sodium storage performance due to the shorter electron/ion transport length and less internal strain change during the lithium/sodium ions insertion/extraction processes. Although downsizing bulk SnO<sub>2</sub> to nanoscale can effectively buffer the volume expansion, inhibit pulverization and maintain the structural integrity of the electrode to some extent, the large specific surface area and surface free energy of nano SnO<sub>2</sub> together with the direct contact with electrolyte, which would lead to the occurrence of undesirable side effects and a large irreversible capacity loss. Furthermore, the intrinsic poor conductivity of SnO<sub>2</sub> can also limit the fast electron transmission and restrict its rate capability. Another effective method is to combine SnO<sub>2</sub> with various carbonaceous materials to curb the volume dilation, agglomeration and cracking of SnO<sub>2</sub>, as well as improve the conductivity of the electrode. The reported carbon materials composited with SnO<sub>2</sub> includes carbon fibers,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000681-bib-0039 #smll202000681-bib-0040"><sup>39,40</sup></link><sup>]</sup> carbon nanotubes,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000681-bib-0004 #smll202000681-bib-0041"><sup>4,41</sup></link><sup>]</sup> graphene,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000681-bib-0042 #smll202000681-bib-0043 #smll202000681-bib-0044"><sup>42–44</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and disordered carbon materials,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000681-bib-0045 #smll202000681-bib-0046 #smll202000681-bib-0047 #smll202000681-bib-0048 #smll202000681-bib-0049 #smll202000681-bib-0050 #smll202000681-bib-0051 #smll202000681-bib-0052"><sup>45–52</sup></link><sup>]</sup> which substantially exhibit improved structural stability and rate capability during intensive cycles. For example, Song and co‐workers<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000681-bib-0042"><sup>42</sup></link><sup>]</sup> have designed and synthesized SnO<sub>2</sub> nanocrystals anchored on nitrogen/sulfur codoped graphene (NSGS) through a molecular engineering of layer‐by‐layer assembly technique. When evaluated as an anode material for LIBs and SIBs, the optimized NSGS delivers a high reversible capacity of 893.4 and 180.2 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at a current density of 1 and 0.5 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 500 cycles, respectively, showing superior electrochemical lithium/sodium storage properties. Lou and co‐workers<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000681-bib-0045"><sup>45</sup></link><sup>]</sup> have rationally designed and fabricated SnO<sub>2</sub>/nitrogen‐doped carbon (SnO<sub>2</sub>/NC) submicroboxes with a carbon shell thickness of 40 nm employing Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> submicrocubes as removable templates. When used as an anode material for LIBs, a high reversible capacity of 491 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> can be maintained at a current density of 0.5 A g<sup>−1</sup> for 100 cycles, displaying enhanced electrochemical performance</p><p>In consideration of the major issues of the SnO<sub>2</sub> (low‐reversible conversion reaction and huge volume expansion) and the improved strategies (reduction of grain size and well coating), herein, we present a quantum dot‐size SnO<sub>2</sub> to significantly solve the above‐mentioned issues, i.e., to shorten the ion diffusion distance and lower the internal strain and improve the conversion reaction efficiency and initial CE. The individual ultrasmall SnO<sub>2</sub> quantum dots (≈5 nm) embedding in porous N‐doped carbon matrix are fabricated by a combination method, containing a simple hydrothermal process and subsequent a self‐polymerization process of dopamine to synchronously synthesize the SnO<sub>2</sub>/NC composites. Owing to the synergistic effect of SnO<sub>2</sub> quantum dots, as well as the protection of conductive carbon matrix, the electrochemical reaction kinetics and long cycle stability of the SnO<sub>2</sub>/NC composites are improved. More meaningfully, the prepared SnO<sub>2</sub>/NC composites with excellent electrochemical properties might be promising anode materials for next‐generation LIBs and SIBs.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202000681-sec-0020"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p><b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll202000681-fig-0001"><b>1</b></link> schematically displays the synthetic process of the as‐prepared SnO<sub>2</sub>/NC composites. Initially, a simple hydrothermal method is carried out to obtain ultrasmall SnO<sub>2</sub> quantum dots. Specifically, at the stage of the hydrothermal reaction, the hydrolysis of Sn<sup>4+</sup> first occurs to form Sn(OH)<sub>4</sub> white precipitate and then transforms to ultrasmall SnO<sub>2</sub> quantum dots with elevated temperature and prolonged reaction time. In the subsequent step, the obtained ultrasmall SnO<sub>2</sub> quantum dots are homogeneously dispersed in the dopamine solution, in which the solvent is a mixture system containing water, ethanol, and ammonia. Meanwhile, polydopamine (PDA) as a carbon precursor can be formed by self‐polymerization of dopamine and penetrated among the SnO<sub>2</sub> quantum dots. The size of PDA can be controlled by tuning the ratio of ammonia to dopamine, and presents a decreased trend with increasing the relative content of ammonia.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000681-bib-0053"><sup>53</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The final carbonization process enables a conversion of PDA into N‐doped carbon matrix, which is favorable for the fast transmission of lithium/sodium ions and accommodate the volume expansion during the insertion of lithium/sodium ions.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000681-bib-0045"><sup>45</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><figure xml:id="smll202000681-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll202000681:smll202000681-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll202000681-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Schematic illustration of the synthesis route for the SnO<sub>2</sub>@NC composites.</caption></figure><p>The panoramic morphology and microstructure of the as‐prepared SnO<sub>2</sub>/NC composites are characterized by SEM and TEM. As displayed in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll202000681-fig-0002"><b>2</b>a</link>–<link href="#smll202000681-fig-0002">d</link>, all of the three SnO<sub>2</sub>/NC composites possess similar porous structure with SnO<sub>2</sub>/NC nanoclusters interconnecting with each other to form a continuous network, which could be served as diffusion channels for the lithium/sodium ions migration and supply extra space to accommodate the volume change in charge/discharge process. TEM analysis is further testified that the nanoclusters of SnO<sub>2</sub>/NC composites are composed of ultrasmall SnO<sub>2</sub> quantum dots embedded in carbon matrix (Figure <link href="#smll202000681-fig-0002">2e</link>,<link href="#smll202000681-fig-0002">f</link>). On the whole, the sizes of the SnO<sub>2</sub>/NC nanoclusters are reduced with increasing ammonia content (Figure S1, Supporting Information). Thus it can be seen that the content of ammonia plays an important role in regulating the size of nanoclusters. The magnified HRTEM image further reveals the state of nanoclusters. As shown in Figure <link href="#smll202000681-fig-0002">2g</link>, the individual SnO<sub>2</sub> quantum dots (≈5 nm) orderly interconnect with each other, and a homogeneous amorphous carbon layer with a thickness of 1–2 nm can be clearly seen, which is acted as a protective medium for stabilizing the structure. In addition, the HRTEM image shows several obvious lattice fringes with different inter‐plane distances of 0.26 and 0.33 nm, corresponding to the (101) and (110) crystal planes of SnO<sub>2</sub>. The selected area electron diffraction (SAED) pattern of the SnO<sub>2</sub>/NC‐2 composite (Figure <link href="#smll202000681-fig-0002">2h</link>) reveals four clear and bright diffraction rings, which represent typical (110), (101), (211), and (301) crystal phases of SnO<sub>2</sub> from inside to outside, implying its polycrystalline nature. The highly distributed characteristic of C, N, O, and Sn elements in the SnO<sub>2</sub>/NC‐2 composite is also illustrated by EDX mapping. Scanning TEM (STEM) (Figure <link href="#smll202000681-fig-0002">2i</link>) and the corresponding EDX mapping (Figure <link href="#smll202000681-fig-0002">2j</link>–<link href="#smll202000681-fig-0002">m</link>) indicate that all the elements of the composite are uniformly dispersed and coexisted. The homogeneous spatial distribution of Sn and O elements are suited in the area of C and N elements, which suggests that SnO<sub>2</sub> quantum dots are well embedded into the carbon matrix, and also signifies the successful doping of N element.</p><figure xml:id="smll202000681-fig-0002"><label>2</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll202000681:smll202000681-fig-0002" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll202000681-fig-0002-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a–c) SEM images of SnO<sub>2</sub>/NC‐1, SnO<sub>2</sub>/NC‐2 and SnO<sub>2</sub>/NC‐3, d) magnified SEM image of SnO<sub>2</sub>/NC‐2, e,f) TEM images, g) HRTEM image, h) SAED pattern, i) STEM image, and its elemental mapping images for j) C, k) N, l) O, and m) Sn elements of SnO<sub>2</sub>/NC‐2.</caption></figure><p>The crystallographic structure and phase purity of the as‐prepared SnO<sub>2</sub>/NC samples are analyzed from the XRD patterns. As depicted in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll202000681-fig-0003"><b>3</b>a</link>, all of the three SnO<sub>2</sub>/NC samples reveal similar XRD patterns and are well indexed to tetragonal structure of SnO<sub>2</sub> (JCPDS No. 41‐1445). No other diffraction peaks belong to Sn or SnO can be detected, indicating the high purity of SnO<sub>2</sub> tetragonal crystalline phase. While a broad peak located at around 23° is attributed to amorphous carbon content, suggesting the SnO<sub>2</sub>/NC composites have been successfully prepared. The chemical bonds in the prepared SnO<sub>2</sub>/NC composites are further examined by FTIR spectra. In the spectra of the three SnO<sub>2</sub>/NC samples (Figure <link href="#smll202000681-fig-0003">3b</link>), the adsorption peaks at about 3410 cm<sup>−1</sup> could be ascribed to the stretching vibration of the OH bond toward water molecules.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000681-bib-0054"><sup>54</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The peaks located at around 1600, 1410, and 1050 cm<sup>−1</sup> are associated with the stretching vibrations of CC/CN, COH, and CO functional group, respectively.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000681-bib-0055"><sup>55</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The band of 1280 cm<sup>−1</sup> is ascribed to the deforming vibration of CH and NH. Figure <link href="#smll202000681-fig-0007">7c</link> exhibits the surface capacitive contribution about 68.7% of the total capacity at a scan rate of 0.5 mV s<sup>−1</sup>. More importantly, the contribution from the surface capacitive effect is gradually increasing as the scan rate rising from 0.1 to 0.9 mV s<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#smll202000681-fig-0007">7d</link>). While the higher capacitive contribution plays an essential role in promoting the rapid electron transfer, lithium ions diffusion, and improving the rate performance of the electrode.</p><p>In order to further investigate the diffusion coefficient of Li<sup>+</sup> (<i>D</i><sub>Li</sub><i><sup>+</sup></i>) in the SnO<sub>2</sub>/NC‐2 composite electrode, the galvanostatic intermittent titration technique (GITT) measurement is performed at room temperature, which is regarded as an efficient method to calculate chemical diffusion coefficients.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000681-bib-0064"><sup>64</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Figure <link href="#smll202000681-fig-0007">7e</link> presents the GITT curves of the SnO<sub>2</sub>/NC‐2 electrode during the second cycle. As the GITT test is applied, the cell is first discharged at a constant current density of 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> for 30 min, followed by an open‐circuit stand for 120 min to allow the cell voltage to reach a near equilibrium state in the voltage range of 0.01–3 V (vs Li<sup>+</sup>/Li). This process is repeated for the whole working potential range until the final cutoff voltage is reached 0.01 V. For the charging process, the cell is operated with the same current density and relaxation time, and the setting was repeated until the final potential ceiling is reached. Figure <link href="#smll202000681-fig-0007">7f</link> illustrates an amplified single GITT curve, schematically presenting the meanings of τ, Δ<i>E</i><sub>s</sub> and Δ<i>E</i><sub>t</sub>, which will be further explained later. Assuming that lithium ions transport in the electrode obeys the Fick’s second law, on the basis of the GITT result, <i>D</i><sub>Li</sub><sup>+</sup> can be calculated from the equation: <i>D</i><sub>Li</sub><i><sup>+</sup></i> = (4<i>L</i><sup>2</sup>/<i>πτ</i>) × (Δ<i>E<sub>s</sub></i>/Δ<i>E<sub>t</sub></i>)<sup>2</sup>, in which <i>L</i> is the Li<sup>+</sup> diffusion length, corresponded to the electrode thickness; τ is the relaxation time (min); Δ<i>E</i><sub>s</sub> stands for the steady‐state potential change via the current pulse; Δ<i>E</i><sub>t</sub> represents the potential change within the pulse time after subtracting the IR drop.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000681-bib-0065"><sup>65</sup></link><sup>]</sup> According to the equation above, the <i>D</i><sub>Li</sub><sup>+</sup> in the SnO<sub>2</sub>/NC‐2 electrode can be calculate from the obtained GITT curves, and are shown in Figure S9a,b (Supporting Information). The values of <i>D</i><sub>Li</sub><sup>+</sup> lie in the range of 1.213 × 10<sup>−13</sup> to 6.531 × 10<sup>−13</sup> cm<sup>−2</sup> s<sup>−1</sup> at lithiation state (discharge process), and vary from 1.28 × 10<sup>−13</sup> to 1.426 × 10<sup>−12</sup> cm<sup>−2</sup> s<sup>−1</sup> at delithiation state (charge process). During most of the potential range, the values of <i>D</i><sub>Li</sub><sup>+</sup> exhibit only a little variation (10<sup>−13</sup>–10<sup>−12</sup> cm<sup>−2</sup> s<sup>−1</sup>). Pure SnO<sub>2</sub> anodes of different structures, such as SnO<sub>2</sub> thin film, ultrasmall nanocrystalline SnO<sub>2</sub> particle, have smaller values of <i>D</i><sub>Li</sub><sup>+</sup>, which are varying from 10<sup>−18</sup> to 10<sup>−13</sup> cm<sup>−2</sup> s<sup>−1</sup>.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000681-bib-0066 #smll202000681-bib-0067"><sup>66,67</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The improved <i>D</i><sub>Li</sub><sup>+</sup> might be due to its developed porous structure, which can provide large electrode/electrolyte contact area and efficient lithium ions diffusion pathways, facilitating the fast diffusion of lithium ions.</p><p>In view of the excellent lithium storage behaviors of the SnO<sub>2</sub>/NC‐2 composite, it is also employed as the anode material for SIBs to investigate the electrochemical properties. <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll202000681-fig-0008"><b>8</b>a</link> exhibits the initial five CV curves of the SnO<sub>2</sub>/NC‐2 composite at a scan rate of 0.1 mV s<sup>−1</sup> within the voltage range of 0.01 to 3 V (vs Na<sup>+</sup>/Na). In the first cathodic scanning, the reduction peak ranging from 0.6 to 1.2 V can be ascribed to the formation of a SEI film on electrode surface and the reaction between SnO<sub>2</sub> and sodium ions to form metallic Sn and Na<sub>2</sub>O matrix (SnO<sub>2</sub> + 4Na<sup>+</sup> + 4e<sup>−</sup> → Sn + 2Na<sub>2</sub>O).<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000681-bib-0011 #smll202000681-bib-0068 #smll202000681-bib-0069 #smll202000681-bib-0070"><sup>11,68–70</sup></link><sup>]</sup> This peak disappears in the following scans, demonstrating that this reaction is irreversible. Another reduction peak positioned in the region of 0.2 to 0.4 V is mainly due to the formation of Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>Sn alloys (Sn + <i>x</i>Na<sup>+</sup> + <i>x</i>e<sup>−</sup> → Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>Sn).<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000681-bib-0071"><sup>71</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In addition, a sharp reduction peak located at around 0.01 V is assigned to the generation of Na<sub>3</sub>Sn and Na<sub>15</sub>Sn<sub>4</sub>, together with the reversible insertion of sodium ions into the amorphous carbon layers and nanocavities (C + <i>x</i>Na<sup>+</sup> + <i>x</i>e<sup>−</sup> → Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>C).<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000681-bib-0071 #smll202000681-bib-0072"><sup>71,72</sup></link><sup>]</sup> During the initial anodic scan process, the oxidation peaks at ≈0.25 and 1.25 V are caused by the dealloying reaction of Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>Sn (<i>x</i> ≤ 3.75) and the extraction of sodium ions from the carbon layer. Furthermore, one weak anodic peak observed at around 2.1 V might be corresponded to the partial formation of SnO<sub>2</sub>, originated from the reaction of Sn and Na<sub>2</sub>O. After the initial irreversible scan, the CV curves are nearly overlapped, indicating the excellent reversibility of the as‐prepared SnO<sub>2</sub>/NC‐2 composite. Figure <link href="#smll202000681-fig-0008">8b</link> shows the typical galvanostatic charge/discharge profiles of the SnO<sub>2</sub>/NC‐2 composite for the 1st, 2nd, 3rd, and 100th cycles tested at a current density of 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> in a voltage range of 0.01–3 V. The initial charge and discharge capacities are 337.3 and 570.1 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively, corresponding to a CE of 59.2%. The irreversible capacity loss in the initial cycle is mainly attributed to the formation of a SEI layer and the inactivated sodium ions in the nanoporous sites of the amorphous carbon, which are difficult to extract. Moreover, the 100th charge/discharge cycle is almost overlapped with the 2nd and 3rd cycles, demonstrating the outstanding reversibility and stability of the SnO<sub>2</sub>/NC‐2 composite electrode. Figure <link href="#smll202000681-fig-0008">8c</link> displays the cycling performance of the as‐prepared three SnO<sub>2</sub>/NC composites at a current density of 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> between 0.01 and 3 V. Obviously, the SnO<sub>2</sub>/NC‐2 composite exhibits higher discharge capacity compared with the SnO<sub>2</sub>/NC‐1 and SnO<sub>2</sub>/NC‐3 composites. After 100 repeated charge/discharge cycles, the SnO<sub>2</sub>/NC‐2 composite still delivers a high discharge capacity of 342.2 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> and maintains a high CE of around 99.5%. The rate capability of the SnO<sub>2</sub>/NC‐2 composite is also evaluated at different current densities. As displayed in Figure <link href="#smll202000681-fig-0008">8d</link>, the SnO<sub>2</sub>/NC‐2 composite reveals average discharge capacities of 344.3, 312.2, 269.2, 236.5, 194.9, 141, 105.6 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, as the current density changes from 0.1 to 0.2, 0.5, 1, 2, 4, and 6 A g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively, suggesting its superior rapid charge/discharge performance. The long cycling stability of the SnO<sub>2</sub>/NC‐2 composite is further investigated at a high current density of 1 A g<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#smll202000681-fig-0008">8e</link>). A reversible discharge capacity of 212.6 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> with a high CE of about 99.5% can be obtained even after 3000 cycles, indicating the impressive stability under high current condition. In comparison with the previously representative reports, the sodium storage performance of the SnO<sub>2</sub>/NC‐2 composite still remains competitive (Table S2, Supporting Information).</p><figure xml:id="smll202000681-fig-0008"><label>8</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll202000681:smll202000681-fig-0008" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll202000681-fig-0008-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) CV curves at a scan rate of 0.1 mV s<sup>−1</sup>, b) galvanostatic charge/discharge curves of SnO<sub>2</sub>/NC‐2 at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>, c) cycling performance of SnO<sub>2</sub>/NC‐1, SnO<sub>2</sub>/NC‐2 and SnO<sub>2</sub>/NC‐3 at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>, d) rate performance of SnO<sub>2</sub>/NC‐2 from 0.1 to 6 A g<sup>−1</sup>, e) cycling performance of SnO<sub>2</sub>/NC‐2 at 1 A g<sup>−1</sup>.</caption></figure><p>As stated above, the as‐synthesized SnO<sub>2</sub>/NC‐2 composite exhibits outstanding lithium and sodium storage properties, which can be ascribed to the synergistic effect of ultrasmall SnO<sub>2</sub> quantum dots, porous structure and N‐doped carbon matrix. In particular, 1) the individual SnO<sub>2</sub> quantum dots (≈5 nm) not only can reduce the absolute volume expansion during the lithium/sodium intercalation process but also can shorten the diffusion distance of lithium/sodium ions and electrons, resulting in the enhancement of electrochemical reaction kinetics; 2) the rich mesopores/micropores in the composite can provide plentiful active sites and conducting channels, which is beneficial for the fast electron transport and ion diffusion, and thus leading to higher discharge capacity and good rate capability. Additionally, the developed porous structure can offer adequate buffer space for alleviating the volume change of SnO<sub>2</sub>; 3) the highly conductive N‐doped carbon matrix can also play an essential role in tolerating the volume change, preventing the aggregation and pulverization of SnO<sub>2</sub> quantum dots during cycling, hence leading to an improved charge/discharge performance; 4) the ingenious structure of individual ultrasmall SnO<sub>2</sub> quantum dots embedding in the porous carbon matrix further strengthens the synergistic effect of the three aspects above; 5) more importantly, appropriate size of the SnO<sub>2</sub>/NC nanoclusters combining with moderate specific surface area can deliver the optimal initial CE during the initial charge/discharge process, and possess higher discharge capacity in the subsequent cycles.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202000681-sec-0030"><title type="main">Conclusions</title><p>In summary, the SnO<sub>2</sub>/NC composites with ingenious structure of individual ultrasmall SnO<sub>2</sub> quantum dots (≈5 nm) embedding in the porous N‐doped carbon matrix have been successfully fabricated through a simple hydrothermal method to acquire the SnO<sub>2</sub> quantum dots, and followed by a self‐polymerization process of dopamine to synchronously form the SnO<sub>2</sub>/NC nanoclusters. In this design, the size of the SnO<sub>2</sub>/NC nanoclusters can be controlled by the concentration of ammonia in solution, and presents a slight decreased trend with increasing the content of ammonia. When investigated as anode materials for LIBs and SIBs, the optimal SnO<sub>2</sub>/NC‐2 composite electrode exhibits excellent electrochemical performance with higher initial CE, outstanding rate performance and long‐term cycling stability due to the synergistic effect of the SnO<sub>2</sub> quantum dots, rich mesopores/micropores and highly conductive N‐doped carbon matrix in the composite, all of which can effectively buffer the volume change of active materials and facilitate the fast electronic transport and ionic diffusion, and thus leading to an improved electrochemical performance. These results will be essential parameters and helpful for developing more stable electrodes with high energy/power density for the application of next‐generation LIBs and SIBs.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202000681-sec-0040" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>This research was financially supported by the National Key Research and development Program of China (Grant No. 2017YFE9128100), the National Natural Science Foundation of China (Grant No. 21221061), and the Open Project Program of Key Laboratory of Preparation and Application of Environmental Friendly Materials (Jilin Normal University), Ministry of Education, China (No. 2019005).</p></section><section xml:id="smll202000681-sec-0050" type="conflictOfInterest" numbered="no"><title type="main">Conflict of Interest</title><p>The authors declare no conflict of interest.</p></section><bibliography xml:id="smll202000681-bibl-0001" style="numbered"><bib xml:id="smll202000681-bib-0001"><citation type="journal" xml:id="smll202000681-cit-0001"><author><givenNames>M.</givenNames> <familyName>Armand</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>J.
