Outside‐in doping of carbon‐based anodes has been extensively explored. Nickel and NiS<sub>2</sub> particles embedded in nitrogen and sulfur codoped porous graphene can significantly improve the electrochemical performance. Herein a built‐in heteroatom “self‐doping” of albumen‐derived graphene for sodium storage is reported. The built‐in sulfur and nitrogen in albumen act as the doping source during the carbonization of proteins. The sulfur‐rich proteins in albumen can also guide the doping and nucleation of nickel sulfide nanoparticles. Additionally, the porous architecture of the carbonized proteins is achieved through removable KCl/NaCl salts (medium) under high‐temperature melting conditions. During the carbonization process, nitrogen can also reduce the carbonization temperature of thermally stable carbon materials. In this work, the NS‐graphene delivered a specific capacity of 108.3 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 800 cycles under a constant current density of 500 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. In contrast, the Ni/NiS<sub>2</sub>/NS‐graphene maintained a specific capacity of 134.4 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>; thus the presence of Ni/NiS<sub>2</sub> particles improved the electrochemical performance of the whole composite.</p></abstract><abstract type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p><b>Egged on anodes</b>: Nitrogen‐ and sulfur‐rich doped graphene (Ni/NiS<sub>2</sub>/NS‐graphene and NS‐graphene) composites are obtained through the built‐in heteroatom “self‐doping” of albumen in a facile molten‐salt process. These composites exhibit excellent electrical stability and reversibility (see figure).<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:09476539:media:chem201902575:chem201902575-toc-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/chem201902575-toc-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta><noteGroup xml:id="chem201902575-ntgp-0002"><note xml:id="chem201902575-note-1001"><p>These authors contributed equally to this work.</p></note></noteGroup></header><body sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="chem201902575-sec-0001" numbered="no"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>The storage of green, renewable energy has been a challenge for decades.<link href="#chem201902575-bib-0001"/> Owing to widespread sodium reserves, with excellent abundance and low cost, sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) hold great promise to meet extremely high material demands for electric storage, especially large, stationary electrical energy storage (EES) systems in the application of renewable energy sources and smart grids.<link href="#chem201902575-bib-0002"/> However, the current dominant carbon‐based anodes for SIBs have encountered many bottlenecks, such as rapid reduction in reversible capacity, low coulombic efficiency, and inferior rate performance.<link href="#chem201902575-bib-0003"/> Hence, the upgrading of carbon‐based anodes has been one of the major focuses in SIB research.<link href="#chem201902575-bib-0004"/> Heteroatom doping of carbon‐based anodes has proved to be an effective method. In this regard, nitrogen and sulfur doping could increase the electron mobility and surface hydrophilicity, which is beneficial to charge transfer and electrolyte uptake of the electrode.<link href="#chem201902575-bib-0005"/> Additionally, nanostructured transition‐metal chalcogenide doping could alleviate volume changes during sodiation/desodiation<link href="#chem201902575-bib-0006"/> and nickel particles can promote electrochemical performance,<link href="#chem201902575-bib-0005b"/> whereas a commixture of Ni/NiS<sub>2</sub> particles with nitrogen and sulfur codoped porous graphene can significantly improve the electrochemical performance. Molten‐salt medium is an efficient way to capture nitrogen during the preparation of carbon nitride.<link href="#chem201902575-bib-0007"/> Moreover, during the carbonization process, nitrogen can reduce the carbonization temperature of thermally stable carbon materials.<link href="#chem201902575-bib-0008"/> In particular, it has been demonstrated that sodium ions can penetrate into carbon matrixes, and therefore, induce the realignment of amorphous carbon and manipulate the cross‐linked network.<link href="#chem201902575-bib-0009"/> It is highly desirable to combine these means of heteroatom doping and metal sulfide/metallic nanoparticle doping with the molten‐salt method towards the development of high‐performance anodes for SIBs.</p><p>Herein, we developed a “self‐doping” strategy based on albumen. Albumen is mainly composed of nitrogen‐ (≈12 wt %) and sulfur‐rich (≈9 wt %) protein.<link href="#chem201902575-bib-0010"/> The protein can be employed as a unique carbon source. Built‐in nitrogen and sulfur in albumen can enable the self‐doping of protein‐derived graphene, thereby avoiding additional doping procedures. In view of protein‐assisted biomineralization, sulfur‐rich proteins in albumen can also guide the doping and nucleation of nickel sulfide nanoparticles.<link href="#chem201902575-bib-0011"/> In addition, the porous graphene architecture was fabricated through the use of removable KCl/NaCl salts under high‐temperature melting conditions. Accordingly, we have successfully prepared hierarchically porous, Ni/NiS<sub>2</sub> nanoparticle anchored, and N,S‐doped graphene (denoted as Ni/NiS<sub>2</sub>/NS‐graphene) materials through the pyrolysis of a mixture of albumen and pore‐forming agents, as well as nickel nitrate, followed by washing and drying. The as‐prepared Ni/NiS<sub>2</sub>/NS‐graphene exhibits a remarkable electrochemical performance as an anode for SIBs.</p></section><section xml:id="chem201902575-sec-0002" numbered="no"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p>The procedure for the synthesis of the Ni/NiS<sub>2</sub>/NS‐graphene composites is illustrated in Scheme <link href="#chem201902575-fig-5001"/>. Initially, certain amounts of Ni(NO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub><b>⋅</b>6 H<sub>2</sub>O, NaCl and KCl are added to the fresh solution of albumen to form the precursor suspension liquid. Notably, the Ni<sup>2+</sup> ions can be captured by the protein due to complexation or biomineralization and excess NaCl and KCl can accelerate protein precipitation. The precursor suspension liquid is directly dried in an oven at 150 °C and then ground through ball‐milling. After annealing in high‐purity argon at 700 °C for 2 h, and rinsing with deionized water and ethanol under ultrasonication, the Ni/NiS<sub>2</sub>/NS‐graphene composites are obtained. During this reaction, the N‐ and S‐rich bulk protein is gradually exfoliated into nanosheets as the temperature increases and the exfoliated nanosheets are transformed into N,S‐doped graphene at temperatures above 650 °C. Meanwhile, graphene layers that encapsulate metallic Ni/NiS<sub>2</sub> nanoparticles are generated in situ on the surface of graphene nanosheets. Additionally, the melting salt developed during carbonization of the protein accelerates the interconnection of graphene nanosheets and facilitates the formation of pores and voids, resulting in the hierarchically porous interconnected network structure. Similar to Ni/NiS<sub>2</sub>/NS‐graphene composites, pure N‐ and S‐doped graphene (NS‐graphene) was prepared without the use of Ni(NO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub><b>⋅</b>6 H<sub>2</sub>O salt.</p><figure xml:id="chem201902575-fig-5001"><title type="figureName">Scheme</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:09476539:media:chem201902575:chem201902575-fig-5001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/chem201902575-fig-5001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Fabrication strategy for the preparation of Ni/NiS<sub>2</sub>/NS‐graphene composites and NS‐graphene by using the molten‐salt method.</p></caption></figure><p>The morphology and microstructure of the as‐prepared samples were investigated by using SEM and TEM. As illustrated by the low‐magnification SEM image in Figure <link href="#chem201902575-fig-0001"/> a, the as‐synthesized Ni/NiS<sub>2</sub>/NS‐graphene composite is a fluffy state and features a 3D macroporous network. The high‐magnification SEM image (Figure <link href="#chem201902575-fig-0001"/> b) of Ni/NiS<sub>2</sub>/NS‐graphene exhibits an interconnected structure consisting of uniform nanosheets, similar to that of NS‐graphene (Figure <link href="#chem201902575-fig-0001"/> c, d) without the Ni additive. The SEM–energy‐dispersive X‐ray spectroscopy (EDX) mapping images (Figure S1 in the Supporting Information) of Ni/NiS<sub>2</sub>/NS‐graphene indicate a uniform distribution of C, Ni, S, and N elements (the atomic percentages are shown in Table S1 in the Supporting Information) throughout Ni/NiS<sub>2</sub>/NS‐graphene nanosheets. The ultrathin characteristics of Ni/NiS<sub>2</sub>/NS‐graphene (Figure <link href="#chem201902575-fig-0002"/> a) and NS‐graphene (Figure <link href="#chem201902575-fig-0002"/> c, d) were further confirmed by means of high‐resolution (HR) TEM. HR TEM images captured from different regions in Figure <link href="#chem201902575-fig-0002"/> a clearly suggest the existence of metallic Ni and NiS<sub>2</sub> nanoparticles (Figure <link href="#chem201902575-fig-0002"/> b) incorporated in NS‐graphene nanosheets. Specifically, the lattice fringe of 2.04 Å is typical of the (111) lattice plane of metallic nickel, whereas the lattice spacing of 2.84 Å corresponds to the (200) lattice plane of the vaesite NiS<sub>2</sub> phase. The phase composition of Ni/NiS<sub>2</sub>/NS‐graphene was characterized by means of powder XRD. As illustrated in Figure <link href="#chem201902575-fig-0003"/>, the diffraction pattern shows three sets of diffraction peaks, in which peaks at 2 <i>θ</i>=31.78, 45.48, and 56.59° can be assigned to the (200), (220), and (222) planes, respectively, of vaesite‐type NiS<sub>2</sub> (JCPDS card no. 11‐0099), and peaks at 2 <i>θ</i>=44.42 and 51.86° can be indexed to the (111) and (200) planes, respectively, of metallic Ni. The band centered at around 25° can be attributed to multilayered graphene nanosheets. All diffraction peaks of Ni and NiS<sub>2</sub> matched well with the standard diffraction peaks of Ni and NiS<sub>2</sub>; this indicates the combination of NS‐graphene and Ni and NiS<sub>2</sub> nanoparticles without any impurities. The thermal behavior of Ni/NiS<sub>2</sub>/NS‐graphene nanosheets was investigated by means of thermogravimetric (TG) and differential scanning calorimetry (DSC) studies. As shown in Figure S2 in the Supporting Information, Ni/NiS<sub>2</sub>/NS‐graphene undergoes a two‐step mass loss: the initial slight weight loss of 6.51 % between 80 and 120 °C corresponds to the gradual loss of absorbed water molecules;<link href="#chem201902575-bib-0012"/> the second step, with a notable weight loss of 74.0 %, in the range of 120 to 650 °C is attributed to the combustion of the carbonaceous component and the transformation of encapsulated metallic Ni and NiS<sub>2</sub> into the final residue of NiO.<link href="#chem201902575-bib-0013"/> The corresponding DSC curve is consistent with that obtained through TG analysis. The change in heat flow at around 500 °C in the DSC curve suggests the oxidation of nickel atoms. Because the mass fraction of the NiO residue is 19 %, the content of NS‐graphene in Ni/NiS<sub>2</sub>/NS‐graphene composites can be calculated (details are shown in the Supporting Information) on the basis of the chemical reaction and is found to be 62 %.<link href="#chem201902575-bib-0013"/></p><figure xml:id="chem201902575-fig-0001"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:09476539:media:chem201902575:chem201902575-fig-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/chem201902575-fig-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>SEM images of Ni/NiS<sub>2</sub>/NS‐graphene composites (a, b) and NS‐graphene composites (c, d).</p></caption></figure><figure xml:id="chem201902575-fig-0002"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:09476539:media:chem201902575:chem201902575-fig-0002"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/chem201902575-fig-0002-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>TEM (a) and HR TEM (b) images of Ni/NiS<sub>2</sub>/NS‐graphene composite and TEM images NS‐graphene (c, d).</p></caption></figure><figure xml:id="chem201902575-fig-0003"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:09476539:media:chem201902575:chem201902575-fig-0003"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/chem201902575-fig-0003-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Raman of Ni/NiS<sub>2</sub>/NS‐graphene (red) and NS‐graphene (black) composites; XRD pattern of Ni/NiS<sub>2</sub>/NS‐graphene (red) and NS‐graphene (black) composites.</p></caption></figure><p>To investigate the chemical composition and valence states of the nickel species, Ni/NiS<sub>2</sub>/NS‐graphene was characterized by means of X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS). The XPS survey spectrum of Ni/NiS<sub>2</sub>/NS‐graphene demonstrated the existence and atomic percentages of C, N, Ni, S, and O elements (Figure S3 and Table S2 in the Supporting Information). Figure <link href="#chem201902575-fig-0004"/> a–d shows the typical C 1 s, N 1 s, Ni 2 p, and S 2 p XPS spectra of Ni/NiS<sub>2</sub>/NS‐graphene, as obtained through the Gaussian fitting technique. The deconvoluted C 1 s XPS spectrum shows binding energies at 284.4, 285.1, 286.3, 287.5, and 289.4 eV, which suggest the presence of C−C, C−OH, C−N−C, C−S, and O=C−OH groups.<link href="#chem201902575-bib-0014"/> For the N 1 s spectrum, three separated peaks at 398.1, 399.4, and 400.6 eV can be observed, which correspond to pyridinic N, pyrrolic N, and graphitic N, respectively.<link href="#chem201902575-bib-0015a"/> In the Ni 2 p spectrum of Ni/NiS<sub>2</sub>/NS‐graphene, strong peaks at 855.3 eV for Ni 2 p<sub>3/2</sub> and 873.3 eV for Ni 2 p<sub>1/2</sub> are detected; these suggest the Ni<sup>2+</sup> state.<link href="#chem201902575-bib-0015"/> Two satellite peaks centered at about 861 and 880 eV can be attributed to nickel–oxygen species on the NiS<sub>2</sub> surface. In the S 2 p XPS spectrum, peaks at 162.0 eV for S 2 p<sub>1/2</sub> and 163.5 eV for S 2 p<sub>3/2</sub> are spin‐orbit characteristics of S<sub>2</sub><sup>2−</sup>, which is bonded to Ni<sup>2+</sup> ions in NiS<sub>2</sub>.<link href="#chem201902575-bib-0015b"/> In addition, the peak at 164.8 eV is reasonably assigned to bridging S<sup>−</sup> and/or apical S<sup>2−</sup> ligands, whereas the binding energies at 167.9 and 169.1 eV can be attributed to the −S−O− and S=O bonds,<link href="#chem201902575-bib-0015b"/> which are generally related to surface oxidation. Raman scattering spectroscopy (Figure <link href="#chem201902575-fig-0003"/>) was performed to obtain further evidence of the presence of graphene.<link href="#chem201902575-bib-0014b"/> The ratios of D to G bands for Ni/NiS<sub>2</sub>/NS‐graphene composites and NS‐graphene are 0.94 and 0.93, respectively; thus indicating the formation of graphene. The broad 2 D band suggests that the two samples are mainly composed of multilayered graphene, rather than single‐layered graphene. The porous structure and specific surface area of Ni/NiS<sub>2</sub>/NS‐graphene and NS‐graphene were characterized by nitrogen adsorption–desorption measurements at 77 K. Figure <link href="#chem201902575-fig-0005"/> shows that Ni/NiS<sub>2</sub>/NS‐graphene features type I adsorption behavior characterized by a micro‐ and mesoporous nature. The corresponding BET specific surface area, pore volume, and average pore size are calculated to be 491.58 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup>, 3.96 cm<sup>3</sup> g<sup>−1</sup>, and 4.20 nm, respectively. In general, carbon materials obtained by direct pyrolysis of biomass normally possess a relatively low surface area, unless other chemical treatments, for example, KOH etching, are included.<link href="#chem201902575-bib-0016"/> In this work, Ni/NiS<sub>2</sub>/NS‐graphene with a high BET surface area was produced through a simple melting method, with the aid of a molten salt, which is more attractive in terms of green chemistry. For use as ab anode in SIBs, the porous structure and high surface area of Ni/NiS<sub>2</sub>/NS‐graphene are expected to provide a 3D interconnected conductive network that facilitates charge transfer and leads to enhanced electrochemical performance.</p><figure xml:id="chem201902575-fig-0004"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:09476539:media:chem201902575:chem201902575-fig-0004"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/chem201902575-fig-0004-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>HR XPS spectra of a) C 1 s, b) N 1 s, c) Ni 2 p, and d) S 2 p for the Ni/NiS<sub>2</sub>/NS‐graphene composites.</p></caption></figure><figure xml:id="chem201902575-fig-0005"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:09476539:media:chem201902575:chem201902575-fig-0005"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/chem201902575-fig-0005-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>N<sub>2</sub> adsorption–desorption isotherms and pore size distribution curves of Ni/NiS<sub>2</sub>/NS‐graphene.</p></caption></figure><p>To assess the electrochemical properties of Ni/NiS<sub>2</sub>/NS‐graphene and NS‐graphene, these composites were directly characterized by using a half‐cell system versus Na metal. Figure S4 a and b in the Supporting Information shows cyclic voltammetry (CV) results of the initial three scans for Ni/NiS<sub>2</sub>/NS‐graphene and NS‐graphene at a scan rate of 0.1 mV s<sup>−1</sup> over the voltage window of 0.01–3 V (vs. Na/Na<sup>+</sup>). The composites exhibited almost the same electrochemical behavior. The broad reduction plateau between 0.2 and 0.5 V (vs. Na/Na<sup>+</sup>) appeared during the first cathodic scan, which could be attributed to the formation of the solid–electrolyte interface (SEI) layer. In subsequent scans, reduction and oxidation peaks between 0.01 and 0.2 V are observed in both CV curves. The reduction peak is due to the insertion of Na<sup>+</sup> ions into the pores or voids in porous Ni/NiS<sub>2</sub>/NS‐graphene, whereas the oxidation peak corresponds to the desodiation process. In addition, for the current peak at around 0.18 V for both composites during the first three scans, the current intensity increases and the corresponding potential slowly shifts to lower voltages from the first scan to the third scan; this indicates gradually improving desodiation kinetics during cathodic cycling.</p><p>As shown in Figure <link href="#chem201902575-fig-0006"/>, Ni/NiS<sub>2</sub>/NS‐graphene exhibited a higher specific capacity than that of NS‐graphene. Moreover, under a constant current density of 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, Ni/NiS<sub>2</sub>/NS‐graphene and NS‐graphene maintained specific capacities of 156.6 and 116.2 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively (Figure <link href="#chem201902575-fig-0006"/> b), after 500 cycles, which was consistent with the galvanostatic charge–discharge data for both samples (Figure S6 in the Supporting Information) and with the rate capability comparison. Impressively, even at a high current density of 500 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, Ni/NiS<sub>2</sub>/NS‐graphene delivered a higher discharge capacity of 134.4 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 800 cycles, outpacing that of NS‐graphene (108.3 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, Figure <link href="#chem201902575-fig-0006"/> c); this demonstrates excellent electrical stability and reversibility. The observed superb performance is attributed to the improvement to the electrochemical performance of the whole composite by the Ni/NiS<sub>2</sub> particles. The rate and cycling performances of Ni/NiS<sub>2</sub>/NS‐graphene were assessed under continuously varying current densities. Figure <link href="#chem201902575-fig-0006"/> d shows the rate capability of Ni/NiS<sub>2</sub>/NS‐graphene at different currents, along with the rate capability of pristine N/S‐graphene for comparison. Clearly, Ni/NiS<sub>2</sub>/NS‐graphene is capable of holding stable discharge capacities of 256, 221, 186, 152, and 119 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at current densities of 20, 50, 100, 200, and 500 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively; these values are much higher than that of pristine NS‐graphene. The discharge capacity of Ni/NiS<sub>2</sub>/NS‐graphene could be basically restored for repeated cycles once switching the current density returned to 20 mA g<sup>−1</sup> after deep cycling at various current densities. The superior rate performance of Ni/NiS<sub>2</sub>/NS‐graphene can be attributed to low impedance, as demonstrated by electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) experiments. The uniform macroporous structure and the rational combination of metallic Ni and NS‐graphene indicate that the presence of Ni can enhance the conductivity of the graphene‐based hybrid. Both Ni/NiS<sub>2</sub>/NS‐graphene and pristine NS‐graphene exhibit excellent coulombic efficiency.</p><figure xml:id="chem201902575-fig-0006"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:09476539:media:chem201902575:chem201902575-fig-0006"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/chem201902575-fig-0006-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a) Cyclic properties and coulombic efficiency at 50 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. b) Cyclic properties and coulombic efficiency at 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. c) Cyclic properties and coulombic efficiency at 500 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. d) Rate charge capacities and coulombic efficiency at various current densities from 20 to 500 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, then back to 20 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. Figure S7 in the Supporting Information shows a representative Nyquist plot for the Ni/NiS<sub>2</sub>/NS‐graphene anode in SIBs after cycling tests, along with that of pristine NS‐graphene anode for comparison. Both plots show a semicircle in the high‐frequency range, corresponding to charge‐transfer resistance at the electrode/electrolyte interface, and a straight line in the low‐frequency region, corresponding to sodium‐ion diffusion inside the electrode. Clearly, the Ni/NiS<sub>2</sub>/NS‐graphene electrode semicircle is smaller than that of the pristine NS‐graphene electrode, which implies that the Ni/NiS<sub>2</sub>/NS‐graphene electrode has a lower interfacial charge‐transfer resistance. The inset in Figure S7 in the Supporting Information shows the equivalent circuit mode, which involves the electrolyte resistance, <i>R</i><sub>e</sub>; the charge‐transfer resistance, <i>R</i><sub>ct</sub>; and the Warburg impedance, <i>Z</i><sub>W</sub>, that was used to simulate the EIS spectra. The corresponding fitted data are given in Table S3 in the Supporting Information. The fitted <i>R</i><sub>ct</sub> value for the Ni/NiS<sub>2</sub>/NS‐graphene electrode is 274.5 Ω, which is much smaller than that of the pristine NS‐graphene electrode (420.0 Ω). The improved electrochemical performance of Ni/NiS<sub>2</sub>/NS‐graphene indicates that the electrochemical characteristics and charge transport of NS‐graphene anode materials can be improved by the incorporation of metallic Ni and NiS<sub>2</sub> species.</p></section><section xml:id="chem201902575-sec-0003" numbered="no"><title type="main">Conclusion</title><p>We reported built‐in heteroatom self‐doping of albumen‐derived graphene for sodium storage through a facile molten‐salt route. As shown in Table <link href="#chem201902575-tbl-0001"/>, compared with NS‐graphene, Ni/NiS<sub>2</sub>/NS‐graphene delivered a superior rate performance and retained a higher specific capacity after long‐term electrochemical cycling (156.6, 116.2 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, 500 cycles, respectively). The sulfur‐rich proteins in albumen can also guide the doping and nucleation of nickel sulfide nanoparticles. Egg white was extracted from fresh eggs. Powder XRD measurements were conducted on a Rigaku AXS D8 diffractometer with Cu<sub>Kα</sub> radiation. TG analysis was performed on a TG instrument (TGA/DSC3+) from 50 to 800 °C at a heating rate of 5 °C min<sup>−1</sup> under a continuous flow of air. The morphology of the samples was examined by using a field‐emission scanning electron microscope (Utral 55) equipped with an EDX detector, and the microstructures were investigated by using a transmission electron microscope (TEM, Jeol Jem2100f). XPS measurements were performed on an X‐ray microprobe (ESCALAB 250Xi) with Al<sub>Kα</sub> radiation.</p></section><section xml:id="chem201902575-sec-0006" numbered="no"><title type="main">Synthesis of Ni/NiS<sub>2</sub>/NS‐graphene and NS‐graphene composites</title><p>To obtain uniformly distributed NiS<sub>2</sub>/protein fluid precursor, Ni(NO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub><b>⋅</b>6 H<sub>2</sub>O (1 g) was dispersed into albumen (35 mL) separated from fresh eggs. After stirring for 30 min at room temperature, NaCl (18 g) and KCl (22 g) were added and stirred intensely for 30 min to form a uniformly dispersed suspension. Then the mixture was dried at 150 °C. Afterwards, the obtained precursor was ground through ball‐milling and subsequently heat‐treated at 700 °C under high‐purity Ar for 2 h at a ramping rate of 10 °C min<sup>−1</sup>.<link href="#chem201902575-bib-0017"/> The obtained black powders were washed several times with deionized water and ethanol to completely remove NaCl, KCl, and other impurities, and then dried at 100 °C under vacuum overnight to remove moisture and yield the Ni/NiS<sub>2</sub>/NS‐graphene composites. For comparison, the N‐ and S‐doped graphene composite was also synthesized by using the same approach without the addition of Ni(NO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub><b>⋅</b>6 H<sub>2</sub>O.</p></section><section xml:id="chem201902575-sec-0007" numbered="no"><title type="main">Electrochemical measurements</title><p>The electrochemical properties of the as‐prepared anode materials were evaluated by using coin cells (CR2032), which were assembled in an Ar‐filled glove box with metallic sodium foil as the counter/reference electrode, glass microfiber filters (Whatman, GF/A) as the separator, and 1 <sc>m</sc> NaClO<sub>4</sub> dissolved in ethylene carbonate and propylene carbonate (v/v 1:1) as the electrolyte. The resulting active materials, Ni/NiS<sub>2</sub>/NS‐graphene and NS‐graphene, were mixed with Super‐P carbon and polyvinylidene fluoride (PVDF) in a 70:20:10 ratio in <i>N</i>‐methyl‐2‐pyrrolidone (NMP) and cast onto Cu foil. Afterward, the casted electrodes were vacuum‐dried at 100 °C overnight. A Land battery tester (LANHE CT2001A) was used for the voltage range of 0.01–3 V (vs. Na/Na+). EIS was carried out on a Solartron 1260 instrument with an alternating current (AC) voltage of 5 mV over the frequency range from 0.01 Hz to 100 kHz. CV curves, at a scan rate of 0.1 mV s<sup>−1</sup> over the range of 0.01 to 3 V (vs. Na/Na<sup>+</sup>), were recorded on a CHI 660E electrochemical workstation.</p></section></section><section xml:id="chem201902575-sec-0008" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>This work was supported by the National Key R&amp;D Program of China (grant no. 2018YFB0104300) and the National Natural Science Foundation of China (51802284).</p></section><section xml:id="chem201902575-sec-0009" type="conflictOfInterest" numbered="no"><title type="main">Conflict of interest</title><p>The authors declare no conflict of interest.</p></section><bibliography style="numbered"><bib xml:id="chem201902575-bib-0001"><citation type="other" xml:id="chem201902575-cit-0001"> </citation></bib><bib xml:id="chem201902575-bib-0001a"><label>1a</label><citation type="journal" xml:id="chem201902575-cit-0002"><author><givenNames>P.</givenNames> <familyName>Huilin</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>H.</givenNames> <familyName>Yong-Sheng</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>C.</givenNames> <familyName>Liquan</familyName></author>, <journalTitle>Energy Environ.
