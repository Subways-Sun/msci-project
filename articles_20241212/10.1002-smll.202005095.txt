type="main">Abstract</title><p>Engineering intriguing electrode with exceptional kinetics behaviors is imperative for boosting sodium storage systems. Herein, the uniform nanoframes are threaded by the interwoven carbon nanotube (CNT) conductive network to form an ingenious beads‐on‐a‐string structured NiFePBA nanoframe/CNT (NFPB‐NF/CNT) cathode and the corresponding derivative NiFeSe nanoframe/CNT (NFS‐NF/CNT) anode. NFPB‐NF/CNT exhibited remarkable cycling life along with outstanding rate capability and low voltage decay per cycle. The fast Na<sup>+</sup> conduction and sufficient sodiation reaction is proved using Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion models. Meanwhile, an exceptional sodium storage capacity and prolonged cycling life is achieved using binary NFS‐NF/CNT anode at a high rate. In situ and ex situ investigations are used to reveal the sodium storage mechanisms and structural evolution process. Furthermore, a sodium‐ion full battery based on the above electrodes shows stable performance accompanied by high energy conversion efficiency. The material design strategy provides a new solution for exploiting smart multicomponent composites toward advanced energy storage devices.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="smll202005095-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>Uniform nanoframes are threaded by the interwoven CNT conductive network to form an ingenious beaded‐string structured NiFePBA nanoframe/CNT cathode and the corresponding derivative NiFeSe nanoframe/CNT anode. xml:id="smll202005095-sec-0010"><label role="interim">1</label><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>As the most promising alternative to commercial lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs), sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) are now being extensively explored for large‐scale energy storage systems.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202005095-bib-0001 #smll202005095-bib-0002 #smll202005095-bib-0003"><sup>1–3</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, the larger ion radius of Na<sup>+</sup> triggers slow kinetics, followed by deteriorating electrochemical performance.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202005095-bib-0004"><sup>4</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Cathodes with the available channels and rigid structures, such as Prussian blue (PB) and its analogs (PBAs), possess faster charge transfer kinetics and longer cycle life.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202005095-bib-0005"><sup>5</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The Na ions could be accommodated and inserted into interstitial voids of the host lattices formed by transition metals and cyano ligands.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202005095-bib-0006"><sup>6</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The (CN)<sup>−</sup> anions in PB or PBA increase the site face framework, whereas, their small charges and triple bonds also weaken the interaction between Na<sup>+</sup> and <i>p</i><sub>π</sub> electrons on diffusing across the site face.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202005095-bib-0007"><sup>7</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Nevertheless, the low‐spin redox pair of Fe<sup>2+</sup>/Fe<sup>3+</sup> and instability of the structure have caused incomplete sodiation/desodiation process and low capacity performance at high current density.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202005095-bib-0008"><sup>8</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Therefore, exploiting diverse and complex PBA nanostructure is a crucial requirement to minimize the diffusion barrier, expedite the reaction kinetics, and thus improve the sodium storage properties.</p><p>However, the realization of high‐performance SIBs is still hindered seriously by anode materials with inferior sodium storage capability.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202005095-bib-0009 #smll202005095-bib-0010 #smll202005095-bib-0011"><sup>9–11</sup></link><sup>]</sup> PBAs as a controllable precursor have been extensively investigated to provide various functional derivatives such as metal oxide,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202005095-bib-0012"><sup>12</sup></link><sup>]</sup> metal sulfides,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202005095-bib-0013"><sup>13</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and metal phosphides.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202005095-bib-0014"><sup>14</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Our previous work has reported that Fe<sub>1‐</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>S/MoS<sub>2</sub> composite derived from PB greatly reduced the Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion barrier and facilitated the charge transfer kinetics.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202005095-bib-0015"><sup>15</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Remarkably, recent studies have demonstrated that metal selenides show lower energy consumption for conversion reaction distinguishing from metal oxides/sulfides, due to their high electronic kinetics and weak electronegativity.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202005095-bib-0016 #smll202005095-bib-0017"><sup>16,17</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, the cycling stability and rate capability are still restricted by the sluggish sodium storage kinetics and severe volume swelling. The effective oxygen evolution reactions (OER) from NiFe‐based materials have shown the possibility to promote pseudocapacitive behaviors and enhance rate capability in batteries simultaneously.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202005095-bib-0018"><sup>18</sup></link><sup>]</sup> It is therefore highly promising to use engineered PBA‐derived nickel‐iron selenide nanoarchitecture with smart configuration to effectively improve the sodium storage capacities.</p><p>Similar to anode materials of SIBs, the cathode materials also sufferers from sluggish kinetics and unyielding volume changes in the conventional SIB designs. Thus, it is essentially important to simultaneously improve the sodium storage capability of both anodes and cathodes by rationally constructing a complex nanoarchitecture based on PBA materials. However, most of the previous SIB studies mainly focus on exploring one electrode material with Na metal in half‐cell, which limited the evaluation of the full cell and understanding of the reaction mechanism. Herein, we propose the simultaneous realization of a beads‐on‐a‐string structure with carbon nanotubes (CNT) connecting NiFePBA nanoframes (i.e., NFPB‐NF/CNT, cathode) and the corresponding derivative NiFeSe nanoframe (i.e., NFS‐NF/CNT, anode) for a high‐performance sodium‐ion full battery. Benefiting from the 3D open architecture and the embedded CNT conductive network, both electrodes exhibit excellent cycling stability and rate capability. The Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion models further confirm its fast Na<sup>+</sup> conduction and deep sodiation reaction. Furthermore, the complicated reaction mechanism and structural evolution are deeply revealed by in situ and ex situ investigations. Our design strategy not only dramatically improves the sodium storage performance of electrode materials, but also provides a new strategy for engineering diverse composites toward advanced energy storage devices.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202005095-sec-0020"><label role="interim">2</label><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p><b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll202005095-fig-0001"><b>1</b>a</link> shows the synthesis processes of the beads‐on‐a‐string structure composed of nanoframes/CNT. First, the NiFePBA nanocubes were nucleated on CNT forming a continuous conductive network. X‐ray diffractiion (XRD) result exhibits a typical PBA‐type phase with the cubic crystal structure (Figure S1a, Supporting Information). Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) images indicate that NiFePBA nanocubes with an average size of around 300 nm were monodispersed and threaded in the CNT fibers (Figure S1b–d, Supporting Information). Energy‐dispersive X‐ray spectroscopy (EDX) elemental mapping images confirm the presence of Ni, Fe, C, N, and O elements in the composite (Figure S1e, Supporting Information). Next, after treating with an appropriate amount of ammonia solution, the solid nanocubes were converted to hollow nanoframes, then forming the beads‐on‐a‐string structure of NiFePBA nanoframes/CNT.</p><figure xml:id="smll202005095-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll202005095:smll202005095-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll202005095-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) Schematic illustration of the synthesis process for nanoframes/CNT composite. NFPB‐NF/CNT: b) XRD pattern, c,d) SEM, e) TEM, f) element mappings. NFS‐NF/CNT: g) XRD pattern, h,i) SEM, j) TEM, k) element mappings.</caption></figure><p>The XRD analysis confirms that its crystalline phase is nearly identical with that of the precursor nanocubes/CNT (Figure <link href="#smll202005095-fig-0001">1b</link>). From SEM images (Figure <link href="#smll202005095-fig-0001">1c,d</link>), the nanoframes well inherit the size of the nanocube templates. And the cavities can be clearly observed at the corners of the nanoparticles, indicating a 3D open nanoarchitecture. Besides, the intertwined CNT passes through the frame‐like particles and string them together, forming a beads‐on‐a‐string composite in which good electrical conductivity between the active nanoframes. Transmission electron microscope (TEM) images further confirm the intimate contact between nanoframes and CNT, which is the consequence of nucleation and growth of the NiFePBA particles anchored on heterogeneous spots in the CNT (Figure <link href="#smll202005095-fig-0001">1e</link>). The corresponding EDX results manifest the composition of the composite (Figure <link href="#smll202005095-fig-0001">1f</link>).</p><p>Finally, NiFeSe nanoframes/CNT was obtained via a selenization process. The XRD pattern can be well indexed to the crystal structures of hexagonal NiSe (JCPDS NO. 65–9451) (Figure <link href="#smll202005095-fig-0001">1g</link>). Meanwhile, except for a broad peak corresponding to the CNT, other diffraction peaks slightly move toward to a larger angle, indicating that the existence of Fe induces the slight shrinkage of the unit cell volume. SEM images indicate that the nickel‐iron selenide well retains the original nanostructures, but with rougher surfaces (Figure <link href="#smll202005095-fig-0001">1h,i</link>). TEM observation further confirms the structural feature of the derived NiFeSe nanoframes/CNT (Figure <link href="#smll202005095-fig-0001">1j</link>). Due to the strong interaction between nanoframes and CNT, the beads‐on‐a‐string structure is still maintained even after selenization. High‐resolution TEM (HRTEM) image shows clear lattice fringes with a d‐spacing of 0.28 nm, corresponding to the (101) plane of NiSe (inset in Figure <link href="#smll202005095-fig-0001">1j</link>). EDX analysis shows a homogeneous distribution of the Ni, Fe, Se, and C elements in the composite, suggesting an approximate Ni/Fe/Se element ratio of about 0.63:0.37:1.0 with a possible chemical formula of Ni<sub>0.63</sub>Fe<sub>0.37</sub>Se (Figure <link href="#smll202005095-fig-0001">1k</link> and Figure S2, Supporting Information). X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) survey spectrum further reveals the presence of Ni, Fe, Se, and C elements in the composite (Figure S3, Supporting Information). For the Ni 2p spectrum, the peaks located at 856.6 and 874.1 eV along with two corresponding satellites, are related to Ni 2p<sub>3/2</sub> and Ni 2p<sub>1/2</sub>, respectively.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202005095-bib-0019 #smll202005095-bib-0020"><sup>19,20</sup></link><sup>]</sup> For the Fe 2p spectrum, two peaks centered at 710.3 and 723.3 eV are correlated to Fe 2p<sub>3/2</sub> and Fe 2p<sub>1/2</sub>, respectively, along with two satellite peaks.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202005095-bib-0021 #smll202005095-bib-0022 #smll202005095-bib-0023"><sup>21–23</sup></link><sup>]</sup> For Se 3d XPS spectrum, two peaks observed at 59.3 and 58.4 eV corresponds to Se 3d<sub>5/2</sub> and Se 3d<sub>3/2</sub>, respectively.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202005095-bib-0024 #smll202005095-bib-0025"><sup>24,25</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In addition, nitrogen adsorption‐desorption isotherms reveal that these two composites possess the high Brunauer‐Emmett‐Teller (BET) surface area of 71.3 and 93.6 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively (Figure S4, Supporting Information). Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) in Figure S5, Supporting Information, determines that the contents of carbon in the NFPB‐NF/CNT and NFS‐NF/CNT composites are around 7.9 and 5.6 wt%, respectively.</p><p>For comparison, bare NiFePBA nanocubes were also synthesized by a similar route without CNT (Figure S6a and b, Supporting Information). Subsequently, the NiFePBA nanoframes were obtained after etching with ammonia solution. The SEM results confirm the formation of nanoframe structure with hollow interior space (as shown in Figure S6c and d, Supporting Information). Finally, the bare NiFePBA nanoframes (NFPB‐NF) and NiFePBA nanocubes/CNT (NFPB‐NC/CNT) were converted to bare NiFeSe nanoframes (NFS‐NF) and NiFeSe nanocubes/CNT (NFS‐NC/CNT) composites through the selenization process. From SEM images, the derived selenides will retain original nanostructure, but with rougher surfaces (Figure S7, Supporting Information).</p><section xml:id="smll202005095-sec-0030"><label role="interim">2.1</label><title type="main">NiFePBA Nanoframes/CNT Cathode Materials</title><p>As expected, the attractive physicochemical merits would arouse the evolution of electrochemical properties as shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll202005095-fig-0002"><b>2</b></link>. At 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>, their initial discharge capacities are 81.5 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> of bare NFPB NF, 83.7 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> of NFPB‐NC/CNT, and 84.2 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> of NFPB‐NF/CNT, respectively (Figure <link href="#smll202005095-fig-0002">2a</link>). The higher specific capacity is mainly attributed to more exposed active sites on the nanoframes/CNT composite for the sodium‐storage reaction.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202005095-bib-0026"><sup>26</sup></link><sup>]</sup> After 100 cycles, the NFPB‐NF/CNT electrode delivers a stable sodium storage capacity of 78.3 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, showing much higher capacity retention (93.0%) than that of NFPB‐NC/CNT (90.4%) and bare NFPB‐NF (82.2%). In addition, NFPB‐NF/CNT shows the lowest voltage gap in Figure <link href="#smll202005095-fig-0002">2a</link> and stable voltage plateau at around 3.3 V in Figure <link href="#smll202005095-fig-0002">2c</link> (Figure S8, Supporting Information). After repeat cycling, the average voltage decay per cycle is about only 0.35 mV, much smaller than the 1.20 mV and 1.90 mV of the NFPB‐NC/CNT and bare NFPB‐NF cathode materials, respectively, suggesting the stable electrochemical kinetics (Figure <link href="#smll202005095-fig-0002">2d</link>).</p><figure xml:id="smll202005095-fig-0002"><label>2</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll202005095:smll202005095-fig-0002" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll202005095-fig-0002-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) First discharge/charge profiles of three samples. b) Cycling performance at 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. c) Galvanostatic charge‐discharge profiles of NFPB‐NF/CNT composite electrode at 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. d) Mid‐working voltage per cycle at 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. e) Rate capability at various current densities. f) Galvanostatic charge‐discharge profiles at various current densities. g) Long‐term cycling performance at 1.0 A g<sup>−1</sup>.</caption></figure><p>The superiority of the structure is also highlighted by its exceptional rate performance. Notably, NFPB‐NF/CNT exhibits the best rate capability among these three electrodes: 85.2, 76.9, 73.5, 69.7, 64.7, and 59.0 mAhg<sup>−1</sup> at the current densities of 0.1, 0.2, 0.5, 1.0, 2.0, and 5.0 A g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively (Figure <link href="#smll202005095-fig-0002">2e</link>). When the current density returns to 0.1 Ag<sup>−1</sup>, the capacity can be remarkably retained at approximately 94.0%. The corresponding galvanostatic charge/discharge curves at different current densities demonstrate that the nanoframes/CNT composite not only promotes the sodium storage capability, but also contributes to less electrochemical polarization (Figure <link href="#smll202005095-fig-0002">2f</link>). Furthermore, even after considerably prolonged cycling at 1.0 A g<sup>−1</sup>, NFPB‐NF/CNT retains a reversible capacity of 65.2 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, indicating its outstanding long‐term cycling stability and favorable sodium ion reaction kinetics (Figure <link href="#smll202005095-fig-0002">2g</link>), which is confirmed by the ex situ SEM image (Figure S9, Supporting Information).</p><p>Driven by the superior electrochemical performance of nanoframes/CNT, its detailed kinetics were further investigated. From <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll202005095-fig-0003"><b>3</b>a</link>, NFPB‐NF/CNT exhibits the sharpest redox peaks and lowest voltage gap, well matching with the sodiation/desodiation profiles. The width of redox peaks clearly reveals that the energy distributions are increased in order from bare NFPB‐NF, NFPB‐NC/CNT to NFPB‐NF/CNT, implying the favorable sodium‐storage reaction induced the overwhelming superiority of composite structure. Scan‐rate‐dependent cyclic voltammetry (CV) measurements were carried out to offer an insight into the charge storage mechanism of NFPB NF/CNT. As shown in Figure <link href="#smll202005095-fig-0003">3b</link>, the similar shapes of CV curves are well remained at various scan rates, indicating its reversible redox reaction. The introduction of the embedded CNT network and the construct of the nanoframes with an open structure and hollow interior facilitates the electron transport for the electrochemical reaction between NiFePB and Na<sup>+</sup> so that a significantly enhanced sodiation rate can be achieved (Figure <link href="#smll202005095-fig-0003">3h</link>). Fascinating merits including abundant active sites, rapid molecular accessibility, efficient electronic transport, and fast ion diffusion are miraculously merged in nanoframes/CNT composite structures. As SIB cathode, NFPB‐NF/CNT show excellent cycling stability, rate capability, and low voltage decay per cycle (0.35 mV at 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup> after 100 cycles). The dominant sodium storage mechanisms and structural evolution process have been expounded thoroughly by in situ and ex situ technologies.
