It is found that the cubic bimodal interconnected mesoporous framework of CMK‐9 plays a crucial role in achieving the excellent electrochemical performances by assisting the rapid mass and charge transfer. Among the prepared nanocomposites, CoO(10)@CMK‐9 delivers a discharge capacity of 830 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 200 cycles at a current density of 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup> in lithium‐ion batteries. At a higher current density of 1000 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, the anode presents an outstanding discharge capacity of 636 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 200 cycles. In sodium‐ion batteries, the anode provides a discharge capacity of 296 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 250 cycles at a current density of 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. The remarkable performances of CoO(10)@CMK‐9 demonstrate the promising potentials of the nanocomposite as the anode for rechargeable batteries.</p></abstract><abstract type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p><b>Confined in carbon</b>: CoO(10)@CMK‐9 delivers excellent discharge capacities of 830 and 636 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at current densities of 100 and 1000 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively, after 200 cycles in a lithium‐ion battery. In a sodium‐ion battery, it delivers a discharge capacity of 296 mA h g<sup>‐1</sup> after 250 cycles at a current density of 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. The unique 3D bimodal ordered mesoporous structure of CMK‐9, pseudocapacitance, and diffusion processes have roles in the lithium/sodium‐ion storage.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18645631:media:cssc202000009:cssc202000009-toc-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/cssc202000009-toc-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="cssc202000009-sec-0001" numbered="no"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Rechargeable batteries are becoming increasingly prevalent in the present society with the transformation of the automobile sector from fossil fuel‐based systems to electrical systems as well as with the availability of various powerful electronic gadgets.<link href="#cssc202000009-bib-0001 #cssc202000009-bib-0002 #cssc202000009-bib-0003"/> As the world is turning toward greener and clean energy, many auto companies are racing for lithium‐based energy supplies. Therefore, there is a paradigm shift in battery research to obtain high power and high energy density battery systems together with a faster charging time. Lithium and sodium‐ion batteries (LIBs and SIBs) are two promising storage systems that can assist in fulfilling the present‐day requirements. To meet the increasing demand, high‐performance anode materials are being intensely studied in comparison to commercial graphite.<link href="#cssc202000009-bib-0004 #cssc202000009-bib-0005"/> Transition metal oxides are interesting materials to be used as anodes in LIBs and SIBs as they can deliver excellent electrochemical performances. Among them, CoO has received increased attention as one of the materials of choice for LIBs and SIBs owing to ease of preparation, excellent chemical properties, good thermal stability, and higher theoretical capacity (716 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>).<link href="#cssc202000009-bib-0006 #cssc202000009-bib-0007"/> In addition, the cobalt ion possesses a higher oxidation potential in comparison to other transition metal ions, resulting in favorable conditions for the fabrication of electrode materials with high energy densities. However, poor capacity retention and cycling stability because of its low electrical conductivity and large specific volume change during the charge/discharge process hinder their progress as a reliable anode material for LIBs and SIBs. Although improvements in electrochemical performances have been made by designing various morphological nanostructures of CoO such as nanosheets,<link href="#cssc202000009-bib-0008"/> nanowires,<link href="#cssc202000009-bib-0009"/> hollow and porous structures,<link href="#cssc202000009-bib-0010 #cssc202000009-bib-0011"/> it is still a challenge to find a suitable electrode that can overcome the unfavorable volume expansion during Li<sup>+</sup> insertion and extraction. Efforts have been made to circumvent these problems by introducing graphite,<link href="#cssc202000009-bib-0012"/> carbon nanotube,<link href="#cssc202000009-bib-0013"/> carbon nanofiber,<link href="#cssc202000009-bib-0014"/> graphene,<link href="#cssc202000009-bib-0015"/> reduced graphene oxide,<link href="#cssc202000009-bib-0016"/> etc., along with CoO nanoparticles to make composite anodes and enhance their electrochemical performances. Recently, ordered mesoporous carbons (OMCs) with hexagonal or cubic mesopores have been employed as ideal supports to develop metal oxide nanoparticles embedded nanocomposite electrodes with improved performances.<link href="#cssc202000009-bib-0017 #cssc202000009-bib-0018 #cssc202000009-bib-0019"/> OMCs have various fascinating properties, such as highly ordered mesoporous structure, high surface area, large pore volume, and their ability to hold the metal oxide nanoparticles within the mesopores, which can accommodate the volume change during the charge/discharge cycles, prevent aggregation of nanoparticles, help the electrolytes infiltrate into the mesopores to improve the mass and charge transport around the embedded metal oxide nanoparticles, and deliver higher capacity and rate capability than traditional graphite‐based anodes. Various framework configurations and specific pore structures also make these OMCs unique from other carbon materials. Normally, ordered mesoporous carbons are synthesized by a soft‐template method from the self‐assembly of amphiphilic block co‐polymers and phenolic resins<link href="#cssc202000009-bib-0020 #cssc202000009-bib-0021"/> or by the nanocasting strategy (i.e., the hard‐template method) in which ordered mesoporous silicas are used as hard templates.<link href="#cssc202000009-bib-0022 #cssc202000009-bib-0023"/> Although the hard‐template method is tedious and time consuming, it can produce a highly crystalline product and has low sensitivity to the reaction conditions. On the other hand, the soft‐template method delivers a poor carbon yield with a relatively low crystalline product owing to the loss during carbonization. Meanwhile, development of the mesostructure by using the soft‐templating method is greatly sensitive to the reaction environment.<link href="#cssc202000009-bib-0024"/> Most interestingly, some pore arrangements are possible through the hard‐template approach only. Hexagonal mesoporous silica SBA‐15 is used as a hard template for the synthesis of mesoporous carbons CMK‐3 and CMK‐5 while cubic mesoporous silica KIT‐6 is used as a hard template for the synthesis of mesoporous carbons CMK‐8 and CMK‐9 through the nanocasting method.<link href="#cssc202000009-bib-0022 #cssc202000009-bib-0025"/> These mesoporous carbons are reverse replicas of their silica counterparts. Depending on the synthesis conditions, different mesostructures of OMCs can be formed. Carbonization of sucrose within the hard template can eventually result in rod‐type OMCs such as CMK‐3 and CMK‐8. On the other hand, pyrolysis of furfuryl alcohol leads to tube‐type OMCs like CMK‐5 and CMK‐9, consisting of connected interpenetrating nanotubes.<link href="#cssc202000009-bib-0025"/> Although CMK‐5 possesses a bimodal hexagonal pore structure, CMK‐9 exhibits a bimodal cubic pore structure. Normally, in CMK‐5 and CMK‐9 type OMCs, the inner pore diameter of the nanotube framework is larger (ca. 5–6 nm) whereas intra pores generated from adjacent nanotubes are smaller (ca. 3–4.5 nm). Among these OMCs, CMK‐3, CMK‐5, and CMK‐8 are used extensively as hosts to synthesize metal oxide nanoparticle embedded nanocomposite electrodes for LIBs and SIBs and show improvement in electrochemical performances.<link href="#cssc202000009-bib-0026 #cssc202000009-bib-0027 #cssc202000009-bib-0028 #cssc202000009-bib-0029 #cssc202000009-bib-0030"/> However, OMCs have not been intensively investigated with CoO to prepare electrode materials. The CoO/CMK‐3 nanocomposite has been synthesized by Zhang et al. and used in LIBs to obtain a discharge capacity of 709 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 20 cycles at a current density of 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup>.<link href="#cssc202000009-bib-0017"/> Sun et al. have shown that CoO/CMK‐3 can also be used as a high performance cathode catalyst for lithium‐oxygen batteries.<link href="#cssc202000009-bib-0031"/> Park et al. have synthesized a Co<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> nanoparticle embedded OMC‐based anode and achieved a reversible capacity of 1025 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 100 cycles at a current density of 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup>.<link href="#cssc202000009-bib-0032"/> They have attributed the improved cycling stability and rate capability to the mesoporous nanostructure of the OMC and homogeneous distribution of the Co<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> nanoparticles within the pores of the OMC, which provided effective pathways for Li<sup>+</sup> ion movement as well as prevented particle aggregation. Despite the advantages of the 3D hollow interpenetrating network structure and high surface area of CMK‐9, its exploitation as a support is still limited to catalytic applications.<link href="#cssc202000009-bib-0033 #cssc202000009-bib-0034"/> To the best of our knowledge, however, a CMK‐9‐based anode has not yet been used in LIBs or SIBs.</p><p>Apart from LIBs, SIBs have also emerged as promising cell systems for applications in large‐scale grid energy storage and load levelling because of the wide availability and lower cost of sodium.<link href="#cssc202000009-bib-0030 #cssc202000009-bib-0035"/> SIBs can provide an alternative chemistry to LIBs and may become competitive in certain areas of applications. CoO‐based anode materials have also been used in SIBs to evaluate their performances. Chang et al. have reported the synthesis of a hierarchical CoO microflower film with a capacity of 277.8 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 100 cycles at a current density of 90.9 mA g<sup>−1</sup>.<link href="#cssc202000009-bib-0036"/> The same group has also reported the synthesis of a CoO microsphere film with a capacity of 172 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 100 cycles at a current density of 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup>.<link href="#cssc202000009-bib-0011"/> However, there is no information on fabrication of the nanocomposite anodes with CoO and ordered mesoporous carbons, especially with ordered mesoporous carbon CMK‐9 with bimodal 3D cubic pore architecture and <i>Ia</i>3<i>d</i> symmetry for SIBs. Therefore, the design and synthesis of a new nanocomposite anode that combines cubic mesoporous carbon CMK‐9 and CoO nanoparticles offers a potential application in lithium and sodium‐ion batteries.</p><p>In this work, CMK‐9 is employed as a support to synthesize the CoO@CMK‐9 nanocomposite anode for the first time by following the nanocasting and wet‐impregnation methods as shown in Scheme S1 in Supporting Information. This procedure results in uniformly dispersed ultra‐small CoO nanoparticles confined within the cubic bimodal mesopores of CMK‐9. The structural, textural, and morphological properties of the CoO@CMK‐9 nanocomposite are examined with XRD, N<sub>2</sub> adsorption/desorption isotherms, TEM, X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS), and Raman spectroscopy. The electrochemical performances of the CoO@CMK‐9 nanocomposite anode for LIBs and SIBs are evaluated for the first time. Excellent long‐term cycling electrochemical behaviors with higher reversible capacity and remarkable rate capability together with good after‐cycle structural durability make the present nanocomposite a worthy candidate for LIBs and SIBs. A mechanism is also put forward to elucidate the higher capacity of the current material than its theoretical capacity. The present study offers a new perspective to design the long‐life, high power metal oxide‐based nanocomposite anodes for LIBs and SIBs through architectural modification of the tubular ordered mesoporous carbon support.</p></section><section xml:id="cssc202000009-sec-0002" numbered="no"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><section xml:id="cssc202000009-sec-0003" numbered="no"><title type="main">Structural studies</title><p>The structure and crystallinity of the CoO(<i>x</i>)@CMK‐9 nanocomposite materials were examined by XRD measurements. As observed from the small‐angle XRD patterns shown in Figure <link href="#cssc202000009-fig-0001"/> A, the mesoporous silica KIT‐6 exhibits three well‐resolved peaks, which can be assigned to the (2 1 1), (2 2 0), and (4 2 0) diffractions of ordered 3D cubic structure (Figure <link href="#cssc202000009-fig-0001"/> A a). As CMK‐9 is the reverse replica of KIT‐6, the ordered array of carbon nanotubes in CMK‐9 gives rise to similar XRD peaks as observed in KIT‐6 but with lower intensities, demonstrating a 3D cubic structure of CMK‐9 with <i>Ia</i>3<i>d</i> space group (Figure <link href="#cssc202000009-fig-0001"/> A b). However, a slight shifting of the peaks toward higher angles was observed for the CMK‐9 sample owing to the structural shrinkage resulting from the carbonization process.<link href="#cssc202000009-bib-0037"/> The patterns revealed <i>d</i><sub>(2 1 1)</sub>‐spacing values of 9.35 and 8.14 nm for KIT‐6 and CMK‐9, respectively, implying that the contraction of the framework was around 9 %. It was observed that the intensity of the most intense peak was reduced with the increase in the concentration of the CoO precursor, suggesting a gradual decrease in the structural ordering of mesoporous CMK‐9 (Figure <link href="#cssc202000009-fig-0001"/> A b and c). After incorporation of 10 wt % CoO nanoparticles into the mesoporous CMK‐9, one resolved peak with a lower intensity was observed for the (2 1 1) diffraction, although higher order reflections were not prominent (Figure <link href="#cssc202000009-fig-0001"/> A c). This signified the deterioration of the mesoporous structure of CMK‐9 after partial occupation of the mesopores by the CoO nanoparticles. When the CoO loading was increased to 20 wt %, the mesoporous structure of CMK‐9 was further deteriorated with the observation of a small (2 1 1) diffraction peak (Figure <link href="#cssc202000009-fig-0001"/> A d). The ordered mesoporous structure was almost collapsed when the loading amount of CoO nanoparticles increased to 30 wt % (Figure <link href="#cssc202000009-fig-0001"/> A e). This indicated that a higher loading amount of CoO into CMK‐9 generated some strain in the volume, which led to deterioration of the ordered structure of the mesopores.<link href="#cssc202000009-bib-0017"/></p><figure xml:id="cssc202000009-fig-0001"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18645631:media:cssc202000009:cssc202000009-fig-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/cssc202000009-fig-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>(A) Small‐angle XRD patterns of (a) KIT‐6, (b) CMK‐9, (c) CoO(10)@CMK‐9, (d) CoO(20)@CMK‐9, and (e) CoO(30)@CMK‐9 nanocomposites. (B) Wide‐angle XRD patterns of (a) CoO (JCPDS No. 43‐1004), (b) pristine CoO nanoparticles, (c) CMK‐9, (d) CoO(10)@CMK‐9, (e) CoO(20)@CMK‐9, and (f) CoO(30)@CMK‐9 nanocomposites.</p></caption></figure><p>In the wide‐angle XRD pattern of the pristine CoO nanoparticles (Figure <link href="#cssc202000009-fig-0001"/> B b), five characteristic peaks corresponding to (1 1 1), (2 0 0), (2 2 0), (3 1 1), and (2 2 2) diffractions were observed, which matched well with the JCPDS data (No. 43‐1004) of CoO (Figure <link href="#cssc202000009-fig-0001"/> B a). The average particle size of the CoO nanoparticles was calculated by the Scherrer equation and found to be around 40 nm. Two broad peaks were observed in the wide‐angle XRD pattern of CMK‐9 for (0 0 2) and (1 0 0) diffractions of turbostratic carbon, suggesting that ordered mesoporous carbon had a low degree of graphitization (Figure <link href="#cssc202000009-fig-0001"/> B c).<link href="#cssc202000009-bib-0038"/> After loading the CoO nanoparticles into the mesopores of CMK‐9 (Figure <link href="#cssc202000009-fig-0001"/> B d–f), that is, the CoO(<i>x</i>)@CMK‐9 nanocomposites, especially for <i>x=</i>20 and 30, displayed all the five characteristics peaks at 2<i>θ</i>=36.5°, 42.4°, 61.5°, 73.7°, and 77.5° corresponding to (1 1 1), (2 0 0), (2 2 0), (3 1 1), and (2 2 2) diffractions of cubic CoO.<link href="#cssc202000009-bib-0015"/> The peaks owing to CoO were not clearly visible in the CoO(10)@CMK‐9 sample owing to a lower amount of CoO nanoparticles, which possibly were encapsulated mostly inside the mesopores and also hindered by a slightly higher noise level. The observation of the peak related to (0 0 2) diffraction together with the peaks owing to CoO suggested effective development of the CoO(<i>x</i>)@CMK‐9 nanocomposites. Although the intensity of the diffraction peaks increased gradually with the increase in the concentration of the CoO precursor, the key distinctive diffraction peaks were still wide in nature, suggesting that the size of the CoO nanoparticles remained small up to the highest concentration studied. This suggested that the mesoporous structure as well as the high level of branched 3D nanotube channels of CMK‐9 dominated the growth of CoO particles so that ultra‐small CoO nanoparticles were formed and mostly confined within the connecting interpenetrating nanotubes of the CMK‐9 matrix.</p></section><section xml:id="cssc202000009-sec-0004" numbered="no"><title type="main">Textural and thermogravimetric analysis</title><p>The N<sub>2</sub> adsorption/desorption isotherms with pore size distributions of the pristine CMK‐9 and CoO(<i>x</i>)@CMK‐9 nanocomposites with different CoO loadings are depicted in Figure <link href="#cssc202000009-fig-0002"/>. According to the IUPAC classifications, all the isotherms exhibited hysteresis loops belonging to type IV isotherms, clearly indicating the mesoporous characteristics of the materials. The surface and structural properties of CMK‐9 and CoO(<i>x</i>)@CMK‐9 nanocomposites are summarized in Table <link href="#cssc202000009-tbl-0001"/>. The pristine CMK‐9 exhibited a high Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) surface area of 1689 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup>, a pore volume of 1.55 cm<sup>3</sup> g<sup>−1</sup> and bimodal pore sizes of 4.0 and 4.9 nm. As CMK‐9 is a hollow‐type ordered mesoporous carbon, it generates two mesopore sizes, that is, one is the inner tube diameter of the carbon framework (4.9 nm) and the other is the inter‐tube diameter (4.0 nm).<link href="#cssc202000009-bib-0025"/> After encapsulation of CoO nanoparticles within the mesopores of CMK‐9, the surface area and pore volume of the CoO(<i>x</i>)@CMK‐9 nanocomposites were considerably decreased to 1380–577 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup> and 1.12–0.41 cm<sup>3</sup> g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively, for the CoO loadings of 10 to 30 wt %. It was also found that the pore size distribution was broadened for the nanocomposite samples in comparison to the pristine CMK‐9, although the average pore diameter was lowered slightly. The partial filling of mesopores by the CoO nanoparticles and partial blocking of the channel cross‐section because of wall coverage by the CoO nanoparticles in some cases reduced the surface area, pore volumes, and pore diameters of the nanocomposite samples. The appearance of the hysteresis loop even after the high loading of CoO nanoparticles suggests that a good number of mesopores are still open in the CoO(<i>x</i>)@CMK‐9 nanocomposite samples despite the decrease in the mesopore width. The unoccupied mesopore volume should be beneficial for the infiltration of electrolytes, and thus may improve the contact between the electrolyte and the active electrode material, accelerate the diffusion of charges, and offer sufficient spaces for the volume change of the CoO nanoparticles during the lithiation/delithiation cycles.</p><figure xml:id="cssc202000009-fig-0002"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18645631:media:cssc202000009:cssc202000009-fig-0002"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/cssc202000009-fig-0002-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Nitrogen adsorption/desorption isotherms of (a) CMK‐9, (b) CoO(10)@CMK‐9, (c) CoO(20)@CMK‐9, and (d) CoO(30)@CMK‐9 nanocomposites. CMK‐9 was treated with nitric acid to make it hydrophilic; this treatment introduces oxygen‐containing surface groups (e.g., carboxylic acid, hydroxyl, and carbonyl) on the surface of CMK‐9. At a lower concentration of the CoO precursor, the strong electrostatic interaction between the Co<sup>2+</sup> ions and negatively charged surface groups of CMK‐9 helps to adsorb most of the Co<sup>2+</sup> ions within the mesopores of CMK‐9, and then CoO grows afterwards during the heat treatment. Hence, almost all the CoO nanoparticles are encapsulated within the mesopores of CMK‐9. However, with the increase in the precursor concentration, the concentration of the Co<sup>2+</sup> ions also increases. At higher concentrations, the interaction between Co<sup>2+</sup> and negatively charged surface groups of CMK‐9 is not as strong as in the case of lower precursor concentration, and thus cannot effectively control the particle growth. Consequently, some larger sized CoO nanoparticles might grow on the surface along with the nanoparticles inside the mesopores. Therefore, the average nanoparticle size became larger with the increase in the precursor concentration. The high‐resolution TEM (HRTEM) image of the CoO(10)@CMK‐9 nanocomposite in Figure <link href="#cssc202000009-fig-0004"/> a shows <i>d</i>‐spacing values of 0.25 and 0.21 nm corresponding to the (1 1 1) and (2 0 0) plane of crystalline CoO.<link href="#cssc202000009-bib-0040"/> This confirms the effective incorporation of CoO nanoparticles in CMK‐9. The decrease in the surface energy owing to the interaction of CoO with Li<sub>2</sub>O during the first cycle of lithiation resulted in the higher potential of lithiation in the successive cycles.<link href="#cssc202000009-bib-0015 #cssc202000009-bib-0055"/> Moreover, the peak intensity reduced significantly, which suggested the formation of a stable solid electrolyte interface (SEI). The cathodic peak below 0.1 V was attributed to the lithium insertion into the carbon mesostructure of CMK‐9.<link href="#cssc202000009-bib-0056"/> In the anodic process, two broad peaks were observed around 2.2 and 1.32 V, which could be attributed to the oxidation of Co<sup>0</sup> to CoO accompanied by the decomposition of Li<sub>2</sub>O and delithiation of carbon, respectively.<link href="#cssc202000009-bib-0014"/> Similar electrochemical behavior was observed for the CoO(20)@CMK‐9 and CoO(30)@CMK‐9 nanocomposites with the first cycle cathodic peak at 0.67 and 0.82 V, respectively. No change was observed in the subsequent CV curves after the first three–four cycles, suggesting good reversibility and cyclic stability of the CoO(<i>x</i>)@CMK‐9 nanocomposite electrodes. The CV curves of the pristine CoO nanoparticles showed a cathodic peak at 0.41 V owing to the development of the SEI layer and formation of Co and Li<sub>2</sub>O from the reduced CoO (Figure S4 a in the Supporting Information). In the subsequent cycles, the cathodic peak at 0.41 V was shifted to the range of 1–1.16 V. The peak at 1.1 V was related to the reduction of Li with CoO. The shifting of the cathodic peak could be related to pulverization of the CoO nanoparticles.<link href="#cssc202000009-bib-0012"/> One strong anodic peak was observed for the CoO nanoparticles in the range 2.15–2.24 V because of regeneration of CoO.</p><p>Galvanostatic charge/discharge characteristics of the CoO(<i>x</i>)@CMK‐9 anodes are depicted in Figure <link href="#cssc202000009-fig-0007"/> B from the first to tenth cycles in the chosen voltage range of 0.01 to 3.0 V and at a current density of 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. For the CoO(10)@CMK‐9 anode, the initial discharge capacity value of 2142 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> was obtained, whereas initial capacity values dropped to 1850 and 1088 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> for CoO(20)@CMK‐9 and CoO(30)@CMK‐9, respectively. The first cycle charge capacities of 1661, 1424, and 778 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> were achieved for CoO(10)@CMK‐9, CoO(20)@CMK‐9, and CoO(30)@CMK‐9, respectively, with the corresponding coulombic efficiency (CE) values of 77.5, 76.9, and 71.5 %. The irreversible capacity depletion could be ascribed to the degradation of electrolyte owing to the reaction with lithium and formation of a SEI layer.<link href="#cssc202000009-bib-0057"/> Moreover, the large surface area of mesoporous carbon CMK‐9 could assist the irreversible capacity loss as reversible faradic reactions could be subdued. In addition, the occurrence of irreversible storage of some lithium ions within the mesopores or voids of CMK‐9 impacted the reversible capacity.<link href="#cssc202000009-bib-0012"/> In addition, a voltage plateau was observed around 0.5–1.2 V in the first discharge cycle because of the reduction of CoO to Co and Li<sub>2</sub>O, which was similar to the observation discussed in the CV data analysis.<link href="#cssc202000009-bib-0006"/> This CoO reduction also contributed to the first cycle irreversible capacity loss. During the charge process, the observation of a voltage plateau around 1.1–1.6 V in the initial cycle could be attributed to the oxidation of Co with Li<sub>2</sub>O.<link href="#cssc202000009-bib-0058"/> As cobalt is catalytically active, the formed metallic cobalt enables the degradation of Li<sub>2</sub>O during the charging operation and makes the electrochemical reaction more reversible, resulting in higher initial coulombic efficiency value for the anode.<link href="#cssc202000009-bib-0059"/> From the fourth cycle onwards, the charge/discharge curves are almost overlapped, suggesting good reversibility and stable electrochemical performances for the CoO(<i>x</i>)@CMK‐9 nanocomposite anodes.</p><p>The rate capabilities of CMK‐9 and CoO(<i>x</i>)@CMK‐9 nanocomposite anodes were investigated at various current rates to evaluate the electrochemical characteristics of the electrodes. As observed in Figure <link href="#cssc202000009-fig-0008"/> A, the current densities of the electrode were varied from 100 to 200, 500, 1000, and 2000 mA g<sup>−1</sup> with stable discharge capacity values of 895, 773, 693, 642, and 390 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively, attained for CoO(10)@CMK‐9. Once the current density again returned to 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup> after 50 cycles, the CoO(10)@CMK‐9 electrode delivered a capacity of 815 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, which was marginally lower than the above value obtained by the same rate. Therefore, the CoO(10)@CMK‐9 anode could retain over 91 % capacity after 50 cycles, demonstrating the robustness of the electrode even after cycling at a high rate. At lower current densities of 100 and 200 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, the capacities of CoO(10)@CMK‐9 were higher than the theoretical capacity of CoO (716 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>).<link href="#cssc202000009-bib-0006"/> Similarly, CoO(20)@CMK‐9 delivered stable discharge capacities of 764, 664, 571, 523, 256, and 696 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, whereas CoO(30)@CMK‐9 provided discharge capacities of 579, 490, 433, 392, 187, and 501 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at current densities of 100, 200, 500, 1000, 2000, and 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. The lower capacities at higher CoO loadings, for example, CoO(20)@CMK‐9 and CoO(30)@CMK‐9, may be due to the larger particle size of CoO in these samples than CoO(10)@CMK‐9. As many particles might grow outside the mesopores of CMK‐9 at higher CoO loadings, these particles can agglomerate or are less effective in mass and charge transfer processes (Figure <link href="#cssc202000009-fig-0003"/> d). In contrast, most of the particles are smaller and encapsulated well inside the mesopores of CMK‐9 for CoO(10)@CMK‐9 (Figure <link href="#cssc202000009-fig-0003"/> a). These encapsulated nanoparticles come into contact with absorbed electrolyte in the mesopores with ease, whereas Li<sup>+</sup> ions can travel freely from the electrolyte to CoO particles and reciprocally throughout the lithiation/delithiation procedure, and thus results in shorter diffusion pathways for Li<sup>+</sup> ions and enhanced electrochemical performance for CoO(10)@CMK‐9.<link href="#cssc202000009-bib-0060"/> It is interesting to note that the discharge capacity of CMK‐9 is even better than CoO(30)@CMK‐9 at a lower current density of 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, although the capacity is higher for the latter at higher current densities. As CMK‐9 consists of connected interpenetrating nanotubes with a bimodal cubic pore structure, the charge and mass transfer processes are faster at lower current rates. However, the CMK‐9 mesostructure cannot withstand the high current densities, and thus the discharge capacity decreases at a rapid pace compared with CoO(30)@CMK‐9. Figure <link href="#cssc202000009-fig-0010"/> shows the schematic illustration of plausible conduction mechanisms of charge particles in the CoO(<i>x</i>)@CMK‐9 anode. First and foremost is the capacity contribution from CMK‐9. The 3D interpenetrating bimodal structure consists of large amounts of mesopores, void spaces, and disordered carbon atom stacking zones where excess lithium ions can be stored. Moreover, the interconnected carbon network of CMK‐9 with ordered mesopore arrangement and higher surface area helps in the effective infiltration and uniform distribution of electrolyte inside the mesopores and voids, leading to larger electrode/electrolyte interfacial contacts and thus providing abundant active reaction sites for lithium ion intercalation and deintercalation. Therefore, the contribution of capacity from CMK‐9 helps to achieve the capacity of CoO(10)@CMK‐9 beyond the theoretical capacity of CoO. Well‐dispersed and encapsulated CoO nanoparticles within the CMK‐9 mesopores are free from serious aggregation and volume expansion problems throughout the lithiation/delithiation procedure, leading to the long‐term cycle stability. As the encapsulated CoO nanoparticles can easily make contact with the electrolyte inside the mesopores, the lithium ions can travel rapidly from the electrolyte to CoO and in the reverse direction at the time of lithiation/delithiation, and consequently reduce the diffusion length of lithium ions to enhance the capacity. The highly ordered mesoporous structure of CMK‐9 can effectively confine the CoO nanoparticles as well as absorb and retain sufficient electrolytes within the mesopores of CMK‐9, resulting in rapid diffusion of lithium ions to boost the rate capability. The confinement effect assists in regulating the size and distribution of nanoparticles and reduces nanoparticle aggregation. Among the nanocomposites investigated, the CoO(10)@CMK‐9 nanocomposite delivered a discharge capacity of 830 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 200 cycles at a current density of 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, which was higher than the theoretical capacity of CoO, and retained 96 % capacity compared with the fourth cycle in lithium ion batteries. At a higher current density of 1000 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, the CoO(10)@CMK‐9 electrode exhibited a remarkable discharge capacity of 636 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 200 cycles with 97 % capacity retention in comparison to the capacity of the second cycle. The CoO(10)@CMK‐9 electrode even performed well at a higher current density of 2000 mA g<sup>−1</sup> by delivering a decent discharge capacity of 380 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>. In sodium‐ion batteries, the CoO(10)@CMK‐9 electrode delivered an initial discharge capacity of 1380 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, which was eventually decreased to 300 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after the third cycle and became stable to deliver a discharge capacity of 296 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 250 cycles at a current density of 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. In comparison to the third cycle, the CoO(10)@CMK‐9 electrode could retain more than 98 % of capacity after 250 cycles. The higher capacity, remarkable rate capability, longer cycle life, and higher coulombic efficiency of over 99 % demonstrate the great potentials of the CoO(10)@CMK‐9 nanocomposite as an anode for both lithium‐ and sodium‐ion batteries.</p></section><section xml:id="cssc202000009-sec-0011" numbered="no"><title type="main">Experimental Section</title><section xml:id="cssc202000009-sec-0012" numbered="no"><title type="main">Materials preparation</title><section xml:id="cssc202000009-sec-0013" numbered="no"><title type="main">Synthesis of mesoporous silica KIT‐6 and mesoporous carbon CMK‐9</title><p>Ordered mesoporous silica KIT‐6 was prepared by dissolving and stirring Pluronic P123 (2 g, Sigma–Aldrich, Average <i>M</i><sub>n</sub>≈5800) in 0.5 <sc>m</sc> HCl (3.72 g 37 % HCl+69.96 g H<sub>2</sub>O) at 35 °C for 4 h. Afterwards, BuOH (2 g, J. T. Baker) was mixed into the solution with continuous stirring at 35 °C for 1 h. Then, tetraethyl orthosilicate (TEOS, (5.2 g, Sigma–Aldrich) was slowly added to the mixed solution and further stirred for 24 h at 35 °C. The resulting liquid mixture was then hydrothermally treated at 100 °C for 24 h inside an oven. The solid thus obtained was filtered and then washed with deionized H<sub>2</sub>O and dried for another 24 h at 100 °C. Finally, the material was calcined at 550 °C for 6 h under air environment to obtain KIT‐6.</p><p>The as‐prepared KIT‐6 was used as a hard template to synthesize CMK‐9 by following the nanocasting process.<link href="#cssc202000009-bib-0025"/> The synthesis procedure of CMK‐9 is presented in Scheme S1 (in the Supporting Information). Furfuryl alcohol (FA, Sigma–Aldrich) and oxalic acid (Riedel‐de Haën) were used as the carbon precursor and polymerization catalyst, respectively. In a common synthesis procedure, oxalic acid (40 mg) was dissolved in a mixture of FA (4 mL) and TMB (4 mL, trimethyl benzene, Alfa Aesar) solvents. Then, KIT‐6 (0.5 g) was added to a certain amount of the prepared solution and stirred for 3 h. Afterwards, the mixture was heated for 24 h at 50 °C, followed by an additional heating period for 24 h at 90 °C. The solid product was then calcined at 850 °C for 4 h in a nitrogen environment to obtain the carbon‐silica complex. The removal of silica template was accomplished by stirring the carbon‐silica composite in 10 % hydrofluoric acid (HF, 48 %, Fluka) solution for 24 h. Finally, the filtrate was washed with deionized H<sub>2</sub>O several times and dried at 70 °C for 24 h in air to obtain CMK‐9.</p></section><section xml:id="cssc202000009-sec-0014" numbered="no"><title type="main">Preparation of CoO@CMK‐9 nanocomposites</title><p>The CMK‐9 powder was initially treated with 2 <sc>m</sc> HNO<sub>3</sub> acid (50 mL, Honeywell Fluka) by stirring it at 60 °C for 2 h to make it hydrophilic. Then, the material was filtered, washed with deionized H<sub>2</sub>O, and dried in an oven at 70 °C. To synthesize the metal oxide embedded OMC composites, different amounts of Co(NO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub><b>⋅</b>6 H<sub>2</sub>O precursor (0.108, 0.243, 0.416 g, ≈10, 20, 30 wt % of CoO, Alfa Aesar) were mixed with EtOH (20 mL) and stirred continuously for 1 h to make a solution (Scheme S1 in the Supporting Information). Then, the acid‐treated CMK‐9 was added to the as‐prepared solution and sonicated for 2 h. Afterwards, the mixed solution was stirred at 50 °C in a fumehood until complete evaporation of EtOH. Finally, the solid was calcined at 300 °C for 3 h in a nitrogen environment to obtain the CoO(<i>x</i>)@CMK‐9 nanocomposite, where <i>x</i> denotes the weight percentage of CoO (10, 20, or 30 wt %) in the nanocomposite.</p><p>For comparison, pristine CoO nanoparticles were also synthesized by dissolving Co(NO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub><b>⋅</b>6 H<sub>2</sub>O (20 mmol) and urea (5 mmol) in deionized (DI) H<sub>2</sub>O (40 mL). Then, the solution was transferred into a Teflon autoclave and hydrothermally treated at 180 °C for 5 h. The obtained product was washed with DI H<sub>2</sub>O and ethanol several times and then dried at 80 °C. The final product was calcined at 500 °C for 3 h in nitrogen atmosphere to get the CoO nanoparticles.</p></section></section><section xml:id="cssc202000009-sec-0015" numbered="no"><title type="main">Fabrication of LIBs and SIBs</title><p>The working electrode was prepared by mixing 70 wt % active material (CoO(<i>x</i>)@CMK‐9), 20 wt % Super P carbon (Alfa Aesar), and 10 wt % poly(vinylidene fluoride) (Sigma–Aldrich) in <i>N</i>‐methyl‐2‐pyrrolidone (Sigma–Aldrich) solvent. The mixture was stirred for over 4 h at 300 rpm to make it homogeneous and coated on clean copper sheet. The coated sheet was then dried at 70 °C for 12 h, roller‐pressed, and cut into circular sheets with a diameter of 1 cm. The mass loading of the active material was around 1.4–1.5 mg cm<sup>−2</sup>. The CR‐2032 coin cell setup was used to assemble the lithium‐ion battery inside an Ar‐filled glovebox where lithium foil (Sigma–Aldrich) was chosen as the counter and reference electrodes and the prepared electrode was selected as the working electrode. Polypropylene (PP) film was utilized as the separator and the liquid electrolyte was made of 1 <sc>m</sc> LiPF<sub>6</sub> in ethylene carbonate (EC)/diethyl carbonate (DEC; 1:1, v/v, Tomiyama Pure Chemicals). For the preparation of sodium‐ion batteries, sodium metal was employed as the counter and reference electrodes along with the prepared working electrode. 1 <sc>m</sc> NaClO<sub>4</sub> in propylene carbonate (PC)/fluoroethylene carbonate (FEC; 95:5, v/v) was applied as the electrolyte along with Whatman glass microfiber (GF/A) membrane as separator.</p></section><section xml:id="cssc202000009-sec-0016" numbered="no"><title type="main">Characterization methods</title><p>Structural analysis of the nanocomposite samples was carried out by measuring the small‐angle X‐ray diffraction (SXRD) patterns in a synchrotron beamline (Wiggler‐A, <i>λ</i>=0.1322281 nm) at the National Synchrotron Radiation Research Center, Taiwan. Wide‐angle X‐ray diffraction (WXRD) patterns were collected with a Shimadzu LabX XRD‐6000 diffractometer equipped with monochromatic Cu<sub>Kα</sub> radiation (<i>λ</i>=0.15406 nm) to identify the crystalline phases. A Quantachrome Autosorb iQ‐2 analyzer was used to measure the textural properties. The specific surface area was calculated by using the Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) method, whereas the Barrett–Joyner–Halenda (BJH) method gave the pore size distribution. Pore volume was measured from the volume of nitrogen adsorbed at <i>P</i>/<i>P</i><sub>0</sub>=0.99. Thermal analysis of the samples was performed in a thermogravimetric analyzer (TGA, TA Instruments Q50) in air environment at a heating rate of 10 °C min<sup>−1</sup>. The microstructure and particle size of the samples were detected by using a JEOL JEM2100F high‐resolution transmission electron microscope (HRTEM). The chemical state of the elements was explored by X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS, Thermo VG Scientific Sigma Probe). Raman spectra were acquired with a micro Raman spectrometer (UniRaman, Protrustech Co. Ltd.).</p><p>The galvanostatic charge/discharge tests were executed at room temperature with a LANHE (CT2001A) and Chroma Programmable Charge‐Discharge Tester (17216M‐10‐6) at various current densities in the voltage range of 0.01 to 3.0 V. An Autolab PGSTAT302 potentiostat/galvanostat was used to evaluate the cyclic voltammetry (CV). Electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) was measured with an Autolab PGSTAT302 potentiostat/galvanostat in the frequency range of 10<sup>5</sup>–10<sup>−4</sup> Hz at room temperature.</p><p>Post‐cycle characterizations of the cycled cells were evaluated to explore the structural and morphological variation of the electrode material after prolonged cycling.
