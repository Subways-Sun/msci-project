Information</caption></supportingInfoItem></supportingInformation><abstractGroup><abstract type="main" xml:lang="en"><title type="main">Abstract</title><p>Antimony (Sb) is considered a promising anode material for sodium‐ion batteries due to its high specific capacity and moderate working potential. However, the non‐negligible volume variation leads to the rapid decay of capacity, which hinders the practical application of Sb anode materials. Here, an economical and scalable route with high yield is proposed to obtain Sb ultrafine nanocrystals embedded in a porous carbon skeleton. Notably, the synergetic effect of the heterogeneous structure is maximized by inducing the interfacial coupling SbOC and creating buffering space for the volume effect of Sb. The high‐entropy phase interface creates the doping site breaking the periodicity of atoms and alters the electronic structure, also bridging the slip of intergranular defects. Thus, the electronic conductivity and phase interface structural stability are reinforced. The mechanism of accelerating electron migration at the heterogeneous phase interface is visualized through the density functional theory method, and the mass/charge‐transfer kinetics is analyzed via the calculation of surface‐induced capacitive contribution.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="smtd202100188-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>Antimony (Sb) quantum dots confined by functional carbon are obtained through one‐step carbothermal reduction. The synergetic effect of the heterogeneous structure is maximized by inducing the interfacial coupling SbOC and creating buffering space for the volume effect of Sb, which effectively promotes the sodium‐storage performance of Sb.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:23669608:media:smtd202100188:smtd202100188-gra-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smtd202100188-gra-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body xml:id="smtd202100188-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="smtd202100188-sec-0010"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs), which serve as the most predominant energy‐storage device, have been widely applied commercially for over 30 years. Nevertheless, the increasing requirements for large‐scale energy‐storing devices stimulate the research interests for other available and economical substitutes. Sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) are considered as the suitable option with the privilege of natural abundance, low costs, and little environmental impact. Therefore, pursuing appropriate electrode materials with high specific capacity and long cycling stability could be pivotal for the further application of SIBs.</p><p>Antimony (Sb) has been regarded as a promising anode for SIBs on account of its high theoretical capacity (660 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> given by fully sodiation to Na<sub>3</sub>Sb), unique puckered‐layer structure that promotes Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion, and moderate operating voltage (0.5–0.8 vs Na<sup>+</sup>/Na) that could be conductive to avoiding Na deposition. However, the bottleneck for the further promotion of Sb anode is the relatively low intrinsic conductivity. Sb possess resistivity (370 nΩ m<sup>−1</sup>) between metals and nonmetals, like other several types of metals (e.g., Ge and Bi) that have been considered as semimetal.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smtd202100188-bib-0001"><sup>1</sup></link><sup>]</sup> These semimetals contain very small overlap between the top of the conduction band and the bottom of the valance band, making the density of states near Fermi level close to 0.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smtd202100188-bib-0001 #smtd202100188-bib-0002 #smtd202100188-bib-0003"><sup>1–3</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Naturally, these undesirable physical properties hinder the charge‐transport performance and rate capability of Sb anode from the electrochemical angle. Another well‐known drawback for the alloying‐type anode material is the huge volume expansion (≈390% for Sb)<sup>[</sup><link href="#smtd202100188-bib-0004"><sup>4</sup></link><sup>]</sup> during sodiation/desodiation, which leads to the pulverization of Sb anode and the disconnection of active materials with current collector. Moreover, the generation of extra solid–electrolyte interface (SEI) layer on newly formed exposed surface of electrode causes the retardation of kinetics and degradation in capacity.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smtd202100188-bib-0005"><sup>5</sup></link><sup>]</sup> To alleviate these problems, tremendous endeavors have been made, including the design of micro/nanostructure Sb, Sb‐based alloys, and Sb/C composite.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smtd202100188-bib-0006 #smtd202100188-bib-0007"><sup>6,7</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>It is widely regarded that the rational interface design and atom doping route could enhance the charge‐transport properties; however, the essential mechanism and the visualization of the charge migration at the heterogeneous site (doping site and heterogeneous interface) are still ambiguous.</p><p>Besides, in terms of the nanostructure fabrication, the Sb nanoparticles, Sb nanospheres, and Sb nanotubes are typical strategies to enhance the electrochemical performance.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smtd202100188-bib-0005 #smtd202100188-bib-0008 #smtd202100188-bib-0009"><sup>5,8,9</sup></link><sup>]</sup> These methods could effectively accelerate the mass‐transfer kinetics and the reaction kinetics through downsizing Sb particle. Nonetheless, the capacity retention and long cycle life are still being restricted, as the nanostructure brings about the increased exposed surface area, which speeds up the aggregation of particles. On this basis, introducing a buffering matrix phase arouses research interest. The Sb‐based alloys are of two types: the active–active type (e.g., Sn–Sb, Bi–Sb, and Sn–Ge–Sb) and active–inactive type (e.g., Cu<sub>2</sub>Sb, Mo<sub>3</sub>Sb<sub>7</sub>, Zn<sub>4</sub>Sb, and FeSb<sub>2</sub>).<sup>[</sup><link href="#smtd202100188-bib-0009 #smtd202100188-bib-0010 #smtd202100188-bib-0011"><sup>9–11</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The introduction of the active phases helps alleviate the concentrated volume expansion as different elements react at different potentials. As for inactive metals, the incorporation of new phase serves as the structural reinforcement that buffers the volume expansion.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smtd202100188-bib-0012 #smtd202100188-bib-0013"><sup>12,13</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Moreover, the incorporation of buffering matrix carries out several merits, which help improve the cycling/rate performance:<sup>[</sup><link href="#smtd202100188-bib-0007"><sup>7</sup></link><sup>]</sup> i) the direct explosion between Sb nanoparticles and electrolyte are restrained. ii) Aggregation of nanoarchitectured Sb with high surface energy into bigger grains is prevented. iii) The intermetallic compounds such as SnSb are beneficial for relieving mechanical stress. However, the shortcoming of Sb–M alloys such as poor, long‐term cycling stability, high costs, low efficiency, and poor scalability could not be neglected. What is more important, the introduction of inactive components would sacrifice some specific capacity and lower the gravimetric energy density. It is resulted from the fact that the inert elements with high atomic mass reduce the percentage of active material in electrode, while they do not participate in the electrochemical reaction. Correspondingly, the requirement for low‐cost, scalable and efficient electrode with better cycling stability is increasing. Hence, carbon, serving as a buffering matrix with relatively low mass ratio and enhanced electrical conductivity, is emerged as a prevalent combined element to enhance the cycling/rate performance.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smtd202100188-bib-0013"><sup>13</sup></link><sup>]</sup> It is worth mentioning that carbon itself provides a certain amount of sodium‐storage capacity (250–300 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>). The carbon skeleton could help disperse and anchor the metallic Sb nanoparticles into fragile conductive framework uniformly, protecting the active materials from exfoliation and aggregation to some extent.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smtd202100188-bib-0014"><sup>14</sup></link><sup>]</sup> These merits of Sb/C composited electrode greatly generate the enhancement of cycling stability and rate performance in SIBs. In recent years, micro–nanostructure architecture of carbonaceous skeleton for Sb/C hybrid materials has been investigated. Song et al. prepared and reported an antimony/carbon composite with a yolk–shell structure and applied it as an SIB anode material,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smtd202100188-bib-0015 #smtd202100188-bib-0016"><sup>15,16</sup></link><sup>]</sup> exhibiting a specific capacity of ≈554 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>. Cao's group designed and reported Sb–C nanofiber through the single‐nozzle electrospinning and calcination method; when used in SIBs, it delivers an improved rate capability of 337 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 5 C with 93% capacity retention over 300 cycles at 0.2 A g<sup>−1</sup>.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smtd202100188-bib-0017"><sup>17</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Most of the morphology architecture for Sb–C hybrid materials aims to create a buffering space or a structure‐strengthened dispersed phase to accommodate the volume variation of metallic antimony. Nevertheless, the extra cavity in Sb/C composites, happened commonly in core–shell and hollow structures, usually provides excess noneffective volume. Further, though carbonaceous framework contributes sodium‐storage capacity, it is still much less than the value of pure metallic Sb. Thereby, a reasonable mass ratio of carbon and antimony is also crucial in elevating specific capacity. The current dilemma is the tradeoff of sacrificing volumetric energy density, specific capacity, and packing density to attain a high long‐term cycling life and cycling stability.</p><p>Considering these two factors, designing a heterogeneous phase interface for Sb composite is an effective method for advanced anode materials, which can not only accelerate the electron/mass transfer and but also alleviate the volume expansion. Herein, we report an economical, high‐yielding, and scalable strategy for the in situ preparation of self‐confined Sb quantum dots (3 nm in average) encapsulated inside amorphous carbon (denoted as SbQD@C). Also, the visualization of electron transfer at the phase interface is realized, the mechanism that the charge/mass migration being speeded up at heterogeneous interface is clarified. The interconnected porous carbon framework provides a reasonable Sb/C atomic ratio, which minimizes the inoperative buffering space to elevate the specific capacity and energy density. The uniformly dispersed ultrafine antimony quantum dots in carbon skeleton not only ease the mechanical stress, but also construct abundant nanocomposite heterointerface, which facilitates the ion/charge transport and accelerates reaction kinetics.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smtd202100188-bib-0018 #smtd202100188-bib-0019"><sup>18,19</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Also, the interfacial coupling interaction brings about a boosted ion‐diffusion kinetic and thermal stability, with the introduction of SbOC bonding.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smtd202100188-bib-0020"><sup>20</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The Sb quantum dots are uniformly confined in well‐conductive elastic carbon network with little aggregation, which reinforces the structural integrity.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smtd202100188-bib-0009 #smtd202100188-bib-0021"><sup>9,21</sup></link><sup>]</sup> These two phases were mixed and evenly distributed spontaneously with numerous phase boundaries and heterointerfaces, which markedly facilitate the ion‐storage performance, and optimize the SEI layer.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smtd202100188-bib-0022"><sup>22</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Also, the ultrafine Sb nanocrystals and the carbon framework improve the electrochemical properties for the SbQD@C electrode with synergistic effect. Notably, the SbQD@C electrode exhibits a reversible specific capacity of 375 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 100 cycles at a high current density of 1000 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, with a high capacity retention of 92.8%. The method we proposed may present a universal and scalable route to prepare carbon‐matrix‐confined metal quantum dot nanocomposite for high‐performance SIB anode material.</p></section><section xml:id="smtd202100188-sec-0020"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p>The in situ evolution of crystallographic phases was characterized by the high‐temperature X‐ray diffraction (HT‐XRD). In <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smtd202100188-fig-0001"><b>1</b></link>, the annealing protocol for HT‐XRD follows the temperature programming with a constant heating rate of 10 °C min<sup>−1</sup> from 50 to 600 °C, with a scanning rate of 5° min<sup>−1</sup>. The pristine XRD pattern of the mixture displays four typical diffraction peaks of cubic phase NaSbO<sub>3</sub>, and a weak peak at around 16° is assigned to be the impure phase of Sb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>·<i>x</i>H<sub>2</sub>O, which is contained in the reactant (Figure <link href="#smtd202100188-supitem-0001">S1</link>, Supporting Information). The instinctive diffraction signal at 20° could be ascribed to the existence of hydrate phase (Figure <link href="#smtd202100188-supitem-0001">S1</link>, Supporting Information), which is completely diminished at 150 °C, matching well with the thermogravimetric (TG) patterns (Figure <link href="#smtd202100188-supitem-0001">S2a</link>, Supporting Information). The intensive peak for metallic Sb phase (JCFDF No.35–0732) appears at 325 °C, with the synchronous degradation of the oxide peak. As can be seen from the HT‐XRD results, the carbothermal reduction is completely finished at 350 °C. In the meantime, the evolution from the tetragonal prism phase NaSbO<sub>3</sub> to cubic metallic Sb is completed. As can be seen from <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smtd202100188-fig-0002"><b>2</b></link>, the Sb quantum dots underwent a heterogeneous nucleation and in situ formation process. Before the reduction being finished, the carbon source (sodium oleate) went through a decarboxylic reaction and isomerization reaction that enable carbon chain elongated to form the precursor of elastic framework. During this, the long carbon chain produces surface activity and ensures the severance of antimony nanoparticles. After the reduction process, with the elevating of temperature, the integrity and crystalline degree of Sb phase were enhanced. It is worth noting that the melted sodium oleate (over 275 °C) plays the role of the liquid dispersion phase that covers and redistributes the antimonide, and provides a self‐confinement environment to prevent the agglomeration. Therefore, the antimonate was uniformly rearranged and embedded in the fluid carbonaceous dispersant. It should be mentioned that the formation of ultrafine antimony nanocrystal is a tradeoff between the cohesive energy inside bulk phase and the repulsive energy induced by the fluid shear force and double electric layer on grain surface. Confined by the competition of these two types of energy, the final size of the nanograin was determined. The in situ reduction and heterogeneous nucleation happened simultaneously in the annealing period. Notably, the capillarity effect of liquid carbonaceous dispersant added on antimonate nanoparticles drives an intimate interpenetration between these two phases and enhances the surface diffusion,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smtd202100188-bib-0023"><sup>23</sup></link><sup>]</sup> during which the disordered heterogeneous phase interface was created. Further, the capillarity‐driven local interphase rearrangement could also take place by forming quasispherical shape and aggregating to larger particles. In addition, the capillarity‐driven surface diffusion could weaken the grain boundary to some extent, and then form a membrane‐like amorphous species, as shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smtd202100188-fig-0003"><b>3</b>e</link>. After the fully infiltration of the phase interface, the in situ carbothermal reduction began to happen. The “carbon sea” environment could greatly suppresses the grain growth and prevents the nanocrystalline from aggregation.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smtd202100188-bib-0024"><sup>24</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The majority of Sb particles were reduced under the confinement of carbon skeleton to form uniformly dispersed quantum dots despite carbonaceous framework is pelelith‐like porous structure. In the larger magnification SEM, the matrix was stacked by flexible interlinked nanoflacks, which could promote the contact between active materials and electrolyte, and shorten the ion migration pathway;<sup>[</sup><link href="#smtd202100188-bib-0025"><sup>25</sup></link><sup>]</sup> also the conductive carbon skeleton with mechanical strength largely reduces the aggregation and collapses of the Sb quantum dots in electrochemical reaction. The scanning electron microscopy at higher magnification was performed in Figure <link href="#smtd202100188-fig-0003">3b,c</link>; the nanoflack‐stacked microstructure could be seen clearly. The hierarchical porous morphology may be related to the activation effect of sodium‐rich environment and the release of CO<i><sub>x</sub></i> gas under thermal decomposition of precursor. The Sb quantum dots were embedded in amorphous carbon uniformly, with a 3–4 nm average diameter, as detected in transmission electron microscopy (TEM) images. In high‐resolution TEM, the grain boundary and lattice of Sb dots are ambiguous. This may be resulted from the capillarity effect facilitating the surface diffusion, leading to an increased interface intimacy, thus may weaken the grain boundary. In Figure <link href="#smtd202100188-fig-0003">3f</link>, the light dots in high‐angle annular dark‐field scanning transmission electron microscopy (HAADF‐STEM) indicate the Sb dots, which match well with the bright field TEM. The bigger particles are composed of several individual Sb nanograins, as shown in Figure <link href="#smtd202100188-fig-0003">3g</link>. As can be seen from Figure <link href="#smtd202100188-fig-0003">3g</link>, the lattice fringes of (104) and (110) planes are attributed to the hexagonal Sb (JCPDS: 85–1233), which match well with the XRD result. On the whole, the dispersion of Sb and carbon is uniform, as characterized by energy dispersive spectrometry (EDS). Endowed by the buffering effect of conductive carbon matrix, the tiny Sb dots could maintain the morphology during the alloying–dealloying process. The band structure and the relative density of states for SbQD@C and intrinsic Sb (<i>R‐3m</i> space group) are measured in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smtd202100188-fig-0008"><b>8</b></link>.</p><figure xml:id="smtd202100188-fig-0008"><label>8</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:23669608:media:smtd202100188:smtd202100188-fig-0008" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smtd202100188-fig-0008-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) Band structure of a) SbQD@C and c) Sb (<i>R‐3m</i>); the total density of states and partial density of states for b) SbQD@C and d) Sb (<i>R‐3m</i>).</caption></figure><p>The disordered doping oxygen atom between the amorphous carbon and Sb distinctly altered the electronic states of atoms at interphase, which is the essential cause that accelerates the charge‐transfer performance.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smtd202100188-bib-0040 #smtd202100188-bib-0046"><sup>40,46</sup></link><sup>]</sup> By establishing the high‐entropy phase interphase, the obvious difference could be seen in the band structure and total/partial density of states. In Figure <link href="#smtd202100188-fig-0008">8a</link>, the band structure for intrinsic Sb shows a typical semimetal nature, with small overlap between full band and conduction band, and the density of states near the Fermi level is close to 0. While introducing the heterogeneous interface, the overall impurity energy level for SbQD@C system is increased with the lift of the impurity peak; thus, extra electron states for the system were produced and, further, the band across the Fermi level increased. In Figure <link href="#smtd202100188-fig-0008">8b</link>, the total density of states for Sb at 0–5 eV is significantly strengthened at the interphase, while the energy from −5 to 0 eV decreased. The reason behind it is the partial density of states for Sb 3p orbital and O 2p orbital overlapped at that section, contributing to the intense orbital hybridization effect; as a matter of fact, the peak of the density of states under Fermi level also became more dispersed and delocalized. Besides, the peak for C 2p and O 2p appeared at the same district of the Sb 3s and 3p, manifesting the resonance coupling among C, O, and Sb. It can be seen from the band structure and the density of states that the impurity peaks and energy level increased by introducing the heterogeneous phase interface. Also, the system energy band is widened with the dispersion of density of states. Consequently, part of the atoms at phase interface breaks the periodicity, then the neighbor electron clouds turn from extended states and localized states to delocalized states, and the charge‐transfer rate is accelerated therewith.</p><p>To further visualize the electron structure of the interface, the charge density difference is performed in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smtd202100188-fig-0009"><b>9</b></link>. It is well known that the oxygen atom has strong electronegativity, which serves as the bridge to smoothing the electron cloud distribution, simultaneously inducing the rearrangement of outer‐shell electron.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smtd202100188-bib-0054 #smtd202100188-bib-0055 #smtd202100188-bib-0056"><sup>54–56</sup></link><sup>]</sup> As can be seen in Figure <link href="#smtd202100188-fig-0009">9a</link>, the electron cloud distribution is dramatically changed at the heterogeneous interface. The charge density at the green region is close to 0 based on the charge density difference. It is notable that the charge‐deficient area is enlarged compared with the separated triangle‐shaped area in bulk phase. The charge‐deficient region (green area) which is induced by the Sb‐O‐C bonding is linked and connected with each other due to the electron‐withdrawing effect of O atoms. It represents the outer shell electron of Sb is captured by O atom. Consequently, the Fermi level moves down and the density of states is increased, making the electron conductivity of composite increased. In brief, the interface atoms break the lattice periodicity inside and rearrange the electron clouds, also creating permanent dipoles, in which the oxygen attract the electron of C and Sb and alter the band structure eventually.</p><figure xml:id="smtd202100188-fig-0009"><label>9</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:23669608:media:smtd202100188:smtd202100188-fig-0009" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smtd202100188-fig-0009-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) The difference charge density of SbQD@C at the heterogeneous interface (e Å<sup>‐3</sup>), b) the difference charge density of bulk Sb (<i>R‐3m</i>).</caption></figure><p>The interaction of surface atoms altered the electron states and band structure, also minimizes the work function of the electron. The work functions of Sb and SbQD@C were measured through the ultraviolet photoelectron spectroscopy (UPS) test in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smtd202100188-fig-0010"><b>10</b></link>. Based on the difference value between the Fermi energy (<i>E</i><sub>F</sub>) and the secondary electron cutoff edge (<i>E</i><sub>cutoff</sub>), the work function of Sb and SbQD@C was calculated. The work function for SbQD@C (φ = 4.8 eV) is lower than that of the Sb (φ = 3.3 eV), which are in accordance with the simulation results of the first principles calculation. Distinctly, the interface electronic properties are altered by the construction of the high‐entropy interphase, as the SbOC coupling at the interface induces the dipoles that smooth the phase interfacial electron clouds’ arrangement. The decrease of work function is significant, as it represents that the charge migration at the surface is enhanced, which could boost the rate performance for the electrode.</p><figure xml:id="smtd202100188-fig-0010"><label>10</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:23669608:media:smtd202100188:smtd202100188-fig-0010" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smtd202100188-fig-0010-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>UPS diagram of a) Sb and d) SbQD@C, and the linear extrapolation of UPS, b) with the electron cutoff energy and c) the Fermi energy level of SbQD@C and the electron cutoff energy of e) Sb and f) Fermi energy level of Sb.</caption></figure></section><section xml:id="smtd202100188-sec-0030"><title type="main">Conclusion</title><p>In conclusion, the general route for high‐yielding metal‐nanocrystal‐anchored carbon nanocomposite was proposed. There are several morphological advantages of as‐prepared samples, such as the abundant phase interface and quantum‐dot‐sized nanocrystals, which greatly accelerate the mass/charge‐transport kinetics. The construction of high‐entropy phase interface includes the doping sites that break the symmetric arrangement of atoms in bulk phase. In addition, the SbOC interaction at the interface greatly enhances the electronic conductivity and stabilizes the slip of the dislocation. Thanks to the synergetic effect of heterogeneous structured M–C nanocomposite, the as‐prepared electrode exhibits advanced sodium‐storage performance.</p></section><section xml:id="smtd202100188-sec-0040"><title type="main">Experimental Section</title><section xml:id="smtd202100188-sec-0050" numbered="no"><section xml:id="smtd202100188-sec-0060" numbered="no"><section xml:id="smtd202100188-sec-0070" numbered="no"><title type="main">Synthesis of SbQD@C Electrode</title><p>The products were prepared through carbothermal reduction. Typically, 3.0 g of the sodium oleate and 4.0 g of sodium antimonate (NaSbO<sub>3</sub>) were mixed and ground thoroughly in an agate mortar for 10 min. The mixed powder was put into a porcelain boat. Then the porcelain boat was heated to 600 °C for 2 h in a furnace tube under Ar atmosphere with a heating rate of 3 °C min<sup>−1</sup>. After that, the calcinated product was washed by HCl solution (1 <sc>m</sc>) until no effervescence of small bubbles was found. Then the obtained product was separated by vacuum suction filtration washed with deionized water for several times, and finally dried at 80 °C for 12 h. The final product, Sb quantum dots confined by porous carbon, was abbreviated as SbQD@C.</p></section><section xml:id="smtd202100188-sec-0080" numbered="no"><title type="main">Characterization</title><p>A field‐emission scanning electron microscope (FESEM, FEI Quanta 200) was employed to characterize the morphology. The TEM, HRTEM, and elements mapping images were acquired by a transmission electron microscope (JEM‐2100F). The crystal structure was explored by XRD (Rigaku Ultima IV, 18 kW, Cu Kα radiation). The element characterizations were collected by using in XPS (K‐Alpha 1063, UK). The HT‐XRD (Rigaku, TTR3, 40 kV, Cu Kα radiation) was carried out with a scanning speed of 5° min<sup>−1</sup> to identify the phase transition and formation of Sb quantum dots. The sample was calcinated with a heating rate of 10 °C min<sup>−1</sup> at N<sub>2</sub> atmosphere from 50 to 600 °C, with a step of 25 °C. The HT‐XRD data were obtained in the range 2θ = 5°–80°, and the angular range 10°–30° was selected because the characteristic peaks for the reduction product (Sb) and reactant (NaSbO<sub>3</sub> and the impurity phase Sb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>·<i>x</i>H<sub>2</sub>O) were contained in this domain.</p></section><section xml:id="smtd202100188-sec-0090" numbered="no"><title type="main">Electrochemical Measurements</title><p>The electrochemical properties of the obtained samples were evaluated by CR2016 coin cells, which were assembled in an Ar‐filled glove box with the oxygen content and water content lower than 0.5 ppm. The working electrodes were prepared from a slurry, which was composed of the mixture of 70 wt% SbQD@C active material, 15 wt% carboxymethyl cellulose (CMC), and 15 wt% conductive carbon (Super P). The well‐mixed slurry was coated on copper foil and dried at vacuum oven for 10 h at 70 °C. The copper foil was punched into circular pieces (≈1.44 cm<sup>2</sup>) and applied in half‐cells as working electrode with a mass loading range of 0.5–1.0 mg, and the sodium metal was used as the counter electrode. 1 <sc>m</sc> solution of NaClO<sub>4</sub> in propylene carbonate (PC) with 5% fluoroethylene carbonate (FEC) was utilized as the electrolyte. Galvanostatic charging/discharging tests were performed on Land test systems (CT 2001A).
