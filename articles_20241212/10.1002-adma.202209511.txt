Information</caption></supportingInfoItem></supportingInformation><abstractGroup><abstract type="main" xml:lang="en"><title type="main">Abstract</title><p>The sodium (Na)‐metal anode with high theoretical capacity and low cost is promising for construction of high‐energy‐density metal batteries. However, the unsatisfactory interface between Na and the liquid electrolyte induces tardy ion transfer kinetics and dendritic Na growth, especially at ultralow temperature (−40 °C). Herein, an artificial heterogeneous interphase consisting of disodium selenide (Na<sub>2</sub>Se) and metal vanadium (V) is produced on the surface of Na (Na@Na<sub>2</sub>Se/V) via an in situ spontaneous chemical reaction. Such interphase layer possesses high sodiophilicity, excellent ionic conductivity, and high Young's modulus, which can promote Na‐ion adsorption and transport, realizing homogenous Na deposition without dendrites. The symmetric Na@Na<sub>2</sub>Se/V cell exhibits outstanding cycling life span of over 1790 h (0.5 mA cm<sup>−2</sup>/1 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup>) in carbonate‐based electrolyte. More remarkably, ab initio molecular dynamics simulations reveal that the artificial Na<sub>2</sub>Se/V hybrid interphase can accelerate the desolvation of solvated Na<sup>+</sup> at −40 °C. The Na@Na<sub>2</sub>Se/V electrode thus exhibits exceptional electrochemical performance in symmetric cell (over 1500 h at 0.5 mA cm<sup>−2</sup>/0.5 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup>) and full cell (over 700 cycles at 0.5 C) at −40 °C. This work provides an avenue to design artificial heterogeneous interphase layers for superior high‐energy‐density metal batteries at ambient and ultralow temperatures.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="adma202209511-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>A multifunctional interphase consisting of disodium selenide and metal vanadium is developed for the Na anode via an in situ spontaneous chemical reaction. xml:id="adma202209511-sec-0010"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Recently, a series of emerging metal anodes such as lithium, sodium (Na), potassium, zinc, and aluminum have caught considerable attentions for high‐energy‐density batteries.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202209511-bib-0001 #adma202209511-bib-0002 #adma202209511-bib-0003 #adma202209511-bib-0004 #adma202209511-bib-0005 #adma202209511-bib-0006 #adma202209511-bib-0007 #adma202209511-bib-0008 #adma202209511-bib-0009"><sup>1–9</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Among them, Na metal anode presents obviously practical advantages of easy raw material availability, cost‐effectiveness, low redox potential (−2.714 V vs standard hydrogen electrode), and high specific capacity (1166 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>).<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202209511-bib-0010 #adma202209511-bib-0011 #adma202209511-bib-0012 #adma202209511-bib-0013"><sup>10–13</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Nevertheless, the practical implementation of Na metal anode is still plagued by key bottlenecks of its high chemical reactivity and infinite volume change during the cycles.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202209511-bib-0014 #adma202209511-bib-0015 #adma202209511-bib-0016 #adma202209511-bib-0017"><sup>14–17</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Owing to the high reactivity and low redox potential of Na, once it contacts with the electrolyte, a solid electrolyte interface (SEI) layer is formed on the Na metal surface.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202209511-bib-0018 #adma202209511-bib-0019 #adma202209511-bib-0020"><sup>18–20</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, the formed passivation film is generally not robust enough to withstand the volume fluctuation during plating and stripping processes of Na, which would lead to the crack of the SEI layer and then induce a nonuniform Na‐ion flux distribution.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202209511-bib-0021 #adma202209511-bib-0022 #adma202209511-bib-0023 #adma202209511-bib-0024"><sup>21–24</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Consequently, Na dendrites are generated and the continued growth would puncture the separator ultimately, causing serious safety hazards.</p><p>To overcome these challenges, a simple and effective method is to construct an ideal SEI with the following favorable characteristics: i) high electronical insulating and ionic conductivity realizing fast ion diffusion without reduction in SEI; ii) uniform composition and structural stability guarantying reversible Na plating/stripping process; and iii) high mechanical toughness mitigating volume fluctuation and suppressing dendrite growth.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202209511-bib-0025 #adma202209511-bib-0026 #adma202209511-bib-0027 #adma202209511-bib-0028"><sup>25–28</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Therefore, artificial SEI protection layer stands out, owing to controllable structure, morphology, and composition. For example, artificial Na<sub>3</sub>P and Na<sub>2</sub>Te protective layers have been proven to have high mechanic strength and fast ion conductivity.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202209511-bib-0029 #adma202209511-bib-0030"><sup>29,30</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, a mass of interface layers with a single component are hardly to meet all the requirements of ideal SEI. Recently, artificial hybrid interlayers constituted by various functional components (e.g., rich‐Na alloy and NaF) have been successful to promote ionic transfer kinetic and stability for high‐performance metal batteries.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202209511-bib-0031 #adma202209511-bib-0032 #adma202209511-bib-0033 #adma202209511-bib-0034 #adma202209511-bib-0035"><sup>31–35</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Moreover, inducing the high metal‐philic components into heterogeneous interphase can promote the even distribution of ion flux at the electrolyte/electrode interface, realizing homogenous ion deposition.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202209511-bib-0034 #adma202209511-bib-0036"><sup>34,36</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Even though the SEI regulation strategy has taken some extent effect on metal batteries at room temperature, it is rarely progressed at subzero temperatures (especially at ≤−40 °C).</p><p>It is well‐known that, under low‐temperature operating condition, insufficient dynamics caused by sluggish cation desolvation at the electrode/electrolyte interface and high energy barrier of ion diffusion through SEI will further exacerbate the formation of Na dendrites and deteriorate cycling performance.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202209511-bib-0037 #adma202209511-bib-0038 #adma202209511-bib-0039 #adma202209511-bib-0040 #adma202209511-bib-0041 #adma202209511-bib-0042 #adma202209511-bib-0043 #adma202209511-bib-0044"><sup>37–44</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Based on these concerns, recently, Zhou et al.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202209511-bib-0045"><sup>45</sup></link><sup>]</sup> regulated the Na<sup>+</sup> solvation structure to mitigate the energy barrier of Na<sup>+</sup> desolvation, but the symmetric Na||Na cells were merely cycled for around 550 h at −40 °C (1 mA cm<sup>−2</sup>). In addition, Huang and co‐workers<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202209511-bib-0046"><sup>46</sup></link><sup>]</sup> reported a two‐pronged approach via altering the solvation structure and fabricating artificial SEI to enhance the abilities of Na‐ion desolvation and diffusion, while the symmetric cells still presented short life span (250 h) at −20 °C. Therefore, it is thus very keen to develop an advanced artificial heterogeneous interlayer for superior sodium‐metal batteries (SMBs) under ambient and low temperatures (−40 °C).</p><p>Herein, we designed an artificial heterogeneous interphase consisting of disodium selenide (Na<sub>2</sub>Se) and metal vanadium (V) to protect sodium metal anode via an in situ reaction of VSe<sub>2</sub> with Na. The density functional theory (DFT) calculation and experiment results demonstrate that such interlayer possesses high sodiophilicity, excellent ionic conductivity, and high Young's modulus, which can promote fast ion adsorption and transport and induce homogenous deposition of Na ion without dendrite growth. As a result, the obtained Na@Na<sub>2</sub>Se/V symmetric cell exhibits an excellent cycle life over 1790 h (0.5 mA cm<sup>−2</sup>/1 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup>). For practical application, coupling with the Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> (NVP) cathode, the assembled full cell can maintain ≈90 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 1800 cycles at 5 C. More attractively, under a low operating temperature of −40 °C, long cycling life is also achieved for the symmetric Na@Na<sub>2</sub>Se/V cell (over 1500 h at 0.5 mA cm<sup>−2</sup>/0.5 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup>) and full cell (over 700 cycles at 0.5 C).</p></section><section xml:id="adma202209511-sec-0020"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p>The fabrication procedure of artificial Na<sub>2</sub>Se/V interphase protecting layer on the surface of Na metal foil (Na@Na<sub>2</sub>Se/V) is illustrated in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202209511-fig-0001"><b>1</b>a</link>. The Na<sub>2</sub>Se/V interlayer (Figure <link href="#adma202209511-supitem-0001">S1</link>, Supporting Information) was constructed via a simple rolling of VSe<sub>2</sub> nanosheets (Figure <link href="#adma202209511-supitem-0001">S2</link>, Supporting Information) on the Na surface in Ar‐filled glovebox, and then a reaction of VSe<sub>2</sub> + 4Na → 2Na<sub>2</sub>Se + V was occurred spontaneously. Such protective interlayer demonstrates high mechanical strength to suppress volume‐change‐induced stress during the repeated Na plating/stripping, excellent sodiophilicity, and fast Na‐ion conductivity, realizing uniform high‐rate Na deposition.</p><figure xml:id="adma202209511-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202209511:adma202209511-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202209511-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) Schematic illustration of preparation of artificial heterogeneous Na<sub>2</sub>Se/V interphase and its advantages. b) XRD pattern of Na@Na<sub>2</sub>Se/V. c–e) Cryo‐TEM characterization of Na<sub>2</sub>Se/V interphase. c) Cryo‐TEM image and the relative elemental mapping of Na, Se, and V; d) SAED pattern; and e) Cryo‐HRTEM image. f) SEM image of Na@Na<sub>2</sub>Se/V electrode surface. g) Young's modulus of Na@Na<sub>2</sub>Se/V protective layer.</caption></figure><p>The X‐ray diffraction (XRD) pattern of the as‐prepared interlayer reveals the presence of Na<sub>2</sub>Se (JCPDS No. 77‐2155) and V (JCPDS No. 22‐1058) phases on the metallic Na surface (Figure <link href="#adma202209511-fig-0001">1b</link>). Additionally, the chemical states and composition of the artificial interphase were further validated by X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS). The high resolution of Se 3d spectrum can be deconvoluted into two peaks of Se 3d<sub>5/2</sub> (52.3 eV) and Se 3d<sub>3/2</sub> (53.2 eV) in Se<sup>2−</sup> (Figure <link href="#adma202209511-supitem-0001">S3</link>, Supporting Information).<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202209511-bib-0047 #adma202209511-bib-0048"><sup>47,48</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Moreover, an obvious peak located at 62.8 eV corresponds to the NaSe bond,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202209511-bib-0049 #adma202209511-bib-0050"><sup>49,50</sup></link><sup>]</sup> revealing the formation of Na<sub>2</sub>Se in the artificial interlayer. And the composition of artificial interphase was also investigated by cryogenic transmission microscope (Cryo‐TEM). It can be seen that the interphase exhibits particle character (Figure <link href="#adma202209511-supitem-0001">S4a</link>, Supporting Information) and Cryo‐TEM mapping images show the uniform distribution of Na, Se, and V elements (Figure <link href="#adma202209511-fig-0001">1c</link>), indicating the sufficient reactions between VSe<sub>2</sub> powder and Na. As illustrated by selected‐area electron diffraction (SAED, Figure <link href="#adma202209511-fig-0001">1d</link>) and high‐resolution Cryo‐TEM (Cryo‐HRTEM, Figure <link href="#adma202209511-fig-0001">1e</link> and Figure <link href="#adma202209511-supitem-0001">S4b,c</link> (Supporting Information)), the crystalline Na<sub>2</sub>Se and V can be observed in the artificial interphase. The above analysis reveals that the artificial interphase layer is comprised of Na<sub>2</sub>Se and V via spontaneous reaction between Na and VSe<sub>2</sub>.</p><p>The morphology characteristic of artificial Na<sub>2</sub>Se/V interphase layer was studied. The scanning electron microscopy (SEM) image shows that the artificial protective layer consists of tightly attached particles (Figure <link href="#adma202209511-fig-0001">1f</link>). Energy‐dispersive spectrometer (EDS) element mapping reveals that the Na, V, and Se elements are evenly distributed on the surface of Na substrate (Figure <link href="#adma202209511-supitem-0001">S5</link>, Supporting Information), indicating the homogeneous coating of the artificial Na<sub>2</sub>Se/V interphase layer. And the cross‐sectional SEM image and EDS element mapping present the thickness of the artificial interphase of about 40 µm (Figure <link href="#adma202209511-supitem-0001">S6</link>, Supporting Information). Importantly, the adhesion between the Na<sub>2</sub>Se/V layer and Na substate is very compact, ensuring the marvelous structure stability. Moreover, the mechanical strength of the protective Na<sub>2</sub>Se/V interphase layer was quantitatively examined by atomic force microscope. As shown in Figure <link href="#adma202209511-fig-0001">1g</link>, its Young's modulus is as high as 9.9 GPa, which is much higher than that of the previously reported pure Na (3.0 GPa),<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202209511-bib-0029"><sup>29</sup></link><sup>]</sup> demonstrating the robust mechanical property of the Na<sub>2</sub>Se/V layer. Moreover, the surface roughness of the artificial interphase is about 30 nm (Figure <link href="#adma202209511-supitem-0001">S7</link>, Supporting Information), indicating that the artificial interphase is relatively homogeneous. To better demonstrate the mechanical strength of artificial Na<sub>2</sub>Se/V interphase, the Young's modulus was further studied by the DFT calculations and nanoindentation testing (Figure <link href="#adma202209511-supitem-0001">S8</link>, Supporting Information). The theoretical Young's modulus of metal V and Na<sub>2</sub>Se is 104.42 and 38.22 GPa, respectively, which is significantly higher than that of metal Na (7.66 GPa). For the conventional SEI, the electron insulation is poor and Na‐ion diffusion is slow, making Na deposition occur inside the SEI, which can destroy the stability of SEI structure to form the cracks, and then induce nonuniform Na‐ion flux distribution and dendrite growth. By contrast, the artificial Na<sub>2</sub>Se/V hybrid SEI with controllable structure and component can achieve fast ion diffusion and high electron insulation (Figure <link href="#adma202209511-fig-0003">3b</link>), which are beneficial to Na‐ion nucleation and deposition at the SEI/Na interface, ensuring the structural stability of SEI with dendrite‐free morphology. To better elaborate the effect of artificial Na<sub>2</sub>Se/V hybrid interphase on suppressing dendrite growth, DFT theoretical calculation was conducted. As shown in Figures <link href="#adma202209511-supitem-0001">S16–S18</link> (Supporting Information), there are three stable sites (i.e., hollow, bridge, and top sites) for Na‐ion adsorption on the Na, Na<sub>2</sub>Se, and V (note that Na<sup>+</sup> at the top site of Na<sub>2</sub>Se is misconvergence, i.e., no adsorption site). The adsorption energy of Na ion on Na<sub>2</sub>Se is −0.118 eV (hollow site) and −0.580 eV (bridge site), which are obviously lower than that of bare Na (−1.100 eV (hollow site), −1.078 eV (bridge site), and −1.098 eV (top site)). The above results reveal that the adsorbed Na<sup>+</sup> on the metal V surface is more stable than that of bare Na and Na<sub>2</sub>Se surfaces, indicating that metal V has higher affinity toward Na<sup>+</sup>. Temperature‐dependent electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) was conducted to evaluate the activation energy of Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion through SEI. The high sodiophilicity of metal V and the electronical insulating effect of Na<sub>2</sub>Se can make quick Na<sup>+</sup> adsorption on the electrode surface without reduction. The benefits of such heterogeneous Na<sub>2</sub>Se/V interlayer in protecting the Na anode are demonstrated experimentally and theoretically. The sodiophilic V and superionic conductor of Na<sub>2</sub>Se can promote Na<sup>+</sup> adsorb and diffusion, achieving homogeneous Na deposition. Moreover, the high mechanical toughness of the hybrid interlayer can mitigate the volume fluctuations and guarantee excellent structural stability. As a result, exceptional electrochemical performances are achieved for the symmetric Na@Na<sub>2</sub>Se/V cells at ambient temperature, e.g., demonstrating a long life span over 1790 h with a low voltage hysteresis of 40 mV in carbonate electrolyte (0.5 mA cm<sup>−2</sup> and 1 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup>). Pushing into a new stride forward, the artificial hybrid interphase possesses fast desolvation ability, and the Na@Na<sub>2</sub>Se/V electrode also exhibits superior properties under harsh operating temperature of −40 °C, e.g., presenting remarkable cycling stability (over 1500 h) at 0.5 mA cm<sup>−2</sup> with 0.5 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup>. Additionally, the Na@Na<sub>2</sub>Se/V||NVP full cell can still run for 700 cycles at 0.5 C (−40 °C). The design strategy of artificial heterogeneous interphase in this work sheds a fresh insight into the development of SMBs with high power density and long life span under ambient temperature and −40 °C.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202209511-sec-0040" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>This work was supported by the National Natural Science
