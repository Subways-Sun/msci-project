5</caption></supportingInfoItem></supportingInformation><abstractGroup><abstract type="main" xml:lang="en"><title type="main">Abstract</title><p>It is a major challenge to achieve a high‐performance anode for sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) with high specific capacity, high rate capability, and cycling stability. Bismuth sulfide, which features a high theoretical specific capacity, tailorable morphology, and low cost, has been considered as a promising anode for SIBs. Nevertheless, due to a lack of direct atomistic observation, the detailed understanding of fundamental intercalation behavior and Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>'s (de)sodiation mechanisms remains unclear. Here, by employing in situ high‐resolution transmission electron microscopy, consecutive electron diffraction coupled with theoretical calculations, it is not only for the first time identified that Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> exhibits specific ionic transport pathways preferred to diffuse along the (110) direction instead of the (200) plane, but also tracks their real‐time phase transformations (de)sodiation involving multi‐step crystallographic tuning. The finite‐element analysis further disclosed multi‐reaction induced deformation and the relevant stress evolution originating from the combined effect of the mechanical and electrochemical interaction. These discoveries not only deepen the understanding of fundamental science about the microscopic reaction mechanism of metal chalcogenide anodes but also provide important implications for performance optimization.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="smtd202200995-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>The investigation on sodium ions diffusion behavior and storage mechanism in anodes is quite critical for the performance optimization of sodium‐ion batteries. Here, by employing in situ high‐resolution transmission electron microscopy and density functional theory calculations, it is not only for the first time identified that Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> exhibits specific ionic transport pathways, but also tracks their real‐time phase transformations (de)sodiation involving multi‐step crystallographic tuning down to atomic scale.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:23669608:media:smtd202200995:smtd202200995-gra-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smtd202200995-gra-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body xml:id="smtd202200995-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="smtd202200995-sec-0010"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>High‐performance rechargeable batteries, for instance, lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs), are in high demand due to the fast‐developing markets for portable electronic gadgets.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smtd202200995-bib-0001 #smtd202200995-bib-0002 #smtd202200995-bib-0003 #smtd202200995-bib-0004 #smtd202200995-bib-0005 #smtd202200995-bib-0006 #smtd202200995-bib-0007 #smtd202200995-bib-0008 #smtd202200995-bib-0009 #smtd202200995-bib-0010 #smtd202200995-bib-0011 #smtd202200995-bib-0012"><sup>1–12</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, LIBs will ultimately be constrained for emerging large‐scale applications, such as power grid storage and electric vehicles, due to the limited natural resources and geographically unequal distribution of lithium supplies. Owing to the vast natural abundance and low cost of sodium resources, sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) have piqued the scientific and industrial sectors’ interest in response to the growing demand for energy storage.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smtd202200995-bib-0013 #smtd202200995-bib-0014 #smtd202200995-bib-0015 #smtd202200995-bib-0016 #smtd202200995-bib-0017 #smtd202200995-bib-0018 #smtd202200995-bib-0019 #smtd202200995-bib-0020"><sup>13–20</sup></link><sup>]</sup> As sodium placed in the vicinity of lithium in the periodic table has physicochemical characteristics similar to lithium, SIBs and LIBs operate similarly. However, for reversible insertion/extraction of Na<sup>+</sup>, the traditional graphite anode is rather ineffective due to very sluggish Na<sup>+</sup>‐interaction kinetics.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smtd202200995-bib-0021 #smtd202200995-bib-0022"><sup>21,22</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The electrochemical capacity is substantially influenced by the intercalation diffusion behavior of Na<sup>+</sup> in electrode materials, which is primarily driven by the electrochemical interactions between Na<sup>+</sup> and electrode materials. As a result, research into Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion behavior in electrode materials is crucial for electrode material optimization that allows for rapid Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion while maintaining an unusually stable structure.</p><p>Carbons, metal phosphors, metal oxides, and metal sulfides, are currently under extensive investigation for SIBs anode materials.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smtd202200995-bib-0023 #smtd202200995-bib-0024 #smtd202200995-bib-0025 #smtd202200995-bib-0026 #smtd202200995-bib-0027"><sup>23–27</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Particularly, bismuth sulfide (Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>) is a compound semiconductor that belongs to the V‐VI group.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smtd202200995-bib-0028 #smtd202200995-bib-0029"><sup>28,29</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Its narrow band gap of 1.3–1.7 eV and weak metal–sulfur bonding resulted in long‐term storage ability of Na<sup>+</sup> via conversion followed by a de/alloy process, resulting in comparably high specific capacity.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smtd202200995-bib-0030 #smtd202200995-bib-0031 #smtd202200995-bib-0032"><sup>30–32</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Sun et al. first proved a high‐capacity of Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> for SIBs and found that the intercalation of Na<sup>+</sup> ions into Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> would generate an intermediate phase of NaBiS<sub>2</sub>, which was subsequently transformed into Bi and Na<sub>2</sub>S by through ex situ X‐ray diffraction (XRD).<sup>[</sup><link href="#smtd202200995-bib-0033"><sup>33</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Lu et al. clarified that the local microstructure of Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> directly generated the conversion phases Bi and Na<sub>2</sub>S, without the intermediate phase NaBiS<sub>2</sub>.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smtd202200995-bib-0034"><sup>34</sup></link><sup>]</sup> They also pointed out that Bi and Na ions undergo further alloying reaction to generate a Na<sub>3</sub>Bi phase. Obviously, the complete examination of dynamic electrochemical reaction mechanisms is still under debated. Furthermore, due to a lack of direct evidence, adequate understandings of lively microstructural evolution generated by Na<sup>+</sup> ionic diffusion and atomic re‐organization, critical for optimizing battery performance, remain rather vague, which is yet to be investigated.</p><p>To this end, in this work, the atomic‐scale origins of sodium ionic transport pathway, microstructure/phase evolution, and detailed reaction mechanism encountered in Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> during live sodiation–desodiation cycles are explicitly elucidated by combined in situ transmission electron microscopy (TEM), consecutive electron diffraction coupled with theoretical calculations, for the first time. The basic atomic structure of Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> is revealed using spherical aberration–corrected scanning TEM (Cs‐STEM). The in situ HRTEM images and DFT calculations demonstrated the existence of distinct ionic transport channels in Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> along the (110) direction faster than the (200) direction. In addition, the intercalation of Na<sup>+</sup> ions into Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> generates the NaBiS<sub>2</sub> phase; following that, continuing Na<sup>+</sup> intercalation triggered the conversion process, resulting in the formation of Na<sub>2</sub>S and Bi phases. Finally, Bi could alloy with Na<sup>+</sup> to produce the Na<sub>3</sub>Bi phase via successive intermediate NaBi phases. Modeling of reaction‐induced deformation by finite‐element analysis (FEA) clearly shows that the shape of the multiple‐phase reaction front and stress evolution is the combined effect of the mechanical and electrochemical interaction. It is anticipated that these discoveries will provide deep insights into inherent fundamental science about atomistic mechanism for Na‐ion storage and transport, phase transformation, and performance degradation of metal chalcogenide anodes, eventually providing significant implications for high‐performance SIBs.</p></section><section xml:id="smtd202200995-sec-0020"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p>The crystal structure of Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> was determined via X‐ray diffraction (XRD) analysis. <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smtd202200995-fig-0001"><b>1</b>a</link> shows the characteristic signals corresponding to orthorhombic Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>, which matched well with the standard data (JCPDS No. 75–1306, space group: <i>Pbnm</i>, <i>a </i>= 11.11 Å, <i>b</i> = 11.25 Å, <i>c</i> = 3.97 Å).<sup>[</sup><link href="#smtd202200995-bib-0034"><sup>34</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The chemical composition and electronic states of Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> were verified using X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) measurements. Our atomic‐scale observation for the first time demonstrated a highly anisotropic diffusion pathway for sodium ions transport in which sodium ions in Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> prefer to diffuse along the (110) direction instead of the (200) plane. The electrochemical sodiation processes were confirmed as three stages: i) first, the Na<sub>x</sub>Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> phase is formed as a result of the initial intercalation reaction, with the expansion of the original 2D crystal lattices; ii) second, the conversion reaction continues via the nucleation and growth of NaBi and Bi nanoparticles that remain coherent to the original structure; iii) finally when deep sodiation is performed, Bi further reacts with Na to form Na<sub>3</sub>Bi via an alloying reaction. During desodiation, the incomplete conversion of Na<sub>2</sub>S and Bi phases back to Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> was thought to induce initial capacity loss, despite the reversible dealloying of Na<sub>3</sub>Bi. Chemo‐mechanical simulations using FEA method further reveal the reaction‐induced deformation and the relevant stress evolution originating from the combined effect of the mechanical and electrochemical interaction. We believe this work would furnish valuable insights towards understanding the electrochemistry and ionic transport encountered in metal chalcogenide anodes and provide important implications for performance optimization.</p></section><section xml:id="smtd202200995-sec-0040"><title type="main">Experimental Section</title><p>The Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> sample was procured from Hangzhou Semiconductor materials. A Ga<sup>+</sup> focused ion beam (FIB, Helios G4 UC, Thermal Fisher) was used in conjunction with a lift‐off approach to create multilayer Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> nanorods. The Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> nanorods attached with Cu grid were served as the working electrode. An electrically grounded sharp tungsten probe was covered with sodium metal as the counter electrode. The components were together put onto a Pico Femto TEM specimen holder integrated with a positioning probe for electrical biasing. During the holder's transfer to the TEM chamber, the sodium metal was purposefully exposed to air (for &lt;10 s), thereby generating a thin passivation layer of NaO<sub>x</sub> on the surface, which behaved as a solid‐state electrolyte and allowed sodium ions to be transported. A minimal negative bias was applied between the Cu grid and the tungsten probe to begin the sodium insertion, whereas a small positive bias was used to start the sodium extraction.</p></section><section xml:id="smtd202200995-sec-0050" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>This work was supported by the National Key R&amp;D Program of China (Grant No. 2019YFB130970), the National Natural Science Foundation of China (Grant Nos. 12004034, U1813211, 52072323, 52122211, 11904372), the Beijing Natural Science Foundation (Grant No. Z210006), the General Research Fund of Hong Kong (Project No. 11217221), China Postdoctoral Science Foundation Funded Project (Grant No. 2021M690386).
