type="main" xml:lang="en"><title type="main">Abstract</title><p>MoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets as a promising 2D nanomaterial have extensive applications in energy storage and conversion, but their electrochemical performance is still unsatisfactory as an anode for efficient Li<sup>+</sup>/Na<sup>+</sup> storage. In this work, the design and synthesis of vertically grown MoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheet arrays, decorated with graphite carbon and Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> nanoparticles, on flexible carbon fiber cloth (denoted as Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>@C@MoS<sub>2</sub>/CFC) is reported. When evaluated as an anode for lithium‐ion batteries, the Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>@C@MoS<sub>2</sub>/CFC electrode manifests an outstanding electrochemical performance with a high discharge capacity of 1541.2 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> and a good capacity retention of 80.1% at 1.0 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 500 cycles. As for sodium‐ion batteries, it retains a high reversible capacity of 889.4 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.5 A g<sup>−1</sup> over 200 cycles. The superior electrochemical performance mainly results from the unique 3D ordered Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>@C@MoS<sub>2</sub> array‐type nanostructures and the synergistic effect between the C@MoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheet arrays and Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> nanoparticles. The Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> nanoparticles act as spacers to steady the structure, and the graphite carbon could be incorporated into MoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets to improve the conductivity of the whole electrode and strengthen the integration of MoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets and CFC by the adhesive role, together ensuring high conductivity and mechanical stability.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="smll201901083-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p><b>The design of vertically grown MoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheet arrays with expanded spacing of (002) plane, decorated with graphite carbon and Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> nanoparticles,</b> on flexible carbon fiber cloth is reported. Due to the unique 3D ordered Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>@C@MoS<sub>2</sub> array‐type nanostructures, these electrodes manifest an outstanding electrochemical performance for lithium‐ion and sodium‐ion batteries.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll201901083:smll201901083-gra-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll201901083-gra-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body xml:id="smll201901083-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="smll201901083-sec-0010"><label role="interim">1</label><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>With the increasing requirement of renewable energy, searching for sustainable and efficient energy‐storage systems is becoming a universal topic. In recent years, rechargeable batteries such as lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs) and sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) have been widely investigated as the promising candidates for electronic devices owing to high energy density, outstanding reversibility, and relatively low cost.<link href="#smll201901083-bib-0001 #smll201901083-bib-0002 #smll201901083-bib-0003 #smll201901083-bib-0004 #smll201901083-bib-0005 #smll201901083-bib-0006 #smll201901083-bib-0007 #smll201901083-bib-0008 #smll201901083-bib-0009 #smll201901083-bib-0010 #smll201901083-bib-0011"/> To advance their practical applications, exploration of suitable electrode materials with higher capacity, faster charging, and longer life plays an important role in improving the performance of the batteries.<link href="#smll201901083-bib-0002 #smll201901083-bib-0003 #smll201901083-bib-0004 #smll201901083-bib-0011 #smll201901083-bib-0012 #smll201901083-bib-0013 #smll201901083-bib-0014 #smll201901083-bib-0015 #smll201901083-bib-0016 #smll201901083-bib-0017"/></p><p>2D nanomaterials, like graphene, transition metal oxides (TMOs), and transition metal dichalcogenides (TMDs), have been widely investigated for energy storage and conversion devices due to their unique 2D structural characteristics, including the thin nanosheets and the wide interlayer spacing.<link href="#smll201901083-bib-0002 #smll201901083-bib-0003 #smll201901083-bib-0004 #smll201901083-bib-0006 #smll201901083-bib-0012"/> Among them, molybdenum disulfide (MoS<sub>2</sub>) with sandwich‐like structure (S–Mo–S) stacked together, as a representative member of 2D TMDs, has been paid extensive attention as promising anode material for lithium‐ion batteries and sodium‐ion batteries due to its high intercalation potential with Li<sup>+</sup>/Na<sup>+</sup> and high theoretical capacity (670 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>) compared with graphite.<link href="#smll201901083-bib-0013 #smll201901083-bib-0015"/> Especially, owing to the broad interlayered space (≈0.62 nm) of MoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets, the space contributes to the diffusion of electrolyte ions and insertion/extraction of lithium ions and sodium ions, enhancing the charge storage capabilities.<link href="#smll201901083-bib-0010 #smll201901083-bib-0013"/> However, there are still some challenges for the MoS<sub>2</sub> anode materials: a) In view of the large surface energy of MoS<sub>2</sub> layers, they are inclined to stack/restack together via van der Waals attraction, leading to the rapid capacity decay and drastic volume variation during the cycling. b) The low electronic/ionic conductivity limits efficient charge transport and thus results in the poor rate performance. c) Polysulfide dissolution produced in the charging–discharging arouses the loss of active substances.<link href="#smll201901083-bib-0013"/></p><p>To address aforementioned issues, some effective strategies can be adopted: a) expanding the interlayer distance of MoS<sub>2</sub> by intercalating different agents to facilitate the lithium and sodium intercalation/deintercalation, b) combing the MoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets with carbonaceous materials to improve the electrical conductivity,<link href="#smll201901083-bib-0003 #smll201901083-bib-0007 #smll201901083-bib-0010 #smll201901083-bib-0015"/> c) decorating the MoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheet surface with nanoparticles acting as spacers to stabilize the nanosheets,<link href="#smll201901083-bib-0012"/> and d) designing the 3D array‐type architectures on current collectors like carbon fiber cloth (CFC) and graphene foam to ensure high electrical conductivity and mechanical stability.<link href="#smll201901083-bib-0006"/></p><p>Herein, we report the design and synthesis of 3D Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> nanoparticles decorated vertical MoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheet arrays incorporating with carbon nanostructure grown on flexible carbon fiber cloth (denoted as Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>@C@MoS<sub>2</sub>/CFC) as a flexible and high‐performance anode for Li/Na‐ion batteries. This preparation procedure includes the vertical growth of MoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheet arrays with carbon nanostructure on the CFC (denoted as C@MoS<sub>2</sub>/CFC) by a solvothermal process and subsequent fabrication of Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> nanoparticles on C@MoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets via an easy self‐sacrificing template method. In such unique architecture design, first, Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> nanoparticles act as spacers to separate the MoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets to prevent the structural degradation and stabilize the structure during the electrochemical process. On the other hand, the MoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets can provide substrates for the growth of Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> nanoparticles, preventing the particle aggregation. Second, the addition of glucose can not only be reacted into carbon, improving conductivity of the whole electrode, but also promote the better growth of MoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets without structural deformation and further strengthen the integration of C@MoS<sub>2</sub> and CFC by the adhesive role. Moreover, the addition of the glucose expanded interlayer distance of MoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets, which facilitates the mobility of Li<sup>+</sup>/Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion. Third, the 3D array‐type architectures on the CFC can ensure high conductivity and mechanical stability. In summary, benefiting by the unique 3D ordered Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>@C@MoS<sub>2</sub> array‐type nanostructures and the synergistic effect between the C@MoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheet arrays and Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> nanoparticles, Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>@C@MoS<sub>2</sub>/CFC exhibits a high electrochemical performance as a novel flexible anode for LIBs and SIBs.</p></section><section xml:id="smll201901083-sec-0020"><label role="interim">2</label><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p>The fabrication process of the Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>@C@MoS<sub>2</sub>/CFC is illustrated in <b>Scheme</b> <link href="#smll201901083-fig-0007"><b>1</b></link>. First, C@MoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheet arrays were synthesized on the CFC by solvothermal and subsequent calcination process (C@MoS<sub>2</sub>/CFC). The MoS<sub>2</sub>/CFC was obtained by the similar procedure without the addition of glucose. The functions of glucose will be discussed in detail later. Subsequently, ZnO nanoparticles were loading on the C@MoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets as self‐sacrificing template by a solvothermal process (ZnO@C@MoS<sub>2</sub>/CFC). Finally, to achieve the Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>@C@MoS<sub>2</sub>/CFC, the ZnO@C@MoS<sub>2</sub>/CFC was immersed in an aqueous solution of the Fe<sup>3+</sup>, and further annealed in Ar. <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll201901083-fig-0001"><b>1</b></link>a–d reveals the scanning electron microscopy (SEM) images of MoS<sub>2</sub>/CFC, C@MoS<sub>2</sub>/CFC, ZnO@C@MoS<sub>2</sub>/CFC, and Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>@C@MoS<sub>2</sub>/CFC, respectively. The MoS<sub>2</sub>‐based nanosheet array‐type architectures retain well during the preparation of the Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>@C@MoS<sub>2</sub>/CFC. Additionally, the optical images show color changes of the formation of the Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>@C@MoS<sub>2</sub>/CFC, indicating good surface coverage levels of Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>@C@MoS<sub>2</sub> on the CFC (insets of Figure <link href="#smll201901083-fig-0001"/>). As shown in Figure S1a,b in the Supporting Information, the MoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets on the CFC are inclined to stacking and cracking. With the addition of glucose, this problem has been well solved (Figure S1c,d, Supporting Information). In comparison to MoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets, the structural formation of C@MoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheet becomes close‐packed but short‐length, which reduces structural deformation of the nanosheets and short height reduces the Li<sup>+</sup>/Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion resistance (Figure S2, Supporting Information). To obtain hierarchical structures of ultrasmall Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>‐encapsulated C@MoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheet, several methods, including dip‐coating (Figure S3, Supporting Information), hydrothermal growth (Figure S4, Supporting Information), and self‐sacrificing template, have been developed. In contrast, it is easier to achieve uniform loading Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> nanoparticles on C@MoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets through self‐sacrificing template. Self‐sacrificing template is a suitable and general methodology for the preparation of complex Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> hierarchical structures with well‐defined secondary microstructures, and it can ensure the close connection between the Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> particles and C@MoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets without destroying the nanosheets. Energy‐dispersive X‐ray (EDX) mapping further demonstrates the homogeneous distribution of the chemical compositions (Mo, S, C, O, and Fe) of the Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>@C@MoS<sub>2</sub>/CFC (Figure <link href="#smll201901083-fig-0001"/>d–j).</p><figure xml:id="smll201901083-fig-0007"><title type="figureName">Scheme</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll201901083:smll201901083-fig-0007" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll201901083-fig-0007-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Schematic illustration of the preparation of MoS<sub>2</sub>/CFC, C@MoS<sub>2</sub>/CFC, and Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>@C@MoS<sub>2</sub>/CFC.</caption></figure><figure xml:id="smll201901083-fig-0001" resumeNumberingAt="1"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll201901083:smll201901083-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll201901083-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>High‐magnification SEM images of a) MoS<sub>2</sub>/CFC, b) C@MoS<sub>2</sub>/CFC, c) ZnO@C@MoS<sub>2</sub>/CFC, and d) Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>@C@MoS<sub>2</sub>/CFC (insets: optical images). e) Low‐magnification SEM image of Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>@C@MoS<sub>2</sub>/CFC. f–j) EDS mapping images of Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>@C@MoS<sub>2</sub>/CFC: Mo (blue), S (yellow), C (red), O (light blue), and Fe (pink).</caption></figure><p>To further explore structure and crystallinity information on MoS<sub>2</sub>, C@MoS<sub>2</sub>, and Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>@C@MoS<sub>2</sub>, transmission electron microscopy (TEM) observation was performed. The low magnification TEM images (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll201901083-fig-0002"><b>2</b></link>a,c) show the structure of nanosheet array, and the C@MoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheet arrays are packed more closely than MoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheet arrays, which is consistent with the SEM observations. The inset of Figure <link href="#smll201901083-fig-0002"/>b shows the selected area electron diffraction (SAED) pattern of MoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets, which are indexed to (002), (110), (100), and (103) planes of 2H‐MoS<sub>2</sub> (JCPDS: 37‐1492). High‐resolution TEM (HRTEM) image (Figure <link href="#smll201901083-fig-0002"/>b) shows an interlayer spacing of 0.62–0.65 nm, which is a little larger than the MoS<sub>2</sub> crystal (0.612 nm).<link href="#smll201901083-bib-0018 #smll201901083-bib-0019"/> The expanded interlayer distance is due to the intercalation of solvent and Na<sup>+</sup> during the solvothermal process.<link href="#smll201901083-bib-0018"/> As shown in the inset of Figure <link href="#smll201901083-fig-0002"/>c, the interlayer spacing of the C@MoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets is larger than MoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets, which is primarily ascribed to the participation of glucose molecules into interlamination of MoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheet.<link href="#smll201901083-bib-0019"/> Elemental distribution of Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>@C@MoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets is detected by EDX elemental mapping analyses, which shows the well‐dispersed Mo, S, C, Fe, and O elements, and indicates the successful manufacture of Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>@C@MoS<sub>2</sub> nanostructure (Figure <link href="#smll201901083-fig-0002"/>d–i). Meanwhile, the HRTEM image (Figure <link href="#smll201901083-fig-0002"/>j) of the Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>@C@MoS<sub>2</sub>/CFC shows the existence of (006) plane of Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> and (100) plane of MoS<sub>2</sub>. The rings in SAED could be assigned to (006), (110), and (116) planes of the Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> phase, respectively, and the SAED pattern also demonstrates the (002), (100), and (110) planes of MoS<sub>2</sub> (Figure <link href="#smll201901083-fig-0002"/>k). The phase crystallinity and purity of the prepared samples were further demonstrated by X‐ray powder diffraction (XRD). As shown in Figure <link href="#smll201901083-fig-0002"/>l, two sets of diffraction peaks indexed to 2H‐MoS<sub>2</sub> (JCPDS No. 37‐1492) and α‐Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> (JCPDS No. 33‐0664), which were further identified by Raman spectroscopy of Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>@C@MoS<sub>2</sub>/CFC (Figure S5, Supporting Information). Those results are consistent with the TEM results. The MoS<sub>2</sub> (002) peak of MoS<sub>2</sub>/CFC and Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>@MoS<sub>2</sub>/CFC appears at 13.8° and is calculated with a lattice spacing (d) of 6.42Å by Bragg equation, while this peak of C@MoS<sub>2</sub>/CFC and Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>@C@MoS<sub>2</sub>/CFC shifts to 12.28° with an increased interlayer spacing (0.72 nm), which is in good agreement with the HRTEM observations. By studying the CV curves of the Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>@C@MoS<sub>2</sub>/CFC and MoS<sub>2</sub>/CFC electrodes (Figure S6b, Supporting Information), it is worth noting that the former shows a higher integral area than the latter, indicating that the Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>@C@MoS<sub>2</sub>/CFC electrode should have a better lithium‐ion storage with a larger specific capacity. capacity, superior rate performance, and excellent long‐term cycling stability, can be attributed to the unique 3D ordered Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>@C@MoS<sub>2</sub> array‐type nanostructures and the synergistic effect between the C@MoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheet arrays and Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> nanoparticles. Furthermore, the interface effects of the four components including C, MoS<sub>2</sub>, Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>, and carbon fibers play an important role in the electrochemical performances of the anode. In detail, the interface effects are mainly including the interfaces between Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> nanoparticles and C@MoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets, C and MoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets, and C@MoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets and carbon fiber cloth. As shown in Figure <link href="#smll201901083-fig-0006"/>g, the superior performance may be attributed to the following factors: a) The resultant Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> nanoparticles with ultrasmall sizes can provide many active sites and act as spacers to hinder the restacking of C@MoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets during the charge–discharge process. b) The large interlayer spacing (0.72 nm) and vertically distributed C@MoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets promote the intercalation and shorten the diffusion paths of lithium and sodium ions. Moreover, these nanosheet arrays also furnish substrates with Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> nanoparticles, preventing the agglomeration of Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>. c) The addition of glucose can not only be reacted into graphite C, improving conductivity of the whole electrode, but also promote the better growth of MoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets without structural deformation and further strengthen the integration of C@MoS<sub>2</sub> and CFC by the adhesive role.<link href="#smll201901083-bib-0019 #smll201901083-bib-0044"/> d) The vertically distributed 3D array‐type hierarchical architectures can provide large interface area about electrolyte/electrode, which accelerate Li<sup>+</sup>/Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion and accommodate the volume expansion during cycling. Meanwhile, the 3D ordered nanostructures and their close connection to the CFC can enable good electrical contact between the collector and active material, facilitating fast electron transmission and enhancing the structure integrity, and all these distinct advantages positively play a key role in endowing the Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>@C@MoS<sub>2</sub>/CFC electrode with the superior electrochemical performance.</p></section><section xml:id="smll201901083-sec-0030"><label role="interim">3</label><title type="main">Conclusions</title><p>In summary, we have synthesized 3D Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> nanoparticles decorated MoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheet arrays, incorporating with carbon nanostructure grown on flexible carbon fiber cloth, mainly through a facile solvothermal and self‐sacrificing template method. The as‐prepared Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>@C@MoS<sub>2</sub>/CFC electrode was explored as an anode for LIBs and SIBs, exhibiting excellent storage properties, including high capacity, good rate capability, and wonderful cycling stability. For LIBs, the Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>@C@MoS<sub>2</sub>/CFC electrode delivers 1541.2 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> and capacity retention of 80.1% after 500 cycles at 1.0 A g<sup>−1</sup>. As for SIBs, it retains a high reversible capacity of 889.4 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.5 A g<sup>−1</sup> over 200 cycles. The significantly improved performance is mainly the synergistic effect between the C@MoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheet arrays and Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> nanoparticles and the 3D array‐type architectures on the CFC to ensure high conductivity and mechanical stability. Based on our results, the good electrochemical behavior of the Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>@C@MoS<sub>2</sub>/CFC opens new opportunities for designing of 2D MoS<sub>2</sub>‐based anode materials for high‐performance Li‐ and Na‐ion batteries.</p></section><section xml:id="smll201901083-sec-0040"><label role="interim">4</label><title type="main">Experimental Section</title><p><i>Material Preparation—Synthesis of MoS<sub>2</sub>/CFC</i>: The MoS<sub>2</sub>/CFC was synthesized by a hydrothermal method.<link href="#smll201901083-bib-0018"/> In a typical process, first, CFC (WOS1002 from CeTech) was cleaned by deionized water and ethanol, and annealed at 450 °C for 1 h in air. Na<sub>2</sub>MoO<sub>4</sub>·2H<sub>2</sub>O (0.3056 g), <sc>l</sc>‐cysteine (0.4889 g), H<sub>2</sub>O (22 mL), and DMF (33 mL) were mixed under stirring for 30 min. The mixture was added into a 100 mL Teflon‐lined stainless steel autoclave with a piece of the annealed CFC immersed into the reaction solution. The autoclave was sealed and maintained at 200 °C for 12 h. After the reaction, the resulting carbon fiber cloth coated with a black product was washed by deionized water and ethanol, and then dried at 60 °C for 24 h. Finally, the MoS<sub>2</sub>/CFC electrode was obtained by calcination at 700 °C for 2 h in Ar. The loading mass of the active materials was about 1.0 mg cm<sup>−2</sup>.</p><p><i>Material Preparation—Synthesis of Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>@C@MoS<sub>2</sub>/CFC</i>: First, C@MoS<sub>2</sub>/CFC was prepared which included a hydrothermal method and subsequent heat treatment. Specifically, in the hydrothermal process, except for adding the glucose (0.12 g), which was under the same conditions with MoS<sub>2</sub>/CFC, the hydrothermal product was then annealed at 550 °C for 5 h in Ar with heating rate of 4 °C min<sup>−1</sup> to synthesize C@MoS<sub>2</sub>/CFC. The loading mass of the C@MoS<sub>2</sub>/CFC was about 1.2 mg cm<sup>−2</sup>. After obtaining the C@MoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheet arrays, Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> nanoparticles on the C@MoS<sub>2</sub>/CFC were prepared according to self‐sacrificing. At first, ZnO nanoparticles were obtained on C@MoS<sub>2</sub>/CFC by a wet chemical process as sacrificial template.<link href="#smll201901083-bib-0045"/> Subsequently, the obtained ZnO‐coated arrays (ZnO@C@MoS<sub>2</sub>/CFC) were soaked in an aqueous solution of Fe(NO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>3</sub>·9H<sub>2</sub>O (0.013 <sc>m</sc>) for 24 h at room temperature. After soaking, the sample was rinsed by distilled water, dried in air, and treated at 550 °C in Ar atmosphere for 5 h to obtain the Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>@C@MoS<sub>2</sub>/CFC. The loading mass of the Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>@C@MoS<sub>2</sub>/CFC was about 1.5 mg cm<sup>−2</sup>. The Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>@MoS<sub>2</sub>/CFC electrode was also obtained by the same procedure only based on the MoS<sub>2</sub>/CFC electrode and the loading mass of the active materials was about 1.3 mg cm<sup>−2</sup>.</p><p><i>Characterization</i>: X‐ray diffraction patterns were recorded on a Rigaku 18 KW D/max‐2550 with a Cu Kα radiation. Field emission scanning electron microscopy (FESEM) images were collected using a JEOL JEM‐2100F at an acceleration voltage of 200 kV. Energy dispersive spectroscopy (EDS) was recorded by using Oxford EDS IE250. TEM and HRTEM images were recorded using a JEOL JEM‐2100F STEM/EDS microscope. XPS analysis was carried out on a Kratos Axis Ultra DLD X‐ray photoelectron spectrometer. Raman‐shift spectra were recorded on a Renishaw in plus laser Raman spectrometer with an excitation wavelength of 633 nm.</p><p><i>Electrochemical Measurements</i>: The Li/Na‐storage performance of anode materials was evaluated in a half cell configuration using Li/Na metals as the counter electrode and Celgard 2400/glass microfiber (Whatman) as the separator. CR2032‐type coin cells were assembled in an Ar‐filled glove box with the electrodes. The LIB electrolyte was 1 <sc>m</sc> LiPF<sub>6</sub>/EC (ethylene carbonate) and DMC (dimethyl carbonate) (1:1 v/v). The NIB electrolyte was 1 <sc>m</sc> NaClO<sub>4</sub> in PC (propylene carbonate) and EC with 5 wt% of FEC (fluoroethylene carbonate). Galvanostatic charge–discharge measurements were performed using a multichannel battery testing system (LAND CT2001A). Cyclic voltammetry and electrochemical impedance spectra (EIS) in the frequency range of 100 kHz to 0.01 Hz were performed using an Autolab CHI660E. All the measurements were carried out at room temperature.</p></section><section xml:id="smll201901083-sec-0050" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>J.Z., K.W., and X.Y. contributed equally to this work.
