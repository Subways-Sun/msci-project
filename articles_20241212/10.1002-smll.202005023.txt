Information</caption></supportingInfoItem></supportingInformation><abstractGroup><abstract type="main" xml:lang="en"><title type="main">Abstract</title><p>Conversion‐alloying type anode materials like metal sulfides draw great attention due to their considerable theoretical capacity for sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) and potassium‐ion batteries (PIBs). However, poor conductivity, severe volume change, and harmful aggregation of the material during charge/discharge lead to unsatisfying electrochemical performance. Herein, a facile and green strategy for yolk–shell structure based on the principle of metal evaporation is proposed. SnS<sub>2</sub> nanoparticle is encapsulated in nitrogen‐doped hollow carbon nanobox (SnS<sub>2</sub>@C). The carbon nanoboxes accommodate the volume change and aggregation of SnS<sub>2</sub> during cycling, and form 3D continuous conductive carbon matrix by close contact. The well‐designed structure benefits greatly in conductivity and structural stability of the material. As expected, SnS<sub>2</sub>@C exhibits considerable capacity, superior cycling stability, and excellent rate capability in both SIBs and PIBs. Additionally, in situ Raman technology is unprecedentedly conducted to investigate the phase evolution of polysulfides. This work provides an avenue for facilely constructing stable and high‐capacity metal dichalcogenide based anodes materials with optimized structure engineering. The proposed in‐depth electrochemical measurements coupled with in situ and ex situ characterizations will provide fundamental understandings for the storage mechanism of metal dichalcogenides.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="smll202005023-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>The successful structural engineering of SnS<sub>2</sub>@C gives effective accommodation to the volume change of SnS<sub>2</sub> during charge/discharge as well as the enhancement of the conductivity. xml:id="smll202005023-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="smll202005023-sec-0010"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>The ever‐increasing demand for lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs) is confronted with the challenges of the low abundance and uneven distribution of lithium resource.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202005023-bib-0001 #smll202005023-bib-0002 #smll202005023-bib-0003"><sup>1–3</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Currently, sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) and potassium‐ion batteries (PIBs) have been considered as promising alternatives to LIBs due to their similar electrochemical properties, abundant reserves, low cost, and applicable of cost‐effective Al current collector.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202005023-bib-0004 #smll202005023-bib-0005 #smll202005023-bib-0006 #smll202005023-bib-0007 #smll202005023-bib-0008 #smll202005023-bib-0009"><sup>4–9</sup></link><sup>]</sup> For applications, the major challenge is to develop high‐performance electrode materials.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202005023-bib-0010"><sup>10</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Up to now, various electrode categories like carbon, metal/alloy, and metal oxide/sulfide have been investigated as anode materials for SIBs and PIBs.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202005023-bib-0011 #smll202005023-bib-0012 #smll202005023-bib-0013 #smll202005023-bib-0014 #smll202005023-bib-0015"><sup>11–15</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Among them, tin‐based compounds exhibit promising prospect, their high theoretical capacity is encouraging researchers to explore further.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202005023-bib-0016 #smll202005023-bib-0017 #smll202005023-bib-0018 #smll202005023-bib-0019 #smll202005023-bib-0020 #smll202005023-bib-0021"><sup>16–21</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>Tin disulfide (SnS<sub>2</sub>) is layered metal sulfide with CdI<sub>2</sub>‐type crystal structure.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202005023-bib-0006 #smll202005023-bib-0022"><sup>6,22</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The c axially stacked triple layers (consist of one layer of tin cations sandwiched between two hexagonally‐close‐packed sulfur anions) possess an interlayer space of ≈0.59 nm, which is higher than the widely reported graphite anode materials (≈0.34 nm) and can facilitate fast ionic diffusion.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202005023-bib-0023"><sup>23</sup></link><sup>]</sup> For SIBs, it is demonstrated that SnS<sub>2</sub> experiences a combination of sequential conversion and alloying reacts delivering a high theoretical capacity of 1136 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202005023-bib-0010 #smll202005023-bib-0024 #smll202005023-bib-0025 #smll202005023-bib-0026"><sup>10,24–26</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Encouraged by the high specific capacity in SIBs, SnS<sub>2</sub>‐based anode is expected to achieve a satisfying utilization in PIBs as well.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202005023-bib-0027"><sup>27</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, SnS<sub>2</sub>‐based electrode suffers insufficient play of its capacity which arises from the inherently low conductivity and inevitable pulverization caused by the volume expansion.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202005023-bib-0028"><sup>28</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In view of this, researchers combined SnS<sub>2</sub> with carbonaceous materials to release the volume expansion and enhance the conductivity.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202005023-bib-0023 #smll202005023-bib-0027 #smll202005023-bib-0029 #smll202005023-bib-0030"><sup>23,27,29,30</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Despite great efforts made by researchers, SnS<sub>2</sub>/C based electrodes suffer inevitable pulverization in SIBs owing to the larger radius of Na<sup>+</sup> (1.02 Å, 34% larger than Li<sup>+</sup>, 0.76 Å).<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202005023-bib-0023 #smll202005023-bib-0031 #smll202005023-bib-0032"><sup>23,31,32</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Similarly, SnS<sub>2</sub>‐based materials face more serious challenges of sluggish transfer of K<sup>+</sup> (radius of 1.38 Å, 81% larger than Li<sup>+</sup>) and severe volume change during the cycling process.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202005023-bib-0033 #smll202005023-bib-0034"><sup>33,34</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>Designing nanostructures with interior void space has become an effective strategy for confining electrode materials with high volume change like Si, Sn, P, and Sb, etc.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202005023-bib-0035 #smll202005023-bib-0036 #smll202005023-bib-0037 #smll202005023-bib-0038 #smll202005023-bib-0039 #smll202005023-bib-0040 #smll202005023-bib-0041"><sup>35–41</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, to the best of our knowledge, for modification of SnS<sub>2</sub>‐based materials, very few researches of SnS<sub>2</sub>@void@C are reported. The existing few works suffer tedious preparation process, requiring caustic NaOH and hyper toxic H<sub>2</sub>S directly or indirectly, which is harmful considering the possible application.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202005023-bib-0028 #smll202005023-bib-0042 #smll202005023-bib-0043"><sup>28,42,43</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>Herein, a green, cost‐effective, and facile fabrication strategy of SnS<sub>2</sub>@void@N‐doped carbon (SnS<sub>2</sub>@C) is proposed. SnS<sub>2</sub> nanoparticles are encapsulated into N‐doped carbon nanoboxes. The unique structure can effectively accommodate the volume change of SnS<sub>2</sub> during charge/discharge and benefit the conductivity as well. Owing to the positive effect of the nitrogen doped carbon shell with interior void space, the as‐prepared SnS<sub>2</sub>@C anode exhibits excellent cycling stability and superior rate capability, surpassing the composite by mixing SnS<sub>2</sub> and carbon (SnS<sub>2</sub>/C) dramatically. A high reversible capacity of 660 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> is achieved at 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup> in SIBs, with a remarkable retention of 88.6% after 100 cycles (100th versus 4th). Quantitative analyses were performed to investigate the Na<sup>+</sup> storage behavior, attributing the remarkable performance to the domination of capacitive behavior. As for the application in PIBs, the SnS<sub>2</sub>@C anode exhibits distinctly better cycling stability (72% vs 15%) and rate capability than SnS<sub>2</sub>/C and other reported SnS<sub>2</sub>‐based materials. In situ Raman spectra were unprecedentedly conducted to investigate the transition mechanism of sodium/potassium polysulfide during discharge/charge, along with the ex situ X‐ray diffraction (XRD) characterizations, offering insightful illustrations to the Na<sup>+</sup>/K<sup>+</sup> storage mechanism from a unique perspective. This work demonstrates a facile strategy for designing SnS<sub>2</sub>@void@C material, providing an avenue to modify metal dichalcogenide to applicable anode material, with effectively accommodated volume change and extraordinarily enhanced electrochemical performance.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202005023-sec-0020"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><section xml:id="smll202005023-sec-0030"><title type="main">Morphology and Structure Characterization</title><p><b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll202005023-fig-0001"><b>1</b></link> typically illustrates the fabrication of SnS<sub>2</sub>@C. First, the synthesized ZnSn(OH)<sub>6</sub> is uniformly coated by polydopamine (PDA) first. Afterwards, the ZnSn(OH)<sub>6</sub>@PDA is calcinated (600 °C, Ar/H<sub>2</sub> atmosphere), during which the reduced Zn evaporates and the PDA is carbonized as carbon shell. After sulfurization of the as‐prepared Sn@C, the final product is obtained. As comparison, SnS<sub>2</sub>/C composite is synthesized by mixing sulfurized Sn particles and acid etched Sn@C (morphologies, Figure S1a,b, Supporting Information).</p><figure xml:id="smll202005023-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll202005023:smll202005023-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll202005023-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Schematic illustration of the fabrication process of SnS<sub>2</sub>@C.</caption></figure><p>The morphologies and microstructures were observed by field‐emission scanning electron microscope (FESEM) and transmission electron microscope (TEM). The synthesized ZnSn(OH)<sub>6</sub> exhibits nanocubic morphology with an average particle size of ≈100 nm (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll202005023-fig-0002"><b>2</b>a</link>; Figure S2a, Supporting Information). The dispersed nanocubes are uniformly coated by PDA shell (Figure <link href="#smll202005023-fig-0002">2b</link>; Figure S2b, Supporting Information), of which the thickness can be easily controlled by tuning the amount of dopamine hydrochloride. During the calcination process, PDA is carbonized and the void is created due to the escape of the elements (O, H, and Zn). The core of the yolk–shell structure tends to be spherical owing to the surface tension of the liquid Sn (Figure <link href="#smll202005023-fig-0002">2c</link>,<link href="#smll202005023-fig-0002">e</link>).<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202005023-bib-0044 #smll202005023-bib-0045"><sup>44,45</sup></link><sup>]</sup> After sulfurization, the core presents a degree of growth (Figure <link href="#smll202005023-fig-0002">2d</link>,<link href="#smll202005023-fig-0002">f</link>), which is ascribed to the formation of SnS<sub>2</sub> (Sn + 2S → SnS<sub>2</sub>). The designed void space is capable of accommodating the volume change and aggregation of SnS<sub>2</sub> nanoparticles during charge/discharge, consequently preventing the pulverization of the electrode and thereby benefiting the cycling stability. Besides, the carbon shell provides well contact area and conductivity between super P and active materials, fully exerting the capacity of the SnS<sub>2</sub> nanoparticles. Figure <link href="#smll202005023-fig-0002">2g</link> exhibits the image of high‐resolution TEM (HRTEM). The lattice fringes, of which the interlayer distance is determined to be 0.59 nm on average, are clearly observed (inset of Figure <link href="#smll202005023-fig-0002">2g</link>), corresponding to the (001) plane of SnS<sub>2</sub>.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202005023-bib-0046"><sup>46</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Additionally, elected‐area electron diffraction (SAED) was employed, proving the polycrystalline feature of SnS<sub>2</sub> (Figure <link href="#smll202005023-fig-0002">2h</link>). Furthermore, elemental mapping was performed to investigate the distribution of C, Sn, and S (Figure <link href="#smll202005023-fig-0002">2i</link>), clearly demonstrating the yolk–shell structure of SnS<sub>2</sub>@C.</p><figure xml:id="smll202005023-fig-0002"><label>2</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll202005023:smll202005023-fig-0002" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll202005023-fig-0002-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>SEM images of a) ZnSn(OH)<sub>6</sub>, b) ZnSn(OH)<sub>6</sub>@PDA, c) Sn@C, d) SnS<sub>2</sub>@C. TEM images of e) Sn@C, f) SnS<sub>2</sub>@C. g) HRTEM images of SnS<sub>2</sub>@C and lattice fringes of SnS<sub>2</sub>. h) SAED pattern of SnS<sub>2</sub>@C. i) Elemental mapping of SnS<sub>2</sub>@C (C, S, and Sn).</caption></figure><p>XRD analyses were performed to track the phase transformation of the materials throughout. As shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll202005023-fig-0003"><b>3</b>a</link>, the synthesized ZnSn(OH)<sub>6</sub>, Sn@C, and SnS<sub>2</sub>@C fit well with PDF#20‐1455, PDF#04‐0673, and PDF#23‐0677, respectively, indicating the complete phase transformation during each synthesis step. Raman spectra (Figure <link href="#smll202005023-fig-0003">3b</link>) present bands at ≈310 cm<sup>−1</sup> (A<sub>1g</sub> vibration of SnS<sub>2</sub>), ≈1355 cm<sup>−1</sup> (disorder/defect‐induced D band), 1590 cm<sup>−1</sup> (in‐plane vibrational G band), confirming the existences of SnS<sub>2</sub> and carbon in both SnS<sub>2</sub>@C and SnS<sub>2</sub>/C.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202005023-bib-0047 #smll202005023-bib-0048 #smll202005023-bib-0049 #smll202005023-bib-0050"><sup>47–50</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Considering the possible existence of N (inherited from the PDA) and S (incorporated into the carbon shell during sulfurization) elements, it is necessary to conduct X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) to investigate the valence state of the elements. The survey spectrum (Figure <link href="#smll202005023-fig-0003">3c</link>) confirms the existence of C, N, O, S, and Sn. High‐resolution XPS spectra are depicted in Figure <link href="#smll202005023-fig-0003">3d</link> (N 1s and S 2p), Figures S3a (C 1s) and S3b (Sn 3d) (Supporting Information). As is shown, the spectrum of N 1s is deconvoluted into three peaks at ≈400.1 eV (N‐5, Pyrrolic‐N), ≈398.3 eV (N‐6, Pyrindinic‐6), and ≈401.5 eV (N‐Q, Graphitic‐N), respectively.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202005023-bib-0051 #smll202005023-bib-0052 #smll202005023-bib-0053"><sup>51–53</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In S 2p spectrum, three deconvoluted peaks located at 161.5, 163.1, and 164.5 eV, corresponding to S 2p<sub>3/2</sub>, S 2p<sub>1/2</sub>, and C–S bonds, respectively.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202005023-bib-0054 #smll202005023-bib-0055"><sup>54,55</sup></link><sup>]</sup> This is positive because strong coupling effect will occur at the heterointerface due to the heteroatoms doping induced electron cloud bias, thereby effectively accelerating the electron transport and offering more active sites for Na<sup>+</sup>/K<sup>+</sup> storage.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202005023-bib-0056"><sup>56</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In view of the successfully doped N into the carbon shell, the material is expected to exhibit an enhanced ionic diffusion rate, which will benefit the rate capability.</p><figure xml:id="smll202005023-fig-0003"><label>3</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll202005023:smll202005023-fig-0003" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll202005023-fig-0003-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) XRD patterns of ZnSn(OH)<sub>6</sub>@PDA, Sn@C, and SnS<sub>2</sub>@C. b) Raman spectra of SnS<sub>2</sub>@C and SnS<sub>2</sub>/C. c) XPS survey spectrum of SnS<sub>2</sub>@C. d) High‐resolution N 1s and S 2p spectra of SnS<sub>2</sub>@C. e) TGA curves of SnS<sub>2</sub>@C and SnS<sub>2</sub>/C from 35 to 800 °C (air atmosphere). f) N<sub>2</sub> adsorption/desorption isotherm of SnS<sub>2</sub>@C with inset of the related pore width distribution.</caption></figure><p>Thermogravimetric analyses (TGA) were conducted to determine the mass ratio of SnS<sub>2</sub> in SnS<sub>2</sub>@C and SnS<sub>2</sub>/C. xml:id="smll202005023-sec-0080"><title type="main">Conclusion</title><p>In summary, the facile and green strategy for yolk–shell structure engineering benefits the electrochemical performance of SnS<sub>2</sub>@C overall. The unique structure of the carbon nanobox accommodates the huge volume change and prohibits the severe aggregation of SnS<sub>2</sub> nanoparticles during charge/discharge, benefiting the structural stability and thereby achieving superior cycling stability. Meanwhile, the close‐contact nanoboxes form 3D continuous conductive carbon matrix, along with the positive effect of nitrogen doping, enhancing the fast electron/ionic transportation. The SnS<sub>2</sub>@C electrode exhibits considerable capacity, superior cycling stability, and excellent rate capability in both SIBs and PIBs. The expectably good electrochemical performance of SnS<sub>2</sub>@C electrode is attributed to the high capacity contribution of surface‐induced capacitive behavior, the enhanced conductivity, the structural accommodation for the volume change, and the prohibited aggregation of SnS<sub>2</sub> nanoparticles. In situ Raman technology is unprecedently introduced to investigate the phase evolution of polysulfides in SIBs and PIBs, shedding great light on the mechanism understanding of Na<sup>+</sup>/K<sup>+</sup> storage in SIBs and PIBs. Meaningfully, this work provides an avenue to modify metal dichalcogenide anode materials with high capacity but severe volume change, boosting the application of high‐performance anode materials.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202005023-sec-0090"><title type="main">Experimental Section</title><section xml:id="smll202005023-sec-0100" numbered="no"><section xml:id="smll202005023-sec-0110" numbered="no"><section xml:id="smll202005023-sec-0120" numbered="no"><title type="main">Synthesis of SnS<sub>2</sub>@C</title><p>ZnSn(OH)<sub>6</sub> nanocubes were synthesized via a precipitation method.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202005023-bib-0074"><sup>74</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Typically, 10 mmol ZnSO<sub>4</sub>·7H<sub>2</sub>O and 10 mmol Na<sub>2</sub>SnO<sub>3</sub>·3H<sub>2</sub>O were respectively dissolved in two beakers containing deionized water (50 mL for each) under stirring. After ZnSO<sub>4</sub> was completely dissolved, Na<sub>2</sub>SnO<sub>3</sub> solution was poured into the ZnSO<sub>4</sub> solution under stirring for 5 h. The acquired suspension was centrifuged, washed by deionized water and ethanol. The collected white ZnSn(OH)<sub>6</sub> product was dried at 100 °C for 10 h. Afterwards, 100 mg ZnSn(OH)<sub>6</sub> was weighed and ultrasonically dispersed in a prepared Tris‐buffer (9 mmol C<sub>4</sub>H<sub>11</sub>NO<sub>3</sub>, 50 mL deionized water, and 50 mL ethanol). 100 mg dopamine hydrochloride was added into the dispersed suspension, followed by stirring for 24 h to obtain ZnSn(OH)<sub>6</sub>@PDA. With a ramping rate of 5 °C min<sup>−1</sup>, ZnSn(OH)<sub>6</sub>@PDA was pyrolyzed at 600 °C for 8 h to evaporate Zn and carbonize PDA (atmosphere: mixture of 5% H<sub>2</sub> and 95% Ar). The obtained black Sn@C powder and sulfur were placed in two quartz boats separately (mass ratio: Sn@C : sulfur = 1:5), and transferred into a tube furnace for sulfurization process (conditions: N<sub>2</sub> atmosphere, 5 °C min<sup>−1</sup>, 400 °C for 3 h). The final product is obtained and labeled as SnS<sub>2</sub>@C.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202005023-sec-0130" numbered="no"><title type="main">Synthesis of SnS<sub>2</sub>/C</title><p>Sn@C was etched by hydrochloric acid (36 wt%) and collected to obtain carbon. SnS<sub>2</sub> particles were fabricated by commercial Sn and sulfur using the same procedure as SnS<sub>2</sub>@C fabrication. The as‐prepared SnS<sub>2</sub> was mixed with the obtained carbon by grinding, the as‐obtained composite is denoted as SnS<sub>2</sub>/C.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202005023-sec-0140" numbered="no"><title type="main">Characterization</title><p>For morphology and structure characterization, a JEOL JSM‐7610F field emission scanning electron microscopy (FESEM) and a JEOL JEM‐2100 TEM were used. A Rigaku Miniflex 600 X‐ray diffractometer (40 kV, Cu Kα radiation, λ = 1.5406 Å) is used for XRD and a Renishaw Invia Reflex for Raman spectra (532 nm). XPS characterization was conducted on an ESCALAB‐250 spectrometer (Al Kα X‐ray source, excitation energy = 1486.71 eV). The N<sub>2</sub> adsorption/desorption isotherms for specific surface area (SSA) and pore width distributions were acquired on an ASAP‐2020 analyzer. A Mettler‐Toledo TGA2 Thermo Analyzer was applied for the thermogravimetric analyses (air atmosphere, 800 °C, 5 °C min<sup>−1</sup>, 20 mL min<sup>−1</sup>). For ex situ characterization, the coin cells were disassembled in an Ar‐filled glove box. Afterwards, the obtained electrodes were rinsed in DEC solvent to dissolve the residual electrolyte. The electrodes were vacuum dried at 60 °C for 8 h before the ex situ characterization. For in situ Raman characterization, the modularized cell for in situ Raman spectra was assembled in an Ar‐filled glovebox (H<sub>2</sub>O &lt; 1 ppm, and O<sub>2</sub> &lt; 1 ppm). Afterwards, the modularized cell was placed on the Raman spectrometer and connected with an electrochemical workstation in GCD mode (Autolab, Metrohm). The acquisition interval for the spectra was 20 min for fear of overheating. Image of the in situ Raman characterization platform is presented in Figure S8 in the Supporting Information.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202005023-sec-0150" numbered="no"><title type="main">Electrochemical Measurements</title><p>The electrodes were fabricated by mixing the as‐prepared active materials (SnS<sub>2</sub>@C and SnS<sub>2</sub>/C, 80 wt%), super P (10 wt%), and sodium carboxymethyl cellulose (CMC, 10 wt%). After dissolving the mixture in deionized water and magnetic stirring, the well dispersed slurry was coated onto copper foil, and then dried in a vacuum oven at 90 °C for 12 h. The completely dried electrodes were punched into circles (Φ14 mm). The mass loading of the electrode is approximate 4 mg cm<sup>−2</sup>. The coin cells (CR‐2032) were assembled with sodium metal and potassium metal as counter electrodes in a glove box (Ar‐filled, H<sub>2</sub>O &lt; 1 ppm, and O<sub>2</sub> &lt; 1 ppm). As for the electrolyte, 1 <sc>m</sc> NaClO<sub>4</sub> was dissolved in ethylene carbonate/propylene carbonate (EC/PC v/v = 1:1 with 5 wt% fluorinated ethylene carbonate as additive) for SIBs and 0.8 <sc>m</sc> KPF<sub>6</sub> in ethylene carbonate/diethyl carbonate (EC/DEC v/v = 1:1) for PIBs. The electrolyte for each cell is about 150 µL. Whatman GF/D glass fiber membranes were applied as separators. The charge/discharge performance was tested on a LAND‐CT2001A within 0.01–2.5 V. The CV measurements were conducted at various sweep rates from 0.1 to 1.5 mV s<sup>−1</sup> on an electrochemical workstation (Autolab, Metrohm).</p></section></section></section></section><section xml:id="smll202005023-sec-0160" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>This work was supported by High‐level Talents’ Discipline Construction Fund of Shandong University (31370089963078), Shandong Provincial Science and Technology Major Project (2016GGX104001, 2017CXGC1010, and 2018JMRH0211), the Fundamental Research Funds of Shandong University (2016JC005, 2017JC042 and 2017JC010), the Natural Science Foundation of Shandong Province (ZR2017MEM002), China Postdoctoral Science Foundation (Grant No. 2019M661276), and Guangdong Basic and Applied Basic Research Foundation (Grant No. 2019A1515110756).</p></section><section xml:id="smll202005023-sec-0170" type="conflictOfInterest" numbered="no"><title type="main">Conflict of Interest</title><p>The authors declare no conflict of interest.</p></section><bibliography xml:id="smll202005023-bibl-0001" style="numbered"><bib xml:id="smll202005023-bib-0001"><citation type="journal" xml:id="smll202005023-cit-0001"><author><givenNames>N.</givenNames> <familyName>Yabuuchi</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>K.</givenNames> <familyName>Kubota</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>M.</givenNames> <familyName>Dahbi</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>S.</givenNames> <familyName>Komaba</familyName></author>, <journalTitle>Chem.
