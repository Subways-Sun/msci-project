Information</caption></supportingInfoItem></supportingInformation><abstractGroup><abstract type="main" xml:lang="en"><title type="main">Abstract</title><p>Transition metal chalcogenides have received great attention as promising anode candidates for sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs). However, the undesirable cyclic life and inferior rate capability still restrict their practical applications. The design of micro–nano hierarchitectures is considered as a possible strategy to facilitate the electrochemical reaction kinetics and strengthen the electrode structure stability upon repeated Na<sup>+</sup> insertion/extraction. Herein, urchin‐like Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> hierarchitectures are successfully prepared and developed as a novel anode material for SIBs. Impressively, the as‐prepared urchin‐like Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> can present an ultrahigh rate capacity of 200.2 mAh g<sup>‐1</sup> at 30 A g<sup>‐1</sup> and a prominent capacity retention of 99.9% over 1000 cycles at 1 A g<sup>‐1</sup>, meanwhile, a respectable initial coulombic efficiency of ≈100% is achieved. Through the conjunct study of in situ X‐ray diffraction, ex situ X‐ray absorption near‐edge structure spectroscopy, as well as cyclic voltammetry curves, it is intriguing to reveal that the phase transformation from monoclinic to amorphous structure accompanied by the pseudocapacitive Na<sup>+</sup> storage behavior accounts for the superior electrochemical performance. When paired with the Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> cathode materials, the assembled full cell enables high energy density and decent cyclic stability, demonstrating potential practical feasibility of the present urchin‐like Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> anode.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="smll202000504-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>A Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> anode featuring urchin‐like hierarchitectures presents ultrahigh rate capability and outstanding cyclic stability in sodium‐ion batteries. As revealed by electrochemical analyses and in situ XRD, the phase transformation from monoclinic to amorphous structure accompanied by the pseudocapacitive Na<sup>+</sup> storage behavior accounts for the superior electrochemical performance.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll202000504:smll202000504-gra-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll202000504-gra-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body xml:id="smll202000504-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="smll202000504-sec-0010"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>The growing demand for renewable energy has greatly promoted the development of electrical energy storage (EES) technologies. As a promising alternative to lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs) especially for the grid‐scale EES, sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) have recently attracted tremendous attention owing to the advantages of low cost and wide distribution of sodium resources.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000504-bib-0001 #smll202000504-bib-0002 #smll202000504-bib-0003"><sup>1–3</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, the radius and weight of Na<sup>+</sup> are much larger than those of Li<sup>+</sup>, leading to unsatisfactory cyclic stability and frustrated rate capability related to the sluggish electrochemical reaction kinetics.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000504-bib-0004 #smll202000504-bib-0005 #smll202000504-bib-0006"><sup>4–6</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Therefore, it is highly desirable to design suitable electrode materials with considerable capacity and decent lifespan.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000504-bib-0007"><sup>7</sup></link><sup>]</sup> At present, various anode materials with different reaction mechanisms have been evaluated in SIBs, such as carbonaceous materials,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000504-bib-0008 #smll202000504-bib-0009"><sup>8,9</sup></link><sup>]</sup> intercalation‐type titanium‐based materials (TiO<sub>2</sub>, Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub>, and NaTi<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>),<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000504-bib-0010 #smll202000504-bib-0011"><sup>10,11</sup></link><sup>]</sup> conversion‐type materials (CuO, MnFe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>, NiS<sub>2</sub>, MoS<sub>2</sub>, and CoSe<sub>2</sub>),<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000504-bib-0012 #smll202000504-bib-0013 #smll202000504-bib-0014 #smll202000504-bib-0015 #smll202000504-bib-0016 #smll202000504-bib-0017"><sup>12–17</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and alloy‐type materials (P, Sn, and Sb).<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000504-bib-0018 #smll202000504-bib-0019 #smll202000504-bib-0020 #smll202000504-bib-0021"><sup>18–21</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Among them, the conversion‐type materials, which possess higher capacity compared with the intercalation‐type materials, while present lower volume change upon Na<sup>+</sup> extraction/insertion in comparison to the alloy‐type materials, are promising candidates for SIBs.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000504-bib-0022 #smll202000504-bib-0023 #smll202000504-bib-0024 #smll202000504-bib-0025"><sup>22–25</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Specifically, as a main member of the conversion‐type anodes, transition metal chalcogenides have been extensively investigated.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000504-bib-0026 #smll202000504-bib-0027 #smll202000504-bib-0028 #smll202000504-bib-0029 #smll202000504-bib-0030 #smll202000504-bib-0031 #smll202000504-bib-0032 #smll202000504-bib-0033 #smll202000504-bib-0034"><sup>26–34</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Very recently, iron selenides have become a research hotspot because of their desirable specific capacity, low cost, as well as high conductivity.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000504-bib-0035 #smll202000504-bib-0036 #smll202000504-bib-0037 #smll202000504-bib-0038 #smll202000504-bib-0039 #smll202000504-bib-0040 #smll202000504-bib-0041 #smll202000504-bib-0042 #smll202000504-bib-0043 #smll202000504-bib-0044 #smll202000504-bib-0045 #smll202000504-bib-0046 #smll202000504-bib-0047"><sup>35–47</sup></link><sup>]</sup> For example, Chen's group first evaluated FeSe<sub>2</sub> microspheres as a high‐performance anode for SIBs,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000504-bib-0041"><sup>41</sup></link><sup>]</sup> which could display 372 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> with a capacity retention of 89.0% over 2000 cycles at 1 A g<sup>−1</sup> and a fascinating rate capability of 226 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 25 A g<sup>−1</sup>. Afterward, a few works have focused on the morphology design and carbon coating to further optimize the cyclic performance and electrical conductivity. Mai's group designed FeSe<sub>2</sub> nanorod clusters as anode for SIBs,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000504-bib-0042"><sup>42</sup></link><sup>]</sup> an impressive capacity retention of 97.4% at 1 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 400 cycles and an admirable rate capability of 128 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 35 A g<sup>−1</sup> were obtained. Then, Kang's group fabricated FeSe<sub>2</sub>/graphitic carbon hollow microspheres through a salt‐temethod, which enable outstanding rate performance of 417 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 5 A g<sup>−1</sup> as Na‐storage anodes.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000504-bib-0043"><sup>43</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Lately, Fe<sub>7</sub>Se<sub>8</sub> has also been demonstrated to be a potential anode material for SIBs.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000504-bib-0044 #smll202000504-bib-0045 #smll202000504-bib-0046 #smll202000504-bib-0047"><sup>44–47</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Huang's group reported that the Fe<sub>7</sub>Se<sub>8</sub> nanoparticles embedded in N‐doped carbon matrix could deliver remarkable cyclic stability of 339 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 1200 cycles at 1 A g<sup>−1</sup>.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000504-bib-0044"><sup>44</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Though very encouraging advancements have been made, the achieved electrochemical properties are still far from meeting the requirements of practical applications. It is of great significance but still remains a big challenge to explore a novel iron selenide‐based anode that can simultaneously present high capacity, high rate capability, and excellent cycling stability for sodium‐ion storage.</p><p>NiAs‐structured Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> is an important semiconductor material, which has been widely studied in the fields of magnetics,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000504-bib-0048"><sup>48</sup></link><sup>]</sup> thermoelectricity,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000504-bib-0049"><sup>49</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and electrochemistry.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000504-bib-0050"><sup>50</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, to the best of our knowledge, there is no report concerning its anode application for SIBs up to date; furthermore, there is a strong interest in its Na‐storage behavior. Herein, hierarchical urchin‐like Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> was prepared via a solvothermal method and investigated as a novel anode material for SIBs. Based on the previous reports and our own experience,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000504-bib-0016 #smll202000504-bib-0023 #smll202000504-bib-0031 #smll202000504-bib-0051 #smll202000504-bib-0052 #smll202000504-bib-0053 #smll202000504-bib-0054"><sup>16,23,31,51–54</sup></link><sup>]</sup> the micro–nano hierarchitectures enabled facilitated electrochemical reaction kinetics and strengthened electrode structure stability upon repeated sodium insertion/extraction processes. Thus, the as‐prepared urchin‐like Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> could display outstanding rate capacities in terms of remarkable 410.4 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.05 A g<sup>−1</sup> and 200.2 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at ultrahigh 30 A g<sup>−1</sup>, meanwhile, a prominent capacity retention of 99.9% over 1000 cycles at 1 A g<sup>−1</sup> was demonstrated. More impressively, the electrode exhibited a coulombic efficiency (CE) approaching 100% during the initial cycle. The reaction mechanism behind the electrochemical performance has been revealed by a conjunct study of in situ X‐ray diffraction (XRD), ex situ XRD, X‐ray absorption near‐edge structure (XANES) spectroscopy, and X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS). The reaction kinetics regarding the surface‐controlled pesudocapacitive storage behaviors were investigated by cyclic voltammetry (CV) curves.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202000504-sec-0020"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p>The urchin‐like Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> was synthesized by a facile solvothermal method. The crystal structure of the obtained Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> sample was characterized by XRD and the Rietveld refinement was performed using the FullProf Suite software. As shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll202000504-fig-0001"><b>1</b>a</link>, the as‐prepared Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> crystallizes in the monoclinic structure with a <i>I</i>12/<i>m</i>1 space group, which is obviously different from those of FeSe<sub>2</sub> (space group: <i>Pnnm</i>) and Fe<sub>7</sub>Se<sub>8</sub> (space group: <i>P</i>31).<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000504-bib-0041 #smll202000504-bib-0044"><sup>41,44</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The low reliability factor (<i>R</i><sub>wp</sub> = 8.27%) indicates a good accordance between the experimental and calculated patterns. The Rietveld refined cell parameters of Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> are <i>a</i> = 6.188 Å, <i>b</i> = 3.525(5) Å, <i>c</i> = 11.289(4) Å, and β = 91.840°, respectively, which are very close to the previously reported data.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000504-bib-0055"><sup>55</sup></link><sup>]</sup> This further verifies the high purity of the product. Figure <link href="#smll202000504-fig-0001">1b</link> presents the 2 × 2 × 2 supercell structure of the monoclinic Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>. The crystal structure consists of edge‐shared FeSe<sub>6</sub> octahedra, in which Fe occupies the octahedral sites and coordinates with six Se anions. Inductively coupled plasma (ICP) was also conducted to determine the chemical compositions. The Fe:Se molar ratio of 1:1.36 agrees well with the nominal chemical formula.</p><figure xml:id="smll202000504-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll202000504:smll202000504-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll202000504-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Structure and morphology of the urchin‐like Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>: a) Rietveld refinement of the XRD pattern. b) Crystal structure of Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>. c,d) SEM images. e) TEM and f) HRTEM images. g) EDS mapping images.</caption></figure><p>Morphology and microstructure of the as‐synthesized Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> were characterized by scanning electron microscopy (SEM) and transmission electron microscopy (TEM). As shown in Figure <link href="#smll202000504-fig-0001">1c,d</link>, the Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> features nanorod‐assembled urchin‐like monodispersed spheres with sizes ranging from 500 to 800 nm and the diameters of nanorods are about 20–60 nm. The unique hierarchal nanostructure can ensure short paths for fast Na<sup>+</sup> transportation.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000504-bib-0016"><sup>16</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The hierarchitectures have also been observed by the TEM image (Figure <link href="#smll202000504-fig-0001">1e</link>). The high‐resolution TEM (HRTEM) image in Figure <link href="#smll202000504-fig-0001">1f</link> displays well‐defined lattice fringes with a spacing of 0.54 nm, corresponding to the (−101) plane of Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>. In addition, the energy dispersive spectroscopy (EDS) mapping images (Figure <link href="#smll202000504-fig-0001">1g</link>) indicate that the elements of Fe and Se are uniformly distributed in the urchin‐like architecture. The N<sub>2</sub> adsorption–desorption isotherms of the urchin‐like Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> are provided in Figure S1 in the Supporting Information. The Brunauer–Emmett–Teller specific surface area is calculated to be 14.06 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup>, such a value is close to those of FeSe<sub>2</sub> nanorods and NiSe<sub>2</sub> microspheres.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000504-bib-0031 #smll202000504-bib-0036"><sup>31,36</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Meanwhile, for comparison, the bulk Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> was synthesized through a solid‐state reaction. The as‐prepared bulk Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> appears as irregular aggregated particles (Figure S2, Supporting information). In order to better understand the formation process of the urchin‐like Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>, the SEM images of the products at different synthetic temperatures are given in Figure S3 in the Supporting Information. The morphology displays aggregated rod‐like nanostructures at 200 °C (Figure S3a, Supporting Information); when the temperature is raised to 250 °C, urchin‐like spheres gradually take shapes (Figure S3b, Supporting Information), suggesting that elevating the reaction temperature can promote the growth of urchin‐like architectures. After a solvothermal reaction at 300 °C for 1 h, the uniform urchin‐like hierarchical nanostructures can be achieved (Figure S3c, Supporting Information). In line with the above observations, the HRTEM images (Figure S4, Supporting Information) also confirm the gradual growth of the Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> crystal structure with the increase of reaction temperature.</p><p>The half‐cell electrochemical properties of the urchin‐like Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> as an anode for SIBs were evaluated by assembling CR2025 coin cells. <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll202000504-fig-0002"><b>2</b>a</link> presents the CV curves of the urchin‐like Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> electrode at a scan rate of 0.1 mV s<sup>−1</sup>. During the initial cathodic process, a minor peak located at 1.62 V can be attributed to the intercalation of Na<sup>+</sup> into Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> lattice to form Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>. Besides, a sharp and intense cathodic peak located at around 1.17 V and a small peak at 0.77 V are characterized, corresponding to the conversion reaction of Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> into metallic Fe and Na<sub>2</sub>Se. In the first anodic process, a strong peak at around 1.5 V is ascribed to the transformation of metallic Fe and Na<sub>2</sub>Se into Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> and the peaks at 2.04 and 2.30 V indicate the deintercalation reaction and the formation of Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>. In the subsequent cycles, the reduction peaks at 1.85 and 1.68 V, along with the oxidation peaks at 2.04 and 2.30 V, are related to reversible Na<sup>+</sup> intercalation reaction; the reduction peaks at 0.90 and 0.75 V manifest the multiple steps of the conversion reaction of Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>. It is worth noting that the redox peaks overlap very well from the second cycle onward, indicating the highly reversible sodium insertion/extraction reaction over the whole electrochemical process. The difference in CV curves between the initial and other cycles is attributed to the activation process upon sodiation during the first cycle (as will be discussed later).<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000504-bib-0040 #smll202000504-bib-0047"><sup>40,47</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Figure <link href="#smll202000504-fig-0002">2b</link> depicts the charge–discharge profiles of the urchin‐like Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> under the voltage window of 0.4–3.0 V at a current density of 0.05 A g<sup>−1</sup>. In coincidence with the CV curves, the discharge and charge voltage plateaus situated at 1.9 and 2.25 V, respectively, are correlated with the (de)intercalation reaction; while those at 0.8 and 1.5 V are related to the conversion reaction. The electrode can deliver an initial discharge capacity of 412.1 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, and charge capacity of 410.4 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, rendering an impressive CE of 99.6%. The obtained capacity is a little lower than the theoretical value of 443.6 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, which is mainly due to the relatively high discharge cutoff voltage of 0.4 V for avoiding the overdischarge of electrode and its structure failure.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000504-bib-0015 #smll202000504-bib-0026 #smll202000504-bib-0031 #smll202000504-bib-0036 #smll202000504-bib-0038 #smll202000504-bib-0041"><sup>15,26,31,36,38,41</sup></link><sup>]</sup> After ten cycles, the charge capacity still maintains 405.1 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> and the voltage profiles are well overlapped, demonstrating the excellent reversibility of the urchin‐like Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> electrode. For comparison, the cycling performance of the urchin‐like Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> within a wider voltage window of 0.01–3.0 V was examined (Figure S5, Supporting Information). Although a higher initial charge capacity of 440 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 1 A g<sup>−1</sup> is delivered, only 118 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> can be sustained after 80 cycles. The rapid capacity fading confirms the adverse effect of overdischarge (triggering the excess conversion reaction) on the cycling life of Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>.</p><figure xml:id="smll202000504-fig-0002"><label>2</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll202000504:smll202000504-fig-0002" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll202000504-fig-0002-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Sodium‐ion storage properties of the urchin‐like Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>: a) CV curves at a scan rate of 0.1 mV s<sup>−1</sup> in the voltage range of 0.4–3.0 V versus Na<sup>+</sup>/Na. b) Charge–discharge profiles at a current density of 0.05 A g<sup>−1</sup>. c) Rate performance. d) Summarized rate performances of the recently reported selenides. e) Long‐term cycling performance at 1 A g<sup>−1</sup>.</caption></figure><p>To investigate the rate capability of Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> with different morphologies, the bulk sample with irregular particles was also examined. As shown in Figure <link href="#smll202000504-fig-0002">2c</link>, the bulk Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> electrode delivers an initial charge capacity of only 352.2 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>, at higher rates, charge capacities of 351.6, 328.9, 155.5, and 105.3 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> are obtained at 0.2, 0.5, 20, and 30 A g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. In contrast, the urchin‐like Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> (Figure <link href="#smll202000504-fig-0002">2c</link>) shows a significantly enhanced rate capability and displays 397.2, 395.3, 396.0, 396.2, 394.7, and 371.1 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1, 0.2, 0.5, 1.0, 2.0, and 5.0 A g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. Intriguingly, even at higher current densities of 20 and 30 A g<sup>−1</sup>, outstanding charge capacities of 262.9 and 200.2 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> can be achieved, respectively. This indicates that the morphology play a vitally crucial role in the rate performance of Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>. More specifically, the nanorods of the hierarchical Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> can expose abundant active sites readily accessible to electrolyte and efficiently shorten the ionic diffusion paths; at the meantime, the assembly of nanorods to form the urchin‐like spheres can enhance the structural stability. The superior rate performance of urchin‐like Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> relative to other reported transition metal selenides for sodium‐ion storage is further highlighted in Figure <link href="#smll202000504-fig-0002">2d</link>.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000504-bib-0016 #smll202000504-bib-0026 #smll202000504-bib-0036 #smll202000504-bib-0041 #smll202000504-bib-0044 #smll202000504-bib-0047 #smll202000504-bib-0056 #smll202000504-bib-0057"><sup>16,26,36,41,44,47,56,57</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The long‐term cycling stability of the urchin‐like Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> anode was investigated at a current density of 1 A g<sup>−1</sup>. As shown in Figure <link href="#smll202000504-fig-0002">2e</link>, the electrode affords an initial charge capacity of 391.2 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> and an admirable reversible capacity of 390.9 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> is attained after 1000 cycles, corresponding to a nearly 100% capacity retention. The electrochemical impedance spectra (EIS) measurements of Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> electrode upon cycling were then carried out (Figure S6, Supporting Information). The pristine electrode exhibits a small charge transfer resistance (<i>R</i><sub>ct</sub>) of 24 Ω and the <i>R</i><sub>ct</sub> gradually decreases after the first cycle, indicating the high electronic conductivity and the low polarization. The <i>R</i><sub>ct</sub> is no longer reduced after 50 cycles and becomes stable after 100 cycles, which may account for the capacity increase in the initial several cycles and the excellent cyclic stability of the urchin‐like Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>. For the purpose of clarifying whether there are some changes in the sodium‐ion storage behaviors during cycling, the CV curves and charge/discharge profiles of the urchin‐like Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> anode after the capacity increase (after 200 cycles) are provided in Figure S7 in the Supporting Information. It is worth noticing that the redox peaks and voltage plateaus become more obvious compared with those in the initial several cycles, further validating the activation process upon cycling.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000504-bib-0041 #smll202000504-bib-0044"><sup>41,44</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Moreover, the electrolyte of 1 <sc>m</sc> NaClO<sub>4</sub> in propylene carbonate (PC) with 5% fluoroethylene carbonate (FEC) additive was employed to make comparisons with the ether‐based electrolyte (Figure S8, Supporting Information). The specific capacity of urchin‐like Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> in ester‐based electrolyte can sustain 303 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 1 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 60 cycles; however, the electrode suffers from rapid capacity decay in the subsequent cycles, elucidating the great significance of ether‐based electrolyte to the cyclic stability in this system.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000504-bib-0016 #smll202000504-bib-0026 #smll202000504-bib-0041 #smll202000504-bib-0044 #smll202000504-bib-0047 #smll202000504-bib-0056"><sup>16,26,41,44,47,56</sup></link><sup>]</sup> According to the previous study,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000504-bib-0011"><sup>11</sup></link><sup>]</sup> the ether‐based electrolyte can promote the formation of more sodium alkoxides (RCH<sub>2</sub>ONa) in the solid electrolyte interphase film than that of the ester‐based electrolyte, where the RCH<sub>2</sub>ONa enables the decreased resistance at the electrolyte/electrode interfaces, facilitates the fast Na<sup>+</sup> transport, and benefits the high interfacial stability. The TEM image of the electrode in ether‐based electrolyte after 200 cycles is provided in Figure S9 in the Supporting Information, the urchin‐like morphology has decomposed to some extent, while the rod‐like nanostructures are still preserved. This is beneficial for the facile transportation of Na<sup>+</sup> and demonstrates the favorable effect of the initial urchin‐like hierarchitectures on the decent electrochemical performance.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000504-bib-0016"><sup>16</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The detailed electrochemical properties regarding initial CE, rate, and cycling performances of the urchin‐like Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> are summarized in Table S1 in the Supporting Information, which compare favorably with those previously reported for the transition metal selenides sodium‐ion storage anode materials.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000504-bib-0016 #smll202000504-bib-0026 #smll202000504-bib-0036 #smll202000504-bib-0041 #smll202000504-bib-0044 #smll202000504-bib-0056 #smll202000504-bib-0057"><sup>16,26,36,41,44,56,57</sup></link><sup>]</sup> It is worthwhile to note that the electrode mass loading of about 0.9–1.2 mg cm<sup>−2</sup> in the present work is comparable to the commonly used 0.6–1.5 mg cm<sup>−2</sup> in other reports.</p><p>For the purpose of figuring out the origin of the outstanding rate performance of urchin‐like Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>, CV curves at different scan rates ranging from 0.2 to 1.0 mV s<sup>−1</sup> were recorded to study the Na<sup>+</sup> reaction dynamics (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll202000504-fig-0003"><b>3</b>a</link>). Since the <i>b</i> values for most peaks are close to 1.0, the pseudocapacitive behavior is supposed to dominate the whole charge storage process. The pseudocapacitive contributions are quantified according to Equation (<link href="#smll202000504-disp-0003">3</link>), of which <i>k</i><sub>1</sub><i>v</i> and <i>k</i><sub>2</sub><i>v</i><sup>1/2</sup> represent the capacitive and diffusion‐limited currents, respectively.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000504-bib-0059"><sup>59</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Figure <link href="#smll202000504-fig-0003">3c</link> displays the capacitive contributions at various scan rates, it can be observed that the capacitive effect gradually strengthens with the increase of scan rate, demonstrating that the pseudocapacitive Na<sup>+</sup> storage is responsible for the high capacity at high rates. During the initial discharge, all peaks exhibit gradual leftward shift accompanied by the decrease of peak intensity, indicating the volume expansion with Na<sup>+</sup> intercalation into the Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> framework (<i>x</i>Na<sup>+</sup> + <i>x</i>e<sup>−</sup> + Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> → Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>). When the electrode was further discharged to around 1.0 V, all peaks gradually disappear, demonstrating the conversion reaction of Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> and the generation of metallic Fe and Na<sub>2</sub>Se (Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> + (8−<i>x</i>)Na<sup>+</sup> + (8−<i>x</i>)e<sup>−</sup> → 4Na<sub>2</sub>Se + 3Fe), which is consistent with the CV results in the first cathodic process. Upon discharging to 0.4 V, the characteristic peaks totally vanish except a minor peak of Na<sub>2</sub>Se at around 2θ = 37°. To get more information about the fully discharged products, the ex situ XRD was conducted. Figure S10 in the Supporting Information provides the XRD pattern of the discharged electrode in the 5th cycle. The signals of metallic Fe and Na<sub>2</sub>Se are clearly detected, validating the conversion from Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> to Fe and Na<sub>2</sub>Se. Moreover, when the electrode was recharged to 3.0 V, no diffraction peaks of Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> reappear, indicating the formation of an amorphous phase after the first sodiation/desodiation process. The similar phenomenon has also been reported in many other conversion‐type anodes.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000504-bib-0015 #smll202000504-bib-0026 #smll202000504-bib-0033 #smll202000504-bib-0040 #smll202000504-bib-0042 #smll202000504-bib-0054 #smll202000504-bib-0060"><sup>15,26,33,40,42,54,60</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The amorphous structure containing abundant defects and vacancies produces plentiful surfaces/interfaces within the material, benefiting the high pseudocapacitive contribution during charge storage and the capacity increase in the initial several cycles.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000504-bib-0011 #smll202000504-bib-0061"><sup>11,61</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Therefore, it can be deduced that the high initial CE approaching 100% is attributed to the structure activation process after the first discharge, the ether‐based electrolyte facilitating the reaction kinetics as well as the relatively high discharge cutoff voltage that well relieves the electrolyte decomposition and enhances the reversibility of Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> anode.</p><figure xml:id="smll202000504-fig-0004"><label>4</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll202000504:smll202000504-fig-0004" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll202000504-fig-0004-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Sodium‐ion storage mechanism of the urchin‐like Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>: a) Image plots of the (−202), (−112), (202), (303), and (020) diffraction profiles and b) in situ XRD patterns collected during the first discharge/charge cycle. c) XANES spectra of the Fe–K edge at different states.</caption></figure><p>It is known that XANES is an effective approach to investigate the sample with low crystallinity and amorphous phase. To gain a deep understanding of the difference between the pristine and cycled electrodes, ex situ XANES measurements were carried out on the pristine sample and the sample charged to 3.0 V. In order to avoid exposure in air, the electrodes were disassembled without scraping from Cu current collector in a glove box and directly sealed in a Kapton film. The XANES spectra at the Fe K‐edge are presented in Figure <link href="#smll202000504-fig-0004">4c</link>. The energy of pre‐edge peak of the electrode charged to 3.0 V (7111 eV) is very close to that of the pristine electrode, indicating the similar valence state. It is worth noting that the profile of white line peak (the region of red circle in Figure <link href="#smll202000504-fig-0004">4d</link>) of the fully charged sample is a bit different from that of the pristine sample, suggesting the different coordination environments of Fe. This is caused by the structure reconstruction from the conversion reaction during the first discharge/charge process. This result further explains the difference in CV curves between the first and subsequent cycles. Ex situ XPS spectra (Figure S11, Supporting Information) were also carried out to probe the detailed valence states. The Fe 2p XPS spectrum of the pristine material shows the coexistence of Fe<sup>2+</sup> and Fe<sup>3+</sup>, where the peaks at 710.7 and 724.5 eV can be assigned to the 2p3/2 and 2p1/2 of Fe<sup>2+</sup>, respectively; and the peaks at 712.6 and 726.1 eV are related to the 2p3/2 and 2p1/2 of Fe<sup>3+</sup>, respectively.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000504-bib-0044 #smll202000504-bib-0047"><sup>44,47</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The fitted XPS spectrum of the fully charged electrode (3.0 V) demonstrates the coexistence of Fe<sup>2+</sup> and Fe<sup>3+</sup> as well, which is in great agreement with the XANES results. This further attests the good reversibility of the whole electrochemical reaction.</p><p>In addition, the exceptional rate capability and superb cyclic stability of the urchin‐like Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> inspire us to explore its practical feasibility in full cells. The urchin‐like Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> anode was matched with the classical Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> (NVP) cathode to assemble a prototype Na‐ion full cell, as schematically illustrated in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll202000504-fig-0005"><b>5</b>a</link>. The Na‐storage behaviors of the NVP cathode in a half cell are provided in Figure S12 in the Supporting Information. In order to balance the capacity, the cathode active mass loading was set to 4.2 times of the anode mass loading. Figure <link href="#smll202000504-fig-0005">5b</link> depicts the charge/discharge profiles of the full cell within the voltage window of 1.0 to 2.9 V at a current density of 0.5 A g<sup>−1</sup>. The full cell could deliver an initial discharge capacity of 372 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> with the average voltage output of 1.9 V. This corresponds to a promising energy density of 135.9 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup> based on the total mass of anode and cathode. The relatively low initial CE of the full cell compared with that of the half‐cell is mainly caused by the NVP cathode (Figure S12a, Supporting Information). After 500 cycles, the discharge capacity still maintains 203 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#smll202000504-fig-0005">5c</link>). The cyclic stability may be further improved by precisely controlling the full cell parameters (e.g., electrodes mass ratio, electrolyte content, voltage window) and optimizing the assembly processing. The full‐cell performance in terms of initial CE, reversible capacity, and cycling life of the present work is outstanding among the related reports, as compared in Table S2 in the Supporting Information. Besides, the light‐emitting diode bulbs can be continuously lit up by a fully charged coin‐type full cell (inset of Figure <link href="#smll202000504-fig-0005">5c</link>), corroborating the application prospects of the urchin‐like Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> anode.</p><figure xml:id="smll202000504-fig-0005"><label>5</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll202000504:smll202000504-fig-0005" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll202000504-fig-0005-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Na‐ion full batteries: a) Schematic illustration of the Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>//NVP full cell. b) Galvanostatic charge/discharge profiles at different cycles and c) cyclic performance of the full cell at a current density of 0.5 A g<sup>−1</sup>.</caption></figure></section><section xml:id="smll202000504-sec-0030"><title type="main">Conclusion</title><p>In summary, hierarchical urchin‐like Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> was investigated as a new anode material for sodium‐ion batteries. The as‐prepared urchin‐like Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> could present superior cycling stability with an ≈100% capacity retention over 1000 cycles at 1 A g<sup>−1</sup>, and outstanding rate performance of 200.2 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at an ultrahigh current density of 30 A g<sup>−1</sup>. More impressively, the electrode displayed a coulombic efficiency approaching 100% in the initial cycle. CV curves elaborately revealed that the pseudocapacitive Na<sup>+</sup> storage behavior dominated the electrochemical reaction, which was responsible for the remarkable rate capability. In addition, the assembled Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>//Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> full cell could deliver reversible capacity of 372 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> with an average operating voltage of 1.9 V. The superior performance enables Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> a promising candidate for the anode application in sodium‐ion batteries.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202000504-sec-0040"><title type="main">Experimental Section</title><section xml:id="smll202000504-sec-0050" numbered="no"><section xml:id="smll202000504-sec-0060" numbered="no"><section xml:id="smll202000504-sec-0070" numbered="no"><title type="main">Materials Synthesis</title><p>The urchin‐like Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> was prepared via a simple solvothermal method. In the typical synthesis, 3 mmol of Fe(acac)<sub>3</sub> (Aladdin, AR) and 4 mmol of Se (Aladdin, AR) powders were added in 30 mL oleylamine solution in a 250 mL three‐neck flask. The above solution was slowly heated to 120 °C in order to eliminate the oxygen and low‐boiling‐point solvent under nitrogen atmosphere. Afterward, the solution was further heated to 200 °C and maintained at this temperature for 1 h. Finally, the temperature was elevated to 300 °C and kept for 1 h. It is worth noting that the flow velocity of nitrogen should be controlled to moderate liquid level to avoid the evaporation of oleylamine. The obtained Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> precipitations were centrifuged and washed by tremendous ethanol.</p><p>For the synthesis of bulk Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>, FeC<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>·2H<sub>2</sub>O (0.25 g) and selenium powder (0.6 g) were thoroughly mixed and ground in a mortar. Then the precursor was annealed at 300 °C for 6 h in Ar atmosphere with a heating rate of 2 °C min<sup>−1</sup>, then the temperature was further raised to 650 °C for 2 h at a heating rate of 4 °C min<sup>−1</sup> to obtain the bulk Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>.</p><p>The Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> (NVP) cathode was synthesized by a facile solid‐state method according to the previous literature.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000504-bib-0062"><sup>62</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p></section><section xml:id="smll202000504-sec-0080" numbered="no"><title type="main">Materials Characterization</title><p>XRD patterns were obtained on a Panalytical X'Pert Pro diffractometer (Cu <i>K</i><sub>α</sub> radiation). The morphology and microstructure of the samples were investigated by SEM (SUPRA 55) and TEM (JEM 2100). Nitrogen adsorption–desorption isotherms were carried out on a Quantochrome Autosorb‐1MP absorption instrument at 77 K. The chemical content of elements was determined by inductively coupled plasma atomic emission spectroscopy (ICP‐AES, Varian 725‐ES). The valence state was measured by XPS on a Kratos AXIS UltraDLD spectrometer.</p><p>For ex situ measurements, the electrodes at various charged/discharged states were disassembled in a glove box and rinsed by dimethylether to remove the remanent electrolyte. For ex situ XANES, the stripped electrodes were sealed by Capton film, in the purpose of avoiding any exposure to air. Ex situ XANES tests were conducted on the Beijing Synchrotron Radiation Facility (BSRF) at 1W1B beamline. All samples were recorded by using fluorescence mode.</p><p>For in situ XRD test, a special cell with Al as window and current collector was assembled to observe in situ reaction during the first cycle. Each scan was collected in 0.04° increments between 20° and 55° at a scanning speed of 0.04° s<sup>−1</sup>.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202000504-sec-0090" numbered="no"><title type="main">Electrochemical Measurements</title><p>For the assembly of half cells, the electrode was prepared by mixing active materials, super P carbon, and sodium carboxymethyl cellulose binder in a weight ratio of 70:20:10 with water as solvent, the homogenous slurry was then casted on copper foil and dried in vacuum at 70 °C for 8 h, the mass loading of active materials for each electrode was about 0.9–1.2 mg cm<sup>−2</sup>.
