In this work, we report the synthesis and electrochemical properties of a novel bimetallic TMCs material CuSbSe<sub>2</sub>. The as‐prepared anode delivers a high reversible capacity of 545.6  mA h g<sup>−1</sup> for SIBs and 592.6  mA h g<sup>−1</sup> for LIBs at a current density of 0.2 A g<sup>−1</sup>, and an excellent rate capability of 425.9  mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 20 A g<sup>−1</sup> for SIBs and 226.0  mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 10 A g<sup>−1</sup> for LIBs without any common‐used surface modification or carbonaceous compositing. In addition, ex situ X‐ray diffraction (XRD) and High‐resolution transmission electron microscopy (HRTEM) reveal a combined conversion‐alloying reaction mechanism of LIBs and NIBs. Our findings suggest bimetallic CuSbSe<sub>2</sub> could be a potential anode material for both SIBs and LIBs.</p></abstract><abstract type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p><b>Bimetallic CuSbSe<sub>2</sub></b> are successfully synthesis and evaluated in sodium‐ and lithium‐ ion batteries. The CuSbSe<sub>2</sub> anode exhibits superior rate behavior and cyclic performance. The excellent electrochemical property is attributed to the synergistic effect and the high electric conductivity of the material.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:09476539:media:chem202203044:chem202203044-toc-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/chem202203044-toc-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="chem202203044-sec-0001" numbered="no"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>In recent years, with the continuous development of electrical‐energy‐storage (EES),<link href="#chem202203044-bib-0001"><sup>[1]</sup></link> lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs) have dominated the energy field and become an indispensable part of the market due to its high energy storage and long cycle life.<link href="#chem202203044-bib-0004"><sup>[2]</sup></link> However, due to the uneven distribution and limited lithium resources in the earth crust,<link href="#chem202203044-bib-0008"><sup>[3]</sup></link> it is of vital importance to identify abundant, economic and efficient alternative candidates for next generation devices.<link href="#chem202203044-bib-0011"><sup>[4]</sup></link> Among various substitutes, sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) have attracted much attention because of the practically inexhaustible and low cost sodium resources.<link href="#chem202203044-bib-0015"><sup>[5]</sup></link> Nevertheless, compared with lithium, sodium ion has larger ionic radius (1.02 Å for Na<sup>+</sup> and 0.76 Å for Li<sup>+</sup>) and higher standard hydrogen electrode (SHE) (2.71 V for Na<sup>+</sup>/Na and 3.04 V for Li<sup>+</sup>/Li),<link href="#chem202203044-bib-0019"><sup>[6]</sup></link> which leads to sluggish reaction kinetics and lower energy density. Therefore, it is essential to develop rational anode materials with excellent electrochemical property and high energy density for SIBs.<link href="#chem202203044-bib-0022"><sup>[7]</sup></link></p><p>In comparison with the carbon‐based anodes, transition metal chalcogenides (TMCs) with conversion reaction mechanism can provide higher theoretical specific capacities via enabling multiple electron transfer.<link href="#chem202203044-bib-0026"><sup>[8]</sup></link> Unfortunately, the practical application of these TMCs is hindered by severe volume changes caused by conversion and alloying reactions, leading to electrode pulverization, contact loss and poor cyclability. In addition, TMCs also suffer from inferior electron conductivity which results in sluggish electrochemical kinetics and unsatisfactory rate performance.<link href="#chem202203044-bib-0030"><sup>[9]</sup></link> In this respect, significant efforts have been devoted in addressing these issues by nanostructured engineering or structured electrodes with carbonaceous materials.<link href="#chem202203044-bib-0033"><sup>[10]</sup></link> Nanostructured engineering of active materials has proven to be a regular and effective way which could shorten the ion/electron transfer distances to nanoscale and buffer the volume expansion simultaneously. Incorporation of carbon materials with TMCs is also a common practice to enhance the electrical conductivity as well as accommodate large volume changes.<link href="#chem202203044-bib-0034"><sup>[11]</sup></link> However, those approaches still need to be further improved for large‐scale applications, because of the high manufacturing cost and low volumetric energy densities of nanostructured or carbonaceous materials. Therefore, there is still a suspicion whether the TMCs materials with unique physicochemical properties could endow acceptable electrochemical performance without any extrinsic modifications.</p><p>Introducing two more transition metal in one material could be one effective strategy to address the abovementioned challenges. In comparison with monometal TMCs, bimetallic chalcogenides show enhanced intrinsic electronic conductivity and rich electrochemical redox sites. Besides, two metal ions can stimulate synergistic effect: because of the different redox potential of the two metal ions, when one component is conducting conversion reaction, the other one can act as the matrix to alleviate the effect of volume expansion.<link href="#chem202203044-bib-0040"><sup>[12]</sup></link> For example, Bimetallic CuCo<sub>2</sub>S<sub>4</sub>@N/S‐Doped Graphene provides a high capacity of 984  mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.2 A g<sup>−1</sup> and an excellent cycling performance of 453  mA h g<sup>−1</sup> after 500 cycles at 2 A g<sup>−1</sup> due to the alleviation of volume variation.<link href="#chem202203044-bib-0041"><sup>[13]</sup></link> NiCo<sub>2</sub>S<sub>4</sub> nanodots were employed as the anode material for SIBs with a specific capacity of 742.2  mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.2 A g<sup>−1</sup> and show dominated pseudo‐capacitive behaviors because of superior electronic conductivity.<link href="#chem202203044-bib-0042"><sup>[14]</sup></link> FeSb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>4</sub>/EN‐rGO as anode material for SIBs can provide a high specific capacity of 782.5  mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> benefits from its conversion‐alloying mechanism.<link href="#chem202203044-bib-0043"><sup>[15]</sup></link></p><p>Although much efforts have been made on bimetallic TMCs, however, most studies focused on the study of bimetallic transition metal sulfides. In comparison with sulfur, selenium lies in the same column with larger radius and less electronegativity. Therefore, transition metal selenides (TMSe) exhibit weaker bonds and higher electronic conductivity, which could lower the electrochemical reaction barrier and possess favorable electrochemical kinetics. Consequently, developing novel bimetallic TMSe could be an effective strategy to improve the electrochemical performance.</p><p>Herein, we successfully synthesized a bimetallic CuSbSe<sub>2</sub> via a simple solid‐state method and investigated its electrochemical properties as the anode material for SIBs and LIBs. Benefiting from the synergistic effect of two metal ions, the multiple redox centers delivered a high reversible capacity of 546.0  mA h g<sup>−1</sup> for SIBs and 592.6  mA h g<sup>−1</sup> for LIBs at a current density of 0.2 A g<sup>−1</sup>. Owing to the high electric conductivity, CuSbSe<sub>2</sub> exhibits superior rate‐performance of 425.9  mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 20 A g<sup>−1</sup>, and excellent cycling stability with a capacity retention of 82.4 % after 1000 cycles at a current density of 5 A g<sup>−1</sup> for SIBs. Finally, ex situ X‐ray XRD and ex situ transmission electron microscope (TEM) measurements reveals a conversion‐alloying reaction mechanism in both SIBs and LIBs. These findings suggest that the bulk CuSbSe<sub>2</sub> could be considered as a promising high‐performance anode for SIBs and LIBs.</p></section><section xml:id="chem202203044-sec-0002" numbered="no"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><section xml:id="chem202203044-sec-0003" numbered="no"><title type="main">Physical Characterization of CuSbSe<sub>2</sub></title><p>The XRD pattern of the as‐synthesized CuSbSe<sub>2</sub> was shown in Figure <link href="#chem202203044-fig-0001">1a</link>. As The Rietveld refined lattice parameters are <i>a</i>=6.3077 Å, <i>b</i>=3.9851 Å and <i>c</i>=15.0131 Å, and the detailed structural information are listed in Table S1.<link href="#chem202203044-bib-0044"><sup>[16]</sup></link> The schematic crystal structure of CuSbSe<sub>2</sub> is shown in Figure <link href="#chem202203044-fig-0001">1b</link> which is composed of CuSe<sub>4</sub> tetrahedra and SbSe<sub>3</sub> tetrahedra linked with each other, forming a pseudo‐layered structure perpendicular to <i>ab</i> plane.<link href="#chem202203044-bib-0045"><sup>[17]</sup></link> The three‐dimensional and pseudo‐layered structure with large open channels provides fast Na/Li‐ion pathways which makes ion transport easier. scanning electron microscope (SEM) (Figure <link href="#chem202203044-fig-0001">1c</link>) and TEM (Figure <link href="#chem202203044-fig-0001">1d</link>) images display micron‐sized CuSbSe<sub>2</sub> particles ranging from 5 to 10 μm. As shown in HRTEM (Figure <link href="#chem202203044-fig-0001">1e</link>), it shows several lattice interplanar spacings of 0.290, 0.306 and 0.311 nm corresponding to the (202), (112) and (103) planes, respectively. selected area electron diffraction (SAED) pattern (Figure <link href="#chem202203044-fig-0001">1f</link>) shows the (102), (114), (122) lattice interplanar of the orthorhombic CuSbSe<sub>2</sub>. The chemical states of CuSbSe<sub>2</sub> were further analyzed by XPS spectrum as displayed in Figure <link href="#chem202203044-fig-0001">1h</link>–<link href="#chem202203044-fig-0001">1j</link>. The two typical peaks at 951.8 and 932 eV are ascribed to the 2p<sub>1/2</sub> and 2p<sub>3/2</sub> levels of Cu<sup>+</sup>, while the peaks at 539.6, 538.3, 530.35 and 528.9 eV are ascribed to the 3d<sub>3/2</sub> and 3d<sub>5/2</sub> levels of Sb<sup>3+</sup>, respectively. (g) Element mapping and (h–j) XPS spectra of the as synthesized CuSbSe<sub>2</sub>.</p></caption></figure></section><section xml:id="chem202203044-sec-0004" numbered="no"><title type="main">Electrochemical properties of SIBs</title><p>To investigate the Na<sup>+</sup> insertion/extraction behavior, cyclic voltammetry (CV) profiles were firstly recorded between 0.01 and 3 V at a scan rate of 0.1 mV s<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#chem202203044-fig-0002">2a</link>). In the first cathodic scan, one hump at about 1.18 V and three intensive peaks appear at 1.05 and 0.47, 0.45 V are observed, where the first hump at 1.18 V represents the intercalation of Na<sup>+</sup> and the peak at 1.05 V is related to the conversion reactions of CuSbSe<sub>2</sub>, forming Na<sub>2</sub>Se and Cu. The lower two peaks at 0.47 and 0.45 V represents the alloying reaction of Na and Sb forming NaSb and Na<sub>3</sub>Sb. In the anodic scan, two distinct broad peaks at 0.73 and 0.85 V represent the processes of the dealloying reaction of Na<sub>3</sub>Sb, resulting amorphous Sb.<link href="#chem202203044-bib-0037 #chem202203044-bib-0047 #chem202203044-bib-0048"><sup>[11c,19,20]</sup></link> Then the peak at 1.45 V stands for the process of the recovery Na<sub>x</sub>CuSbSe<sub>2</sub>, and the small hump at 1.82 V on behalf of the reformation of CuSbSe<sub>2</sub>. During the second charge‐discharge process, the cathodic hump at 1.2 V and the anodic hump at 1.70 V turned into two obvious peaks. As shown Figure <link href="#chem202203044-fig-0002">2b</link>, the cycling performance of the CuSbSe<sub>2</sub> anode was recorded at 0.2 A g<sup>−1</sup> and the electrode remains a high capacity of 453  mA h g<sup>−1</sup> with a capacity retention of 83 % after 30 cycles. The galvanostatic charge‐discharge (GCD) potential profiles of the CuSbSe<sub>2</sub> electrodes at a current rate of 0.2 A g<sup>−1</sup> are depicted in Figure <link href="#chem202203044-fig-0002">2c</link>. CuSbSe<sub>2</sub> anode delivers an initial discharge and charge capacities of 634.9 and 537.6  mA h g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively, with the initial Columbic efficiency (ICE) of 84.7 %, owning to the formation of the solid electrolyte interfaces (SEI) film. Encouragingly, CuSbSe<sub>2</sub> displayed an excellent Columbic efficiency of 98.3 % after the first cycle. As displayed in Figure <link href="#chem202203044-fig-0002">2d</link>, the anode delivers only 22 % capacity loss at a current rate of 20 A g<sup>−1</sup>, demonstrating excellent rate performances. In addition, CuSbSe<sub>2</sub> exhibits a stable and reversible discharge capacity of 371  mA h g<sup>−1</sup> after 1000 cycles at a current density of 5 A g<sup>−1</sup>, corresponding to a capacity retention of 82.4 % and a Columbic Efficiency of nearly 100 % (Figure <link href="#chem202203044-fig-0002">2e</link>). The excellent cycle and rate performance may result from the synergistic effect of the Cu and Sb ions and the high intrinsic conductivity of the CuSbSe<sub>2</sub> material.<link href="#chem202203044-bib-0049"><sup>[21]</sup></link></p><figure xml:id="chem202203044-fig-0002"><label>2</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:09476539:media:chem202203044:chem202203044-fig-0002"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/chem202203044-fig-0002-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>(a) CV curves of the CuSbSe<sub>2</sub> as SIBs anode at a scan rate of 0.1 mV s<sup>−1</sup>. (h) bar chart showing the percentage of pseudocapacitive contribution.</p></caption></figure><p>To gain further insight into the origin of the excellent rate performance, kinetics analysis based on cyclic voltammetry profiles at different scan rates (DSCV) from 1 to 10 mV s<sup>−1</sup> was carried out (Figure <link href="#chem202203044-fig-0002">2f</link>). According to the Randles‐Sevcik (R‐S) equation between the peak current (<i>i</i>) under the certain potential (<i>V</i>) and different sweep rate (<i>v</i>): <displayedItem xml:id="chem202203044-disp-0001" numbered="no" type="mathematics"><mediaResource href="graphic/chem202203044-math-0001.png" alt="urn:x-wiley:09476539:media:chem202203044:chem202203044-math-0001"/></displayedItem></p><p>the <i>b</i>‐value is determined by the ion storage mechanism, to be specific, the value of <i>b</i> less than 0.5 means a diffusion‐dominated process, and the value of <i>b</i> is near 1.0 indicates a pseudocapacitance‐controlled process.<link href="#chem202203044-bib-0053"><sup>[22]</sup></link> As demonstrate in Figure <link href="#chem202203044-fig-0002">2g</link>, the <i>b</i>‐values are calculated to be 0.82 and 0.90 for peak 1 and peak 2, as well as 0.82 and 0.96 for peak 3 and peak 4, suggesting the pseudocapacitance‐dominated behavior during the charge/discharge storage process. The pseudocapacitive contributions are evaluated to be above 85.9 % at scan rates from 1 to 10 mV s<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#chem202203044-fig-0002">2h</link>), which indicates that the pseudocapacitive charge storage dominated the entire electrochemical redox reactions during the whole capacity at all scan rates. Furthermore, electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) measurements at the open circuit voltage (OCV) of CuSbSe<sub>2</sub> is tested (Figure S1), which exhibits a low R<sub>ct</sub> of 1.188 Ω. Figure <link href="#chem202203044-fig-0004">4a</link> displays the XRD patterns of CuSbSe<sub>2</sub> at different charge and discharge stages with its corresponding charge‐discharge profiles. All the diffraction peaks at the open circus voltage can be indexed to CuSbSe<sub>2</sub>. As the voltage decreases from 1.8 to 1.2 V, the original CuSbSe<sub>2</sub> still remains, which means the intercalation of Na<sup>+</sup> forming Na<sub>x</sub>CuSbSe<sub>2</sub>. As the reaction goes to 1.0 V, the peaks of CuSbSe<sub>2</sub> disappears and new peaks at 22.5° and 37.2° are observed, which can be indexed to Na<sub>2</sub>Se phase. This phenomenon indicates the conversion reaction of CuSbSe<sub>2</sub>. After fully discharged to 0.01 V, the peaks at 22.5° and 37.2° are become higher, which means the completely reaction of CuSbSe<sub>2</sub>, forming Na<sub>2</sub>Se, amorphous Na<sub>3</sub>Sb and amorphous Cu. During the charge process, the reflection peaks at 22.5° and 37.5° gradually decreased and a broader peak at 29.2° appeared after fully charged to 3 V, which means the reversable redox reaction of the CuSbSe<sub>2</sub> material. Further evidence is given by HRTEM and SAED images as displayed in Figure <link href="#chem202203044-fig-0004">4b</link>–<link href="#chem202203044-fig-0004">4e</link>. When being fully charged to 3.0 V (Figure <link href="#chem202203044-fig-0004">4d</link>), the lattice fringe of 0.266 nm corresponds to the (203) plane of CuSbSe<sub>2</sub> (PDF No. 97‐014‐8754).</p><figure xml:id="chem202203044-fig-0004"><label>4</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:09476539:media:chem202203044:chem202203044-fig-0004"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/chem202203044-fig-0004-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>(a) Ex situ XRD patterns of CuSbSe<sub>2</sub> as anode in SIBs during the first charge‐discharge process. As displays in XRD (Figure <link href="#chem202203044-fig-0005">5a</link>), the peaks of CuSbSe2 disappeared at 1.4 V, means a conversion reaction and amorphous state of the material. After discharged to 0.7 V, a new peak appears at 25.6°, consistent with the formation of Li<sub>2</sub>Se. Benefit from the synergistic effect, the CuSbSe<sub>2</sub> anode can deliver a high specific capacity of 546.0  mA h g<sup>−1</sup> for SIBs and 592.6  mA h g<sup>−1</sup> for LIBs. A conversion‐alloying mechanism is proved by the ex situ XRD and TEM tests, which explain the superior capacity of CuSbSe<sub>2</sub>. Furthermore, the high ionic conductivity enables fast kinetics with an excellent rate performance of 425.9  mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 20 A g<sup>−1</sup> for SIBs. This study here provides a promising approach to design and synthesize novel anode materials to produce high‐performance SIBs and LIBs.</p></section><section xml:id="chem202203044-sec-0008" numbered="no"><title type="main">Experimental Section</title><p><b>Material Preparation</b>: CuSbSe<sub>2</sub> material were prepared by a simple solid‐state method: Stoichiometric amounts of Cu (Sigma‐Aldrich, 99.9 %), Sb (Sigma‐Aldrich, ≥99.5 %), and Se (Sigma‐Aldrich, 99.9 %) powders were added into an agate mortar and mixed uniformly after stirring for 30 minutes, then the obtained mixture was sealed in an evacuated quartz tube at the pressure of 2×10<sup>−2</sup> Pa. The quartz tube was then heated to 810 °C at a heating rate of 2 °C min<sup>−1</sup> and isothermally kept for 1 week to achieve the final compound CuSbSe<sub>2</sub>.<link href="#chem202203044-bib-0056"><sup>[23]</sup></link></p><p><b>Materials Characterization</b>: The crystal structure of the as‐prepared material was examined by powder X‐ray diffraction (XRD) on a Bruker AXS D8 diffractometer with Cu Kα radiation (λ=1.5406 Å) and the corresponding Rietveld refinement was calculated by Full‐prof software. The structure and morphology of the obtained material was studied using a JSM‐7900F scanning electron microscope (SEM) with an operation voltage of 2.00 kV and a JEOL transmission electron microscope (TEM) with an operation voltage of 200 kV. High‐resolution transmission electron microscopy (HRTEM) and selected area electron diffraction (SAEDs) analysis were also obtained by TEM tests. X‐ray photoemission spectra (XPS) were investigated by using a VG scientific ESCALAB‐250 spectrometer.</p><p><b>Electrochemical Experiments</b>: Electrochemical measurements were performed using CR2032‐type coin cells. For the electrode preparation, active material, super P and Carboxymethyl Cellulose (CMC) with a weight ratio of 7 : 2 : 1 were dissolved in deionized water to obtain a homogeneous slurry, then the resulting mixture was casted on a copper foil. The as‐prepared electrode films were dried at 60 °C for 12 h in a vacuum oven, and then divided into small plates with a loading mass of 1.2‐1.4 mg cm<sup>−2</sup>. The CR2023‐type coin cells were assembled in an Ar‐filled glove box. For sodium half‐cells, Glass fiber (Whatman GF/C) was used as the separator and sodium foils were employed as the counter electrode. As in SIBs, an electrolyte of 1 M sodium hexafluorophosphate (NaPF<sub>6</sub>) in dimethoxyethane (DME) was used. Similary, in LIBs configuration, lithium foils acted as counter electrode, and Celgard‐2500 was used as the separator. The electrolyte was 1 M Lithium‐hexafluorophosphate (LiPF<sub>6</sub>) in ethylene carbonate (EC): diethyl carbonate (DEC)=1 : 1 (Vol %)+10 % fluoroethylene carbonate (FEC). For comparison, coin cells with ester‐based electrolyte in SIBs and ether‐based electrolyte in LIBs were also assembled, the specific electrolytes are 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub> in EC/DEC=1 : 1 (Vol %)+5 % FEC for SIBs and 1 M Lithium bis‐(trifluoromethylsulphonyl)‐imide (LiTFSI) in DME: 1,3‐Dioxolane (DOL)=1 : 1 (Vol %) for LIBs, respectively. Galvanostatic charge‐discharge profiles were obtained in the voltage range of 0.01–3.0 V in SIBs and 0.3–3 V in LIBs by using a Land‐2001 (Wuhan, China) automatic battery tester. Finally, cyclic voltammetry (CV) and electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) were performed on a multichannel potentiostatic‐galvanostatic system (Bio‐Logic SAS, France).</p></section><section xml:id="chem202203044-sec-0009" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgments</title><p>This work was supported by National Natural Science Foundation of China with Grant No. 51972142 and 52202237.
