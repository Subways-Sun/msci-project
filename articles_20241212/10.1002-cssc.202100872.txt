One of the key obstacles to sodium batteries is the lack of efficient and stable anode materials. Compared with traditional inorganic electrode materials, organic materials are more attractive because of their easier sodium transport accessibility and the diversities of organic frameworks and functional groups. In this work, two molecules (Na‐CPN and Na‐CPP) were synthesized and used as anode materials for SIBs. Structurally, the two compounds are isomers, and they are distinguished by the position of N atoms in phenylpyridine. Na‐CPP showed a high reversible capacity of 197 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, and its capacity could maintain 99.1 % of its initial value even after 350 cycles of 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. Moreover, after going through 1200 cycles at a current density of 5 C, the Na‐CPP electrode still retained a capacity rate of 89.9 %. In contrast, Na‐CPN exhibited inferior capacity and rate performance because of its larger polarization, particle size, and charge transport resistance.</p></abstract><abstract type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p><b>Organic anodes</b>: Na‐CPP, an organic carboxylate containing phenylpyridine, is used as the anode of sodium‐ion batteries. This battery demonstrates good cycling stability with 99.1 % capacity retention after 350 cycles and a high reversible capacity of 197 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18645631:media:cssc202100872:cssc202100872-toc-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/cssc202100872-toc-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="cssc202100872-sec-0001" numbered="no"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) are regarded as a potential replacement for lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs) owing to the abundant reserves and wide distribution of sodium.<link href="#cssc202100872-bib-0001"><sup>[1]</sup></link> Host materials in SIBs largely depend on transition‐metal compounds with limited reserves.<link href="#cssc202100872-bib-0005"><sup>[2]</sup></link> Several promising cathode materials have already been developed for SIBs, while the anode material remains the main obstacle for the development of SIBs.<link href="#cssc202100872-bib-0008"><sup>[3]</sup></link> Graphitic carbon is the most commonly used anode material in LIBs, but it is not suitable for SIBs because the intercalated sodium graphite material formed is unstable.<link href="#cssc202100872-bib-0012"><sup>[4]</sup></link> Due to the large radius of sodium ions (2.04 Å), traditional inorganic materials used in LIBs tend to show high chemical inertness to the insertion of sodium ions. A larger ion radius will destroy the rigid structure of the material, resulting in poor cycle performance.<link href="#cssc202100872-bib-0010 #cssc202100872-bib-0013"><sup>[3b,5]</sup></link> Sodium as metal anode is not applicable because it is quite reactive.<link href="#cssc202100872-bib-0014"><sup>[6]</sup></link> Hard carbon has high storage capacity and suitably low working potential, yet the mechanism of Na<sup>+</sup>‐ion insertion in hard carbons is still controversial.<link href="#cssc202100872-bib-0015"><sup>[7]</sup></link> Other anode materials for SIBs such as metal oxides,<link href="#cssc202100872-bib-0016"><sup>[8]</sup></link> selenides,<link href="#cssc202100872-bib-0019"><sup>[9]</sup></link> sulfides,<link href="#cssc202100872-bib-0023"><sup>[10]</sup></link> and alloys<link href="#cssc202100872-bib-0024"><sup>[11]</sup></link> usually contribute to high capacity; however, the serious particle agglomeration during the cycle process usually leads to a fast capacity decay.<link href="#cssc202100872-bib-0020 #cssc202100872-bib-0025"><sup>[9a,12]</sup></link> Therefore, exploring highly efficient electrode material is still urgent and challenging for the development of SIBs.</p><p>Redox‐active organic materials containing carbonyl groups or N‐heterocycles have been reported as promising anode materials for SIBs.<link href="#cssc202100872-bib-0026"><sup>[13]</sup></link> They possess potential advantages such as good electrochemical performance, low cost, recyclability, and structural diversity.<link href="#cssc202100872-bib-0030"><sup>[14]</sup></link> Particularly, unlike the inorganic solids/crystals that are arranged by ionic bonds, the main interaction in organic materials is van der Waals forces.<link href="#cssc202100872-bib-0034"><sup>[15]</sup></link> The non‐covalent interaction in organic anode materials could provide more void room as well as low energy barriers for the accommodation of most metal ions without size concern. For example, the sodium carboxylate compounds are regarded as anodes with promising commercial prospects owing to the suitable operation voltage. Park et al. reported high‐performance SIBs using a small organic molecule, disodium terephthalate (Na<sub>2</sub>TP, Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>8</sub>H<sub>4</sub>O<sub>4</sub>).<link href="#cssc202100872-bib-0035"><sup>[16]</sup></link> Choi et al. evaluated novel 4,4’‐biphenyldicarboxylate (bpdc)‐sodium salts as anodes for SIBs and elucidated the correlation between the structural properties and their electrochemical performances.<link href="#cssc202100872-bib-0036"><sup>[17]</sup></link> Furthermore, pyridine‐based compounds have been reported as electrode materials.<link href="#cssc202100872-bib-0037"><sup>[18]</sup></link> Padhy et al. have shown that the substitution of nitrogen atoms in the benzene ring of terephthalate led to an effect similar to n‐type doping, thereby increasing the conductivity and electrochemical performance.<link href="#cssc202100872-bib-0040"><sup>[19]</sup></link></p><p>Although organic electrode materials have exhibited intriguing advantages, there are still problems that need to be solved.<link href="#cssc202100872-bib-0041"><sup>[20]</sup></link> Most carbonyls with C=O bonds are insulating, preventing the further improvement of SIB performance.<link href="#cssc202100872-bib-0047"><sup>[21]</sup></link> Even worse, most of the previously reported organic SIBs were tested in a very slow charge/discharge process, and those tested under fast charge/discharge process often caused significant performance degradation. However, rational molecule engineering may well address the above concerns. For example, the introduction of biphenyl ligands with extended molecular lengths facilitates the insertion and extraction of Na<sup>+</sup>, thereby improving cycle performance.<link href="#cssc202100872-bib-0048"><sup>[22]</sup></link> The electron‐deficient pyridine units impart the corresponding molecule with high electron affinity, thus improving the electronic conductivity of electrode materials.<link href="#cssc202100872-bib-0040 #cssc202100872-bib-0049"><sup>[19,23]</sup></link> In addition, it has been reported that π‐conjugated compounds with heteroatoms (N, S, O) have good redox activity and can be used as active materials for rechargeable batteries.<link href="#cssc202100872-bib-0050"><sup>[24]</sup></link></p><p>Herein, we designed two isomers, namely sodium 6‐(4‐carboxyphenyl)nicotinate (Na‐CPN) and sodium 5‐(4‐carboxyphenyl)picolinate (Na‐CPP) (Scheme <link href="#cssc202100872-fig-5001">1</link>). They are differentiated by the position of N atoms on the phenylpyridine moiety. The N atom at different substitution positions brings about different electronic properties of the two compounds, and the different stacking modes lead to different particle sizes, which will ultimately affect the electrochemical properties of sodium phenylpyridine dicarboxylate. Compared with Na‐CPN, the galvanostatic results and rate performance of Na‐CPP in Na<sup>+</sup>‐ion half‐cells are more satisfactory. Na‐CPP provides a high reversible capacity of 197 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, maintaining 99.1 % after 350 cycles of 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. Furthermore, during the long‐term cycling, high capacity retention of 89.9 % after 1200 cycles was achieved on the Na‐CPP electrode at 5 C. In contrast, Na‐CPN exhibited inferior capacity performance and rate performance for its larger polarization, particle size, and charge transport resistance. The performances of our anode materials are compared with other reported organic anodes, as shown in Table S1 in the Supporting Information. The results demonstrate that the electrochemical performance of Na‐CPP is impressive. Moreover, all‐organic sodium‐ion full cells composed of Na‐CPP anode and a commercial organic cathode were successfully fabricated, and the results suggest potential application of Na‐CPP in sustainable SIBs. Our work provides a general study on the effect of isomers as anode materials toward electrochemical performance of SIBs.</p><figure xml:id="cssc202100872-fig-5001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Scheme</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18645631:media:cssc202100872:cssc202100872-fig-5001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/cssc202100872-fig-5001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Preparation and reversible electrochemical redox mechanism of Na‐CPN and Na‐CPP.</p></caption></figure></section><section xml:id="cssc202100872-sec-0002" numbered="no"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p>The target products, Na‐CPN and Na‐CPP, were synthesized via the alkalization reaction between CPN [6‐(4‐carboxyphenyl) nicotinic acid, C<sub>13</sub>H<sub>9</sub>NO<sub>4</sub>]/CPP [5‐(4‐carboxyphenyl) picolinic acid, C<sub>13</sub>H<sub>9</sub>NO<sub>4</sub>] and NaOH, and the products were obtained from precipitation in ethanol (Scheme S1). All the structures of the newly obtained compounds are confirmed by <sup>1</sup>H/<sup>13</sup>C nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) spectroscopy. The target products are characterized by powder X‐ray diffraction (XRD), Fourier transform infrared (FTIR) spectroscopy, Elemental analysis (EA), and field‐emission scanning electron microscopy (FESEM).</p><p>The FTIR spectra are shown in Figure <link href="#cssc202100872-fig-0001">1</link>a. The spectra of CPN and CPP showed C=O stretching vibration and O−H bending vibrations of carboxylic acid (−COOH) groups around 1686, 1290, and 938 cm<sup>−1</sup>. After the formation of the sodium carboxylate (−COO−Na) via complete deprotonation by NaOH, the carbonyl (C=O) stretching vibration in Na‐CPN and Na‐CPP shifted to 1589 and 1603 cm<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. Moreover, the two peaks of O−H bending vibrations for the carboxylic acid groups near 1290 and 938 cm<sup>−1</sup> disappeared.<link href="#cssc202100872-bib-0051"><sup>[25]</sup></link> These changes indicate that the −COOH group has been converted to the sodium carboxylate group (−COO−Na) after alkalization with NaOH.</p><figure xml:id="cssc202100872-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18645631:media:cssc202100872:cssc202100872-fig-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/cssc202100872-fig-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>(a) FTIR spectra of CPN, Na‐CPN, CPP, and Na‐CPP. (b) TGA of Na‐CPN and Na‐CPP.</p></caption></figure><p>We compared the powdered XRD patterns of Na‐CPN and Na‐CPP (Supporting Information, Figure S6). Diffraction peaks of both substances are clearly observed, indicating that they are not in an amorphous state and have specific crystal structures. FESEM characterization can intuitively reflect the morphology and particle size. It can be seen from Figure S7 that Na‐CPN represented a regular block morphology, with a large size between 1–3 μm. This may be due to the intramolecular hydrogen bonding interaction between N atom on pyridine and H on the adjacent phenyl ring, leading to a small dihedral angle and facilitating strong molecular stacking, so that large crystals are formed. The particle size of Na‐CPP is at the nanometer level, smaller than that of Na‐CPN, which is very useful for the dynamic transport of electrons and ions in batteries.<link href="#cssc202100872-bib-0036 #cssc202100872-bib-0049 #cssc202100872-bib-0054"><sup>[17,23, 26]</sup></link> The thermal stability of Na‐CPN and Na‐CPP was investigated by thermogravimetric analysis (TGA). From the weight loss rate of 5 %, the thermal decomposition temperatures of Na‐CPN and Na‐CPP are determined to be 657 and 631 °C, respectively (Figure <link href="#cssc202100872-fig-0001">1</link>b). Both compounds show good thermal stability, which is a key factor for rechargeable batteries.</p><p>Considering that Na‐CPN and Na‐CPP have extended conjugated systems and carboxylate functional groups, they were applied as the anodes of SIBs. Cyclic voltammograms (CV), ex‐situ XRD, ex‐situ FTIR spectroscopy, and ex‐situ X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) were employed to study the reversibility and mechanism of the electrochemical reactions of Na‐CPN and Na‐CPP. The multi‐scanned CV of Na‐CPP and Na‐CPN were carried out to study their electrochemical behaviors in Na‐ion half cells, as shown in Figure <link href="#cssc202100872-fig-0002">2</link>a and Figure S8a, respectively. In the first scan (Figure <link href="#cssc202100872-fig-0002">2</link>a), Na‐CPP showed two reduction peaks, and the broad peak around 0.75 V (vs. Na<sup>+</sup>/Na) was assigned to the irreversible formation of the solid‐electrolyte interphase (SEI). Except for the first cycle, a pair of symmetrical redox peaks at 0.47/0.63 V can be observed, and they can completely overlap in the second to fifth cycles, revealing good reversibility. In the first scan of the Na‐CPN electrode, the 0.37 V peak is attributed to sodiated form of the electrode.<link href="#cssc202100872-bib-0035 #cssc202100872-bib-0055"><sup>[16,27]</sup></link> Except for the first cycle, Na‐CPN showed one couple of redox peaks at 0.4/0.66 V. It is worth noting that, compared with Na‐CPP, the CV curve of Na‐CPN has poor symmetry (Figure S8a). This probably due to the large polarization and resistance of Na‐CPN.</p><figure xml:id="cssc202100872-fig-0002"><label>2</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18645631:media:cssc202100872:cssc202100872-fig-0002"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/cssc202100872-fig-0002-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>(a) CV curves of the Na‐CPP anode. (b) Ex‐situ FTIR and (c) ex‐situ XRD results for the samples taken at different states as marked by a (pristine), b (0.5 V discharged), c (0.1 V discharged), d (0.65 V charged), and e (2.5 V charged).</p></caption></figure><p>To further explore the reaction mechanism of Na‐CPN and Na‐CPP, ex‐situ FTIR analysis was conducted at selected potentials (owing to air exposure the hypothesis presented hereby might have some inconsistencies.). The ex‐situ FTIR spectrum of Na‐CPP is shown in Figure <link href="#cssc202100872-fig-0002">2</link>b; the strong vibration absorption nearing 1597 cm<sup>−1</sup> is attributed to the C=O stretching vibration of the carboxylate group. On discharging to 0.4 V (Figure <link href="#cssc202100872-fig-0002">2</link>b, curve b), the C=O signal intensity is weakened, and the =C−O peak stretching vibration belonging to the enolate appears at 1230 cm<sup>−1</sup>.<link href="#cssc202100872-bib-0056"><sup>[28]</sup></link> When fully discharged (Figure <link href="#cssc202100872-fig-0002">2</link>b, curve c), the C=O signal almost disappears and transforms into =C−O stretching vibration. During the recharging process, the C=O signal peak gradually appeared, and the stretching vibration of the =C‐O peak belonging to the enolate disappeared. After fully charging, the Na‐CPP signals shift back to their initial state (Figure <link href="#cssc202100872-fig-0002">2</link>b, curve e). Therefore, the above observations confirm that C=O groups are active sites during the charge and discharge process. As shown in Figure S8b, the ex‐situ FTIR spectrum of Na‐CPN showed the same results as Na‐CPP, suggesting that the different positions of the N atom did not affect its redox mechanism.</p><p>The evolving crystal structures of Na‐CPN and Na‐CPP upon electrochemical cycling are closely interrelated to the cycling stability of the material. To explain the structural evolution of Na‐CPP and Na‐CPN upon Na<sup>+</sup> insertion/extraction, we gathered the electrodes at different potentials during the first discharge–charge cycle. The ex‐situ XRD pattern of Na‐CPP at different potentials is shown in Figure <link href="#cssc202100872-fig-0002">2</link>c (owing to air exposure the hypothesis presented hereby might have some inconsistencies). In the pristine state (Figure <link href="#cssc202100872-fig-0002">2</link>c, curve a), Na‐CPP has characteristic peaks at 16.7, 29.2, 32.4, and 33.9°. After being fully discharged (Figure <link href="#cssc202100872-fig-0002">2</link>c, curve a–c), these peaks almost disappeared, and an amorphous state was observed from the XRD result. After the subsequent desodiation (Figure <link href="#cssc202100872-fig-0002">2</link>c, curve c–e), the characteristic peaks are almost restored, and the XRD curve is basically restored to the original state. In addition, the XRD pattern of the Na‐CPP anode after multiple charges–discharge cycles is almost the same as the XRD pattern in the first cycle (Figure S9), indicating that Na‐CPP and its redox products have good structural stability, and its crystal structures are not destroyed during the Na<sup>+</sup> insertion/extraction process. In contrast, from the ex‐situ XRD of Na‐CPN (Figure S8c), some peaks cannot be completely restored to the original state after being fully charged, which may be due to the larger particle size of Na‐CPN.</p><p>We further carried out XPS measurements on Na‐CPP to prove its redox mechanism. For C 1 s spectra (Figure <link href="#cssc202100872-fig-0003">3</link>a), we could clearly identify the C=O signal in the pristine electrode, and its binding energy of approximately 288 eV. When discharged to 0.1 V, the strength of the C=O groups weakened with the C−O groups′ increase (≈286 eV), which indicates that the C=O bond is reduced to a C−O bond to form sodium enolate. Upon recharging, the peak intensity of the C=O bond increased with the decrease of the C−O bond peak intensity. This storage mechanism can be reconfirmed by the O 1 s spectrum (Figure <link href="#cssc202100872-fig-0003">3</link>b), which is a reversible transition from C=O (≈533 eV) to C−O bond (≈531.8 eV). The result is consistent with the mechanism proposed in Scheme <link href="#cssc202100872-fig-5001">1</link>.</p><figure xml:id="cssc202100872-fig-0003"><label>3</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18645631:media:cssc202100872:cssc202100872-fig-0003"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/cssc202100872-fig-0003-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Ex‐situ (a) C 1 s and b) O 1 s XPS spectra of the Na‐CPP electrode in various states: pristine, discharged to 0.1 V and recharged to 2.5 V.</p></caption></figure><p>Inspired by the reversibility of the above structure and electrochemistry, the cycling performance of Na‐CPN and Na‐CPP was further investigated. As shown in Figure <link href="#cssc202100872-fig-0004">4</link>a, the specific capacity during the first cycle is higher than that in the subsequent cycles, and the initial coulombic efficiencies (CE) of Na‐CPN and Na‐CPP are significantly different at 69 and 82 %, respectively. This most probably can be attributed to the different particle sizes, irreversible formation of SEI, and other side reactions. Apart from the first cycle, Na‐CPN and Na‐CPP showed high discharge specific capacities of 204.1 and 197.2 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively, which was very close to its theoretical specific capacity value (<i>C</i><sub>T</sub>=187 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>) after subtracting the dedication (≈20 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>) from carbon black (Super P, Figure S10). It is worth mentioning that Na‐CPP showed excellent cycle performance. Remarkably, the capacity retention rate was maintained to be 99.1 % even after 350 cycles. The data is rarely seen in previously reported works using small organic molecules as anodes. The retention rate of Na‐CPN after 350 cycles was 79 %. The decrease in battery capacity is attributed to various factors, such as the dissolution of organic electrode materials and battery polarization.</p><figure xml:id="cssc202100872-fig-0004"><label>4</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18645631:media:cssc202100872:cssc202100872-fig-0004"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/cssc202100872-fig-0004-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>(a) Cycling performance and coulombic efficiency of Na‐CPN and Na‐CPP at a current density of 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. (b) Rate capability of Na‐CPN and Na‐CPP at different current densities. (c) Long‐term cyclability of Na‐CPN and Na‐CPP at a current density of 5 C (935 mA g<sup>−1</sup>).</p></caption></figure><p>Interestingly, we found that Na‐CPN had a higher capacity than Na‐CPP in the previous cycles. So their capacity measured at different current densities was tested. From Figure <link href="#cssc202100872-fig-0004">4</link>b, Na‐CPN and Na‐CPP showed similar results at different current densities. Although Na‐CPN shows higher capacity in the first several cycles, Na‐CPP exhibits the best rate capability with a reversible capacity of 159.8 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 10 C (1870 mA g<sup>−1</sup>) and a reversible capacity of 213.1 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> when back to 0.1 C, indicating the strong reaction kinetics of Na‐CPP. Moreover, the galvanostatic charge/discharge curves of Na‐CPP at various current densities are similar without apparent polarization (Figure S11b), indicating Na‐CPP undergoes a stable redox reaction (Figure S11a). The high capacity and superior rate performance contribute to the high power density as well as high energy density of Na‐CPP in SIBs.</p><p>Long‐term stability of Na‐CPP and Na‐CPN at high current densities could also be achieved. Their long‐term cycling stability is shown in Figure <link href="#cssc202100872-fig-0004">4</link>c. For Na‐CPN, a high capacity of 111.5 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> can be maintained after 1200 cycles, and the capacity retention rate is 79.8 %. Remarkably, after 1200 cycles, Na‐CPP delivers a capacity of 129.8 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> and high capacity retention of 89.5 %, demonstrating high reversibility and cycle stability. As a result, Na‐CPP exhibited superior performance than Na‐CPN due to its smaller polarization.</p><p>The interfacial impedance characteristics of the Na‐CPN and the Na‐CPP electrodes were probed by electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS; Figure <link href="#cssc202100872-fig-0005">5</link>a,b), to study the diffusion of Na<sup>+</sup> in the electrolyte, and the migration of charges through SEI, as well as the diffusion in the electrode. The EIS data were fitted with equivalent circuits (Figure S12 and Table S2). As shown in Figure <link href="#cssc202100872-fig-0005">5</link>a, combined with the fitting results of the equivalent circle (Table S2), the charge transfer resistance (<i>R</i><sub>ct</sub>) of the pristine Na‐CPN electrode (<i>R</i><sub>ct</sub>=43.37 Ω) is significantly reduced after the first cycle (<i>R</i><sub>ct</sub>=27.96 Ω). After 50 cycles, the resistance increases (<i>R</i><sub>ct</sub>=35.89 Ω), whereas different trends for charge transfer resistance changes during cycles were observed for Na‐CPP. Although the resistance of the original Na‐CPP electrode (<i>R</i><sub>ct</sub>=33.25 Ω) decreases after the first cycle (<i>R</i><sub>ct</sub>=28.85 Ω), interestingly, little change is detected even after 50 charge–discharge cycles, suggesting the stability of the Na‐CPP electrode. The electrode/electrolyte interface resistance (<i>R</i><sub>f</sub>) of Na‐CPP decreases after cycling, owing to the optimized interface between electrode and electrolyte. Therefore, Na‐CPP has smaller <i>R</i><sub>ct</sub> and <i>R</i><sub>f</sub> than Na‐CPN, ensuring superior cycle performance and rate performance.</p><figure xml:id="cssc202100872-fig-0005"><label>5</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18645631:media:cssc202100872:cssc202100872-fig-0005"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/cssc202100872-fig-0005-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Complex‐plane plots of (a) Na‐CPN and (b) Na‐CPP after charging to 2.5 V under current density of 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup> in the frequency range from 0.01 Hz to 10<sup>5</sup> Hz.</p></caption></figure><p>The successful fabrication of full cells could reflect the potential applications of electrode materials. Therefore, further optimization for the all‐organic full cells is necessary and related work is in progress.</p><figure xml:id="cssc202100872-fig-0006"><label>6</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18645631:media:cssc202100872:cssc202100872-fig-0006"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/cssc202100872-fig-0006-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>(a) CV curves of the as‐prepared Na‐CPP anode and commercial DSR cathode (0.1 mV s<sup>−1</sup>), (b) voltage profiles (at 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup>), and (c) schematic illustration of the all‐organic sodium‐ion full cell.</p></caption></figure></section><section xml:id="cssc202100872-sec-0003" numbered="no"><title type="main">Conclusion</title><p>Two isomers based on dicarboxylated phenylpyridine were developed as anode materials for sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs). They have extended conjugated systems. The <i>ortho</i>‐ and <i>meta</i>‐position of the N atom on the phenylpyridine moiety affects the electrochemical properties of the two compounds. The ex‐situ tests of Fourier‐transform infrared spectroscopy, X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy, and X‐ray diffraction in combination with the analyses of cyclic voltammetry were implemented to ascertain the Na‐storage mechanism. Both sodium 5‐(4‐carboxyphenyl)picolinate (Na‐CPP) and sodium 6‐(4‐carboxyphenyl)nicotinate (Na‐CPN) exhibited clear and reversible discharge and charge platforms in Na‐ion half cells, which resulted from the redox behavior of organic <i>para</i>‐aromatic dicarboxylates. For Na‐CPP, a high reversible capacity of 197 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> is delivered and remains 99.1 % after 350 cycles at 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. Impressively, high capacity retention of 89.9 % after 1200 cycles has been achieved on the Na‐CPP electrode at 5 C. Our results demonstrate that rational molecular structure engineering is essential and important to promote anode performance. Due to the structural diversity, organic electrode materials have potential to be applied in the next‐generation rechargeable batteries.</p></section><section xml:id="cssc202100872-sec-0004" numbered="no"><title type="main">Experimental Section</title><section xml:id="cssc202100872-sec-0005" numbered="no"><title type="main">General information</title><p>Unless otherwise stated, all starting materials were purchased from commercial suppliers. See the Supporting Information for the experimental details of the synthetic route.</p></section><section xml:id="cssc202100872-sec-0006" numbered="no"><title type="main">Electrochemical tests</title><p>For the half cell, the working electrodes were prepared by casting the slurry onto a clean copper foil, where the slurry contained the active materials (70 wt%), carbon black (Super P, 20 wt%), and poly (vinylidene fluoride) binder (PVDF, 10 wt%). Then, the collector was dried overnight at 60 °C. The separator was Whatman glass fiber. The samples of electrochemically active materials were evaluated in 2032‐type coin cells with a Na disk as the counter electrode and 1.0 <sc>m</sc> NaCF<sub>3</sub>SO<sub>3</sub> (in TETRAGLYME=100 vol %, Duoduo chemical reagent Co., LTD, Suzhou). The average loading of active materials was 1.2–1.6 mg cm<sup>−2</sup>. For the full cell, the cathode electrode was prepared similarly as the anode electrode with commercial disodium rhodizonate as active material (except for using aluminum foil as the current collector). The capacity ratio of negative/positive (N/P) electrodes was set to slightly higher than 1.0. The specific capacity of full cells was based on the mass of the cathode only. Both cathode and anode were electrochemically pre‐sodiated for several cycles (at 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup>). All battery assembly and disassembly are performed in a dry Ar‐filled glovebox (H<sub>2</sub>O&lt;0.1 ppm, O<sub>2</sub>&lt;0.1 ppm, Mikrouna).The galvanostatic cycling test was carried on a CT‐4008T instrument (Shen Zhen NEWARE electronic Co.). CVs were tested on a CHI instrument electrochemical workstation (CHI 760) at a scan rate of 0.1 mV s<sup>−1</sup> between 0.1 and 2.5 V (vs. Na<sup>+</sup>/Na). The EIS test was measured by a CHI instrument electrochemical workstation in the frequency range of 10<sup>−2</sup>–10<sup>5</sup> Hz at the amplitude of 5 mV. All the tests were performed at room temperature.</p></section><section xml:id="cssc202100872-sec-0007" numbered="no"><title type="main">Ex‐situ FTIR spectroscopy, XRD, and XPS</title><p>In an argon‐filled glovebox, the electrode sheets were taken out of the batteries at different charging and discharging states, and then washed with 1,2‐Dimethoxyethane(DME) to remove the remaining electrolyte. It was then vacuum dried at 60 °C for 6 h. Then the marked electrodes were stored in an argon atmosphere. The samples were quickly taken out of the argon‐filled glovebox for each test, and these samples were exposed to air when conducting ex‐situ measurements. For ex‐situ FTIR tests, the products at different charge and discharge states were tested using the attenuated total reflectance (ATR) pattern. For ex‐situ XPS tests, the sample was prepared in a stream of Ar‐flow, and then the XPS measurement was conducted. For XPS, the CasaXPS software was used to perform peak fitting of the original data, and the Origin2018 software was used to plot the exported data. In the process of fitting, the full width at half‐maximum is limited to be between 1 and 2 eV.</p></section></section><section xml:id="cssc202100872-sec-0008" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>The work described in this paper was supported by the Fundamental Research Funds for the Central Universities (XDJK2020B002)</p></section><section xml:id="cssc202100872-sec-0009" type="conflictOfInterest" numbered="no"><title type="main">Conflict of interest</title><p>The authors declare no conflict of interest.</p></section><bibliography style="numbered"><bib xml:id="cssc202100872-bib-0001"><citation type="other" xml:id="cssc202100872-cit-0001"> </citation></bib><bib xml:id="cssc202100872-bib-0002"><label>1a</label><citation type="journal" xml:id="cssc202100872-cit-0002"><author><givenNames>S.</givenNames> <familyName>Guo</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>J.</givenNames> <familyName>Yi</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>Y.</givenNames> <familyName>Sun</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>H.</givenNames> <familyName>Zhou</familyName></author>, <journalTitle>Energy Environ.
