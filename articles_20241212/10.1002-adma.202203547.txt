3</caption></supportingInfoItem></supportingInformation><abstractGroup><abstract type="main" xml:lang="en"><title type="main">Abstract</title><p>Sodium storage batteries are one of the ever‐increasing next‐generation large‐scale energy storage systems owing to the abundant resources and low cost. However, their viability is severely hampered by dendrite‐related hazards on anodes. Herein, a novel ultrathin (8 µm) exterior‐nonporous separator composed of honeycomb‐structured fibers is prepared for homogeneous Na deposition and suppressed dendrite penetration. The unhindered ion transmission greatly benefits from honeycomb‐structured fibers with huge electrolyte uptake (376.7%) and the polymer's inherent transport ability. Additionally, polar polymer chains consisting of polyethersulfone and polyvinylidene customize the highly aggregated solvation structure of electrolytes via substantial solvent immobilization, facilitating ion‐conductivity‐enhanced inorganic‐rich solid‐electrolyte interphase with remarkable interface endurance. With the reliable mechanical strength of the separator, the assembled sodium‐ion full cell delivers significantly improved energy density and high safety, enabling stable operation under cutting and rolling. The as‐prepared separator can further be generalized to lithium‐based batteries for which apparent dendrite inhibition and cyclability are accessible and demonstrates its potential for practical application.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="adma202203547-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>A novel ultrathin exterior‐nonporous polymer separator composed of honeycomb‐structured fibers is prepared for high‐energy sodium storage batteries. It effectively suppresses Na dendrites and avoids the related safety risk, as well as shows the ability to customize the highly aggregated solvation structure of electrolytes via substantial solvent immobilization, thereby leading to ion‐conductivity‐enhanced inorganic‐rich solid electrolyte interphase and cathode electrolyte interphase.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202203547:adma202203547-gra-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202203547-gra-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body xml:id="adma202203547-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="adma202203547-sec-0010"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Sodium storage batteries, as resource‐abundant and cost‐effective next‐generation devices for grid‐scale energy storage, play a critical role in the ongoing transition toward a low‐emission and sustainable world.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202203547-bib-0001 #adma202203547-bib-0002 #adma202203547-bib-0003 #adma202203547-bib-0004"><sup>1–4</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Among them, sodium metal batteries hold the promise to achieve the high energy density but suffer from severe dendrite growth on Na metal anode. For sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs), the element‐rich carbon materials are considered as the preferred anode due to their low work platforms and high‐energy density.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202203547-bib-0005 #adma202203547-bib-0006"><sup>5,6</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, similar to Na metal, the irritating local sodium precipitation on carbon‐based anodes would lead to dendrite growth especially at high rates, resulting in visible safety risk and even catastrophic battery incidents.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202203547-bib-0007 #adma202203547-bib-0008"><sup>7,8</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Such detrimental issues severely threaten the practical application of SIBs. In the past decades, considerable efforts have been devoted to addressing the dendrite problems by designing and optimizing structure and engineering for electrode materials, electrolytes, and their interphases, respectively.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202203547-bib-0009 #adma202203547-bib-0010 #adma202203547-bib-0011 #adma202203547-bib-0012 #adma202203547-bib-0013"><sup>9–13</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>Separator, as an indispensable component of batteries with liquid electrolytes, greatly affects the ion transfer and acts as a final physical barrier to prevent short circuits.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202203547-bib-0014 #adma202203547-bib-0015"><sup>14,15</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The macropore in commercially available separator (e.g., polypropylene (PP) and glass fiber (GF)) allow inhomogeneous electric field and ion transfer, enabling local dendrite growth around pores and eventual penetration.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202203547-bib-0016 #adma202203547-bib-0017 #adma202203547-bib-0018"><sup>16–18</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Improving the tolerance of the separator toward dendrites is regarded as a promising way to extend batteries’ cyclability via compositing the ceramic particles like SiO<sub>2</sub> and Al<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>, but always sacrifices the energy density.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202203547-bib-0019 #adma202203547-bib-0020 #adma202203547-bib-0021 #adma202203547-bib-0022"><sup>19–22</sup></link><sup>]</sup> He et al. designed a novel nanoporous separator to tune the bulk and interfacial behavior of electrolyte for achieving the prior metal reduction and dendrite‐free batteries.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202203547-bib-0023"><sup>23</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, this nano structure also relies on the introduction of additional metal–organic framework materials. In this sense, the pore design of separator still needs to be explored to meet the demand for dendrite‐free batteries.</p><p>In principle, the dendrite grows along the pores in separator, especially longitudinal pores directly linking the cathode and anode, thus the removal of which will essentially eliminate dendrite formation.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202203547-bib-0024 #adma202203547-bib-0025 #adma202203547-bib-0026"><sup>24–26</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Nevertheless, consolidated fiber surface inevitably removes channels for rapid ion transport, leading to large battery polarization and low capacity. For the high‐safety batteries, their separators design should balance the complicated relation between dendrite inhibition, ionic conductivity and other performance like mechanical/thermal stability simultaneously, therefore, remains highly challenging.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202203547-bib-0027 #adma202203547-bib-0028 #adma202203547-bib-0029"><sup>27–29</sup></link><sup>]</sup> For example, a composite gel polymer electrolyte nonporous membrane based on polymethyl methacrylate supporting layer and coated acetylene black have been constructed by Wu et al.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202203547-bib-0030"><sup>30</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, due to their limited mechanical strength and large thickness, these semisolid composite layers feature poor interface contact with electrodes, enabling irreversible ion plating/stripping and loose energy storage over long‐term cycles. Hitherto, no separator was able to reach satisfactory functionalization with the nonporous and ultrathin character and the thermal and mechanical stability enabling great practicability in high‐energy‐density and safe batteries.</p><p>Herein, a well‐designed ultrathin exterior‐nonporous separator (denoted as SPF) is prepared from raw materials polyethersulfone (PES) and polyvinylidene fluoride‐hexafluoropropylene (PVDF‐HFP) via dominating a synergistic effect between chemical components and structure. The prepared separator features not only excellent thermostability but also mechanical robustness. Its nonporous characteristic is powerful to suppress dendrites (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202203547-fig-0001"><b>1</b>A</link>). Additionally, the chemical interaction between polar SPF separator and the solvent enables solvent‐less solvation structure in electrolyte, inducing inorganic‐rich interphase formation and beneficial for uniform Na deposition. The unhindered ion transmission is greatly benefited from hydrogen bonds between PES and PVDF‐HFP constructed honeycomb‐like structures inside SPF's fibers, and amorphous region of the polymer chains, enabling enhanced binding energy with Na<sup>+</sup> confirmed by DFT calculation. Therefore, the batteries using the functionalized SPF separators deliver enhanced energy density and safety.</p><figure xml:id="adma202203547-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202203547:adma202203547-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202203547-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Structure and physical characterizations of SPF separator. G) Burning images of SPF and PP separators.</caption></figure></section><section xml:id="adma202203547-sec-0020"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p>Exterior‐nonporous SPF separator is fabricated by electrospinning with PES and PVDF‐HFP polymers, and subsequent rolling treatment (Figure <link href="#adma202203547-supitem-0001">S1</link>, Supporting Information). Within the process, the chemical stability and microstructure of as‐prepared SPF separators are controlled by regulating the proportion of raw materials and the rolled spacing (Figures <link href="#adma202203547-supitem-0001">S2</link> and <link href="#adma202203547-supitem-0001">S3</link>, Supporting Information). Through the optimizing on processing parameters, the raw materials’ ratio and rolled spacing of separators are determined to be 7: 3 (in mass) and 8 µm, respectively. The top‐view scanning electron microscopy (SEM) images depict a nonporous surface whatever fiber surface and gap at large magnification, in which the fibers are closely arranged together after simple rolling (Figure <link href="#adma202203547-fig-0001">1B</link>; Figure <link href="#adma202203547-supitem-0001">S4</link>, Supporting Information). It is also supported by poor air permeability with 3267 Gray seconds in 644 s under 1.22 kPa, compared with commercial PP and GF separators (Figure <link href="#adma202203547-supitem-0001">S5</link>, Supporting Information). The cross‐sectional image of SPF separator shows its thickness approached 8 µm (Figure <link href="#adma202203547-fig-0001">1C</link>), exhibiting a great advantage over other widely used commercial separators, such as PP separator (25 µm) and GF separator (380 µm).<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202203547-bib-0021 #adma202203547-bib-0031"><sup>21,31</sup></link><sup>]</sup> More crucially, honeycomb‐like pores with a diameter ranging from dozens to hundreds of nanometers exist inside the fibers and enable abundant space to accommodate more electrolyte but do not enlarge Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) surface area significantly (Figure <link href="#adma202203547-fig-0001">1D</link>; Figure <link href="#adma202203547-supitem-0001">S6</link>, Supporting Information). For comparison, the separators derived from PES or PVDF‐HFP both feature solid fiber structures (Figure <link href="#adma202203547-supitem-0001">S7</link>, Supporting Information).</p><p>The interaction between PES and PVDF‐HFP components in the as‐synthesized SPF separator is confirmed by UV–vis spectra, X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS), and density functional theory (DFT) calculation (Figure <link href="#adma202203547-fig-0001">1E</link>; Figure <link href="#adma202203547-supitem-0001">S8</link>, Supporting Information). After compositing PES and PVDF‐HFP components, obtained SPF separator with well‐distributed elements shows red shifts of the sulfone group (at λ = 216, 230 nm) and C–F band (around λ = 280 nm), which are related to their enhanced conjugation. Simultaneously, decreased binding energy of C–F band in F 1s spectrum of SPF separator verifies that the fluorine atom's electrons in PVDF‐HFP molecules delocalize onto the hydrogen of phenyl group in PES. DFT calculation further presents the two‐type hydrogen bonds (C–H···F and C–H···O) formed between PES and PVDF‐HFP molecules, which results in stable intermolecular interactions (Table <link href="#adma202203547-supitem-0001">S1</link>, Supporting Information). Therefore, it is reasonably deduced that the increased steric hindrance between the two‐kind polymer chains may prevent SPF fibers from aggregating compactly and facilitate the formation of internal honeycomb‐like pores (Figure <link href="#adma202203547-fig-0001">1F</link>). Regrettably, limited by characterization conditions, direct observation on the formation and evolution of fiber microstructures during electrospinning process is elusive.</p><p>Additionally, the thermal and mechanical stability of SPF separator is significantly benefited from stable intermolecular interactions, enabling excellent dimensional stability until 240 °C without distinguishable shrinkage (Figure <link href="#adma202203547-supitem-0001">S9</link>, Supporting Information) and superhigh tensile strength (72.7 MPa) and Young's modulus (1211.0 MPa) (Figure <link href="#adma202203547-supitem-0001">S10A</link>, Supporting Information), which can decrease the rupture risks from accidental collision and dendrite penetration.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202203547-bib-0032"><sup>32</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Furthermore, as a typical anti‐static polyether, PES polymer can accelerate the escape of surface charge, thereby reducing the interface resistance of fibers in SPF separators.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202203547-bib-0033"><sup>33</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Therefore, the repulsive forces between PES‐containing polymer fibers can be effectively reduced, enabling the SPF separator greater mechanical stability. During the burning tests, the SPF separator also self‐extinguishes quickly, whereas the commercial PP separator burns out entirely (Figure <link href="#adma202203547-fig-0001">1G</link>; Movies <link href="#adma202203547-supitem-0002">S1</link> and <link href="#adma202203547-supitem-0003">S2</link>, Supporting Information). Figure <link href="#adma202203547-supitem-0001">S11</link> (Supporting Information) further shows the digital photos of the robust and flexible SPF separator with potential scalability.</p><p>Having constructed the new polymer skeleton at the molecular level, the ion transfer mechanism of exterior‐nonporous SPF separator is further investigated. In 1 <sc>m</sc> NaClO<sub>4</sub> ethylene carbonate (EC)/propylene carbonate (PC) with 5wt% fluoroethylene carbonate (FEC) electrolyte, SPF, PVDF‐HFP, and PES separators all show good affinity to the electrolyte with low contact angles of almost 0° (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202203547-fig-0002"><b>2</b>A</link>; Figure <link href="#adma202203547-supitem-0001">S12</link>, Supporting Information). When evaluated in other electrolytes, namely, 1 <sc>m</sc> NaClO<sub>4</sub> EC/diethyl carbonate (DEC), 1 <sc>m</sc> LiPF<sub>6</sub> EC/dimethyl carbonate (DMC)/ ethyl methyl carbonate (EMC), and 1 <sc>m</sc> LiPF<sub>6</sub> EC/DMC, SPF separator still maintains excellent wettability with negative Δ<i>G</i> values because of the encouraged interaction between polar and charged particles (–SO<sub>2</sub>– and Na<sup>+</sup>) by the intense polarization of PES molecule and the promoted ion dissociation by PVDF‐HFP molecule with high dielectric constant,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202203547-bib-0034"><sup>34</sup></link><sup>]</sup> which mean that a stable solid‐liquid interface is much easier to form (Figures <link href="#adma202203547-supitem-0001">S10B</link> and <link href="#adma202203547-supitem-0001">S13</link>, Supporting Information). Received good wettability and internal porous structure in fibers blessed, taking the 1 <sc>m</sc> NaClO<sub>4</sub> EC/PC with 5 wt% FEC electrolyte as an example, SPF separator displays a formidable electrolyte uptake (375.3%±2.7%, Figure <link href="#adma202203547-fig-0002">2B,C</link>) to ensure reversible ion migration,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202203547-bib-0035 #adma202203547-bib-0036"><sup>35,36</sup></link><sup>]</sup> whose immobilized electrolyte is further demonstrated by Cryo‐transmission electron microscopy (Cryo‐TEM) image (Figure <link href="#adma202203547-fig-0002">2D</link>). Furthermore, although the mechanical properties of immersed SPF separator become worse due to weakened intermolecular interactions between PES and PVDF‐HFP polymers, it is still superior to the commercial PP separator and fully meets the typical requirements for battery operations (Figure <link href="#adma202203547-supitem-0001">S10B</link>, Supporting Information). Meanwhile, the immersed SPF separator can promote the close contact between electrodes and electrolytes via the gelling of PVDF‐HFP polymers. As shown in Figure <link href="#adma202203547-fig-0002">2E</link>, symmetric Na|SPF|Na cell exhibits a smaller interface impedance (1214 Ω) than those of cells with PVDF‐HFP and PES separators, even GF separator (1500 Ω, Figure <link href="#adma202203547-supitem-0001">S14</link>, Supporting Information), supporting the faster ion transport kinetics and electrolyte infiltration through separators.</p><figure xml:id="adma202203547-fig-0002"><label>2</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202203547:adma202203547-fig-0002" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202203547-fig-0002-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>The ion transfer mechanism in SPF separator. H) Schematic illustration of Na<sup>+</sup> transport through exterior‐nonporous SPF separator. The electrolyte is 1 <sc>m</sc> NaClO<sub>4</sub> EC/PC with 5 wt% FEC.</caption></figure><p>Additionally, distinct separator–electrolyte interaction is found due to polar groups in SPF polymers, which is demonstrated by an additional band around 3074 cm<sup>−1</sup> emerged in the Raman spectra of the SPF–Electrolyte (SPF separator immerses in electrolyte) and the shifts of C=O band to low frequency in Fourier transform infrared spectra (FTIR) (Figure <link href="#adma202203547-supitem-0001">S15</link>, Supporting Information). DFT calculation results show that, compared with ClO<sub>4</sub><sup>−</sup> anion, free EC, PC, and FEC molecules in electrolytes prefer to migrate to the gap region of SPF molecules and form a stable ring‐like structure (Figure <link href="#adma202203547-supitem-0001">S16</link> and Table <link href="#adma202203547-supitem-0001">S2</link>, Supporting Information). The reduced Δ<i>G</i> values indicate an energetically favorable process,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202203547-bib-0037 #adma202203547-bib-0038"><sup>37,38</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and the rich honeycomb‐structured pores in SPF fibers can provide plenty of space for adsorbed electrolytes. However, single PVDF‐HFP and PES molecules cannot bond with EC/PC molecules to fulfill stable ring‐like structures, forcing active C=O in solvents to be more exposed and freer (Figure <link href="#adma202203547-supitem-0001">S17</link> and Table <link href="#adma202203547-supitem-0001">S3</link>, Supporting Information). The large amounts of free solvent molecules in the electrolyte that are immobilized by the SPF molecules inside their pores disappear, promoting the transformation to solvent‐less aggregated solvation structure of electrolytes,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202203547-bib-0039 #adma202203547-bib-0040 #adma202203547-bib-0041"><sup>39–41</sup></link><sup>]</sup> which is demonstrated by FTIR (Figure <link href="#adma202203547-supitem-0001">S18</link>, Supporting Information). Consequently, as the activation time of SPF separator in the electrolyte is prolonged, more free solvents are immobilized by the SPF separator, which results in the increase in electrochemical stability (Figure <link href="#adma202203547-supitem-0001">S19</link>, Supporting Information). In such cases, the binding energy between Na<sup>+</sup> and solvents (EC/PC/FEC) in SPF–electrolyte structures is much higher than those in PES/PVDF‐HFP‐electrolyte structures, which means the greatly improved capture capacity of SPF to Na<sup>+</sup> with enhanced ion transport (Figure <link href="#adma202203547-fig-0002">2F,G</link>). Combined with the well‐maintained rich honeycomb pores in electrolytes and the increased amorphous regions of SPF separator (Figure <link href="#adma202203547-supitem-0001">S20</link>, Supporting Information), effective ion transport channels can be established along with polymer chain and internal pores, which allows moderate ion conductivity for exterior‐nonporous SPF separator (Figure <link href="#adma202203547-fig-0002">2H</link>).</p><p>To further confirm the critical influence of SPF on electrochemistry, the component difference of interphase on Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>/C cathode and Na anode are compared for electrolytes based on SPF and GF separators, which largely depends on the Na<sup>+</sup>–solvation structures of electrolytes (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202203547-fig-0003"><b>3</b>A</link>).<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202203547-bib-0042 #adma202203547-bib-0043"><sup>42,43</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In a solvent‐less solvation structure induced by SPF separator, ClO<sub>4</sub><sup>−</sup> anion will participate more actively in interfacial reactions and generate more abundant Cl‐based derivatives. As shown in Figure <link href="#adma202203547-fig-0003">3B,E</link>, the higher Cl ratio in the survey spectra supports this conjecture, both in the cathode electrolyte interphase (CEI) formed on Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>/C cathode and the solid electrolyte interphase (SEI) formed on Na anode. In the high‐resolution XPS spectra, the C–C/C–H, C–O and CO<sub>3</sub><sup>2−</sup>/C=O peaks are attributed common SEI components such as sodium alkyl carbonate or sodium carbonate (Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub>) and polyester. And –CF<i><sub>x</sub></i> and NaF are the characteristic of FEC‐decomposed species. Compared with the CEI using GF separator, more ClO<sub>3</sub><sup>−</sup> intermediate product and NaCl appear during oxidation process of the electrolyte, constituting an inorganic‐richer interphase on cathode in SPF‐involved cells (Figure <link href="#adma202203547-fig-0003">3C,D</link>). Similarly, more NaCl is observed from the decomposition of ClO<sub>4</sub><sup>−</sup> anions in SEI formed in symmetrical Na|Na cell using the SPF separator (Figure <link href="#adma202203547-fig-0003">3F,G</link>). In view of high mechanical stability and lower barriers for ion transfer of inorganic crystals,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202203547-bib-0044 #adma202203547-bib-0045 #adma202203547-bib-0046"><sup>44–46</sup></link><sup>]</sup> the inorganic‐rich SEI/CEI component introduced by SPF separator can decrease interfacial resistance, which help to stabilize the electrode interface and facilitate dendrite‐free Na plating and stripping.</p><figure xml:id="adma202203547-fig-0003"><label>3</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202203547:adma202203547-fig-0003" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202203547-fig-0003-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Interface chemistry of electrodes with SPF separators. High‐resolution XPS spectra from F) Na|SPF|Na and G) Na|GF|Na cells after 60 h cycling at 1 mA cm<sup>−2</sup>.</caption></figure><p>The dendrite‐suppressed function of the exterior‐nonporous SPF separator on anode is further investigated. Here, we adopt more active Na|Na symmetric cell model, considering that the hard carbon electrodes have obvious dendrites until long‐time cycling. Taking Na|SPF|Na cell as an example, a smaller polarization voltage is supplied with a stable overpotential of 170.8 mV after 270 h at the capacity of 0.25 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup> (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202203547-fig-0004"><b>4</b>A</link>). However, after 146 h, the Na|GF|Na cell becomes unstable that the voltage curves increase abruptly to 1.0 V, which is ascribed to Na dendrite growth accompanied by increased interface resistance. As shown in Figure <link href="#adma202203547-fig-0004">4B</link> and Figure <link href="#adma202203547-supitem-0001">S21</link> (Supporting Information), the Na foil matched with SPF separator maintains its smooth and tidy surface without any visible bulges, both from the top and side view. In comparison, large dendrite clumps grow on the Na foil's surface in the cell with GF separator. Similarly, the unrolled SPF separator (50 µm thick) with loose porous structure delivers an unstable coulombic efficiency after several cycles owing to the large pores across the fibers that allow Na dendrite penetration (Figure <link href="#adma202203547-supitem-0001">S22</link>, Supporting Information). Consequently, it highly affirms the influential contributions made by smooth and nonporous surface (Figure <link href="#adma202203547-fig-0004">4C</link>), which targets uniform ion flow through SPF separator, as well as inhibiting metal dendrite generation, by reducing the overpotential and local current.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202203547-bib-0025 #adma202203547-bib-0030"><sup>25,30</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><figure xml:id="adma202203547-fig-0004"><label>4</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202203547:adma202203547-fig-0004" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202203547-fig-0004-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>The characterization of SPF separator‐suppressed dendrite. C) Atomic force microscope images of SPF and GF separators.</caption></figure><p>Ion/metal batteries coupled with different electrodes are applied to verify the SPF separator's practical performance. The Na|SPF|Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>/C cell displays an initial discharge capacity of 89.7±0.8 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 1 C (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202203547-fig-0005"><b>5</b>A</link>; Figure <link href="#adma202203547-supitem-0001">S23</link>, Supporting Information). After 500 cycles, it remains 89.1 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> with negligible capacity fading (99.3±1.3%). Importantly, a high average coulombic efficiency of 99.75% is achieved, indicating highly reversible Na plating/stripping.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202203547-bib-0047 #adma202203547-bib-0048"><sup>47,48</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Additionally, the temperature‐dependent stability of the SPF separator allows the exploration of stable performance at high and low temperatures (60 and −15 °C) to confirm the dynamics at different rates (Figure <link href="#adma202203547-supitem-0001">S24</link>, Supporting Information).</p><figure xml:id="adma202203547-fig-0005"><label>5</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202203547:adma202203547-fig-0005" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202203547-fig-0005-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Applications of SPF separators for sodium storage batteries. Thus, various full cells containing the SPF separators can be assembled and stably cycling. For example, commercial hard carbon is utilized as an anode to match NaNi<sub>1/3</sub>Fe<sub>1/3</sub>Mn<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> cathode (Figure <link href="#adma202203547-fig-0005">5C</link>), and the obtained full cell exhibits the reversible capacity of 121.4 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at the second cycle (average charge and discharge voltage are 3.38 and 3.15 V, respectively). Based on the mass of active material in both electrodes without the consideration of separator, the mass energy density of such pouch cell with SPF separator is 229.4 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup>, which is substantially higher than that of the GF separator‐containing cell and reported recently literature (Table <link href="#adma202203547-supitem-0001">S4</link>, Supporting Information), proving the potential practical application prospect of SPF separator. When the proportion of separator in the practical cell is further considered, ultrathin SPF separator occupies less space and mass (SPF: 3.2 mm<sup>3</sup> and 5.8 mg, GF:152.0 mm<sup>3</sup> and 15.7 mg), thereby enabling a higher energy density of pouch cell, which is nearly 23 times and 2 times those of cell with GF separator in volume and mass energy density, respectively (Figure <link href="#adma202203547-fig-0005">5D</link>; Figure <link href="#adma202203547-supitem-0001">S26</link>, Supporting Information). While, PP separator, which is thinner than GF separator, used in pouch cell still delivers low capacity and energy density (Figure <link href="#adma202203547-supitem-0001">S27</link>, Supporting Information). Additionally, the full cell with SPF separator can light up 24 light‐emitting diode (LED) bulbs after blending 20 times (Figure <link href="#adma202203547-fig-0005">5E</link>; Movie <link href="#adma202203547-supitem-0004">S3</link>, Supporting Information). Even when cut in half, it still functions well and lights up LED stably.</p><p>To further confirm the generality of SPF separator in other battery systems, symmetric Li|Li cells are assembled to evaluate the growth of Li dendrite (Figure <link href="#adma202203547-supitem-0001">S28</link>, Supporting Information). A smaller polarization voltage with stable voltage curves was observed in Li|SPF|Li cell. After the initial 3 cycles, a smooth surface without Li dendrite formation was visually demonstrated in the Li foil using SPF separator, while the Li foil using PP separator becomes highly porous with loosely stacked bulky particles. Such a compact plating structure enables the superior stability of SPF separator towards Li metal, which highlights that the flatly nonporous surface of SPF separator is more favorable on inhibiting dendrite growth. Moreover, SPF separator can expand to be applied in Li<sub>4</sub>Ti<sub>5</sub>O<sub>12</sub>|LiFePO<sub>4</sub> cell with stable cycling over 100 cycles at 0.2 C and Li|LiFePO<sub>4</sub> cell to function for over 80 cycles at 1 C (Figure <link href="#adma202203547-supitem-0001">S29</link>, Supporting Information). Considering its multi‐functional advantages on significant electrolyte uptake, thermo‐dimensional stability, super mechanical properties, and ultrathin thickness (Table <link href="#adma202203547-supitem-0001">S5</link>, Supporting Information), exterior‐nonporous SPF separator provides a universal and promising route for the development of rechargeable batteries with high safety and dense energy.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202203547-sec-0030"><title type="main">Conclusion</title><p>In this work, an ultrathin exterior‐nonporous SPF separator prepared from PES and PVDF‐HFP is reported for dendrite‐free sodium storage batteries, whose nonporous characteristic not only effectively suppresses Na dendrite via homogeneous Na transfer but also strengthens the mechanical property to prevent dendrite penetration. The weak H‐bonds between PES and PVDF‐HFP polymers improves the thermal stability, and helps construct honeycomb‐like fibers to accommodate high electrolyte uptake (376.7%) and hence benefits the rapid ion transfer in exterior‐nonporous separator. On the other hand, their amorphous region of polymer chains with enhanced binding energy also promotes ion transport, which is demonstrated by DFT calculation. Moreover, this polar separator is capable to immobilize free solvent molecules (EC, PC, or FEC) to facilitate highly aggregated solvation structure in electrolytes and robust inorganic‐rich interphase formation, which is verified through XPS spectra on the SEI on Na metal and CEI on Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>/C cathode. Consequently, the prepared exterior‐nonporous separator enables dendrite‐free long‐lifespan of Na|Na symmetric cell over 270 h with a stable overpotential of 170.8 mV, and highly reversible Na plating/stripping with an average CE as high as 99.75% for 500 cycles. More impressively, the assembled hard carbon|NaNi<sub>1/3</sub>Fe<sub>1/3</sub>Mn<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> pouch cell reaches a higher energy density (separator free vs separator added: 229.4 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup>/157.6 Wh L<sup>−1</sup> vs 71.8 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup>/86.1 Wh L<sup>−1</sup>) and remarkable stable performance that allows it to operate even under cutting in air. The generality of SPF separators was also verified in lithium‐based batteries, for example, Li<sub>4</sub>Ti<sub>5</sub>O<sub>12</sub>|LiFePO<sub>4</sub> pouch cell with 97.4% capacity retention after 100 cycles and Li|LiFePO<sub>4</sub> cell without apparent capacity decay after 80 cycles. This work provides not only an ultrathin exterior‐nonporous separator for high‐energy‐density dendrite‐free batteries, but also sheds fresh light on new design strategy that integrates the design of separator and electrolyte.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202203547-sec-0040" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>X.L., J.Z., and X.G. contributed equally to this work.
