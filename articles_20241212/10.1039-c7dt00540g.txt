    </span><span class="bold italic">, Accepted 16th March 2017</span></div><p class="bold italic">First published on 16th March 2017</p><hr><div class="abstract"><h2>Abstract</h2><p>Sodium-ion batteries have attracted considerable attention in recent years. In order to promote the practical application of sodium-ion batteries, the electrochemical performances, such as specific capacity, reversibility, and rate capability of the anode materials, should be further improved. In this work, a Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/C composite with a well-ordered mesoporous structure is prepared <span class="italic">via</span> a facile co-impregnation method by using mesoporous silica SBA-15 as a hard template. When used as an anode material for sodium-ion batteries, the well-ordered mesoporous structure ensures fast mass transport kinetics. The presence of nano-sized Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small> particles confined within the carbon walls significantly enhances the specific capacity of the composite. The carbon walls in the composite act not only as an active material contributing to the specific capacity, but also as a conductive matrix improving the cycling stability of Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small> nanoparticles. As a result, the well-ordered mesoporous Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/C composite exhibits high specific capacity, excellent cycleability, and high rate capability. It is proposed that this simple co-impregnation method is applicable for the preparation of well-ordered mesoporous transition oxide/carbon composite electrode materials for high performance sodium-ion and lithium-ion batteries.</p></div><hr>
    
      
      <h2 id="sect264"><span class="a_heading">1. Introduction</span></h2>
      <span>Lithium-ion batteries (LIBs) with high energy density and long cycle life have been widely utilized as energy storage devices in portable electronics and electrical vehicles.<a title="Select to navigate to reference" href="#cit1"><sup><span class="sup_ref">1–4</span></sup></a> However, limited resources of lithium restrict their further applications in large-scale energy storage. Compared to Li, Na element is much abundant and cheap. Therefore, sodium-ion batteries (SIBs) with similar insertion chemistry to LIBs are regarded as a suitable alternative in electrical energy storage systems and have received considerable attention in recent years.<a title="Select to navigate to reference" href="#cit5"><sup><span class="sup_ref">5–9</span></sup></a> However, the radius of Na<small><sup>+</sup></small> (1.02 Å) is much larger than that of Li<small><sup>+</sup></small> (0.76 Å),<a title="Select to navigate to references" href="#cit10"><sup><span class="sup_ref">10</span></sup></a> leading to slow solid-state diffusion kinetics of Na ions and poor electrochemical performance of the electrode materials.<a title="Select to navigate to reference" href="#cit5"><sup><span class="sup_ref">5,11–13</span></sup></a> Thus, it is still highly desirable to design and fabricate SIB electrode materials with attractive electrochemical performance, such as high specific capacity, high rate capability and long cycle life.</span>
      <p class="otherpara">Two strategies are demonstrated to be efficient in improving the electrochemical performance of electrode materials. The first one is the introduction of a porous structure into the electrode materials.<a title="Select to navigate to reference" href="#cit14"><sup><span class="sup_ref">14–16</span></sup></a> The pore walls of a porous structure are usually within the nanometer scale, greatly shortening the electron and ion diffusion distance.<a title="Select to navigate to references" href="#cit17"><sup><span class="sup_ref">17</span></sup></a> Given the fact that the ion diffusion coefficient is constant for a particular electrode material, the shortened diffusion distance thus would improve the rate capability. Well-defined pores with tunable pore diameters and open pore channels can facilitate the penetration of electrolytes into the electrode and also provide extra space to accommodate the possible volume variation upon cycling, contributing to the rate and cycling performance. The large specific surface area of a porous structure would generate plenty of active electrode/electrolyte interfaces, facilitating the insertion/extraction of Na<small><sup>+</sup></small> ions. Many porous electrode materials with well-ordered mesoporous channels have been prepared by hard or soft templating methods and showed improved lithium ion storage properties.<a title="Select to navigate to reference" href="#cit18"><sup><span class="sup_ref">18–23</span></sup></a> The other strategy is based on the formation of composites with electronic conductive materials, such as carbon and conducting polymers.<a title="Select to navigate to reference" href="#cit24"><sup><span class="sup_ref">24–28</span></sup></a> The diffusion of the precursors of the active electrode materials into porous carbon matrixes is a popular method for the fabrication of such composites.<a title="Select to navigate to reference" href="#cit29"><sup><span class="sup_ref">29,30</span></sup></a> However, it is difficult to homogeneously disperse the nanoparticles of the active electrode materials within the pores of the carbon matrixes. Consequently, these randomly formed nanoparticles, particularly those aggregated on the inner surface, would block the porous channels of the carbon, impacting the mass transfer efficiency of the composite generated. Thus, the facile formation of porous composite electrode materials with well-ordered mesoporous structures is still highly desired.</p>
      <p class="otherpara">Metal oxides have been investigated as anode materials for LIBs for many years and are also considered as promising high performance anode materials for SIBs.<a title="Select to navigate to reference" href="#cit31"><sup><span class="sup_ref">31–34</span></sup></a> Among them, Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small> with high capacity, low cost, nontoxicity, and chemical stability have attracted considerable attention.<a title="Select to navigate to reference" href="#cit35"><sup><span class="sup_ref">35–37</span></sup></a> Herein, we developed a simple co-impregnation method for the preparation of metal oxides/carbon composites with well-ordered mesoporous structures. A mesoporous Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/C composite (M-Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/C) was prepared by penetration of a sol containing both Fe and C precursors into the mesopores of SBA-15. A mesoporous structure with Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small> nanoparticles confined within the carbon walls is obtained after the dissolution of the mesoporous silica template. Employed as an anode material for SIBs, the mesoporous Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/C composite shows a high specific capacity, excellent cycling stability, and high rate capability. This simple co-impregnation method is applicable for the preparation of other metal oxides/carbon composites with well-ordered mesoporous structures.</p>
    
    
      
      <h2 id="sect294"><span class="a_heading">2. Experimental section</span></h2>
      
        
        <h3 id="sect297"><span class="b_heading">2.1 Preparation of hard template SBA-15</span></h3>
        <span>The mesoporous silica SBA-15 was synthesized following the procedure reported in the literature.<a title="Select to navigate to references" href="#cit38"><sup><span class="sup_ref">38</span></sup></a> First, 5.3 g Pluronic P123 (EO<small><sub>20</sub></small>PPO<small><sub>70</sub></small>EO<small><sub>20</sub></small>) was dissolved in 200 mL of 1.6 M HCl at 40 °C. Then 11.3 g tetraethylorthosilicate was added slowly to the above solution with vigorous stirring. After being stirred at 40 °C for 24 h, the mixture was put into an autoclave and heated at 110 °C for 24 h. The filtered samples were dried and calcined at 600 °C in air to obtain the SBA-15 template.</span>
      
      
        
        <h3 id="sect305"><span class="b_heading">2.2 Synthesis of M-Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/C composite by a co-impregnation method</span></h3>
        <span>The typical co-impregnation procedure is as follows. First, 0.5 g Fe(NO<small><sub>3</sub></small>)<small><sub>3</sub></small>·9H<small><sub>2</sub></small>O and 1.25 g sucrose were dissolved in 5 mL of water containing 0.14 g H<small><sub>2</sub></small>SO<small><sub>4</sub></small>. Then this solution was incorporated into 1.0 g SBA-15 by sonication. The resulting mixture was heated in an oven at 100 °C for 6 h and subsequently at 160 °C for 12 h. The above impregnation process was repeated once with a solution containing Fe(NO<small><sub>3</sub></small>)<small><sub>3</sub></small>·9H<small><sub>2</sub></small>O (0.32 g), sucrose (0.8 g), and H<small><sub>2</sub></small>SO<small><sub>4</sub></small> (0.09 g). After the co-impregnation procedure, the obtained precursor composite was thermally treated at 600 °C for 5 h in N<small><sub>2</sub></small> with a heating ramp rate of 2 °C min<small><sup>−1</sup></small> to carbonize the whole composite. The resulting material was washed with hot 1 M NaOH solution several times to remove the hard template. After washing with distilled water and drying at 60 °C, a well-ordered mesoporous M-Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/C composite was obtained. Mesoporous M-Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/C composites with different Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small> contents can also be prepared by adjusting the amount of Fe precursors.</span>
      
      
        
        <h3 id="sect329"><span class="b_heading">2.3 Synthesis of Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/MC composite</span></h3>
        <span>First, the mesoporous carbon template CMK-3 was prepared by using SBA-15 as the hard template and sucrose as the carbon source <span class="italic">via</span> the procedure reported in the literature.<a title="Select to navigate to references" href="#cit39"><sup><span class="sup_ref">39</span></sup></a> Then, the mesoporous CMK-3 template (0.4 g) was added to a precursor solution prepared by dissolving Fe(NO<small><sub>3</sub></small>)<small><sub>3</sub></small>·9H<small><sub>2</sub></small>O (1 g) into 20 mL ethanol with vigorous stirring. After drying, a similar calcination procedure to that of M-Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/C was adopted to obtain the Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/MC composite.</span>
      
      
        
        <h3 id="sect344"><span class="b_heading">2.4 Characterization</span></h3>
        <span>Powder X-ray diffraction (XRD) patterns were recorded on a Bruker D8 Advance X-ray diffractometer with Cu-Kα radiation (<span class="italic">λ</span> = 1.5418 Å) with a scan rate of 6° min<small><sup>−1</sup></small>. The X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) measurements were conducted on a Kratos Axis Ultra DLD spectrometer using monochromated Al Kα radiation. Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) observation was performed on a FEI Nova NanoSEM 2300. The microstructure was characterized with a JEM-2100F transmission electron microscope (TEM) (JEOL, Japan) operating at an acceleration voltage of 200 kV. Nitrogen adsorption/desorption analyses were conducted with a Quadrasorb at 77 K with samples degassed at 200 °C for 16 h in advance. Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) was performed on a SDT Q600 thermal analyzer at a heating rate of 10 °C min<small><sup>−1</sup></small> in air.</span>
      
      
        
        <h3 id="sect351"><span class="b_heading">2.5 Electrochemical measurements</span></h3>
        <span>Active materials (80 wt%), Super-P (10 wt%), and carboxymethyl cellulose (CMC, 10 wt%) were mixed in water to form a slurry. The slurry was spread onto a Cu foil with a doctor blade method, followed by drying in a vacuum at 70 °C for 12 h. CR2016 coin cells were assembled in an argon-atmosphere glove box with a Millipore glass fiber membrane as the separator, Na metal as the anode, and 1 M NaClO<small><sub>4</sub></small> in ethylene carbonate/propylene carbonate/fluoroethylene carbonate (1<img class="charmap" src="https://www.rsc.org/images/entities/char_2009.gif" alt="[thin space (1/6-em)]">:<img class="charmap" src="https://www.rsc.org/images/entities/char_2009.gif" alt="[thin space (1/6-em)]">1<img class="charmap" src="https://www.rsc.org/images/entities/char_2009.gif" alt="[thin space (1/6-em)]">:<img class="charmap" src="https://www.rsc.org/images/entities/char_2009.gif" alt="[thin space (1/6-em)]">0.1 v/v/v) as the electrolyte. Galvanostatic charge and discharge experiments were performed with a battery tester LANDCT2001A in the potential range of 0.01–3 V (<span class="italic">vs.</span> Na<small><sup>+</sup></small>/Na) at room temperature. The specific capacity was calculated based on the whole mass of the M-Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/C composite. Cyclic voltammetry (CV) was conducted on an electrochemical workstation (Metrohm Autolab PGSTAT302N) at a scanning rate of 0.1 mV s<small><sup>−1</sup></small> in a potential range of 0.01–3 V (<span class="italic">vs.</span> Na<small><sup>+</sup></small>/Na). Electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) analyses were also performed on the electrochemical workstation from 100 kHz to 0.1 Hz.</span>
      
    
    
      
      <h2 id="sect363"><span class="a_heading">3. Results and discussion</span></h2>
      <span>The typical co-impregnation procedure for the fabrication of the M-Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/C composite is shown in <a title="Select to navigate to figure" href="#imgfig1">Fig. 1</a>. Mesoporous silica SBA-15 is employed as the sacrificial template. Fe(NO<small><sub>3</sub></small>)<small><sub>3</sub></small> and sucrose are used as the Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small> precursor and carbon source, respectively. A precursor solution containing Fe(NO<small><sub>3</sub></small>)<small><sub>3</sub></small>, sucrose, and H<small><sub>2</sub></small>SO<small><sub>4</sub></small> is penetrated into the mesoporous channels of SBA-15. After the calcination in N<small><sub>2</sub></small> and the subsequent removal of SBA-15 in NaOH solution, the final well-ordered mesoporous M-Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/C composite with Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small> nanoparticles embedded within the carbon walls is obtained. For comparison, the Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/MC composite was prepared by the incorporation of Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small> precursors into the mesoporous channels of CMK-3.</span>

      <p class="otherpara">The X-ray diffraction (XRD) patterns of M-Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/C and Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/MC composites are shown in <a title="Select to navigate to figure" href="#imgfig2">Fig. 2a</a>. The peaks of M-Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/C and Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/MC can be indexed to the crystalline phase γ-Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small> (JCPDS no. 39-1346), demonstrating the formation of Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>. Compared with that of Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/MC, the peak intensity of M-Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/C is relatively weak, which might indicate the low crystallinity and the small particle size of Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small> in the M-Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/C composite. X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) analyses of these composites are shown in <a title="Select to navigate to figure" href="#imgfig2">Fig. 2b</a>. Two distinct peaks are detected at approximately 710.9 eV and 724.7 eV, attributable to the 2p<small><sub>3/2</sub></small> and 2p<small><sub>1/2</sub></small> configurations of Fe<small><sup>3+</sup></small>, respectively.<a title="Select to navigate to reference" href="#cit40"><sup><span class="sup_ref">40,41</span></sup></a> The XPS analyses suggest the formation of Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small> in the M-Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/C and Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/MC composites, consistent with the XRD analyses.</p>

      <p class="otherpara">The morphology and structural feature of the as-prepared M-Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/C composite were revealed by SEM and TEM observation (<a title="Select to navigate to figure" href="#imgfig3">Fig. 3</a>). A worm-like morphology, similar to the hard template SBA-15 is well preserved (<a title="Select to navigate to figure" href="#imgfig3">Fig. 3a</a>). Well-ordered porous channels with a pore size of approximately 4.0 nm in diameter are observed in the TEM image (<a title="Select to navigate to figure" href="#imgfig3">Fig. 3c</a>). The mesopores are derived from the removal of the hard template. Thus, the pore size of the M-Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/C composite is in the same range with the wall thickness of SBA-15.<a title="Select to navigate to references" href="#cit38"><sup><span class="sup_ref">38</span></sup></a> Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small> nanoparticles with a particle size not more than 5.0 nm are embedded in the carbon walls. The carbon walls can not only prevent the aggregation of the Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small> nanocrystals, but can also suppress the volume variation during the charge/discharge process. The Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/MC composite prepared by the incorporation of the Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small> precursor into the mesopores of CMK-3 also has a worm-like morphology, similar to SBA-15 (<a title="Select to navigate to figure" href="#imgfig3">Fig. 3b</a>). Different from the M-Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/C composite, Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small> nanoparticles are distributed along the direction of mesoporous channels of CMK-3 (<a title="Select to navigate to figure" href="#imgfig3">Fig. 3d</a>). These Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small> particles would block the mesoporous channels of CMK-3, impacting the mass transport efficiency within the composite.</p>

      <p class="otherpara">The structural features of M-Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/C and Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/MC composites are also revealed by the N<small><sub>2</sub></small> adsorption–desorption isotherms (<a title="Select to navigate to figure" href="#imgfig4">Fig. 4a</a>). M-Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/C exhibits type IV isotherms with a marked hysteresis loop in a desorption branch between relative pressures <span class="italic">P</span>/<span class="italic">P</span><small><sub>0</sub></small> of 0.4 and 0.6, which is typical of mesoporous materials. The pore volume and pore size distribution are calculated from the desorption branch using the Barrett–Joyner–Halenda (BJH) method. The M-Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/C composite holds a pore volume of 0.765 cm<small><sup>3</sup></small> g<small><sup>−1</sup></small> and relatively narrow pore size distribution with an average pore size of approximately 3.6 nm (<a title="Select to navigate to figure" href="#imgfig4">Fig. 4b</a>). For the Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/MC composite, the pore volume is reduced to approximately 0.381 cm<small><sup>3</sup></small> g<small><sup>−1</sup></small> and the pore size distribution is around 2.2 to 3.5 nm. The low pore volume, reduced pore size and broadened pore size distribution of Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/MC are resulted from the formation of Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small> nanoparticles within the porous channels of CMK-3, consistent with the TEM observation. As determined by thermal gravimetric analysis (TGA), the contents of Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small> nanoparticles in both M-Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/C and Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/MC are approximately 36.7 wt% (Fig. S1, ESI<a title="Select to navigate to footnote" href="#fn1">†</a>).</p>

      <p class="otherpara">The electrochemical performance of the M-Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/C composite as an electrode material for SIBs was evaluated by performing cyclic voltammogram (CV) and galvanostatic charge/discharge experiments. <a title="Select to navigate to figure" href="#imgfig5">Fig. 5a</a> shows the cyclic voltammograms (CVs) of the M-Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/C electrode at a scan rate of 0.1 mV s<small><sup>−1</sup></small>. In the first cathodic scan, two distinct peaks are observed at 1.19 V and 0.65 V, ascribed to the irreversible formation of a solid electrolyte interface (SEI) and the reduction of Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>, respectively.<a title="Select to navigate to reference" href="#cit42"><sup><span class="sup_ref">42,43</span></sup></a> In the following anodic scan, the reduction peak of Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small> shifts to 0.75 V. The corresponding reversible oxidation process occurs at approximately 0.84 V and 1.6 V, which is ascribed to the oxidation of Fe<small><sup>0</sup></small> to Fe<small><sup>2+</sup></small> and subsequent Fe<small><sup>2+</sup></small> to Fe<small><sup>3+</sup></small>, respectively.<a title="Select to navigate to reference" href="#cit42"><sup><span class="sup_ref">42,44</span></sup></a> Negligible changes are observed in the following scans, suggesting the high reversibility and excellent cycling stability of the M-Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/C composite.</p>

      <p class="otherpara">The first five galvanostatic discharge/charge profiles of the M-Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/C electrode at a current density of 50 mA g<small><sup>−1</sup></small> are shown in <a title="Select to navigate to figure" href="#imgfig5">Fig. 5b</a>. The initial discharge and charge capacities of the M-Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/C electrode calculated based on the total weight of the composite are 980.3 and 423.8 mA h g<small><sup>−1</sup></small>, respectively, giving a coulombic efficiency of 43.2%. The low coulombic efficiency is due to the irreversible formation of a SEI film during the discharge process. Two plateaus are observed in the first discharge profile, consistent with the CV analysis. After 5 cycles, the discharge and charge capacities are 412.7 and 391.9 mA h g<small><sup>−1</sup></small> respectively. A high coulombic efficiency of over 90% is achieved after the second discharge/charge cycle, indicating the high reversibility of the M-Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/C electrode. For comparison, the electrochemical performance of the Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/MC composite was also evaluated by galvanostatic charge/discharge tests at a current density of 50 mA g<small><sup>−1</sup></small> (Fig. S2, ESI<a title="Select to navigate to footnote" href="#fn1">†</a>). The Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/MC composite delivers an initial discharge capacity of 578.6 mA h g<small><sup>−1</sup></small>, much lower than that of M-Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/C. Given the fact that the contents of Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small> in these two composites are quite similar, the high specific capacity of M-Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/C is ascribed to the well-ordered mesoporous structure with open pore channels. For the Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/MC composite, the formation of Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small> nanoparticles within the mesoporous channels would block the transport path for the electrolyte and Na<small><sup>+</sup></small> ions. In addition, it is possible that those Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small> nanoparticles entrapped deeply in the mesopores could not be reached by the electrolyte and Na<small><sup>+</sup></small> ions, consequently leading to the low specific capacity of Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/MC.</p>
      <p class="otherpara">The cycling performances of M-Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/C and Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/MC at the current density of 100 mA g<small><sup>−1</sup></small> are shown in <a title="Select to navigate to figure" href="#imgfig5">Fig. 5c</a>. It is obvious that M-Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/C shows better cycling performance than Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/MC. Discharged/charged at a current density of 100 mA g<small><sup>−1</sup></small>, high discharge and charge capacities of 689.2 and 292.3 mA h g<small><sup>−1</sup></small>, respectively, are achieved for the first cycle, giving an initial efficiency of approximately 42.4%. After 100 cycles, a charge capacity of approximately 270.1 mA h g<small><sup>−1</sup></small> is still maintained for M-Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/C, corresponding to approximately 7.6% fading of the initial capacity. After 100 cycles under the same conditions, the charge capacity of Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/MC is only 168.4 mA h g<small><sup>−1</sup></small>, much lower than that of M-Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/C. For M-Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/C, the specific charge capacity of the second cycle increases to 318.6 mA h g<small><sup>−1</sup></small>. The increase in the specific capacity might be due to the activation of the electrode material. However, such an activation process for Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/MC is over the initial 10 cycles. This gradual activation of Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/MC might be due to the pore blockage of Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/MC.</p>
      <p class="otherpara">The rate capabilities of the M-Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/C and Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/MC anodes are evaluated at different current densities from 100 to 2000 mA g<small><sup>−1</sup></small> (<a title="Select to navigate to figure" href="#imgfig5">Fig. 5d</a>). The specific discharge capacities of M-Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/C are 270, 226, 194, and 166 mA h g<small><sup>−1</sup></small> at current densities of 200, 500, 1000, and 2000 mA g<small><sup>−1</sup></small>, respectively. When the current density was switched from 2000 mA g<small><sup>−1</sup></small> to 100 mA g<small><sup>−1</sup></small>, the specific capacity of approximately 265 mA h g<small><sup>−1</sup></small> is recovered. However, for Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/MC, a specific capacity as low as 79 mA h g<small><sup>−1</sup></small> is achieved at a current density of 2000 mA g<small><sup>−1</sup></small>.</p>
      <p class="otherpara">The excellent cycling stability of the M-Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/C composite was further demonstrated by discharge/charge tests at a high current density of 2000 mA g<small><sup>−1</sup></small> (<a title="Select to navigate to figure" href="#imgfig5">Fig. 5e</a>). After 300 cycles, M-Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/C still delivers a specific discharge capacity of 101.9 mA h g<small><sup>−1</sup></small>, much higher than those reported in the literature.<a title="Select to navigate to reference" href="#cit37"><sup><span class="sup_ref">37,45</span></sup></a> And the coulombic efficiency was almost 100% after 10 cycles, further confirming the superior electrochemical performance of M-Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/C. Composites with Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small> contents of 26.6% and 62.3%, denoted as M-Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/C-1 and M-Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/C-2, respectively were also prepared following the same co-impregnation method and tested under the same conditions (Fig. S3, ESI<a title="Select to navigate to footnote" href="#fn1">†</a>). With the high carbon content and ordered mesoporous structure, M-Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/C-1 shows quite stable cycling performance. However, its specific capacity is approximately 50 mA h g<small><sup>−1</sup></small> after 20 cycles, much lower than that of M-Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/C. M-Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/C-2 with an Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small> content of 62.3% exhibits a high initial specific capacity with poor cycling stability. With the increase in the Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small> content, a large amount of Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small> would attach to the surface of the composite, blocking the ordered mesoporous structure and consequently impacting the electrochemical performance. This result further demonstrates the importance of an accessible mesoporous structure for good sodium ion storage performance. As revealed by the electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) analyses (Fig. S4, ESI<a title="Select to navigate to footnote" href="#fn1">†</a>), the resistance of M-Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/C is much smaller than that of Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/MC after 100 cycles at 200 mA g<small><sup>−1</sup></small>. It is supposed that the well-ordered mesoporous structure would reduce the solid/electrolyte interface resistance effectively, improving the charge transfer rate and ion migration rate.</p>
      <p class="otherpara">The better electrochemical performance is attributed to the structural features of M-Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/C. First, the well-ordered mesoporous structure with a large specific surface area and high porosity plays an essential role in improving the electrochemical performance of M-Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/C. The co-impregnation preparation strategy ensures the formation of ordered open mesoporous channels, which provide a fast transportation path for the electrolyte and Na<small><sup>+</sup></small> ions. The large specific surface area provides plenty of active sites for the diffusion of Na<small><sup>+</sup></small> ions. The high porosity derived from the mesoporous structure would accommodate a significant volume variation upon discharging/charging. The thin mesoporous wall shortens the diffusion distance for Na<small><sup>+</sup></small> ions, contributing to the rate performance. Second, the incorporation of Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small> nanoparticles with a high theoretical capacity significantly increases the specific capacity of the composite. In addition, carbon formed during the heating process not only contributes to the specific capacity of the composite, but also functions as a conducting matrix to enhance the electron conductivity of the composite. In addition, the carbon coating would buffer the large volume expansion of Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small> nanoparticles upon the discharge process.</p>
    
    
      
      <h2 id="sect667"><span class="a_heading">4. Conclusions</span></h2>
      <span>In summary, a well-ordered mesoporous M-Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/C composite has been successfully prepared <span class="italic">via</span> a simple co-impregnation method by using SBA-15 as a hard template. The confined growth of Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small> and the simultaneous carbonization of sucrose within the mesopores of SBA-15 generate a mesoporous structure with structural features, including open mesoporous channels, a large specific surface area, high porosity, and controlled wall thickness. The dispersion of nano-sized Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small> particles within the carbon matrix together with the mesoporous structure leads to high electrochemical performance, such as high specific capacity, good rate capability, and high cycling stability of the M-Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/C composite. When charged and discharged at 2000 mA g<small><sup>−1</sup></small>, the composite delivers a capacity of as high as 101.9 mA h g<small><sup>−1</sup></small> after 300 cycles. The electrochemical performance of the M-Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/C composite is superior to that of Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/MC prepared by incorporation of Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small> nanoparticles within the mesoporous channels of CMK-3. The M-Fe<small><sub>2</sub></small>O<small><sub>3</sub></small>/C composite is a promising anode material for sodium-ion batteries and the co-impregnation method is envisaged as a simple but efficient way for the preparation of well-ordered mesoporous metal oxide/carbon composites with readily accessible porous channels.</span>
