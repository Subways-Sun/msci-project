Information</caption></supportingInfoItem></supportingInformation><abstractGroup><abstract type="main" xml:lang="en"><title type="main">Abstract</title><p>Heterojunction, with the advantage of fast charge transfer dynamics, is considered to be an effective strategy to address the low capacity and poor rate capability of anode materials for sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs). As well, carbonaceous materials, as a crucial additive, can effectively ameliorate the ion/electron conductivity of integrated composites, realizing the fast ion transport and charge transfer. Here, motivated by the enhancement effect of carbon and heterojunction on conductivity, it is proposed that the CoSe<sub>2</sub>/WSe<sub>2</sub> heterojunction as inner core is coated by carbon outer shell and uniformly embedded in porous carbon nanosheets (denoted as CoSe<sub>2</sub>/WSe<sub>2</sub>@C/CNs), which is used as anode material for SIBs. Combining with density functional theoretical calculations, it is confirmed that the structure of heterojunction can introduce built‐in electric‐field, which can accelerate the transportation of Na<sup>+</sup> and improve the conductivity of electrons. Moreover, the introduction of porous carbon nanosheets (CNs) can provide a channel for the transportation of Na<sup>+</sup> and avoid the volume expansion during Na<sup>+</sup> insertion and extraction process. As it is expected, CoSe<sub>2</sub>/WSe<sub>2</sub>@C/CNs anode displays ultrastable specific capacity of 501.9 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> over 200 cycles, and ultrahigh rate capacity of 625 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 100 cycles.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="smll202103005-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>For the first time, reasonable and facile design and synthesis of high‐quality heterojunction with low cost of fabrication by certain chemical treatments are applied by using double transition metal oxide as precursor. Driven by the synergistic effect of dual carbon modification and CoSe<sub>2</sub>/WSe<sub>2</sub> heterojunction, the CoSe<sub>2</sub>/WSe<sub>2</sub>@C/CNs displays excellent performance when used as sodium ion battery.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll202103005:smll202103005-gra-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" xml:id="smll202103005-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="smll202103005-sec-0010"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) have been regarded as ideal alternative to lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs) due to the rich natural resource and low cost of sodium, as well as the similar chemical reaction mechanisms of lithium and sodium.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202103005-bib-0001 #smll202103005-bib-0002"><sup>1,2</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, owing to the ionic characteristics of Na<sup>+</sup> (the radius of Na<sup>+</sup> is larger than that of Li<sup>+</sup>), the diffusion of Na<sup>+</sup> in anode materials is more sluggish than that of Li<sup>+</sup>, which surely causes the poor rate capability, short cycling life and low energy capacity of SIBs.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202103005-bib-0003 #smll202103005-bib-0004"><sup>3,4</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Therefore, in response to the above issues, the most urgent and crucial task is to develop novel anode materials with special crystal design, aiming to improve the kinetics of sodiation and desodiation, which mainly comprises the finding of new materials and the structural design and morphology control of materials.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202103005-bib-0005"><sup>5</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>So far, transition metal chalcogenides, consisting of transition metal oxides,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202103005-bib-0006"><sup>6</sup></link><sup>]</sup> transition metal sulfides,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202103005-bib-0007"><sup>7</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and transition metal selenides,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202103005-bib-0008"><sup>8</sup></link><sup>]</sup> have been considered as promising anode materials for SIBs due to their high theoretical capacities and conductivities, unique layered structures and favorable cycling stabilities.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202103005-bib-0009 #smll202103005-bib-0010"><sup>9,10</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In particular, transition metal selenides, such as MoSe<sub>2</sub>,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202103005-bib-0011"><sup>11</sup></link><sup>]</sup> CoSe<sub>2</sub>,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202103005-bib-0012"><sup>12</sup></link><sup>]</sup> WSe<sub>2</sub>,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202103005-bib-0013"><sup>13</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and FeSe<sub>2</sub>,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202103005-bib-0014"><sup>14</sup></link><sup>]</sup> have drawn great attention, ascribed to their relatively small bandgaps with enhanced conductivities. Additionally, compared with transition metal oxides/sulfides, transition metal selenides have slightly larger and weaker metal–selenium bonds, which can be helpful for the insertion and extraction of sodium.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202103005-bib-0015"><sup>15</sup></link><sup>]</sup> For example, Ali et al. reported transition metal selenide (Fe<sub>2</sub>CoSe<sub>4</sub>) and transition metal sulfide (Fe<sub>2</sub>CoS<sub>4</sub>) as the electrode for SIBs, Fe<sub>2</sub>CoSe<sub>4</sub> exhibited batter battery performance (614.5 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>) than Fe<sub>2</sub>CoS<sub>4</sub> (335.8 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>) at 1 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 100 cycles.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202103005-bib-0016"><sup>16</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Among transition metal selenides, CoSe<sub>2</sub> has drawn great attention as anode material for SIBs because of its unique structure of the cubic pyrite‐type or orthorhombic marcasite‐type in which two selenium atoms are inserted into the crystal lattice of Co.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202103005-bib-0017"><sup>17</sup></link><sup>]</sup> It has been proved that this unique structure is conducive to the diffusion of large‐size sodium ions during the charge/discharge process. WSe<sub>2</sub>, as a 2D selenide, has advantages of narrow energy band and high intrinsic conductivity.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202103005-bib-0018"><sup>18</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Meanwhile, the large interlayer spacing (0.65 nm) of WSe<sub>2</sub> is conducive to the rapid transport of sodium ions. Although CoSe<sub>2</sub> and WSe<sub>2</sub>, as newly but rarely studied materials, have been proved to be potentially suitable candidates for SIBs, their insufficiently high ionic and electronic conductivity have still hindered the charge transfer substantially, limiting SIBs battery advancements, especially the rate performance. On that account, various strategies have been proposed to further boost ionic and electronic conductivity of CoSe<sub>2</sub> or WSe<sub>2</sub> based anode materials.</p><p>As is well known, the combination of active materials and carbonaceous materials, such as the introduction of carbon matrix, carbon coating, and other conventional methods, is an effective strategy to alleviate volume expansion and enhance structural stability, leading to the improved electrochemical performance.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202103005-bib-0019"><sup>19</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Most importantly, carbonaceous materials, as a crucial component, can effectively ameliorate the ion/electron conductivity of integrated composites, realizing the fast ion transport and charge transfer. While, the carbonaceous materials, as a conductive matrix or coating, can only increase the interparticle ion/electron transport of active materials, i.e., the external charge transfer is improved, the internal charge transfer and transport are still not enhanced by the effect of carbonaceous materials.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202103005-bib-0020 #smll202103005-bib-0021"><sup>20,21</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Thus, to enhance the internal ion/electron transport further, the design of a bimetallic selenide heterojunction with unique phase boundaries has been a promising route to accelerate the kinetics of the conversion reaction by coupling nanomaterials with different work functions.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202103005-bib-0022 #smll202103005-bib-0023"><sup>22,23</sup></link><sup>]</sup> For example, our group synthesized a heterojunction structures of Co<sub>3</sub>Sn<sub>2</sub>/SnO<sub>2</sub> as anode material for SIBs. The existence of built‐in electric field via the interface between Co<sub>3</sub>Sn<sub>2</sub> and SnO<sub>2</sub> enables the Na<sup>+</sup> ions and electrons move fast, ameliorating the internal conductivity. The as‐obtained Co<sub>3</sub>Sn<sub>2</sub>/SnO<sub>2</sub> displayed excellent rate capability, and a superior reversible capacity of 254 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.05 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 150 cycles.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202103005-bib-0024"><sup>24</sup></link><sup>]</sup> According to the theory, the work function of WSe<sub>2</sub> 3.7 eV (a higher Fermi level)<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202103005-bib-0025"><sup>25</sup></link><sup>]</sup> is lower than that of CoSe<sub>2</sub> 4.7 eV (a lower Fermi level).<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202103005-bib-0026"><sup>26</sup></link><sup>]</sup> When the CoSe<sub>2</sub>/WSe<sub>2</sub> heterojunction is successfully fabricated, electrons will shift from WSe<sub>2</sub> with higher Fermi level to CoSe<sub>2</sub> (a lower Fermi level), resulting in a positive charge region on the WSe<sub>2</sub> side and a negative charge region on the CoSe<sub>2</sub> side at the CoSe<sub>2</sub>/WSe<sub>2</sub> heterojunction interface. Therefore, the built‐in electric field is constructed, which can accelerate the transport rate of Na<sup>+</sup> and electrons at the heterojunction interfaces to improve the electrochemical performance of the composites. However, recently reasonable and facile design and synthesis of high‐quality heterojunction with low cost of fabrication have been still a great challenge. In most cases, one material is produced initially, followed by the generation of the other coupled material on the surface of the initial material, finally resulting in the formation of heterojunction of two materials. For instance, Li et al introduced ZIF‐8 on the surface of WS<sub>2</sub> nanorods, and subsequent carbonized to form WS<sub>2−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>/ZnS@C heterojunctions.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202103005-bib-0027"><sup>27</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The electrochemical studies manifest that WS<sub>2−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>/ZnS@C as anode material for SIBs delivers a relatively poor reversible capacity of 468.2, 411.1, 352.5, 296.4, 251.4, 206.4, 180.7, and 181.9 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1, 0.2, 0.5, 1, 2, 5, 10, and 20 A g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. Therefore, it is highly necessary to find a one‐step route to fabricate heterojunction. As we all know, the double transition metal oxide, as a suitable precursor, can offer two transition metals in one crystal structure, which can surely lead to high quality heterojunction with low cost of fabrication by certain chemical treatments. For the first time, the double transition metal oxide has been used by us to form high quality interface between two transition metal selenides.</p><p>Herein, in this work, we designed and synthesized a core–shell CoSe<sub>2</sub>/WSe<sub>2</sub> heterojunction@carbon with different work functions, derived from CoWO<sub>4</sub>@PVP precursor, uniformly embedded in porous 3D carbon nanosheets (CNs) as anode material for SIBs. In this synthetic process, the core–shell CoSe<sub>2</sub>/WSe<sub>2</sub> heterojunction@carbon can be built simultaneously with the formation of CoSe<sub>2</sub> and WSe<sub>2</sub> nanomaterials as the core–shell CoWO<sub>4</sub>@PVP precursor is selenized and carbonized. Driven by the synergistic effect of dual carbon modification and CoSe<sub>2</sub>/WSe<sub>2</sub> heterojunction, the external charge transfer of CoSe<sub>2</sub>/WSe<sub>2</sub> is improved by the introduction of carbonaceous materials including the CNs and the carbon coating, and the internal charge transfer enhanced by heterojunction with coupling two semiconductors of CoSe<sub>2</sub> and WSe<sub>2</sub>. It should be noted that the unique structure of CoSe<sub>2</sub>/WSe<sub>2</sub>@C/CNs can provide several advantageous features, summarized as follows. 1) The transition metal selenides of CoSe<sub>2</sub> and WSe<sub>2</sub> have good conductivities and suitable crystal structures for sodium storage. 2) The introduction of porous CNs and carbon shell can effectively alleviate the volume expansion in the process of Na<sup>+</sup> insertion and extraction, and also can provide a Na<sup>+</sup> transport channel for CoSe<sub>2</sub>/WSe<sub>2</sub> heterojunction, which is helpful to accelerate the Na<sup>+</sup> transport and maintain the integrity of CoSe<sub>2</sub>/WSe<sub>2</sub>@C/CNs. 3) The heterostructure of CoSe<sub>2</sub>/WSe<sub>2</sub> can produce lattice defects and distortions at the phase boundaries of the materials, improving the electrochemical performance by adjusting the kinetics of surface reactions. Meanwhile, the redistribution of electrons at phase boundaries would increase the adsorption sites of sodium ions. As we expect, CoSe<sub>2</sub>/WSe<sub>2</sub>@C/CNs shows excellent rate capability of 625 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 100 cycles and ultrastable cycle capacity of 501.9 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 200 cycles. In addition, the sodium storage mechanism and phase transformation of CoSe<sub>2</sub>/WSe<sub>2</sub>@C/CNs are proposed and explored by combining density functional theory (DFT) calculation results and ex situ X‐ray diffraction (XRD), ex situ Raman, ex situ electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS), ex situ HRTEM, and ex situ selected area electron diffraction (SAED) experiments in charge/discharge process.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202103005-sec-0020"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p>The synthesis process of CoSe<sub>2</sub>/WSe<sub>2</sub>@C/CNs is schematically illustrated in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll202103005-fig-0001"><b>1</b></link>, and the detailed synthesis process is given in the Experimental Section. First of all, sodium citrate was pyrolyzed into Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub> as carbon templates in a tubular furnace at 780 °C. The crystal structure and morphology of Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub> were characterized by XRD and scanning electron microscopy (SEM), and the results as shown in Figure <link href="#smll202103005-supitem-0001">S1a,b</link> of the Supporting Information. It is clearly displayed that the Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub> is produced and that the carbon skeleton can be seen along with Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub> particles. After removing Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub>, a porous CNs skeleton can be formed, which is confirmed by XRD pattern of CNs shown in Figure <link href="#smll202103005-supitem-0001">S1c</link> of the Supporting Information. From the SEM image (Figure <link href="#smll202103005-supitem-0001">S1d</link>, Supporting Information) and transmission electron microscope (TEM) images (Figure <link href="#smll202103005-supitem-0001">S1e,f</link>, Supporting Information), the ultrathin porous carbon nanosheets skeleton is observed clearly. Note that, this ultrathin layer of 3D porous carbon can be beneficial to the homogeneous loading and uniform dispersion of target nanomaterials. Subsequently, the ultrathin CNs were dispersed in cobalt nitrate solution, then sodium tungstate dihydrate and PVP were added to the above solution, respectively. The Co<sup>2+</sup> can be crossed link with WO<sub>4</sub><sup>2−</sup> on the CNs because of the electrostatic attraction, accompanies by PVP coating, yielding core–shell CoWO<sub>4</sub>@PVP/CNs as precursors. Figure <link href="#smll202103005-supitem-0001">S2a</link> of the Supporting Information reveals that the as‐prepared precursor is in good coincidence with the standard card of CoWO<sub>4</sub> (JCPDS No. 15‐0867), without obvious impurities. The different magnification SEM and TEM images of core–shell CoWO<sub>4</sub>@PVP/CNs are shown in Figure <link href="#smll202103005-supitem-0001">S2b‐e</link> of the Supporting Information. It can be clearly seen that core–shell CoWO<sub>4</sub>@PVP nanoparticles are uniformly dispersed in CNs. Finally, core–shell CoWO<sub>4</sub>@PVP/CNs and Se powder were annealed under Ar/H<sub>2</sub> atmosphere to attain CoSe<sub>2</sub>/WSe<sub>2</sub>@C/CNs. For comparison, the samples of WSe<sub>2</sub>/CNs and CoSe<sub>2</sub>/CNs were fully ground in a ratio of 1:1 to attain physically mixed CoSe<sub>2</sub> and WSe<sub>2</sub> materials (denoted as P‐CoSe<sub>2</sub>/WSe<sub>2</sub>@C/CNs). As well WSe<sub>2</sub>/CNs and CoSe<sub>2</sub>/CNs were also obtained in the same condition.</p><figure xml:id="smll202103005-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll202103005:smll202103005-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll202103005-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Schematic illustration of the formation process of the CoSe<sub>2</sub>/WSe<sub>2</sub>@C/CNs.</caption></figure><p><b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll202103005-fig-0002"><b>2</b>a</link> presents XRD patterns of CoSe<sub>2</sub>/WSe<sub>2</sub>@C/CNs, P‐CoSe<sub>2</sub>/WSe<sub>2</sub>/CNs, WSe<sub>2</sub>/CNs, and CoSe<sub>2</sub>/CNs. Apparently, it can be seen that all peaks of CoSe<sub>2</sub>/WSe<sub>2</sub>@C/CNs sample can be perfectly indexed into the crystal structures of WSe<sub>2</sub> (JCPDS No. 39‐1388) and CoSe<sub>2</sub> (JCPDS No. 09‐0234), demonstrating the high purity of CoSe<sub>2</sub> and WSe<sub>2</sub>. For all four samples, the three diffraction peaks at 13.6°, 31.4°, and 37.8° are well consistent with the (002), (100), and (103) lattice planes of WSe<sub>2</sub>, respectively. Meanwhile, the four diffraction peaks at 34.2°, 37.6°, 51.8°, and 56.5° can be corresponded to the (210), (211), (311), and (230) lattice planes of CoSe<sub>2</sub>, respectively. According to the Scherrer equation, the mean particle sizes of WSe<sub>2</sub> crystallites are calculated to be 9.8 nm. Meanwhile, Figure <link href="#smll202103005-fig-0007">7c</link> shows the resistance of charge transfer (<i>R</i><sub>ct</sub>) at different voltages state, at the first stage of the discharge (I), the <i>R</i><sub>ct</sub> is 47.6 Ω, in the subsequent process (II–III), the <i>R</i><sub>ct</sub> gradually increased due to the formation of the W and Co metal, and the intercalation of Na<sup>+</sup> ions. During the charging process, the <i>R</i><sub>ct</sub> greatly reduced compared with discharging process. These results further prove the results of CV curves, indicating that the CoSe<sub>2</sub>/WSe<sub>2</sub>@C/CNs electrode has ultrahigh stability and reversibility.</p><figure xml:id="smll202103005-fig-0007"><label>7</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll202103005:smll202103005-fig-0007" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll202103005-fig-0007-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) Ex situ EIS of the CoSe<sub>2</sub>/WSe<sub>2</sub>@C/CNs in the first cycle. b,c) Corresponding <i>R</i><sub>s</sub> and <i>R</i><sub>ct</sub> values of CoSe<sub>2</sub>/WSe<sub>2</sub>@C/CNs at the different voltage states. For hexagonal WSe<sub>2</sub>, which belongs to the D<sub>6h</sub> (<i>6/mmm</i>) crystallographic point group with six symmetry axes, the (100) and (010) surfaces are the same. The other optimized structures together with the adsorption energies were given in <link href="#smll202103005-supitem-0001">Tables S2</link> and <link href="#smll202103005-supitem-0001">S3</link> of the Supporting Information for reference. As shown in Figure <link href="#smll202103005-fig-0007">7d</link>–<link href="#smll202103005-fig-0007">h</link> and <link href="#smll202103005-supitem-0001">Tables S2</link> and <link href="#smll202103005-supitem-0001">S3</link> (Supporting Information) the Na ion could stably adsorb on either of the CoSe<sub>2</sub> and WSe<sub>2</sub> surface due to the negative adsorption energy. Moreover, the Na ion adsorption did not induce significant geometric deformation or lattice expansion, implying theirs promising potential application in SIBs. For CoSe<sub>2</sub>, all the crystal surfaces show eminent affinities to the Na ion and the (001) surface is the most stable one. Conversely, the affinity of WSe<sub>2</sub> surfaces to Na ion shows apparent selectivity, and the adsorption on (100)/(010) surface (−2.90 eV) is more stable than the (001) surface (−0.83 eV).</p><p>As shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll202103005-fig-0008"><b>8</b>a</link>,<link href="#smll202103005-fig-0008">b</link>, the migration energy barrier of Na<sup>+</sup> in WSe<sub>2</sub> is 0.23 eV, which is significantly smaller than that of 0.92 eV in CoSe<sub>2</sub>. The divergent diffusibility of Na<sup>+</sup> in WSe<sub>2</sub> and CoSe<sub>2</sub> is caused by the totally different surrounding in crystal cell. For Na<sup>+</sup> adsorbed in CoSe<sub>2</sub> unit cell, there is relatively strong interaction with nearby Se and Co atoms along diffusion path, however, within WSe<sub>2</sub> unit cell, the Na<sup>+</sup> could migrates along the vacant layer between two Se layers rather freely. Notably, the Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion barrier in the WSe<sub>2</sub> material is even comparable to the graphene.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202103005-bib-0054"><sup>54</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In order to study the electrical conductivity of the two materials, the density of states (DOS) was calculated and depicted in Figure <link href="#smll202103005-fig-0008">8c</link>–<link href="#smll202103005-fig-0008">h</link>. The CoSe<sub>2</sub> with low Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion rate exhibits metallic characteristics (Figure <link href="#smll202103005-fig-0008">8c</link>), and with the addition of the graphene base layer, the intensity of DOS weakens due to the reduction of contribution from Co‐d near Fermi level and also from Se‐p in low and high energy region (Figure <link href="#smll202103005-fig-0008">8d</link>). Furthermore, the intensity of conduction band and forbidden band decreases and increases near the Fermi level, respectively. The mixed CoSe<sub>2</sub>/WSe<sub>2</sub> heterostructure exhibits weak metallic character with relatively low DOS intensity near the Fermi level, which would be slightly enhanced after the addition of the graphene base layer. Therefore, the CoSe<sub>2</sub>/WSe<sub>2</sub> material possesses high Na<sup>+</sup> diffusibility, electrical conductivity, and electrochemical performance, and these properties would be slightly enhanced after the addition of the graphene layer.</p><figure xml:id="smll202103005-fig-0008"><label>8</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll202103005:smll202103005-fig-0008" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll202103005-fig-0008-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion path in a) CoSe<sub>2</sub>. b) WSe<sub>2</sub> crystal lattice between equivalent positions. The CoSe<sub>2</sub>/WSe<sub>2</sub>@C/CNs anode reveals ultrastable specific capacity of 277 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.5 A g<sup>−1</sup> over 200 cycles, and ultrahigh rate capacity of 625 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 100 cycles. This method can not only generate abundant phase boundaries, which can generate built‐in electric‐field to accelerate the rapid migration of Na<sup>+</sup> and improve the conductivity of electrons, but also can introduce carbon shell, which can prevent the active material pulverization during the sodiation/desodiation process. The novel design and the facile synthesis method are expected to be applied to heterojunction of other metal oxide/sulfide for SIBs.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202103005-sec-0040"><title type="main">Experimental Section</title><section xml:id="smll202103005-sec-0050" numbered="no"><section xml:id="smll202103005-sec-0060" numbered="no"><section xml:id="smll202103005-sec-0070" numbered="no"><title type="main">Synthesis of Ultrathin 3D CNs</title><p>The synthesis of 3D ultrathin CNs was based on the previous method. 10 g sodium citrate was carbonized under Ar at 780 °C for 1 h and the heating rate was 5 °C min<sup>−1</sup> to produce sodium carbonate. After cooling to room temperature naturally, grinding the black material and washing out the sodium carbonate with dilute hydrochloric acid were carried out. The final porous carbon was washed several times with deionized water and absolute ethanol and dried at 70 °C for 12 h.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202103005-sec-0080" numbered="no"><title type="main">Synthesis of CoWO<sub>4</sub>@PVP/CNs</title><p>First, 30 mg CNs was added to 20 mL deionized water with 30 min ultrasonication. Then 250 mg cobalt nitrate hexahydrate (Co(NO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub> · 6H<sub>2</sub>O) and 600 mg polyvinylpyrrolidone (PVP K30, Mw = 30 000) were added in the above solution under magnetic stirring for 10 min to form a uniform solution A. Finally, 50 mg sodium tungstate dihydrate (Na<sub>2</sub>WO<sub>4</sub> · 2H<sub>2</sub>O) and 600 mg polyvinylpyrrolidone (PVP K30, Mw = 30 000) were dissolved in 15 mL deionized water under magnetic stirring for 10 min to form a uniform solution B. Then, solution B was added drop by drop into solution A under continuous magnetic stirring for 10 min. The precipitated solution was transferred to 40 mL Teflon lined autoclave, which was maintained at 160 °C for 12 h. After cooling to room temperature, the final product was washed by deionized water and absolute ethanol and dried at 70 °C for 12 h.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202103005-sec-0090" numbered="no"><title type="main">Synthesis of CoSe<sub>2</sub>/WSe<sub>2</sub>@C/CNs</title><p>In a typical synthesis, the as‐obtained CoWO<sub>4</sub>@PVP/CNs and Se powder (1:15 in weight) were placed in the tube furnace and selenium powder placed upstream of tubular furnace. Finally, the porcelain boat was heated at 600 °C for 1 h at a ramp rate of 3 °C min<sup>−1</sup> under reductive atmosphere (H<sub>2</sub>/Ar = 5%/95%), after cooling to room temperature, a black powder was obtained.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202103005-sec-0100" numbered="no"><title type="main">Synthesis of WSe<sub>2</sub>/CNs</title><p>First, 30 mg porous carbon was dissolved to 10 mL dimethyl formamide (DMF) solution with 30 min ultrasonication. Then, 75 mg of NaBH<sub>4</sub>, 178 mg of Se powder, 495 mg of Na<sub>2</sub>WO<sub>4</sub> · 2H<sub>2</sub>O, and 3 mL deionized water in 20 mL DMF solution were gradually added to the above solution. Finally, the obtained mixtures were vigorously stirred for 2 h and transferred into 40 mL Teflon lined autoclave, which were maintained at 200 °C in for 12 h. The final product was washed by deionized water and annealed under Ar atmosphere at 500 °C for 2 h.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202103005-sec-0110" numbered="no"><title type="main">Synthesis of CoSe<sub>2</sub>/CNs</title><p>First, 30 mg CNs, 93.75 mg Co(CH<sub>3</sub>COO)<sub>2</sub> · 4H<sub>2</sub>O, and 500 mg PVP were dispersed in deionized water (30 mL) by sonication. Subsequently, 54 mg Se powder was dissolved in 0.5 mL N<sub>2</sub>H<sub>4</sub> · H<sub>2</sub>O, and then mixed with the above solution. Finally, the obtained mixtures were vigorously stirred for 30 min and transferred into 40 mL Teflon lined autoclave, which were maintained at 180 °C in for 10 h. The final product was washed by deionized water and annealed under Ar atmosphere at 500 °C for 2 h.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202103005-sec-0120" numbered="no"><title type="main">Computational Details</title><p>All calculations in this study were performed by the open‐source code Quantum Espresso software package<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202103005-bib-0055 #smll202103005-bib-0056"><sup>55,56</sup></link><sup>]</sup> with implementation of the DFT. The generalized gradient approximation with the Perdew–Burke–Ernzerhof<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202103005-bib-0057"><sup>57</sup></link><sup>]</sup> functional were used to indicate the exchange and correlation interactions. The Kohn–Sham one‐electron wave functions of the valence electrons for Co (3s<sup>2</sup>3p<sup>6</sup>3d<sup>7</sup>4s<sup>2</sup>), W (5s<sup>2</sup>5p<sup>6</sup>5d<sup>4</sup>6s<sup>2</sup>), Se (4s<sup>2</sup>4p<sup>4</sup>), and Na (2s<sup>2</sup>2p<sup>6</sup>3s<sup>1</sup>) were extended in a plane‐wave basis set. The cutoff energy for the plane‐wave basis set was chosen to be 38 Ry. Structure relaxations were performed until the total energy change was smaller than 10<sup>−4</sup> mRy and the Hellmann–Feynman force on each atom was less than 0.0004 a.u.</p><p>In primitive cell structure optimization, the 8 × 8 × 8 and 10 × 10 × 2 k‐point mesh was selected for CoSe<sub>2</sub> and WSe<sub>2</sub>, respectively, and the optimized lattice constants are <i>a</i> = 3.643 Å, <i>b</i> = 4.888 Å, <i>c</i> = 5.825 Å, α = β = γ = 90° and <i>a</i> = <i>b</i> = 3.317 Å, <i>c</i> = 15.045 Å, α = β = 90°, γ = 120°. The theoretically predicted structures were in consistent with the experimental measured values.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202103005-bib-0058 #smll202103005-bib-0059"><sup>58,59</sup></link><sup>]</sup> To simulate the adsorption of Na ion on the surfaces, a multilayer slab model for each surface was constructed by adding a 15 Å vacuum layer along the <i>c</i> direction to avoid the interactions between the adjacent slabs. In slab structure optimization, the atoms in the top three layers of slab model were relaxed, while the other atoms were fixed. The diffusion pathways of Na<sup>+</sup> in CoSe<sub>2</sub> and WSe<sub>2</sub> crystals were calculated by the climbing image nudged elastic band method<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202103005-bib-0060"><sup>60</sup></link><sup>]</sup> with seven images inserted between the most stable adsorption position of Na<sup>+</sup> and the adjacent equivalent position. The morphologies of the synthesized samples were characterized by SEM (HITACHI SU8010). Moreover, the structure detailed characteristics of samples were recorded by using TEM (FEI Talos F200X, USA) at an accelerating voltage of 200 kV. XPS analysis was performed by using a PHI5000 Versa Probe III XPS with an Al Ka excitation source. BET (ASAP 2020 PLUS HD88) surface area and pore size distributions were obtained by using N<sub>2</sub> adsorption–desorption isotherms. TGA was employed to calculate the carbon contents of the samples by using a 600 SDT over the room temperature to 900 °C temperature range in air. Raman spectra were recorded on a dispersive Raman microscope (XPLORA PLUS) with a wavelength of 532 nm.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202103005-sec-0140" numbered="no"><title type="main">Electrochemical Measurements</title><p>Electrochemical measurements of the samples were conducted using CR2025 coin‐type cells assembled in an Ar‐filled glovebox. The working electrodes were prepared by mixing the active materials (80 wt%), acetylene black (10 wt%), and polyvinylidene fluoride (10 wt%) in <i>N</i>‐methyl‐2‐pyrrolidinone to form slurry. The slurry was coated onto Ni foam, which was pressed at 20 MPa to result in good contact between them and then dried at 80 °C in a vacuum overnight. The mass loading of the active material was 1.2 mg cm<sup>−2</sup>. The SIB electrolyte solution was 1 <sc>m</sc> NaClO<sub>4</sub> dissolved in a mixed solution of propylene carbonate (PC)/ethylene carbonate (EC) (PC:EC = 1:1) with 5 wt% fluoroethylene carbonates as the additive. CV measurements were performed between 0.005 and 3.0 V at a scan rate from 0.1 to 2.0 mV s<sup>−1</sup> and the EIS spectrum measurements were carried out at room temperature within the frequency range from 0.01 to 100 kHz on a CHI 660D electrochemical workstation. The charge–discharge tests were performed in a cut off potential window of 0.005–3.0 V on a LAND battery program‐control test system after aging for more than 10 h.</p></section></section></section></section><section xml:id="smll202103005-sec-0150" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>S.Z. and L.S. contributed equally to this work.
