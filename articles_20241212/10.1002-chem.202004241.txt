The organic ligand 1,5‐naphthalenedisulfonic acid in the metal–organic framework (MOF) precursor is used as a sulfur source, and metal ions are sulfurized in situ to form M<sub><i>x</i></sub>S<sub><i>y</i></sub> nanoparticles, resulting in the formation of M<sub><i>x</i></sub>S<sub><i>y</i></sub>/NSC (M=Fe, Co, Cu, Ni, Mn, Zn) composites. Benefiting from the M<sub><i>x</i></sub>S<sub><i>y</i></sub> nanoparticles and conductive carbon, a synergistic effect of the composite is achieved. For instance, the composite of Fe<sub>7</sub>S<sub>8</sub>/NSC as an anode displays excellent long‐term cycling stability in lithium/sodium ion batteries. At 5 A g<sup>−1</sup>, large capacities of 645 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> and 426.6 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> can be retained after 1500 cycles for the lithium‐ion battery and after 1000 cycles for the sodium‐ion battery, respectively.</p></abstract><abstract type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p><b>Composite anode materials</b>: A general strategy is developed for the preparation of M<sub><i>x</i></sub>S<sub><i>y</i></sub>/NSC (M=Fe, Co, Ni, Cu, Mn, Zn) composites through the in situ pyrolysis of sulfonate‐based M‐MOF, which deliver high rate capability and long‐term cycling stability (645 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> for LIBs after 1500 cycles and 426.6 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> for SIBs after 1000 cycles at 5 A g<sup>−1</sup>).<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:09476539:media:chem202004241:chem202004241-toc-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/chem202004241-toc-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="chem202004241-sec-0001" numbered="no"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>On account of our rapid energy consumption and environmental pollution, there is a tremendous requirement for the development of rechargeable batteries with excellent energy density. Lithium ion batteries (LIBs) are a type of advanced energy storage device, and because of their attractive features of long lifespan, satisfactory energy density, and environmental friendliness, they have been applied widely in portable computers, communication devices, electric automobiles, and so on.<link href="#chem202004241-bib-0001"><sup>[1]</sup></link> On the other hand, sodium ion batteries (SIBs) have also attracted in‐depth investigation, and are viewed as a possible substitute for LIBs as the properties of sodium and lithium are similar.<link href="#chem202004241-bib-0005"><sup>[2]</sup></link> It is well known that the theoretical capacities of commercial graphite for SIBs and LIBs are 35 and 372 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively, so it is difficult for them to meet our energy storage requirements.<link href="#chem202004241-bib-0008"><sup>[3]</sup></link> Therefore, it is necessary to develop new electrode materials with large capacities.</p><p>Transition metal sulfides (TMSs) have been investigated extensively owing to their large theoretical capacity and abundant redox sites.<link href="#chem202004241-bib-0009"><sup>[4]</sup></link> However, because of the pulverization of materials, polysulfide dissolution in the electrolyte during the redox process, and large volume expansion, there are some drawbacks of TMSs anodes that need to be overcome, including their sluggish kinetics, rapid capacity fading, and poor cycling stability.<link href="#chem202004241-bib-0010"><sup>[5]</sup></link> Up to now, nanostructure engineering has been considered an effective way to address the issues mentioned above. A decrease in the mean particle size of metal sulfides not only shortens the diffusion path of Li<sup>+</sup>/Na<sup>+</sup>, but also alleviates the volume expansion, thus improving the electrochemical reactivity.<link href="#chem202004241-bib-0014"><sup>[6]</sup></link> Moreover, hybridization of metal sulfides with carbon matrices is also considered a feasible approach, and conductive carbon has been proved to enhance the electronic conductivity and adsorption of polysulfide intermediates.<link href="#chem202004241-bib-0017"><sup>[7]</sup></link> For example, Mai et al.<link href="#chem202004241-bib-0020"><sup>[8]</sup></link> prepared CoS@carbon yolk–shell microspheres with a high Li/Na‐ion storage capacity (790 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 1 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 500 cycles for Li‐ion storage, 532 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.2 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 100 cycles for Na‐ion storage). Xu et al.<link href="#chem202004241-bib-0021"><sup>[9]</sup></link> reported the FeS<sub>2</sub>@NC‐G composite, which displayed an excellent Li/Na‐ion storage capability.</p><p>Recently, metal–organic frameworks (MOFs), as a class of inorganic–organic compounds, have been investigated widely in many fields (e.g., energy storage, catalysis, solar cells, sensors, gas separation/storage, etc.) because of their tunable pore size, high surface area, and controllable structure.<link href="#chem202004241-bib-0022"><sup>[10]</sup></link> Meanwhile, MOFs have also been applied as self‐sacrificial templates for transformation into various functional composite materials. Tang et al.<link href="#chem202004241-bib-0028"><sup>[11]</sup></link> fabricated the FeS<sub>2</sub>@octahedral carbon composite through the sulfurization of MIL‐53(Fe), which presented an excellent Li‐ion storage performance of 607 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 2 A g<sup>−1</sup>. Zhang et al.<link href="#chem202004241-bib-0029"><sup>[12]</sup></link> synthesized the Fe<sub>7</sub>S<sub>8</sub>@C‐G composite using MIL‐88 as a precursor, and as an anode for the SIB it exhibited a capacity of 449 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> after 150 cycles. So far, various metal sulfide/carbon composites have been fabricated using MOFs as precursors, but the development of a general strategy for the in situ synthesis of composites of M<sub><i>x</i></sub>S<sub><i>y</i></sub>/carbon from metal ions and sulfonic ligands of MOFs is still a challenge.</p><p>Herein, a general and simple route has been realized for the fabrication of the composites of metal sulfides immobilized to N/S co‐doped carbon (M<sub><i>x</i></sub>S<sub><i>y</i></sub>/NSC) by employing sulfonate‐based M‐MOF (M=Fe, Co, Cu, Ni, Zn, Mn) as precursors. Upon application as an anode for LIBs/SIBs, they display a remarkable electrochemical performance. For instance, the composite of Fe<sub>7</sub>S<sub>8</sub>/NSC delivers a reversible capacity of 645 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> for LIBs after 1500 cycles and a capacity of 426.6 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> for SIBs after 1000 cycles at 5 A g<sup>−1</sup>. It is indicated that such a fascinating electrochemical property can be attributed mainly to the synergistic effects between Fe<sub>7</sub>S<sub>8</sub> nanoparticle and N/S co‐doped carbon, which can not only short the Li<sup>+</sup>/Na<sup>+</sup>‐ion diffusion pathway and alleviate the volume expansion, but also enhance the electronic conductivity and inhibit the dissolution of polysulfides.</p></section><section xml:id="chem202004241-sec-0002" numbered="no"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p>The simple, general route for the fabrication of M<sub><i>x</i></sub>S<sub><i>y</i></sub>/NSC composites is illustrated in Figure <link href="#chem202004241-fig-0001">1 a</link>. Typically, the sulfonate‐based M‐MOF (M=Fe, Co, Ni, Cu, Mn, Zn) precursors were synthesized using 1,5‐naphthalenedisulfonic acid and 4,4′‐bipyridine as the organic ligands at room temperature. During the pyrolysis process, 1,5‐naphthalenedisulfonic acid not only served as a sulfur source for the in situ sulfurization reaction with metal ions to form metal sulfides (M<sub><i>x</i></sub>S<sub><i>y</i></sub>=Fe<sub>7</sub>S<sub>8</sub>, Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub>, Ni<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub>, Cu<sub>1.96</sub>S, MnS, ZnS), but also provided N/S co‐doped carbon together with 4,4′‐bipyridine. Figure S1 (Supporting Information) shows the SEM images and XRD patterns of the precursors. The former shows that the morphology and size of the M‐MOF precursors are different, and the well‐defined diffraction peak is detected in the latter, indicating a high degree of crystallinity. The IR spectra of the M‐MOF precursors are displayed in Figure S2 (Supporting Information). The stretching mode peaks of the sulfonate group in 1,5‐naphthalenedisulfonic acid of all precursors are in the range 1000–1250 cm<sup>−1</sup>.<link href="#chem202004241-bib-0030"><sup>[13]</sup></link> The peaks at around 1420 and 1610 cm<sup>−1</sup> can be ascribed to the stretching vibrations of the pyridyl rings of 4,4′‐bipyridine.<link href="#chem202004241-bib-0031"><sup>[14]</sup></link></p><figure xml:id="chem202004241-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:09476539:media:chem202004241:chem202004241-fig-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/chem202004241-fig-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a) Schematic drawing of the formation process of M<sub><i>x</i></sub>S<sub><i>y</i></sub>/NSC; b) XRD patterns of M<sub><i>x</i></sub>S<sub><i>y</i></sub>/NSC; c) N<sub>2</sub> adsorption/desorption isotherms; and d) SEM image of Fe<sub>7</sub>S<sub>8</sub>/NSC.</p></caption></figure><p>The crystal structure of M<sub><i>x</i></sub>S<sub><i>y</i></sub>/NSC was investigated by XRD measurements. As shown in Figure <link href="#chem202004241-fig-0001">1 b</link>, a wide peak at 2<i>θ</i>=25.0° corresponds to the (002) diffraction plane of the graphite carbon.<link href="#chem202004241-bib-0032"><sup>[15]</sup></link> All other identified peaks can be indexed to Fe<sub>7</sub>S<sub>8</sub> (JCPDS card 24‐0220), Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub> (JCPDS card 86‐2273), Ni<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub> (JCPDS card 78‐1886), Cu<sub>1.96</sub>S (JCPDS card 29‐0578), MnS (JCPDS card 72‐1534), and ZnS (JCPDS card 89‐2143), respectively. These results indicate that the M‐MOF precursor has been transferred in situ to M<sub><i>x</i></sub>S<sub><i>y</i></sub> after calcination. The Raman spectra of M<sub><i>x</i></sub>S<sub><i>y</i></sub>/NSC are displayed in Figure S3 (Supporting Information). Three characteristic peaks at 1348, 1590, and 2860 cm<sup>−1</sup> can be assigned to the D, G, and 2D bands of carbon, respectively. The value of <i>I</i><sub>G</sub>/<i>I</i><sub>D</sub> is in the range 1.0–1.2, demonstrating that the carbon is partially graphitic, which is beneficial for improving the electronic conductivity.<link href="#chem202004241-bib-0033"><sup>[16]</sup></link> Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) was performed to calculate the content of M<sub><i>x</i></sub>S<sub><i>y</i></sub> in the M<sub><i>x</i></sub>S<sub><i>y</i></sub>/NSC composite. As displayed in Figure S4 (Supporting Information), the total weight loss can be ascribed to the evaporation of adsorbed water, the conversion of M<sub><i>x</i></sub>S<sub><i>y</i></sub> to metal oxides, and the decomposition of carbon.<link href="#chem202004241-bib-0036"><sup>[17]</sup></link> The XRD patterns of the residue after the TGA test demonstrate that the final product is metal oxides. According to the amount of metal oxides, the content of M<sub><i>x</i></sub>S<sub><i>y</i></sub> in the composite is calculated to be 35–50 %; the specific data are shown in Table S1 (Supporting Information).</p><p>The N<sub>2</sub> adsorption/desorption isotherm of the Fe<sub>7</sub>S<sub>8</sub>/NSC composite (as an example) was measured to analyze the pore size distribution and surface area. As displayed in Figure <link href="#chem202004241-fig-0001">1 c</link>, Fe<sub>7</sub>S<sub>8</sub>/NSC has a typical type IV isotherm, and the specific surface area is about 234.8 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup>. Moreover, the pore size distribution indicates that the Fe<sub>7</sub>S<sub>8</sub>/NSC has a micro/mesoporous structure with mesopores of around 15 nm. Further morphological and structural information on the Fe<sub>7</sub>S<sub>8</sub>/NSC composite was obtained through SEM and TEM measurements. Figure <link href="#chem202004241-fig-0001">1 d</link> and Figure <link href="#chem202004241-fig-0002">2 a</link> show SEM and TEM images of the Fe<sub>7</sub>S<sub>8</sub>/NSC composite; the results show that the bulk morphology of the Fe‐MOF precursor is well maintained after calcination. The same result can be concluded for the other samples (Figure S5, Supporting Information). In addition, the Fe<sub>7</sub>S<sub>8</sub>/NSC was treated with HCl and Fe<sub>7</sub>S<sub>8</sub> nanoparticles were removed, and the N/S co‐doped carbon (NSC) could be observed in the SEM image and XRD pattern, as displayed in Figure S6 (Supporting Information). From the high‐resolution TEM (HRTEM) images displayed in Figure <link href="#chem202004241-fig-0002">2 b</link>–d, the bulk Fe<sub>7</sub>S<sub>8</sub>/NSC composite is composed of Fe<sub>7</sub>S<sub>8</sub> nanoparticles with an average size of around 10–20 nm, in which the Fe<sub>7</sub>S<sub>8</sub> nanoparticles are immobilized uniformly in the porous carbon. As shown in the inset image in Figure <link href="#chem202004241-fig-0002">2 d</link>, there is a well‐defined lattice fringe with a spacing of 0.205 nm, which is consistent with the (206) lattice plane of crystalline Fe<sub>7</sub>S<sub>8</sub>. The elemental mapping images are displayed in Figure <link href="#chem202004241-fig-0002">2 e</link>–i, which demonstrate the homogeneous distribution of Fe, S, N, and C elements in the Fe<sub>7</sub>S<sub>8</sub>/NSC composite.</p><figure xml:id="chem202004241-fig-0002"><label>2</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:09476539:media:chem202004241:chem202004241-fig-0002"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/chem202004241-fig-0002-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a–d) TEM images, and e) STEM image, as well as f–i) elemental mappings of Fe<sub>7</sub>S<sub>8</sub>/NSC.</p></caption></figure><p>XPS investigation was performed to further observe the chemical state and surface composition of the Fe<sub>7</sub>S<sub>8</sub>/NSC composite. As presented in Figure S7 (Supporting Information), the survey spectrum proves the existence of Fe, N, C, and S elements. The presence of the O 1s peak might be attributed to oxygen adsorption and partial oxidation on the surface of Fe<sub>7</sub>S<sub>8</sub>. The high‐resolution spectrum of Fe 2p is displayed in Figure <link href="#chem202004241-fig-0003">3 a</link>; two binding energies located at 720.1 and 710.7 eV can be attributed to the Fe 2p<sub>1/2</sub> and Fe 2p<sub>3/2</sub> states of Fe<sup>2+</sup>, respectively, and the peaks of the Fe 2p<sub>1/2</sub> and Fe 2p<sub>3/2</sub> states of Fe<sup>3+</sup> are located at 724.7 and 714.5 eV, respectively. Benefitting from a synergistic effect of the M<sub><i>x</i></sub>S<sub><i>y</i></sub> nanoparticles and N/S co‐doped carbon, the M<sub><i>x</i></sub>S<sub><i>y</i></sub>/NSC electrode can not only retard the volume expansion and decrease the Li<sup>+</sup>/Na<sup>+</sup>‐ion diffusion pathways, but also inhibit the dissolution of polysulfides and enhance the electronic conductivity. Among these M<sub><i>x</i></sub>S<sub><i>y</i></sub>/NSC materials, the Fe<sub>7</sub>S<sub>8</sub>/NSC electrode exhibited remarkable electrochemical properties. Employed as an electrode material for LIBs, it exhibited a high capacity (825.6 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.2 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 100 cycles), exceptional rate performance (466 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 10 A g<sup>−1</sup>), and good long‐term cycling stability (645 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> after 1500 cycles at 5 A g<sup>−1</sup>), together with large capacities of 564.6 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.5 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 200 cycles and 426.6 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 5 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 1000 cycles for SIBs. These results indicate that such a general strategy is a promising route for manufacturing high‐performance metal sulfide materials in LIBs/SIBs.</p></section><section xml:id="chem202004241-sec-0004" numbered="no"><title type="main">Experimental Section</title><section xml:id="chem202004241-sec-0005" numbered="no"><title type="main">Fabrication of M‐MOF precursor</title><p>Typically, 4,4′‐bipyridine (1 mmol) and 1,5‐naphthalenedisulfonic acid (1 mmol) were dispersed in deionized water (100 mL). After magnetic stirring for 10 min, M(CH<sub>3</sub>COO)<sub>2</sub>⋅4 H<sub>2</sub>O (0.5 mmol) was added with stirring for 5 h at room temperature. Then, the resulting precipitate sulfonate‐based M‐MOF (M=Fe, Co, Ni, Cu, Mn, Zn) precursor was gathered by centrifugation, washed four times with water and ethanol, and finally dried in an oven at 70 °C.</p></section><section xml:id="chem202004241-sec-0006" numbered="no"><title type="main">Fabrication of M<sub><i>x</i></sub>S<sub><i>y</i></sub>/NSC</title><p>The sulfonate‐based M‐MOF precursors were calcined at 750 °C for 3 h in a tube furnace in an Ar atmosphere with a heating rate of 5 °C min<sup>−1</sup>. After cooling to room temperature, M<sub><i>x</i></sub>S<sub><i>y</i></sub>/NSC (M<sub><i>x</i></sub>S<sub><i>y</i></sub>=Fe<sub>7</sub>S<sub>8</sub>, Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub>, Ni<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub>, Cu<sub>1.96</sub>S, MnS, ZnS) composites were obtained.</p></section><section xml:id="chem202004241-sec-0007" numbered="no"><title type="main">Fabrication of NSC</title><p>The Fe<sub>7</sub>S<sub>8</sub>/NSC composite was dispersed in a solution of HCl (4 mol L<sup>−1</sup>) under stirring for 12 h, then washed with water three times by centrifugation, and dried at 70 °C overnight to obtain the NSC.</p></section><section xml:id="chem202004241-sec-0008" numbered="no"><title type="main">Structural characterization</title><p>The Rigaku Ultima IV instrument (CuK<sub>α</sub> radiation) was employed to collect the X‐ray diffraction (XRD) data of products. Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) was performed with a TG209F3 Tarsus® in air atmosphere (10 °C min<sup>−1</sup>). Scanning electron microscopy (SEM, Hitachi 4800) and transmission electron microscopy (TEM, TECNAI G2 F20) were performed to investigate the morphologies of the samples. A Renishaw inVia Reflex Raman system (excitation source: 532 nm Nd:YAG) was used to collect Raman spectra. FTIR spectra were obtained with a BRUKER‐EQUINOX‐55 IR spectrophotometer. X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) was measured on a Thermo ESCALAB 250XI to analyze the element states. The N<sub>2</sub> adsorption/desorption analysis was performed on a Micromeritics ASAP 2020 instrument.</p></section><section xml:id="chem202004241-sec-0009" numbered="no"><title type="main">Electrochemical measurements</title><p>The Li/Na‐ion storage properties of the M<sub><i>x</i></sub>S<sub><i>y</i></sub>/NSC composite were measured using CR2025‐type coin cells. Typically, the active material (70 wt %, M<sub><i>x</i></sub>S<sub><i>y</i></sub>/NSC), acetylene black carbon (20 wt %, AC), and polyvinylidene difluoride (10 wt %, PVDF) were mixed in <i>N</i>‐methyl‐2‐pyrrolidone (NMP) to fabricate a slurry. This was coated on the Cu foil, which was used as the working electrode, and finally dried at 110 °C for 12 h in a vacuum oven. The average active material mass loading was around 0.8–1.0 mg cm<sup>−2</sup>. For LIB test systems, a Celgard 2400 microporous polypropylene membrane and metallic lithium served as the separator and the counter electrode, respectively. The electrolyte was 1 <sc>m</sc> LiPF<sub>6</sub> in a mixture of diethyl carbonate (DEC) and ethylene carbonate (EC) with a volume ratio of 1:1, including 10 % fluoroethylene carbonate (FEC) and 1 % vinylene carbonate (VC). For SIB test systems, the electrolyte was 1 <sc>m</sc> NaPF<sub>6</sub> in dimethoxyethane (DME) solution. Metallic sodium and Whatman glass fiber acted as the counter electrode and the separator, respectively. Galvanostatic charge/discharge profiles and cyclic voltammograms (CV) were measured in the potential range 0.01–3.0 V (vs. Li<sup>+</sup>/Li or Na<sup>+</sup>/Na) with a Land CT 2001A electrochemical workstation at room temperature.</p></section></section><section xml:id="chem202004241-sec-0010" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>This work was supported financially by the Fujian provincial project (2019H6005) and National Key Laboratory (6142808190203).</p></section><section xml:id="chem202004241-sec-0011" type="conflictOfInterest" numbered="no"><title type="main">Conflict of interest</title><p>The authors declare no conflict of interest.</p></section><bibliography style="numbered"><bib xml:id="chem202004241-bib-0001"><citation type="other" xml:id="chem202004241-cit-0001"> </citation></bib><bib xml:id="chem202004241-bib-0002"><label>1a</label><citation type="journal" xml:id="chem202004241-cit-0002"><author><givenNames>J.
