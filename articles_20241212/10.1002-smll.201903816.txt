type="main">Abstract</title><p>Major challenges in developing 2D transition‐metal disulfides (TMDs) as anode materials for lithium/sodium ion batteries (LIBs/SIBs) lie in rational design and targeted synthesis of TMD‐based nanocomposite structures with precisely controlled ion and electron transport. Herein, a general and scalable solvent‐exchange strategy is presented for uniform dispersion of few‐layer MoS<sub>2</sub> (f‐MoS<sub>2</sub>) from high‐boiling‐point solvents (<i>N</i>‐methyl‐2‐pyrrolidone (NMP), <i>N</i>,<i>N</i>‐dimethyl formaldehyde (DMF), etc.) into low‐boiling‐point solvents (water, ethanol, etc.). The solvent‐exchange strategy dramatically simplifies high‐yield production of dispersible MoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets as well as facilitates subsequent decoration of MoS<sub>2</sub> for various applications. As a demonstration, MoS<sub>2</sub>‐decorated nitrogen‐rich carbon spheres (MoS<sub>2</sub>‐NCS) are prepared via in situ growth of polypyrrole and subsequent pyrolysis. Benefiting from its ultrathin feature, largely exposed active surface, highly conductive framework and excellent structural integrity, the 2D core–shell architecture of MoS<sub>2</sub>‐NCS exhibits an outstanding reversible capacity and excellent cycling performance, achieving high initial discharge capacity of 1087.5 and 508.6 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>, capacity retentions of 95.6% and 94.2% after 500 and 250 charge/discharge cycles at 1 A g<sup>−1</sup>, for lithium/sodium ion storages, respectively.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="smll201903816-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:id="smll201903816-sec-0010"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Lithium/sodium ion batteries (LIBs/SIBs) are recognized as ideal energy storage systems due to their high specific capacity and long service life.<link href="#smll201903816-bib-0001"/> However, the promptly increasing demands for high‐performance energy storages of electric vehicles and large‐scale electric grids have caused an instant pursuit for improved battery performance.<link href="#smll201903816-bib-0002"/> Recently, transition‐metal sulfides have been widely applied as promising anode materials for LIBs/SIBs owing to their large abundances, low costs and intriguing properties.<link href="#smll201903816-bib-0003"/> In particular, molybdenum disulfide (MoS<sub>2</sub>), benefiting from sufficient reversible redox reactions provided by four‐electron transfer per formula unit, has been thought as promising anode materials for LIBs/SIBs. MoS<sub>2</sub> owns 2D layered architecture with an interlayer space of ≈0.62 nm, allowing convenient intercalations of Li/Na ions into interlayer spacing.<link href="#smll201903816-bib-0004"/> Moreover, layered S‐Mo‐S sandwiched architecture with multiple active sulfur sites acts as Li/Na ions reservoirs in a two‐step process including an insertion reaction followed by a redox reaction generating Li<sub>2</sub>S/Na<sub>2</sub>S and Mo, which therefore provides a high theoretical capacity of ≈670 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>, around two times that of commercial graphite anode (≈372 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>). Nonetheless, the intrinsically low electrical conductivity and relatively large volume changes of MoS<sub>2</sub> during charge/discharge processes generally bring kinetic inertia, inferior cycling performance and poor rate performance for Li/Na ion storages.<link href="#smll201903816-bib-0005"/> Furthermore, the restacking of 2D MoS<sub>2</sub> into agglomerates upon cycling impedes its complete Li/Na ion storing/releasing process, which significantly deteriorates its energy storage performance.</p><p>Against these drawbacks, typically two strategies have been exploited to optimize the electrochemical performance of MoS<sub>2</sub>‐based electrodes. One strategy is to fabricate unique nanostructured MoS<sub>2</sub> including nanoribbons,<link href="#smll201903816-bib-0006"/> nanosheets,<link href="#smll201903816-bib-0004 #smll201903816-bib-0007" style="qv">4a,7</link> nanotubes,<link href="#smll201903816-bib-0008"/> nanorods,<link href="#smll201903816-bib-0009"/> and nanoboxes,<link href="#smll201903816-bib-0010"/> which can moderate the tension upon cycling and hence maintain its structural steadiness.<link href="#smll201903816-bib-0011"/> The other strategy tackling these obstacles are concentrated on engineering novel MoS<sub>2</sub>/carbon nanocomposites,<link href="#smll201903816-bib-0012"/> which significantly enhance the specific capacity, rate performance and cycling stability due to the formation of unique MoS<sub>2</sub>/carbon interfaces exhibiting fast electron transport. This has been extensively proved by experimental results for improving energy storage performance of LIBs/SIBs by designing and fabricating 3D graphene/MoS<sub>2</sub> nanocomposites,<link href="#smll201903816-bib-0009 #smll201903816-bib-0013"/> carbon/MoS<sub>2</sub> inter‐overlapped nanocomposites,<link href="#smll201903816-bib-0014"/> carbon fiber/MoS<sub>2</sub> core/sheath nanocomposites,<link href="#smll201903816-bib-0015"/> 3D hierarchical carbon/MoS<sub>2</sub> nanocomposites,<link href="#smll201903816-bib-0016"/> etc. However, despite these tactics facilitate the electron/ion transfer and restrain structural collapse due to the presence of carbon protecting layers for partly improved cycling stability, MoS<sub>2</sub>‐based nanocomposites still suffer from the lack of design of electrode/electrolyte interfaces as well as the relatively large carbon contents (&gt;40 wt%), which not only decrease mass loadings of high‐capacity MoS<sub>2</sub> but also severely deteriorate the volume capacities of the whole electrodes. In these regards, combining 2D MoS<sub>2</sub> with ultrathin and rationally designed carbon coatings may generate new class of 2D nanocomposite materials with optimized atomic interfaces boosting Li/Na ion storages, but still meet a great challenge.</p><p>Herein, we demonstrate a novel approach to fabricate a 2D MoS<sub>2</sub>/nitrogen‐rich carbon sphere (MoS<sub>2</sub>‐NCS) heterostructure with a well‐defined interface. A versatile and scalable solvent‐exchange strategy is presented for the preparation of uniform dispersion of high‐concentration few‐layer MoS<sub>2</sub> (f‐MoS<sub>2</sub>) in low‐boiling‐point solvents, which opens up further assembly and functionalization of 2D MoS<sub>2</sub> for broad applications. As a demonstration, the as‐obtained f‐MoS<sub>2</sub> acting as a 2D template tethers in situ growth of polypyrrole (PPy) in a solution‐processed approach. Upon simple pyrolysis, the as‐obtained MoS<sub>2</sub>‐NCS nanocomposites show a well‐controlled morphology of ultrathin NCS coatings on MoS<sub>2</sub> with tailored inorganic/carbon compositions. The MoS<sub>2</sub>‐NCS architecture shows several merits when evaluated as an anode material for LIBs/SIBs: 1) The MoS<sub>2</sub>‐NCS nanocomposites not only prevent the restacking of 2D MoS<sub>2</sub> during cycling but also provide abundant active edge sites derived from NCS for rapid redox reactions. 2) The MoS<sub>2</sub>‐NCS nanocomposites with a closely packaged sphere‐on‐sheet structure display high packaging density and thus further increase volume energy density of the whole electrodes. 3) Robust adhesion of NCS coatings on f‐MoS<sub>2</sub> via strongly coordinated Mo‐N bonding assures an excellent structural stability for long‐term charge/discharge cycles. 4) NCS coatings within the MoS<sub>2</sub>‐NCS nanocomposites exhibits fast electron transport and N‐doping defects of NCS are available for more Li/Na ion storages. 5) Electrolytic ions and electrons will rapidly penetrate into electrode matrix due to tailored interfaces between the f‐MoS<sub>2</sub> and NCS. As a result, the MoS<sub>2</sub>‐NCS nanocomposites with a new designation deliver ultrahigh reversible specific capacities, remarkable rate capability and long cycling life for LIBs/SIBs, demonstrating their great potentials for rechargeable battery applications.</p></section><section xml:id="smll201903816-sec-0020"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p>Herein, a versatile and scalable solvent‐exchange strategy for achieving liquid‐phase exfoliation and uniform aqueous dispersion of f‐MoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets is presented, which enables subsequent functionalizations of MoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets for broad applications. Bath sonication of bulk MoS<sub>2</sub> (b‐MoS<sub>2</sub>) in <i>N</i>,<i>N</i>‐dimethyl formaldehyde (DMF) produces a uniform dispersion of f‐MoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets with an efficient exfoliation (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll201903816-fig-0001"><b>1</b></link>a). In most cases, high‐boiling‐point organic solvents like DMF are difficult to be thoroughly removed in subsequent steps, and organic residuals might greatly limit the viabilities of exfoliated MoS<sub>2</sub> for real manipulations. However, most easily removable low‐boiling‐point solvents such as water have relatively large surface tension (≈72.8 mJ m<sup>−2</sup>), making them not suitable for direct exfoliation of b‐MoS<sub>2</sub> into f‐MoS<sub>2</sub>. Actually, it is rather difficult to achieve a direct exfoliation and dispersion of b‐MoS<sub>2</sub> in water. Surprisingly, the exfoliation and uniform dispersion of f‐MoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets can be achieved in water via a solvent‐exchange strategy, namely, once the uniform dispersion of f‐MoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets in DMF is achieved, the wet‐state gathered f‐MoS<sub>2</sub> will be easily redispersed into water, and subsequently form a high‐concentration f‐MoS<sub>2</sub> suspension in low‐boiling‐point solvents without any stabilizers or surfactants. The possible dispersion mechanism for f‐MoS<sub>2</sub> in water via the solvent‐exchange strategy is explained as follows. Upon bath sonication of b‐MoS<sub>2</sub> in DMF, the flake sizes of f‐MoS<sub>2</sub> remarkably decrease compared with that of b‐MoS<sub>2</sub>, and thus the amounts of edge sulfur atoms of f‐MoS<sub>2</sub> sharply increase, which will be easily get charged with largely improved aqueous dispersibility.<link href="#smll201903816-bib-0017"/> For comparison, the direct sonication of b‐MoS<sub>2</sub> in water cannot produce exfoliated and uniform dispersed f‐MoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets even under long time sonication (Figure S1, Supporting Information). The uniform dispersion of f‐MoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets in low‐boiling‐point solvents facilitates a subsequent assembly and functionalization of f‐MoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets for broad applications. As a demonstration, direct growth of PPy spheres on the f‐MoS<sub>2</sub> template with subsequent pyrolysis procedure produces the MoS<sub>2</sub>‐decorated nitrogen‐rich carbon spheres (MoS<sub>2</sub>‐NCS).</p><figure xml:id="smll201903816-fig-0001"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll201903816:smll201903816-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll201903816-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) Schematic illustration of the preparation of MoS<sub>2</sub>/nitrogen‐rich carbon sphere (MoS<sub>2</sub>‐NCS) nanocomposites. SEM images of b) b‐MoS<sub>2</sub> and c,d) f‐MoS<sub>2</sub>. e) TEM and f) HRTEM images of f‐MoS<sub>2</sub> showing 2H MoS<sub>2</sub> (100) crystal planes. g) Crystal structure of MoS<sub>2</sub> of (100) crystal planes. h) AFM image of f‐MoS<sub>2</sub> and height profile.</caption></figure><p>Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) observations were carried out to monitor the morphological evolution from the b‐MoS<sub>2</sub> into f‐MoS<sub>2</sub> upon the exfoliation in DMF. The b‐MoS<sub>2</sub> shows a morphology of large flakes with lateral sizes of several to several dozens of micrometers (Figure <link href="#smll201903816-fig-0001"/>b). Upon the exfoliation, the f‐MoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets show much smaller lateral sizes than that of b‐MoS<sub>2</sub> (Figure <link href="#smll201903816-fig-0001"/>c). Enlarged SEM image of f‐MoS<sub>2</sub> clearly shows a nanosheet morphology with lateral sizes of several hundred nanometers (Figure <link href="#smll201903816-fig-0001"/>d). TEM image of f‐MoS<sub>2</sub> demonstrates a morphology of ultrathin nanosheets with several nanometers in laterals (Figure <link href="#smll201903816-fig-0001"/>e). HRTEM image of the MoS<sub>2</sub>‐NCS‐2 in Figure <link href="#smll201903816-fig-0001"/>f shows a lattice spacing of 0.29 nm, corresponding to the (100) crystal plane of 2H‐phase MoS<sub>2</sub> (Figure <link href="#smll201903816-fig-0001"/>g). According to atomic force microscope (AFM) results, the thickness of as‐obtained f‐MoS<sub>2</sub> is ≈2.9 nm (Figure <link href="#smll201903816-fig-0001"/>h). The layer number of f‐MoS<sub>2</sub> is calculated less than 5 layers because the thickness of single‐layer MoS<sub>2</sub> is 0.65 nm. X‐ray diffraction (XRD) patterns were carried out to give direct evidences for the exfoliation from b‐MoS<sub>2</sub> to f‐MoS<sub>2</sub>. The b‐MoS<sub>2</sub> shows the diffraction patterns which are well fitted to hexagonal structure of 2H‐phase MoS<sub>2</sub> (JCPDS No. 65–1951) (Figure S2a, Supporting Information). Compared with XRD patterns of b‐MoS<sub>2</sub>, f‐MoS<sub>2</sub> shows a relatively widen and weak primary (002) diffraction peak, indicating that more defects are brought into the f‐MoS<sub>2</sub> with decreased sizes upon sonication, which is expected to provide favorable ion pathways for the lithiation/delithiation processes.<link href="#smll201903816-bib-0018"/> The diffraction pattern with narrowed half peak width is observed when the wet‐state f‐MoS<sub>2</sub> is naturally dried in oven, which is due to the partial disappearance of defect structures of MoS<sub>2</sub> upon drying (Figure S2b, Supporting Information). The naturally dried f‐MoS<sub>2</sub> is difficult to be exfoliated and dispersed again in water. Raman spectra indicate more evidences for the exfoliation from b‐MoS<sub>2</sub> to f‐MoS<sub>2</sub> (Figure S3, Supporting Information). For b‐MoS<sub>2</sub>, there are two strong peaks at 375.7 and 403.0 cm<sup>−1</sup>, corresponding to in‐plane E<sub>12g</sub> and out‐of‐plane A<sub>1g</sub> vibration modes, respectively. Notably, f‐MoS<sub>2</sub> shows that both E<sub>12g</sub> and A<sub>1g</sub> vibrations red shift and soften compared with that of b‐MoS<sub>2</sub>. The peak differences between E<sub>12g</sub> and A<sub>1g</sub> are 27.3 and 25.0 cm<sup>−1</sup> for b‐MoS<sub>2</sub> and f‐MoS<sub>2</sub>, respectively, demonstrating that f‐MoS<sub>2</sub> has much thinner thickness than that of b‐MoS<sub>2</sub> upon sonication and exfoliation.<link href="#smll201903816-bib-0005 #smll201903816-bib-0019" style="qv">5a,19</link> Furthermore, the line widths of f‐MoS<sub>2</sub> with 4.5–5.5 cm<sup>−1</sup> are larger than mechanically exfoliated MoS<sub>2</sub> single crystals with 1.5–4.5 cm<sup>−1</sup>,<link href="#smll201903816-bib-0020"/> indicating that the as‐obtained f‐MoS<sub>2</sub> has smaller crystallite sizes and more defects.<link href="#smll201903816-bib-0021"/></p><p>The scalable solvent‐exchange strategy facilitates subsequent decorations of 2D MoS<sub>2</sub> through a solution‐processed method (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll201903816-fig-0002"><b>2</b></link>a). The f‐MoS<sub>2</sub> as a growth template guides the adsorption and in situ oxidative polymerization of pyrrole. The MoS<sub>2</sub>‐PPy intermediates were then prepared with increasing pyrrole concentrations, which are labeled as MoS<sub>2</sub>‐PPy‐1, MoS<sub>2</sub>‐PPy‐2, and MoS<sub>2</sub>‐PPy‐3, respectively. Upon annealing at 800 °C in a nitrogen atmosphere, the immobilized PPy species convert into NCS while keeping their original shapes, thus obtaining MoS<sub>2</sub>‐NCS‐1, MoS<sub>2</sub>‐NCS‐2, and MoS<sub>2</sub>‐NCS‐3, with increased NCS contents within the MoS<sub>2</sub>‐NCS nanocomposites. The morphologies of immobilized NCS in the MoS<sub>2</sub>‐NCS nanocomposites are precisely tailored by increasing the feeding ratios of pyrrole monomers to f‐MoS<sub>2</sub> template. Specifically, for MoS<sub>2</sub>‐NCS‐1 (Figure <link href="#smll201903816-fig-0002"/>b, Figure S4a, Supporting Information), uniformly distributed NCS with a mean diameter of ≈20 nm are observed on the surface of 2D f‐MoS<sub>2</sub>. When the initial concentration of pyrrole increases, homogeneous NCS coatings with enlarged particle sizes (≈50 nm) are achieved for MoS<sub>2</sub>‐NCS‐2 sample (Figure <link href="#smll201903816-fig-0002"/>c, Figure S4b, Supporting Information). Nevertheless, once the initial concentration of pyrrole monomers further increases (Figure <link href="#smll201903816-fig-0002"/>d, Figure S4c, Supporting Information), the aggregations of the NCS with a diameter of ≈100 nm form on the surface of f‐MoS<sub>2</sub>. TEM images further show that the MoS<sub>2</sub>‐NCS‐2 has unique 2D core–shell morphology (Figure <link href="#smll201903816-fig-0002"/>e,f). EDX elemental mappings of MoS<sub>2</sub>‐NCS‐2 exhibit homogeneous spatial distributions of Mo, S, C and N elements over the inspected fields (Figure <link href="#smll201903816-fig-0002"/>g, Figure S4d, Supporting Information), further verifying the successful nanoscale composition of the f‐MoS<sub>2</sub> with NCS. Large number of active sites from f‐MoS<sub>2</sub> for nucleation and in situ polymerization of PPy can be provided ascribed to certain mutual effects such as van der Waals forces and coordination interactions between N atoms of PPy and Mo atoms of f‐MoS<sub>2</sub>.<link href="#smll201903816-bib-0005" style="qv">5a</link> For comparison, neat NCS shows morphology of spheres with a diameter of ≈100 nm (Figure S5, Supporting Information), and the particle sizes of NCS slightly decrease upon pyrolysis.</p><figure xml:id="smll201903816-fig-0002"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll201903816:smll201903816-fig-0002" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll201903816-fig-0002-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) Schematic illustration of the preparation procedure of MoS<sub>2</sub>‐NCS. The electrochemical performance of the MoS<sub>2</sub>‐NCS nanocomposites with diverse NCS morphologies as anode materials for LIBs is assessed. <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll201903816-fig-0004"><b>4</b></link>a shows the first three cyclic voltammograms (CV) curves of MoS<sub>2</sub>‐NCS‐2 electrodes at a scan rate of 0.1 mV s<sup>−1</sup> within a potential range of 0.01–3.0 V, which clearly reveal the electrochemical lithiation/delithiation process. The cathodic peak at 1.03 V can be ascribed to the formation of Li<sub>x</sub>MoS<sub>2</sub> due to the intercalation of Li ions into MoS<sub>2</sub> interlayer, resulting in the phase transformation of MoS<sub>2</sub> from trigonal prismatic to octahedral crystalline structure.<link href="#smll201903816-bib-0014"/> The cathodic peak at 0.41 V is contributed to the conversion of MoS<sub>2</sub> into elementary Mo particles implanted into Li<sub>2</sub>S substrates and the ongoing electrochemically induced electrolyte degradation to form solid electrolyte interphase (SEI) films. In the subsequent anodic scan, the oxidation peaks center at 1.96, 1.24, and 0.36 V, indicating different lithiation mechanism since the first cycle.<link href="#smll201903816-bib-0005" style="qv">5c</link> In the proceeding anodic scan, a pronounced peak at 2.33 V can be attributed to the delithiation of Li<sub>2</sub>S to form sulfur (Li<sub>2</sub>S → 2Li<sup>+</sup> + S + 2e<sup>−</sup>). We also find a broad and weak peak at 1.60 V in the second and third anodic scans, due to the partial oxidation of Mo to produce MoS<sub>2</sub>.<link href="#smll201903816-bib-0029"/> After the first cycle, the electrode is mainly made up of S, Mo, and few MoS<sub>2</sub>, rather than the initial MoS<sub>2</sub>, and the reversible trend is fixed in subsequent charge/discharge cycles. No obvious redox pairs are observed in the CV curves of NCS electrode (Figure S16a, Supporting Information). CV curves of MoS<sub>2</sub>‐NCS‐1, MoS<sub>2</sub>‐NCS‐3, and f‐MoS<sub>2</sub> electrodes show redox pairs similar to that of MoS<sub>2</sub>‐NCS‐2 in the following two cycles (Figure S16b–d, Supporting Information). In general, Nyquist plots consist of a semicircle in the high‐frequency region and a straight line with a constant inclining angle in the low‐frequency region, which are associated with the charge‐transfer resistance and the solid‐state diffusion resistance of Li ions in the electrode, respectively.<link href="#smll201903816-bib-0005" style="qv">5a</link> In comparison with neat f‐MoS<sub>2</sub>, the diameter of semicircles at the high frequency are greatly lessened for the MoS<sub>2</sub>‐NCS nanocomposites, indicating the remarkably reduced resistances for charge transfer and ion diffusion during the lithiation/delithiation process for the MoS<sub>2</sub>‐NCS nanocomposite electrodes.<link href="#smll201903816-bib-0036"/></p><p>As a result, the MoS<sub>2</sub>‐NCS nanocomposites own several beneficial features as anode materials for high‐performance LIBs: 1) The f‐MoS<sub>2</sub> straightly coated with NCS renders MoS<sub>2</sub>‐NCS nanocomposites boosting the charge transfer rates and electrical contacting areas, especially along <i>x</i>‐axis. 2) The NCS coatings significantly promote the adsorption and intercalation of Li ions. 3) Large volume variations of f‐MoS<sub>2</sub> core during the charge/discharge process can be accommodated by NCS coatings, and meanwhile the 2D f‐MoS<sub>2</sub> offers an ideal template for the decoration of the NCS without agglomerations. The cycling performance of MoS<sub>2</sub>‐NCS‐2, NCS, and f‐MoS<sub>2</sub> electrodes at a current density of 1 A g<sup>−1</sup> is illustrated in Figure <link href="#smll201903816-fig-0004"/>e. Neat NCS electrode exhibits an excellent cycling stability for 500 charge/discharge cycles, but the reversible capacity is only ≈342 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>. Conversely, f‐MoS<sub>2</sub> electrode manifests a fierce capacity decrease, whose specific capacity can only remain at ≈255 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> after 300 charge/discharge cycles. The ever‐reduced electrical conductivity and dissolution of polysulfide intermediates are believed to account for decreased activities and fast capacity decays.<link href="#smll201903816-bib-0037"/> By contrast, the MoS<sub>2</sub>‐NCS‐2 electrode shows an excellent cycling performance with a high initial capacity of 1060 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> in the first cycle and then gradually increases to 1123 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> after 200 charge/discharge cycles, and retains 1038 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> after 500 charge/discharge cycles. The MoS<sub>2</sub>‐NCS‐2 electrode still shows a similar charge/discharge curve as the initial one after 500 charge/discharge cycles, indicating its highly stable cycling performance (Figure S19, Supporting Information). A slightly “activation” process in the initial charge/discharge processes is found from the second cycle onward, and this phenomenon might be ascribed to the cycling‐induced electrolyte penetration of electrolyte and the optimized ion diffusion pathways of electrode matrix. In fact, this phenomenon is widespread, and the current density in the galvanostatic charge/discharge measurements is the main influencing factor. This activation process tends to occur at the high charge/discharge current density, and is not obvious at the low charge/discharge current density.<link href="#smll201903816-bib-0038"/> SEM images of the MoS<sub>2</sub>‐NCS‐2 electrode show no obvious aggregations of f‐MoS<sub>2</sub> core after 500 charge/discharge cycles in the LIBs (Figure S20, Supporting Information), which is due to the efficient confinement effect of surrounded NCS shells of the f‐MoS<sub>2</sub> core during multiple cycles. The MoS<sub>2</sub>‐NCS‐2 electrode for 1000 cycles charge/discharge at a current density of 5 A g<sup>−1</sup> in the LIBs is measured (Figure S21, Supporting Information), realizing 71.6% of its initial discharge capacity after 1000 cycles. The extremely high cycling stability of MoS<sub>2</sub>‐NCS‐2 electrode can be also assigned to the optimized contents of NCS coatings on f‐MoS<sub>2</sub> as well as enhanced charge transfers between NCS and f‐MoS<sub>2</sub>.</p><p>The SIBs, a promising alternative to the LIBs, have attracted increasing attention due to abundance and low cost of sodium. The electrochemical performance of MoS<sub>2</sub>‐NCS‐2 as anode materials for the SIBs is evaluated. CV curves of MoS<sub>2</sub>‐NCS‐2 for first three cycles are presented in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll201903816-fig-0005"><b>5</b></link>a. In the first cycle, three reduction peaks at 1.0–1.1, 0.4–0.5, and 0–0.2 V are observed, which are attributed to the formation of SEI film, the conversion of MoS<sub>2</sub> into Na<sub>x</sub>MoS<sub>2</sub> and the transformation from Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>MoS<sub>2</sub> to Mo, respectively.<link href="#smll201903816-bib-0039"/> The oxidation peak at 1.3–2.1 V in the first cycle corresponds to vulcanizing Mo into MoS<sub>2</sub>. In the following cycles, the oxidation peak at ≈1.7 V and reduction peak at ≈0.8 V are highly overlapped, indicating a stable sodiation/desodiation process of MoS<sub>2</sub>‐NCS‐2 electrode. CV curves of the MoS<sub>2</sub>‐NCS‐2 electrode for 250 cycles are tested to further explore its cycling stability for the SIBs (Figure S22, Supporting Information). The intensity of the oxidation and reduction peaks at ≈1.7 and 0.8 V, respectively, decreases continuously with cycling, which is explained as sulfur depletion during cycling.<link href="#smll201903816-bib-0040"/> There are no obvious redox pairs in the CV curves of NCS electrode (Figure S23a, Supporting Information). The redox pairs similar to the MoS<sub>2</sub>‐NCS‐2 electrode are found in the MoS<sub>2</sub>‐NCS‐1, MoS<sub>2</sub>‐NCS‐3, and f‐MoS<sub>2</sub> electrodes (Figure S23b–d, Supporting Information). Galvanostatic charge/discharge curves of MoS<sub>2</sub>‐NCS‐2 for first three cycles at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> are displayed in Figure <link href="#smll201903816-fig-0005"/>b. The first discharge and charge specific capacities are 1299.6 and 613.4 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>. The initial irreversible decrease of the specific capacity is due to the formation of SEI film. Afterward, the reversible capacity of ≈508.6 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> and high Coulombic efficiency of ≈95% are achieved after three charge/discharge cycles. The MoS<sub>2</sub>‐NCS‐1, MoS<sub>2</sub>‐NCS‐3, NCS, and f‐MoS<sub>2</sub> electrodes in the SIBs show the reversible capacity of 287, 368, 40, and 171 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively (Figure S24a–d, Supporting Information). EIS curves of the MoS<sub>2</sub>‐NCS‐1, MoS<sub>2</sub>‐NCS‐2, MoS<sub>2</sub>‐NCS‐3, and f‐MoS<sub>2</sub> electrodes in the SIBs show that the MoS<sub>2</sub>‐NCS electrode has the smaller diameter of semicircles than that of neat f‐MoS<sub>2</sub> at high‐frequency regions, indicating the reduced resistances for MoS<sub>2</sub>‐NCS nanocomposite electrodes (Figure S25, Supporting Information). Rate performance of MoS<sub>2</sub>‐NCS‐2 is illustrated in Figure <link href="#smll201903816-fig-0005"/>c,d. High reversible specific capacities of 508, 431, 390, 366, 347, and 315 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> are delivered at current densities of 0.1, 0.2, 0.5, 1, 2, and 5 A g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. When current density decreases back to 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>, an average specific capacity of 478 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> is retained showing a superb reversibility. During long‐term cycling tests, MoS<sub>2</sub>‐NCS‐2 electrode exhibits a stable cycling at 1 A g<sup>−1</sup>, which shows a high reversible specific capacity of 292.2 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> after 250 charge/discharge cycles, realizing 94.1% of its initial discharge capacity (Figure <link href="#smll201903816-fig-0005"/>e). The as‐obtained MoS<sub>2</sub>‐NCS nanocomposites with a unique 2D core–shell morphology remarkably enhance the electrical conductivity and structural stability. As a result, the MoS<sub>2</sub>‐NCS‐2 electrodes show a high reversible capacities of 1087.5 and 508.6 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> for LIBs and SIBs, respectively. The excellent electrochemical performance of the MoS<sub>2</sub>‐NCS nanocomposites is ascribed to surrounded NCS coatings on the f‐MoS<sub>2</sub>, which not only accommodate the volumetric swelling and shrinking of f‐MoS<sub>2</sub> during cycles but also boost the charge transfer rate and enlarge the electrode/electrolyte contacting surface. Meanwhile 2D features of the f‐MoS<sub>2</sub> offer an ideal template for the decoration of NCS coatings. More interestingly, the NCS coatings significantly promote the adsorption of polysulfide intermediates and thus greatly enhance the cycling performance of such MoS<sub>2</sub>‐NCS nanocomposites<sub>.</sub> The MoS<sub>2</sub>‐NCS nanocomposites are considered as promising anode materials for high‐performance LIBs/SIBs. Moreover, the efficient and scalable approach to prepare high‐concentration aqueous MoS<sub>2</sub> suspension provides a solution‐processed pathway for fabricating a variety of functional MoS<sub>2</sub>‐based nanocomposites for broad applications.</p></section><section xml:id="smll201903816-sec-0040"><title type="main">Experimental Section</title><p><i>Preparation of the f‐MoS<sub>2</sub></i>: The exfoliation of b‐MoS<sub>2</sub> into f‐MoS<sub>2</sub> in DMF (1 g b‐MoS<sub>2</sub> in 100 mL DMF) was achieved by a bath sonication for 10 h.<link href="#smll201903816-bib-0041"/> The suspension was centrifuged at 1500 rpm for 20 min to remove thick MoS<sub>2</sub> flakes, and then the f‐MoS<sub>2</sub> was collected by filtration. The wet‐state f‐MoS<sub>2</sub> was dispersed in water under sonication for 10 min. The accurate concentration of f‐MoS<sub>2</sub> was determined by a weighting method. In contrast, direct exfoliation of dried f‐MoS<sub>2</sub> or b‐MoS<sub>2</sub> in water cannot achieve an efficient exfoliation and dispersion of MoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets even after a long‐time bath sonication (up to 100 h).</p><p><i>Preparation of the MoS<sub>2</sub>‐NCS</i>: The MoS<sub>2</sub>‐PPy nanocomposites were prepared by in situ polymerization of pyrrole on f‐MoS<sub>2</sub> template. Typically, the pH value of 60 mL aqueous suspension of f‐MoS<sub>2</sub> was adjusted to 1 by HCl. Pyrrole (50, 100, and 150 µL, respectively) and ammonium persulfate (APS) with a molar ratio of 2:1 were added, and the reaction was kelp for 12 h under stirring at 600 rpm at 0 °C, thus giving the products of MoS<sub>2</sub>‐PPy‐1, MoS<sub>2</sub>‐PPy‐2, and MoS<sub>2</sub>‐PPy‐3, respectively. The MoS<sub>2</sub>‐PPy nanocomposites were pyrolyzed at 800 °C for 2 h in a nitrogen atmosphere, thus obtaining the MoS<sub>2</sub>‐NCS nanocomposites denoted as MoS<sub>2</sub>‐NCS‐1, MoS<sub>2</sub>‐NCS‐2, and MoS<sub>2</sub>‐NCS‐3, respectively.</p></section><section xml:id="smll201903816-sec-0050" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title
