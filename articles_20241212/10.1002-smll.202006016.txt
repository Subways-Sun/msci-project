Information</caption></supportingInfoItem></supportingInformation><abstractGroup><abstract type="main" xml:lang="en"><title type="main">Abstract</title><p>As a promising candidate for large‐scale energy storage, sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) have superiority due to their low cost and abundance as a resource. Herein, homogeneous Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub> nanocrystallites embedded in carbon nanofibers (Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub>/CNFs) by electrospinning and selenization treatment are prepared. The obtained Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub>/CNFs exhibit good cycling performance, high reversible capacity, and excellent rate capability as anodes for SIBs. The outstanding performances are attributed to a combination of Na<sup>+</sup> intercalation, conversion reaction, and alloying with Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub>, disclosed through in‐situ X‐ray diffraction. Meanwhile, unique nanostructures provide large contact surface and tolerant accommodation to volume expansion which bring high reversibility and long cycle durability. This distinctive material shows prospective applications of SIBs especially under high current density.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="smll202006016-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub> embedded in carbon nanofibers (Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub>/CNFs) are prepared by electrospinning and tested as anode materials for Na‐ion batteries. Owning to the superior structural flexibility and combined processes, Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub>/CNFs deliver a stable capacity of 302 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 1 A g<sup>−1</sup> over 800 cycles and excellent rate performance (250 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 10 A g<sup>−1</sup>), which are promising for large‐scale energy‐storage systems.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll202006016:smll202006016-gra-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll202006016-gra-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body xml:id="smll202006016-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="no"><section xml:id="smll202006016-sec-0010" type="opening"><p>Li ion batteries are basically capable of meeting the growing demands for portable devices and electric vehicles; however, the fast developing society puts forward higher requirements for large‐scale electrical energy storage systems.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202006016-bib-0001 #smll202006016-bib-0002"><sup>1,2</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In recent years, sodium ion batteries (SIBs) have attracted great attention for their similar physiochemical properties to Li and far more resources.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202006016-bib-0003 #smll202006016-bib-0004 #smll202006016-bib-0005 #smll202006016-bib-0006"><sup>3–6</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, sluggish Na<sup>+</sup>‐intercalation kinetics hinder the direct utilization of graphite as an anode, though many cathode materials have been developed for SIBs;<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202006016-bib-0007"><sup>7</sup></link><sup>]</sup> traditional insertion‐type anodes, such as hard carbon, are unable to match them to achieve the best performance. New anode materials with high performances are urgently needed for SIBs.</p><p>Some anode materials such as P<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202006016-bib-0008 #smll202006016-bib-0009 #smll202006016-bib-0010 #smll202006016-bib-0011 #smll202006016-bib-0012 #smll202006016-bib-0013"><sup>8–13</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and Se<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202006016-bib-0014 #smll202006016-bib-0015 #smll202006016-bib-0016 #smll202006016-bib-0017 #smll202006016-bib-0018 #smll202006016-bib-0019 #smll202006016-bib-0020"><sup>14–20</sup></link><sup>]</sup> can deliver large capacities via conversion reactions during sodiation/desodiation. Metals exemplified by Sn<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202006016-bib-0021 #smll202006016-bib-0022 #smll202006016-bib-0023 #smll202006016-bib-0024 #smll202006016-bib-0025 #smll202006016-bib-0026 #smll202006016-bib-0027 #smll202006016-bib-0028 #smll202006016-bib-0029 #smll202006016-bib-0030"><sup>21–30</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and Sb<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202006016-bib-0031"><sup>31</sup></link><sup>]</sup> undergo alloying reactions also to provide huge capacities.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202006016-bib-0032"><sup>32</sup></link><sup>]</sup> It is obvious that when these two reactions are combined, a better anode could be obtained with desirable performances. Li et al.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202006016-bib-0033"><sup>33</sup></link><sup>]</sup> prepared Sn<sub>4</sub>P<sub>3</sub>/reduced graphene oxide (rGO) hybrids as anodes for SIBs with a capacity of 391 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 2.0 A g<sup>−1</sup>. Xiong et al.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202006016-bib-0034"><sup>34</sup></link><sup>]</sup> reported that Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> on sulfur‐doped graphene sheets enables a stable capacity retention of 83% for 900 cycles. However, those materials inevitably suffer from large volume expansion with severe capacity decay.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202006016-bib-0035"><sup>35</sup></link><sup>]</sup> It is needed to apply some special strategies like carbon coating for better electrochemical performances.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202006016-bib-0036"><sup>36</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>Antimony selenide (Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub>) demonstrates excellent photoelectronic properties and has been widely applied in field‐emission devices and photodetectors.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202006016-bib-0037"><sup>37</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Although not many studies have involved its application as anodes for SIBs, its properties show a very promising prospect, as 12 moles of electrons would be transferred when 1 mole of Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub> is used as the anode in SIBs, on basis of the general reaction, Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub> + 12Na<sup>+</sup> + 12e<sup>−</sup>→2Na<sub>3</sub>Sb + 3Na<sub>2</sub>Se, and when the theoretical capacity is 670 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>. However, the Na storage process is accompanied by a drastic volume expansion, and then the capacity decays rapidly and thus severely restricts further application.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202006016-bib-0038"><sup>38</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>Carbon nanostructured materials possess unique behaviors for ion storage and supply abundant active sites as well as accommodation room for volume expansion. The above features are important for SIBs. So, dispersing active materials into carbon substrates is a good option to alleviate volume expansion and improve the electrochemical kinetics.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202006016-bib-0039 #smll202006016-bib-0040 #smll202006016-bib-0041"><sup>39–41</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Particularly, 1D carbon nanofibers<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202006016-bib-0042"><sup>42</sup></link><sup>]</sup> have attracted much attention by the merits of direct electron conduction channels, high specific surface area, and capability to tolerate volume change.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202006016-bib-0043 #smll202006016-bib-0044 #smll202006016-bib-0045 #smll202006016-bib-0046 #smll202006016-bib-0047"><sup>43–47</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In order to construct nanofiber structures, many techniques, such as hydrothermal method and electrospinning technique, have been explored and applied. Zhu et al.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202006016-bib-0048"><sup>48</sup></link><sup>]</sup> used electrospun Sb/C fibers as SIB anodes, and realized a high capacity and stable long‐term cycling performance.</p><p>In this work, we prepared Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub>/C nanofibers (Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub>/CNFs) as anodes for SIBs, by means of electrospinning followed by selenization treatment. This composite consisted of highly dispersed Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub> nanocrystallites wrapped by 1D carbon nanofibers. Nanocrystallites and their unique structure are keys to achieving an outstanding rate capability and prolonged cycle life. Also, in‐situ X‐ray diffraction (XRD) was performed to further understand the fundamental mechanisms during charge/discharge processes, which disclosed the combination of Na<sup>+</sup> intercalation, conversion, and alloying reactions with Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub>.</p><p>The manufacturing procedure of Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub>/CNFs is shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll202006016-fig-0001"><b>1</b></link>. Firstly, there are SbCl<sub>3</sub> and PAN (polyacrylonitrile, (C<sub>3</sub>H<sub>3</sub>N)<sub>n</sub>) in DMF (N,N‐dimethylformamide) in the needle as the precursor solution for electrospinning. Under appropriate conditions and high voltage, the pristine PAN fibers formed with highly dispersed SbCl<sub>3</sub> as seeds. The nanofibers were dried at 80 °C to remove redundant DMF, and became more stable and robust. Then the precursor was put into a tube furnace and heat treated at 240 °C under Ar atmosphere for stabilization and mild carbonization, and deep brown fibers were obtained for further sintering at 500 °C under H<sub>2</sub>Se/Ar atmosphere, leading to the formation of Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub> and complete carbonization. Finally, a black product was demonstrated as Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub>/CNFs and subjected to a series of the following tests.</p><figure xml:id="smll202006016-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll202006016:smll202006016-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll202006016-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Schematic illustration of the fabrication process of Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub>/CNFs.</caption></figure><p>The samples were characterized with XRD, and the patterns are displayed in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll202006016-fig-0002"><b>2</b>a</link>, and each peak of these two samples are almost identical implying the same phase. The red one is pristine Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub> and the blue one is Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub>/CNFs. However, the intensities are a little different from the standard powder diffraction file (PDF) card which can be ascribed to preferential orientations. All samples can be indexed to the orthorhombic crystal structure of Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub> (JCPDS No. 75–1462) which belongs to <i>Pnma</i> space group. The main peaks located at 2θ of 16.8°, 23.9°, 27.3°, 31.1°, and 34° can be attributed to the (201), (301), (302), (212), and (402) planes of Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub>, respectively. Besides, there is a broad peak between ≈20°–30°, distinguished by green cycles which are considered as carbon.</p><figure xml:id="smll202006016-fig-0002"><label>2</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll202006016:smll202006016-fig-0002" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll202006016-fig-0002-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) XRD patterns and b) TGA curves of Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub>/CNFs and Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub>; c) XRD patterns of the residue after TGA; d) High‐resolution full XPS, e) Sb3d, and f) Se5d XPS of Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub>/CNFs.</caption></figure><p>To confirm the content of carbon in Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub>/CNFs, thermogravimetry and differential thermal analysis (TGA) (Figure <link href="#smll202006016-fig-0002">2b</link>) was performed in air from room temperature to 800 °C during which Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub> was oxidized and decomposed to Sb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> and SeO<sub>2</sub>. After TGA, the residue was analyzed by XRD and the pattern is well matched with Sb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> (JCPDS No.80‐0231, Figure <link href="#smll202006016-fig-0002">2c</link>). It is clear that the volatile SeO<sub>2</sub> and CO<sub>2</sub> caused the mass loss. As shown in Figure <link href="#smll202006016-fig-0002">2b</link>, the content of carbon is calculated as 31.8 wt%.</p><p>X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) was performed to analyze the compositions and the valence states of surface elements of Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub>/CNFs. In Figure <link href="#smll202006016-fig-0002">2d</link>, the complete XPS is displayed, from which C, N, Sb, and Se could be distinctly discovered in Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub>/CNFs. Note that nitrogen was provided a bit by the PAN. Figure <link href="#smll202006016-fig-0002">2e</link> shows two peaks centered at 538 and 528.7 eV which can be assigned to Sb3d<sub>3/2</sub> and Sb3d<sub>5/2</sub>, respectively. Meanwhile two shoulder peaks at 539.4 and 530 eV can be ascribed to the surface oxidation of Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub> and the carbon layer. There is a peak at 55 eV accompanied by a satellite peak at 52.8 eV in Figure <link href="#smll202006016-fig-0002">2f</link>, which are derived from Se3d<sub>3/2</sub> and Se3d<sub>5/2</sub>, respectively.</p><p>Via a scanning electron microscope (SEM), the surface morphology and internal structure of the samples under different resolutions are displayed in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll202006016-fig-0003"><b>3</b>a–c</link>, in which Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub>/CNFs exist as uniform nanofibers. After carbonization and selenization, the fibrous morphology is well maintained without any obvious change compared with original fibers. In Figure <link href="#smll202006016-fig-0003">3d–g</link>, the energy dispersive X‐Ray spectroscopy (EDX) mappings of Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub>/CNFs are displayed, from which Sb and Se could be seen. As shown in Figure S1, Supporting Information, the C mapping is not clear owing to carbon‐based conducting resin used for SEM. In this case, due to the N provided by PAN, the result in Figure <link href="#smll202006016-fig-0003">3g</link> suggests that Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub> particles were wrapped by carbon nanofibers. Figure S2, Supporting Information, exhibits the EDX mapping of pure bulk Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub>, and the existence of Sb and Se with a slight amount nitrogen is obvious.</p><figure xml:id="smll202006016-fig-0003"><label>3</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll202006016:smll202006016-fig-0003" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll202006016-fig-0003-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>SEM images of a) pristine SbCl<sub>3</sub>/PAN fibers, and b,c) Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub>/CNFs; d) EDX mappings of e) Sb, f) Se, and g) N in the selected area of Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub>/CNFs; h) TEM image, i) HRTEM image and j) SAED patterns of Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub>/CNFs.</caption></figure><p>It is also verified from transmission electron microscopy (TEM) that Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub> nano‐crystallites are well dispersed in the carbon matrix (Figure <link href="#smll202006016-fig-0003">3h</link>), and the fibrous morphology is consistent with that in the SEM images. The edge area of one nanofiber was observed by high resolution TEM (HRTEM) (Figure <link href="#smll202006016-fig-0003">3i</link>) and selected area electron diffraction (SAED, Figure <link href="#smll202006016-fig-0003">3j</link>). The HRTEM image shows that the size of Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub> nanocrystallites is about 10–15 nm, revealing an interplanar distance of 0.266 nm, corresponding to the (402) plane of the orthorhombic Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub>. The SAED patterns of Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub>/CNFs agree with the XRD patterns and prove that the obtained Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub> could be indexed to orthorhombic structure. This fiber‐matrix structure can facilitate the interaction between Na<sup>+</sup> and active materials, as well as enhance the contact area between Na<sup>+</sup> and electrolytes.</p><p>A unique nanostructure was successfully fabricated by combining carbon nanofibers and Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub> particles, and then we utilized Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub>/CNFs to evaluate the electrochemical performances. The samples were assembled as anodes in Na half cells, and cyclic voltammetry (CV) tests were conducted with a voltage window of 0.01 and 3.0 V at a scan rate of 0.1 mV s<sup>−1</sup>. For comparison, pure Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub> was prepared and tested in the same condition as the control group.</p><p>The initial five curves are shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll202006016-fig-0004"><b>4</b>a</link>. In the first cathodic scan of Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub>/CNFs, the broad peak between 1.1 and 1.3 V is associated with the solid electrolyte interphase (SEI) formation, and the peak at 0.9 V is considered as the intercalation of Na<sup>+</sup> into Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub>/CNFs. Two sharp cathodic peaks at about 0.79 and 0.28 V are observed and can be attributed to the conversion reaction with Na<sub>2</sub>Se formation and alloying reaction between Sb and Na<sup>+</sup>, respectively. For the anodic scan, an obvious peak at 0.76 V corresponds to the dealloying reaction of Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>Sb (<i>x</i> = 1–3). The oxidation peaks at about 1.3 V relate to the conversion reaction of Na<sup>+</sup> with the formation of Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub>. As shown in Figure <link href="#smll202006016-fig-0004">4a</link>, from the second to the fifth scan, the curves are almost identical, indicating a good reversibility of Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub>/CNF anodes during the cycling. Compared with Figure S4, Supporting Information, the CV curves of pure Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub> recorded in the same conditions are almost identical to those of Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub>/CNFs, which implied that the same electrochemical reactions occurred. Pure Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub> exhibits a less stable performance which can be inferred from the misalignment of the initial three cycles.</p><figure xml:id="smll202006016-fig-0004"><label>4</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll202006016:smll202006016-fig-0004" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll202006016-fig-0004-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) Cyclic voltammetry (CV) in a voltage window of 0.01 and 3.0 V at a scan rate of 0.1 mV s<sup>−1</sup> for the initial five cycles of Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub>/CNFs. b) Galvanostatic charge/discharge curves of Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub>/CNFs at a current density of 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. c) Rate performance at various current densities. Cycling performance at a current density of d) 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> and e) 1.0 A g<sup>−1</sup>.</caption></figure><p>To verify the benefits from nanostructures and the high dispersity of nanoparticles, the rate performances were systematically evaluated from between 0.1 and 10.0 A g<sup>−1</sup>. As shown in Figure <link href="#smll202006016-fig-0004">4c</link>, Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub>/CNFs display higher reversible capacity than the control group under all the different current densities. Encouragingly, the capacity remains 250 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> when the current density is 100‐fold increased (0.1 to 10.0 A g<sup>−1</sup>). Even when the current density turns back to 1.0 A g<sup>−1</sup> after cycling at different rates, the specific capacity could be recovered to 345.8 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> with a high capacity retention of 99.7%, implying excellent reversibility. Available reported results are summarized in Tables S1 and S2, Supporting Information, and by contrast, Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub>/CNFs exhibit excellent electrochemical performance, especially the discharge capacity and cyclic stability under high current density.</p><p>In a voltage window of 0.01–3.0 V, the galvanostatic charge‐discharge cycling was undertaken to further explore the cycling performance of Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub> and Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub>/CNFs. The cyclic performances at a rate of 0.1 and 1.0 A g<sup>−1</sup> are shown in Figure <link href="#smll202006016-fig-0004">4d,e</link>, respectively. In Figure <link href="#smll202006016-fig-0004">4d</link>, Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub>/CNFs deliver a reversible capacity of 532.6 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>, compared with the 322.4 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> given by pure Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub> at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>, which presents a slight capacity decay during 120 cycles. In Figure <link href="#smll202006016-fig-0004">4e</link>, the density current increases by ten times to be 1.0 A g<sup>−1</sup> for long cycling tests. In comparison, the blue pairs which represent the Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub> anode yield a reversible capacity of 302.7 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> after 800 cycles, demonstrating good cycling stability and by contrast, the Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub> anode quickly fades to 24.9 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> after 50 cycles at 1 A g<sup>−1</sup>. Clearly the better cycling performance of Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub>/CNFs is attributed to the introduction of a carbon fiber matrix, which will be discussed in the following paragraph.</p><p>Meanwhile, electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) was performed to reveal the reaction kinetics during charge/discharge processes. According to Figure S3, Supporting Information, the EIS can be divided into two parts, a semicircle and a slope line. The smaller diameter of the semicircle for Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub>/CNFs compared with pure Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub> at the medium‐high frequency region indicates a lower charge resistance benefiting from CNFs. Obviously, the addition of CNFs greatly improves the charge transfer dynamics. Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub>/CNFs have better rate performance and cycling stability compared with pristine Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub> and it is the unique structure introduced by the nanofiber carbon matrix that can shorten the diffusion path of Na<sup>+</sup> and electrons. Meanwhile, the staggered carbon fibers and hierarchical architecture, which can increase the surface area of the electrode material, provide more active sites to store Na<sup>+</sup> to achieve high capacity. In this composite anode, CNFs play a significant role as a rigid framework which can coat the active sites, effectively alleviate the huge volume expansion, and prevent the decomposition and collapse of Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub> in the electrochemical process.</p><p>For the evaluation of the Na<sup>+</sup> coefficients for both Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub> and Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub>/CNFs, the galvanostatic intermittent titration technique (GITT) tests were performed at different voltages. The test parameters and calculation principles are given in Figures S8 and S9, Supporting Information. As shown in Figure S8(b), Supporting Information, the D<sub>Na+</sub> (Na<sup>+</sup> apparent diffusion coefficient) in Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub>/CNFs varies from 3.56 × 10<sup>−12</sup> to 1.67 × 10<sup>−11</sup> cm<sup>2</sup> s<sup>−1</sup>, which demonstrates more rapid Na bulk diffusion compared with pure Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub> (1.14 × 10<sup>−12</sup> to 6.80 × 10<sup>−12</sup> cm<sup>2</sup> s<sup>−1</sup>).</p><p>To explore the potential for practical applications, full cells were assembled with Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub>/CNFs as anodes from this work and Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>@rGO (NVP) as cathodes. The electrochemical performances of NVP@Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub>/CNF full cells are shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll202006016-fig-0005"><b>5</b></link>. The full cell delivers a reversible discharge capacity of 120 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> even after about 600 cycles at 1 A g<sup>−1</sup>. The typical charge‐discharge profiles are depicted in Figure <link href="#smll202006016-fig-0005">5b</link>, and then the rate performance was evaluated from 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> to 10 A g<sup>−1</sup>; the reversible discharge capacity varies from 165 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> to 82 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#smll202006016-fig-0005">5c</link>). Therefore, Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub>/CNFs exhibit both outstanding rate capability and cyclic performance, demonstrating ideal anode materials for SIBs.</p><figure xml:id="smll202006016-fig-0005"><label>5</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll202006016:smll202006016-fig-0005" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll202006016-fig-0005-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) Cycling performance of NVP@Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub>/CNF full cells at a current density of 1.0 A g<sup>−1</sup>. b) Galvanostatic charge/discharge curves of NVP@Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub>/CNF full cells at a current density of 1 A g<sup>−1</sup>. c) Rate performance at various current densities.</caption></figure><p>In‐situ XRD was performed to illustrate structural change and sodiation/desodiation mechanisms of Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub>/CNFs during charge‐discharge cycles. This testing was operated in a specially designed button cell with a sealed window which allows X‐ray transmission, and the cell was operated at a current of 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> within a voltage window of 0.01–3.0 V.</p><p>In <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll202006016-fig-0006"><b>6</b>a</link>, the XRD patterns are exhibited in a 3D diagram which consists of a sequence of 2D patterns collected at different states in the initial charge/discharge cycle. These patterns are piled sequentially according to charging‐discharging curves. Figure <link href="#smll202006016-fig-0006">6b</link> is the contour pattern corresponding to Figure <link href="#smll202006016-fig-0006">6a</link> which is derived from 3D XRD patterns. It is convenient to distinguish the occurrence and vanishing of peaks during the cycle. For example, the blue areas indicate that there is the lowest relative intensity of the XRD peaks and the red areas illustrate the existence of the strongest peaks. Note that the peaks around 38° and 44° belong to stainless steels. The whole process is divided into six parts based on the reaction sequence and are distinguished by various colors.</p><figure xml:id="smll202006016-fig-0006"><label>6</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll202006016:smll202006016-fig-0006" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll202006016-fig-0006-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) In‐situ XRD patterns against the voltage during the initial cycle with description of the six‐stage reaction mechanism and b) contour pattern with schematics of the reaction mechanism.</caption></figure><p>As shown in Figure <link href="#smll202006016-fig-0006">6a</link>, before discharging, the initial XRD pattern of the anode is the first one in Stage I, which displays obvious and well‐matched peaks from Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub> compared with Figure <link href="#smll202006016-fig-0003">3a</link>, indexed as (301), (302), (212), and (402) of Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub>. Then the voltage quickly declines and very slight changes (moving towards the lower angle) occur for the peaks, implying that Stage I is when the intercalation of Na<sup>+</sup> into Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub> as well as the formation of SEI films happens. So, the reaction in Stage I can be described as: Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub> + <i>x</i>Na<sup>+</sup> + <i>x</i>e<sup>−</sup> → Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub>.</p><p>In Stage II, the peaks of Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub> are weaker with the progress of discharging and almost vanish at the end of this stage; in the meantime, several new peaks appear. A new peak at 22.6° can be indexed to (111) of Na<sub>2</sub>Se (JCPDS No.77‐2155) and the peak around 29° is well matched with (012) of Sb (JCPDS No.85‐1323). These results suggest the conversion reaction of Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub> corresponding to a cathodic peak of about 0.79 V in the CV profiles (Figure <link href="#smll202006016-fig-0004">4a</link>). The reaction in Stage II can be described as: Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub> + (6–<i>x</i>)Na<sup>+</sup> + (6–<i>x</i>)e<sup>−</sup> → 2Sb + 3Na<sub>2</sub>Se.</p><p>Stage III is the end of discharging, and the voltage is discharged to 0.01 V. The intensity of the peak from Sb is gradually weakened and vanishes at the discharging tail. There are two new discernible peaks at around 37° and 41°, corresponding to NaSb (JCPDS No.74‐0801) and Na<sub>3</sub>Sb (JCPDS No.74‐1162), respectively. The reaction in Stage III can be described as: Sb + <i>x</i>Na<sup>+</sup> + <i>x</i>e<sup>−</sup> → Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>Sb. During the whole sodiation process, Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub> reacts with Na<sup>+</sup>, including the intercalation, conversion, and alloying reactions. In this case, before the recharging, there are Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>Sb and Na<sub>2</sub>Se left in the anode.</p><p>Between 0.01–0.1 V, Stage IV is a de‐alloying reaction. Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>Sb is decomposed into Na<sup>+</sup> and amorphous Sb with a low degree of crystallinity which accounts for no new peak, and the disappearance of Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>Sb confirms this process. The reaction in Stage IV can be described as: Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>Sb → Sb + <i>x</i>Na<sup>+</sup> + <i>x</i>e<sup>−</sup>.</p><p>In Stage V, the amorphous Sb reacts with Na<sub>2</sub>Se to produce Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub>, accompanying the vanishing of the peak of Na<sub>2</sub>Se. The reaction in Stage V can be described as: 2Sb + 3Na<sub>2</sub>Se → Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub> + (6–<i>x</i>)Na<sup>+</sup> + (6–<i>x</i>)e<sup>−</sup>.</p><p>Until the voltage comes to the maximum value of 3.0 V, the deintercalation of sodium occurs and Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub> forms. Although the peak intensity is feeble due to low crystallinity, certain main planes of Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub> are still visible. The reaction in Stage VI can be described as: Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub> → Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub> + <i>x</i>Na<sup>+</sup> + <i>x</i>e<sup>−</sup>.</p><p>The intercalation, conversion, and alloying processes are clearly disclosed by in‐situ XRD. The whole process is well consistent with CVs and can be another strong evidence for the good reversibility and cyclic stability of Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub>/CNFs.</p><p>As shown above, the results indicate that the outstanding performance of Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub>/CNFs basically benefits from the unique 1D structured carbon nanofibers with highly dispersed Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub> nanoparticles. The main reason that caused the dramatic capacity decay of pure Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub> is the large volume expansion, which is handily solved by the accommodation of carbon nanofibers. Besides, the electrical conductivity is also improved due to the introduction of graphitized carbon and the ion pathway is strongly shortened and the transportation is greatly facilitated by constructing nanofibers. Meanwhile, the agglomeration is well prevented due to the high dispersion of nanoparticles. These favorable performances suggest that Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub>/CNFs are promising as anode materials for SIBs.</p><p>In summary, a composite of Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub> particles embedded in carbon nanofibers was obtained via a relatively simple electrospinning method and the selenization of anodes for SIBs. The Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub>/CNF anode exhibits ultralong cycle durability with high capacity retention as well as prominent rate performance. In depth, the charge/discharge process is revealed by in‐situ XRD and the variation at each stage is clearly disclosed. With the combination of intercalation, conversion, and alloying processes, high capacity can be achieved. Meanwhile, carbon frameworks can furnish the superiority in rate performance which attribute to the improvement in electric conductivity and facilitation of each particle in the matrix for capacity contribution. From these, we can further understand the mechanism of Na<sup>+</sup> storage in Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub>/CNFs, which would help construct more reasonable anodes/cathodes for better performance. This work offers promising anodes for SIBs and the distinctive nano‐structure is proven to be a wonderful route to deal with defects of alloy and conversion‐type materials.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202006016-sec-0020"><title type="main">Experimental Section</title><section xml:id="smll202006016-sec-0030"><section xml:id="smll202006016-sec-0040"><section xml:id="smll202006016-sec-0050"><title type="main">Preparation of Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub>/CNFs</title><p>Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub>/CNFs were prepared through two steps. The first step was dissolving antimony chloride and PAN in DMF to form a precursor solution for electrospinning. In short, 1.0 g of SbCl<sub>3</sub> and 1.0 g of PAN were dissolved in 10 ml DMF with vigorous magnetic agitation overnight. When the clear solution was obtained, it was transferred into a plastic syringe at a constant flow rate of 0.4 mL h<sup>−1</sup>, and the nanofibers were obtained on the foil through electrospinning with a high voltage of 15 kV. The former product was dried at 260 °C in air for 1 h (1 °C min<sup>−1</sup>). The second step was selenizing the above product at 500 °C for 3 h (5 °C min<sup>−1</sup>) in a horizontal tube furnace under H<sub>2</sub>Se/argon atmosphere. As a control experiment, bare Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub> was prepared by a similar route without the addition of PAN.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202006016-sec-0060"><title type="main">Materials Characterization</title><p>The sample was characterized by XRD (Rigaku Ultima IV with Cu‐Kα radiation, λ = 1.5418 Å) to examine crystalline phases. TGA was performed with a Rigaku PTC‐10A TG‐DTA analyzer. The microstructure and morphology of the samples were analyzed through a field emission SEM (FESEM, JEOL‐JSM7500) and HRTEM (FEI Tecnai, model G2F‐20 field emission TEM). The contents of surface elements were detected by XPS (Thermo Scientific ESCALAB 250Xi).</p></section><section xml:id="smll202006016-sec-0070"><title type="main">Electrochemical Measurements</title><p>The electrochemical performance of Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub>/CNFs or bare Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub> as anodes was assessed in CR2025 coin‐type cells. The working electrode was made by mixing 70% active materials, 20% carbon black as the conductive additive, 5% sodium carboxymethyl cellulose (CMC‐Na), and 5% polyacrylamide (PAM) as the polymer binder on copper foil. Subsequently, the film was dried at 70 °C. The working electrode was made of Cu foils with diameters of 12 mm, and the average mass loading of Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub> on each anode was calculated as 0.86 mg. The mass loading was 1.2–1.5 mg cm<sup>−2</sup>. Then the working electrode, glass fiber (Whatman GF/C), and sodium metal as the counter electrode were assembled into a half coin cell in a glove box full of argon atmosphere, with an electrolyte of 1 <sc>m</sc> NaClO<sub>4</sub> in ethylene carbonate/diethyl carbonate (1/1, v/v) with 5 wt% fluoroethylene carbonate. CV tests were performed on a CHI600A electrochemical workstation (Shanghai Chenhua) between the potential range of 0.01 and 3.0 V (versus Na/Na<sup>+</sup>). EIS was conducted on a Zahner‐Elektrik IM6e electrochemical workstation by sweeping the frequency from 100 kHz to 10 mHz with an AC amplitude of 5 mV.</p></section></section></section></section><section xml:id="smll202006016-sec-0080" type="acknowledgments"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>This work was supported by National Key Research and Development Program of China (2016YFA0200200).</p></section><section xml:id="smll202006016-sec-0090" type="conflictOfInterest"><title type="main">Conflict of Interest</title><p>The authors declare no conflict of interest.</p></section><bibliography xml:id="smll202006016-bibl-0001" style="numbered"><bib xml:id="smll202006016-bib-0001"><citation type="journal" xml:id="smll202006016-cit-0001"><author><givenNames>J.
