However, transition‐metal dichalcogenide (TMD) anodes show unsatisfactory Na<sup>+</sup> electrochemical performance owing to the low intrinsic conductivity and inferior ion transport kinetics. Here, an elaborate design is developed to prepare a composite of WSSe nanosheets supported on a 3D cross‐networked porous carbon skeleton (WSSe@CPCS), which possesses en‐rich anion vacancies and WSSe with expanded inter‐layer spacing, as well as an interconnected porous structure. As a result, the WSSe@CPCS anode for sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) exhibits preeminent reversible capacities, excellent cycle stability, and superior rate capability. The systematic electrochemical kinetic analysis and density functional theory results further show that the effect of anion vacancies and CPCS synergistically enhances the conductivity and reduces charge transfer resistance, thus making a great contribution to fast reaction kinetics. Finally, the implementations of the WSSe@CPCS anode in progressive SIB and DIB full‐cell configurations exhibit satisfactory performance, which reveals their widely practical application. This research will provide an exciting approach to designing advanced defect‐structured tungsten‐based TMD materials for SIBs, DIBs, and even a broad range of energy storage.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="smll202206340-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>An elaborate design is developed to prepare a composite of WSSe nanosheets supported on a 3D cross‐networked porous carbon skeleton (WSSe@CPCS), which possesses en‐rich anion vacancies and WSSe with expanded inter‐layer spacing, as well as interconnected porous structure. xml:id="smll202206340-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="smll202206340-sec-0010"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs), with their high energy density and excellent cycling performance, have become one of the most valuable energy storage systems in the field of portable electronic equipment and electric vehicles in the past decades.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202206340-bib-0001 #smll202206340-bib-0002 #smll202206340-bib-0003 #smll202206340-bib-0004"><sup>1–4</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, the limited reservoir and uneven geographical distribution of lithium resources on earth seriously affect its practical application and restrict the development of LIBs.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202206340-bib-0005 #smll202206340-bib-0006"><sup>5,6</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Compared with lithium element, metallic sodium element is more abundant, and the charging–discharging mechanism of sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) is similar to LIBs.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202206340-bib-0007"><sup>7</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Therefore, the development of high‐capacity and long‐life electrode materials for SIBs is of great significance for the application and progress of energy storage systems.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202206340-bib-0008"><sup>8</sup></link><sup>]</sup> It is worth mentioning that the sodium‐based dual‐ion battery (SDIB) has become a kind of sodium‐ion energy storage device with wide application prospects due to its use of relatively inexpensive expanded graphite (EG) as cathode material and its high discharge capacity and excellent rate stability.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202206340-bib-0009"><sup>9</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Compared with SIBs, SDIBs have many advantages: 1) SDIBs have a very high operating voltage (&gt;4 V), which shows a high energy density and a great application potential; 2) Compared with the traditional sodium metal or sodium‐containing compound cathode materials for SIBs, the cathode material for SDIBs is usually graphite with relatively low price, which reduces the production cost of batteries; 3) Unique charge storage mechanism‐cathode and anode can simultaneously store and release ions. However, the Na<sup>+</sup> dynamic diffusion during the discharge–charge process is slower because the radius of Na<sup>+</sup> (1.02 Å) is larger than that of Li<sup>+</sup> (0.76 Å), resulting in large volume expansion and structural damage of electrode material during cycling, which ultimately leads to the deterioration of battery capacity and cycle life.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202206340-bib-0010 #smll202206340-bib-0011"><sup>10,11</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Especially for the carbonaceous anode materials, the above shortcomings are particularly obvious, and eventually lead to their low theoretical capacity (NaC<sub>64</sub>: 30 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>).<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202206340-bib-0012"><sup>12</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Conversion/alloying‐based materials with improved capacity have thus been studied but tend to suffer from various physical and chemical changes during the conversion and alloying process with unsatisfactory cycling stability.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202206340-bib-0013 #smll202206340-bib-0014"><sup>13,14</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Therefore, the design and development of ideal anode materials for SIBs and SDIBs have become the key link to breaking through the bottleneck of energy storage.</p><p>Transition‐metal dichalcogenides (TMDs) have the advantages of a unique layered structure, environmentally friendly, and low cost.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202206340-bib-0015 #smll202206340-bib-0016"><sup>15,16</sup></link><sup>]</sup> When used as anodes material for SIBs and SDIBs, their conversion reaction can convert more electrons than other materials, so it is expected to obtain higher charge storage and specific capacity compared with carbonaceous materials. Tungsten selenide (WSe<sub>2</sub>), as a typical 2D lamellar TMDs, has attracted extensive attention due to its relatively large interlamellar spacing (0.65 nm) and weak van der Waals interaction between the Se‐W‐Se layer.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202206340-bib-0017 #smll202206340-bib-0018"><sup>17,18</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, the poor electrical conductivity and ion transport kinetics of the WSe<sub>2</sub> anode lead to high charge transfer resistance and low ion diffusion coefficient, resulting in severe capacity fading.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202206340-bib-0019 #smll202206340-bib-0020"><sup>19,20</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In addition, a drastic volume variation occurs during the conversion reaction between WSe<sub>2</sub> and Na<sup>+</sup>, leading to electrode fracture.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202206340-bib-0021"><sup>21</sup></link><sup>]</sup> To conquer the above obstacles, the most advanced strategy used is to design nanoscale materials and combine them with carbonaceous materials. The transport path of diffusion ions within the electrode matrix can be significantly shortened by nanoscale materials.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202206340-bib-0021 #smll202206340-bib-0022"><sup>21,22</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The integration of several carbonaceous materials (e.g., graphene, carbon nanotubes, and coating carbon shells) with the WSe<sub>2</sub> electrode was introduced to improve the electrical conductivity of the WSe<sub>2</sub> electrode, providing reliable support for rapid charge transfer and enhanced structural stability of WSe<sub>2</sub> electrode. However, these modification methods mainly belong to the regulation of morphology and micro‐structure, which cannot effectively accelerate the ion/electron transfer rate in the crystal of electrode materials, resulting in unsatisfactory long cycle life.</p><p>Recently, vacancy engineering has been considered as a promising strategy to further optimize the physical and chemical properties of nanomaterials and improve electrochemical properties.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202206340-bib-0023"><sup>23</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In particular, the vacancies created by anion doping or substitution can enhance intrinsic electronic conductivity, and offer sufficient active sites for Na<sup>+</sup> adsorption, resulting in rapid Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion kinetics and improved Na storage performance.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202206340-bib-0024"><sup>24</sup></link><sup>]</sup> For example, previous reports have successfully developed electrode materials with O vacancies and S vacancies.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202206340-bib-0025 #smll202206340-bib-0026"><sup>25,26</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Based on the above considerations and viewpoints, in‐depth and systematic research is urgently needed to enhance sodium storage performance by combining micromorphological structure regulation with vacancy engineering.</p><p>Herein, we successfully fabricated WSSe nanosheets supported on 3D cross‐networked porous carbon skeleton composite (WSSe@CPCS) by partially replacing Se atoms in WSe<sub>2</sub> nanosheets with S atoms by a facile alloying technique. The anion vacancies induced by S atom doping are more favorable for Na<sup>+</sup> adsorption, and greatly improve the electronic conductivity, as well as accelerate the Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion kinetics.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202206340-bib-0027"><sup>27</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Moreover, the interconnected porous structure of CPCS can alleviate the structural changes during repeated insertion/extraction of Na<sup>+</sup>. Therefore, the WSSe@CPCS anode in the SIB half‐cell exhibits preeminent reversible capacities, excellent cycle stability, and superior rate capability. Combined with a series of electrochemical kinetic analyses, ex situ X‐ray diffraction (XRD), Raman and transmission electron microscope (TEM) characterization, and density functional theory (DFT) results further suggest that the synergistic effect of anion vacancies and CPCS enhances transport kinetics and the mechanism of the electrochemical conversion reaction. Significantly, we prepared a SIB full cell coupled with Na<sub>3</sub>(VPO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>F<sub>3</sub> (NVPF) cathode and an SDIB full cell with EG cathode to assess the feasibility of WSSe@CPCS anode in advanced storage systems. As expected, the as‐built SIB and SDIB full cells both exhibit excellent cycle performance. This study will provide a promising avenue for designing advanced defect‐structured tungsten‐based TMD materials for SIBs, DIBs, and even a broad range of energy storage.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202206340-sec-0020"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p>The synthesis process of WSSe@CPCS is schematically illustrated in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll202206340-fig-0001"><b>1</b>a</link>. The 3D‐CPCS is prepared by using a NaHCO<sub>3</sub> template and methyl cellulose (Figures <link href="#smll202206340-supitem-0001">S1–S3</link>, Supporting Information). These CO<sub>3</sub><sup>2−</sup> groups on the 3D‐CPCS inner surface can act as the active sites to induce the nucleation and growth of WSe<sub>2</sub> nanosheets on the inner and outer surfaces of 3D‐CPCS (Figure <link href="#smll202206340-supitem-0001">S4</link>, Supporting Information), thus realizing the self‐growth and uniformity of WSe<sub>2</sub> sheets in the cross‐networked porous carbon structure.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202206340-bib-0028"><sup>28</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The as‐prepared 3D‐CPCS consists of many interconnected cavities/channel structures with pore diameters of 80–100 nm, which offer many micro‐reactors for growing WSe<sub>2</sub> (Figures <link href="#smll202206340-supitem-0001">S5</link> and <link href="#smll202206340-supitem-0001">S6</link>, Supporting Information). As expected, WSe<sub>2</sub> nanosheets are uniformly distributed on the surface or embedded in 3D‐CPCS for the WSe<sub>2</sub>@CPCS sample (Figure <link href="#smll202206340-supitem-0001">S7a–c</link>, Supporting Information). After vulcanization, the morphology of the WSSe@CPCS is basically unchanged, indicating that the doping of sulfur atoms does not affect the overall structure of the WSSe@CPCS (Figure <link href="#smll202206340-fig-0001">1b</link> and Figure <link href="#smll202206340-supitem-0001">S7d–f</link>, Supporting Information). For comparison, bare WSe<sub>2</sub> was also successfully prepared and shown in Figures <link href="#smll202206340-supitem-0001">S7g–i</link> and <link href="#smll202206340-supitem-0001">S8</link>, Supporting Information. It can be seen that bare WSe<sub>2</sub> is seriously agglomerated during the synthesis process, leading to poor Na<sup>+</sup> storage performance, which further illustrates the use of 3D‐CPCS to achieve uniform WSe<sub>2</sub> and WSSe dispersion. The close observations by TEM exhibit that the WSSe@CPCS nanosheets are distributed on the 3D‐CPCS to form a structure that is similar to nanoflowers (Figure <link href="#smll202206340-fig-0001">1c,d</link>). The high‐resolution TEM (HRTEM) image (Figure <link href="#smll202206340-fig-0001">1e</link>) is obviously shown that WSSe nanosheets appear few‐layered structure. The measured lattice fringe of the WSSe nanosheet is ≈0.72 nm, larger than the lattice fringe of the (002) plane of WSe<sub>2</sub> (0.65 nm) and WS<sub>2</sub> (0.62 nm). The result demonstrates that the doped‐S atoms can effectively enlarge inter‐layer spacing. Meanwhile, the selected area electron diffraction (SAED) image in Figure <link href="#smll202206340-fig-0001">1f</link> also signifies several diffraction rings, corresponding to the (100), (103), (105), and (110) crystal planes, respectively. It is worth noting that the disappearance of the diffraction ring of the (002) plane indicates that the (002) crystal plane of the original WSe<sub>2</sub> is changed after S‐doping.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202206340-bib-0029"><sup>29</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Figure <link href="#smll202206340-fig-0001">1g,h</link> is the lattice patterns of HRTEM images after the Fourier transform. In Figure <link href="#smll202206340-fig-0001">1g</link>, the red circle marks partial anion defects, and Figure <link href="#smll202206340-fig-0001">1h</link> is also obviously found in the curving lattice fringe. These results illustrate that the doped‐S atoms successfully changed the original crystal structure of WSe<sub>2</sub> and caused a lattice distortion.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202206340-bib-0030 #smll202206340-bib-0031"><sup>30,31</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The corresponding element mapping images show the uniform distribution of W, Se, S, and C elements, suggesting the successful formation of S‐doped WSe<sub>2</sub>@CPCS (Figure <link href="#smll202206340-fig-0001">1i</link>). The atom ratio of Se/S is around 1:1(Figure <link href="#smll202206340-supitem-0001">S9</link>, Supporting Information) according to the energy dispersive spectroscopy (EDS).</p><figure xml:id="smll202206340-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll202206340:smll202206340-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll202206340-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) Illustration of the formation process of WSSe@CPCS; b) SEM image of WSSe@CPCS; c,d) TEM images, e) HRTEM image, f) SAED pattern, g,h) the IFFT pattern of HRTEM, and i) STEM and elemental mapping images of WSSe@CPCS.</caption></figure><p>The crystal phase structure of the samples was examined by XRD powder diffraction (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll202206340-fig-0002"><b>2</b>a</link>). One can observe that the pure WSe<sub>2</sub> and WSe<sub>2</sub>@CPCS exhibit high crystallinity and their diffraction patterns correspond well to the hexagonal WSe<sub>2</sub> (JCPDS #38‐1388).<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202206340-bib-0032"><sup>32</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Interestingly, the crystallinity of the resulting WSSe@CPCS decreases after the doping of S atoms into WSe<sub>2</sub>, which can be caused by the increased local lattice disorder and distortion during the alloying reaction. Simultaneously, the angles of the main peaks slightly shift to the right because of the shrinkage of the unit cell caused by the substitution of smaller S atoms for Se atoms, which indicates that S and Se are mixed at the atomic level. The Raman spectroscopy tests of WSSe@CPCS, WSe<sub>2</sub>@CPCS, and bare WSe<sub>2</sub> were carried out to further gain the structural changes (Figure <link href="#smll202206340-fig-0002">2b</link>). For the three materials, two peaks at 250 and 805 cm<sup>−1</sup> are all attributed to the E<sub>2g</sub> and A<sub>1g</sub> modes of the WSe bond, respectively. Additionally, the new two peaks at 350 and 415 cm<sup>−1</sup> in the WSSe@CPCS sample are ascribed to E<sub>2g</sub> and A<sub>1g</sub> modes of the WS bond respectively, verifying the formation of a covalent bond between W and S atoms.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202206340-bib-0033 #smll202206340-bib-0034 #smll202206340-bib-0035"><sup>33–35</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Meanwhile, the D‐bond (1350 cm<sup>−1</sup>) and G‐bond (1590 cm<sup>−1</sup>) of the carbon materials have existed in both WSSe@CPCS and WSe<sub>2</sub>@CPCS samples. The intensity ratio of D‐bond and G‐bond (<i>I</i><sub>D</sub>/<i>I</i><sub>G</sub>) for WSSe@CPCS is 1.002, larger than the ratio of WSe<sub>2</sub>@CPCS (0.981). It is demonstrated that the WSSe@CPCS has more defects than WSe<sub>2</sub>@CPCS, which is more conducive to Na ion transport. Furthermore, the WSSe@CPCS exhibits a very strong electron paramagnetic resonance (EPR) signal closed to a g‐value of 2.003,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202206340-bib-0031"><sup>31</sup></link><sup>]</sup> greatly different from the WSe<sub>2</sub>@CPCS, which is apparently due to increased local lattice disorder and distortion during S doping process, resulting in a large number of anionic defects (Figure <link href="#smll202206340-fig-0002">2c</link>).</p><figure xml:id="smll202206340-fig-0002"><label>2</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll202206340:smll202206340-fig-0002" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll202206340-fig-0002-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) XRD patterns of WSSe@CPCS, WSe<sub>2</sub>@CPCS, and bare WSe<sub>2</sub>; b) Raman spectra of WSSe@CPCS, WSe<sub>2</sub>@CPCS, bare WSe<sub>2</sub>, and 3D‐CPCS; c) EPR results of WSSe@CPCS and WSe<sub>2</sub>@CPCS; d) TGA curves of WSSe@CPCS and WSe<sub>2</sub>@CPCS; e) XPS survey spectra of WSSe@CPCS and WSe<sub>2</sub>@CPCS; High‐resolution XPS spectra of WSSe@CPCS and WSe<sub>2</sub>@CPCS: f) W 4f, g) Se 3d, h) C 1s; High‐resolution XPS spectra of WSSe@CPCS: i) S 2p.</caption></figure><p>The weight contents of WSSe and WSe<sub>2</sub> in WSSe@CPCS, WSe<sub>2</sub>@CPCS, and bare WSe<sub>2</sub>, respectively, were analyzed by thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) (Figure <link href="#smll202206340-fig-0002">2d</link> and Figure <link href="#smll202206340-supitem-0001">S10</link>, Supporting Information). It is obvious that the WSSe was completely oxidized into WO<sub>3</sub>, SO<sub>2</sub>, and SeO<sub>2</sub>, and carbon was converted into CO<sub>2</sub> between 350 and 600 °C. The weight increase at 350 °C was attributed to the formation of SeO<sub>2</sub>. Based on the mass of the final content (WO<sub>3</sub>), the content of WSSe in the WSSe@CPCS is 86.7%. In consequence, the carbon content of WSSe@CPCS is 13.3%. Furthermore, the components and valence bond structure analysis of the samples were analyzed by X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS). This is also the reason why there is irreversible capacity in the first cycle and the battery performance is reduced.</p><figure xml:id="smll202206340-fig-0007"><label>7</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll202206340:smll202206340-fig-0007" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll202206340-fig-0007-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) Diagram illustration of SDIBs; b) Performance test of different voltage windows; c) charge–discharge curves of EG electrode, WSSe@CPCS electrode, and WSSe@CPCS//EG dual‐ion cell in the three cycles at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> ; d) CV curves of SDIBs at 0.4, 1, 2, 3, and 5 mV s<sup>−1</sup>; e) Cycling stability under specific current of 0.5 A g<sup>−1</sup> for WSSe@CPCS//EG dual‐ion cells with different anode/cathode mass ratio (1:3, 1:4, 1:5, and 1:7); f) Nyquist plots for SDIBs before and after three cycles (inset presents the initial section); g) rate capacity of SDIBs; h) charge–discharge curves of SDIBs at 0.5, 1, 2, and 4 A g<sup>−1</sup>; i) Long‐term cycling stability under specific current of 1 A g<sup>−1</sup> for WSSe@CPCS//EG dual‐ion cells.</caption></figure><p>To explore the effect of different ratios of anode and cathode materials on the cycling performance of WSSe@CPCS//EG SDIBs, we tested the different ratios of anode and cathode materials of 1:3, 1:4, 1:5, and 1:7, respectively, and the results are shown in Figure <link href="#smll202206340-fig-0007">7f</link>. When the mass ratio is 1:4, the reversible performance of the battery is the best, and the capacity of the battery has no significant attenuation after 110 cycles. After the charge–discharge cut‐off voltage was determined, the rate test and cycle test were carried out (Figure <link href="#smll202206340-fig-0007">7g,i</link>). When the current density is 0.5, 1, 2, and 4 A g<sup>−1</sup>, the reversible discharge specific capacities are 123, 108, 80, and 65 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively (the corresponding time‐voltage curves are displayed in Figure <link href="#smll202206340-supitem-0001">S30b</link>, Supporting Information). When the current density is restored to 0.5 A g<sup>−1</sup>, the discharge‐specific capacity can be recovered to 117 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, indicating that it has good rate performance. As shown in Figure <link href="#smll202206340-fig-0007">7i</link>, the reversible capacity of the dual‐ion battery is maintained at 78 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 700 cycles at 1 A g<sup>−1</sup>. In addition, we also calculated the specific capacity of WSSe@CPCS//EG SDIBs according to the total mass of the cathode and anode. The WSSe@CPCS//EG SDIBs cycled at 300 mA g<sup>−1</sup> could also exhibit a discharge capacity of 25.6 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, which is aligned with the state‐of‐the‐art performance of SDIBs (Figure <link href="#smll202206340-supitem-0001">S32</link>, Supporting Information).<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202206340-bib-0051 #smll202206340-bib-0052 #smll202206340-bib-0053"><sup>51–53</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The WSSe@CPCS//EG SDIB can continuously output electric energy and light up the heart‐shaped Logo for more than 2 min (Figure <link href="#smll202206340-supitem-0001">S33</link>, Supporting Information), which indicates that the SDIBs has certain practical application prospects. More importantly, the WSSe@CPCS//EG SDIB also has certain advantages in calculating the energy density and power density based on the overall mass of the anode and cathode (Figure <link href="#smll202206340-supitem-0001">S35</link>, Supporting Information).</p><p>The improved electrochemical performance of WSSe@CPCS anode for both SIBs and SDIBs can be ascribed to the following factors: 1) the CPCS support can not only enhance the electrode structural integrity but also facilitate charge transfer stability, ensuring superior cycling stability. 2) The anion vacancies induced by S atom doping are more favorable for Na<sup>+</sup> adsorption, and greatly improve the electronic conductivity, as well as accelerate the Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion kinetics. 3) The interconnected porous structure can provide sufficient space to alleviate the structural changes during discharging/charging process. However, it is unfortunate that low initial Coulombic efficiencies and imperfect cycle stability of the WSSe@CPCS electrode for SIBs and SDIBs are obtained. In the energy storage system, electrolytes and separators are also crucial factors affecting the electrochemical performance of electrode materials.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202206340-bib-0054 #smll202206340-bib-0055 #smll202206340-bib-0056"><sup>54–56</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Therefore, it is necessary to further optimize and develop the electrolyte type, electrolyte concentration, and separator modification, so as to further improve the electrochemical performance of future large‐scale energy storage applications.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202206340-sec-0030"><title type="main">Conclusion</title><p>In conclusion, we have proposed a facile approach for the fabrication of a nano‐architectured hybrid with a CPCS supporting WSSe nanosheets. Benefiting from the enhanced electronic conductivity, expanded inter‐layers space (0.72 nm), and interconnected porous structure, the WSSe@CPCS electrode delivers excellent specific capacity, superior rate performance, and long cycling life for Na<sup>+</sup> storage. A series of kinetic analyses, ex situ techniques, and DFT calculations were performed to gain insight into the fast intercalation and conversion mechanism of WSSe@CPCS and the underlying reasons for the fast transport kinetics. Importantly, the WSSe@CPCS anode can be directly applied as implemented in SIB and SDIB full cells, which indicates its favorable conditions for ultra‐stable cycles in the Na<sup>+</sup>‐based energy storage field. Furthermore, we also enabled the as‐constructed WSSe@CPCS//EG SDIBs to exhibit long‐term cycling stability by optimizing the voltage window and anode/cathode mass ratio, demonstrating its reliability and practicability. This work presents a promising approach to designing advanced defect‐structured tungsten‐based TMD materials, which can be applied and used in SIBs, SDIBs, or a broad range of energy storage.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202206340-sec-0040"><title type="main">Experimental Section</title><section xml:id="smll202206340-sec-0050" numbered="no"><section xml:id="smll202206340-sec-0060" numbered="no"><section xml:id="smll202206340-sec-0070" numbered="no"><title type="main">Synthesis of 3D Cross‐Networked Porous Carbon Skeleton</title><p>First, a mixture of 1 g of methylcellulose and 7 g of sodium bicarbonate were grounded for 30 min to obtain a homogenous precursor. Afterward, the mixture was first annealed at 200 °C for 2 h, and then heated to 800 °C for 6 h. After cooling to room temperature, the annealed product was added to a hydrochloric acid solution and stirred for 24 h to remove the NaHCO<sub>3</sub> template. Finally, the mixed solution was centrifuged and dried at 70 °C overnight to obtain a 3D‐CPCS.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202206340-sec-0080" numbered="no"><title type="main">Synthesis of WSe<sub>2</sub>@CPCS</title><p>WSe<sub>2</sub>@CPCS was synthesized through the hydrothermal method. Typically, as‐prepared 3D‐CPCS was dispersed into 10 mL of <i>N</i>,<i>N</i>‐dimethylformamide (DMF) for ultrasonic dissolution as mixture A. Then, 660 mg of Na<sub>2</sub>WO<sub>4</sub>·2H<sub>2</sub>O, 100 mg of NaBH<sub>4</sub>, and 237 mg Se powder were dispersed in a mixture of 20 mL DMF and 3 mL deionized water under stirring as solution B. The two solutions of A and B were mixed and stirred for 1.5 h and further transferred into a 50 mL autoclave and kept at 200 °C for 24 h. Finally, the reaction product WSe<sub>2</sub>@CPCS was collected by centrifugation, washed several times with ethanol and distilled water, and dried for 12 h at 80 °C. Subsequently, the as‐obtained materials were put in the porcelain boat and then annealed at 500 °C for 2 h under an Ar atmosphere. For comparison, bare WSe<sub>2</sub> was prepared in the same way as WSe<sub>2</sub>@CPCS without adding carbon‐based porous materials.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202206340-sec-0090" numbered="no"><title type="main">Synthesis of WSSe@CPCS</title><p>In a typical synthesis, the as‐prepared WSe<sub>2</sub>@CPCS and certain amounts of S powder (1:4 by mass) were put in the different sides of a porcelain boat and calcined at 700 °C for 2 h under Ar atmosphere to obtain WSSe@CPCS.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202206340-sec-0100" numbered="no"><title type="main">Material Characterizations</title><p>The morphologies and structures of the sample were characterized by a scanning electron microscope (SEM, Hitachi SU8010) and TEM (FEI Talos F200X, USA) with EDS. Powder XRD is given by the D8 ADVANCE instrument X‐ray powder diffractometer using Cu Kα at a sweep speed of 0.05°S<sup>−1</sup> in the 2θ range of 10 to 80. The Raman spectrum was measured using a dispersive Raman microscope (XPLORA PLUS) and the text wavelength was selected as 532 nm. XPS analysis was performed by using a PHI 5000 Versa Probe III XPS with an Al Kα excitation source. TGA of the sample was recorded with a thermogravimetric analyzer (600 SDT) at a room temperature of 800 °C. EPR (Bruker‐E500) spectra were collected to investigate the defect status. BET (ASAP 2020 PLUS HD88) surface area and pore size distributions were obtained by using N<sub>2</sub> adsorption‐desorption isotherms.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202206340-sec-0110" numbered="no"><title type="main">Electrochemical Measurements</title><p>The electrochemical performance measurement was performed using CR2025‐type coin cells assembled in a glove box filled with an argon atmosphere (H<sub>2</sub>O &lt; 0.1 ppm, O<sub>2</sub> &lt; 0.1 ppm). For anode working electrodes in the SIB half‐cell, the slurry was obtained by mixing and grinding 80 wt% synthesized materials, 10 wt% acetylene black, and 10 wt% polyvinylidene fluoride in <i>N</i>‐methylpyrrolidone solvent and then uniformly coated on a nickel foam disc (14 mm). The average mass of active material loading of the nickel foam disc was maintained at about 1.2 mg cm<sup>−2</sup>. For half SIBs, Na metal foil was applied as the counter electrode. 1 <sc>m</sc> NaClO<sub>4</sub> dissolved in a mixed solution of propylene carbonate (PC)/ethylene carbonate (EC) (PC:EC = 1:1) with 5 wt% fluoroethylene carbonate (FEC) and the mixed solution was used as SIB half‐cell electrolyte solution. For full‐cell SIBs, WSSe@CPCS was used as anode active material (presodiation treatment at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> for five cycles), and NVPF was utilized as the cathode material. For SDIBs, WSSe@CPCS and EG were used as anode active material electrodes and cathode active material electrodes, respectively (presodiation treatment at 0.5 A g<sup>−1</sup> for three cycles). The anode (WSSe@CPCS) and cathode (EG) mass ratio were about 1:3, 1:4, 1:5, and 1:7, respectively. For SDIBs, the electrolyte solution was 1 <sc>m</sc> NaPF<sub>6</sub> dissolves in a mixed solution of EC/ethyl methyl carbonate (EMC)/dimethyl carbonate (DMC) (EC:EMC:DMC = 1:1:1, v:v:v). The glass fibers were used as separators both in SIBs and SDIBs. In addition, the cyclic charge–discharge with different current densities and constant current charge–discharge experiments at room temperature were performed on a CT2001A LAND test system. CS310H electrochemical workstations were used to record CV curves. The scanning rate was between 0.2 and 2 mV s<sup>−1</sup> and the voltage test window of the SIB half‐cell was 0–3 V. For SIB and SDIB full cell, the voltage test window of CV curves were 1.5–4.2 V and 1–4.2 V, respectively. EIS measurements were carried out on the same instrument with the same sweep speed as the CV test, and the test frequency ranged from 0.01 Hz to 100 kHz. Galvanostatic charge/discharge tests were finished on the LAND‐CT2001A test system with different current densities.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202206340-sec-0120" numbered="no"><title type="main">DFT Computational Detail</title><p>DFT was calculated by using projector enhanced wave method. The generalized gradient approximation of the functional was used as the commutative relation function. The cut‐off energy was set at 500 eV until the convergence criteria for energy and force were 1 × 10<sup>−5</sup> eV and 0.02 eV Å<sup>−1</sup>. A 15 Å vacuum layer was built to eliminate the interaction of the periodic structure of the surface model. The adsorption energy (Δ<i>E</i><sub>ads</sub>) of Na adsorption on the surface is defined as Δ<i>E</i><sub>ads</sub> = <i>E</i>(*Na)−<i>E</i>(*)−<i>E</i>(Na), where <i>E</i>(*Na) and <i>E</i>(*) represent the total energy of surface systems with and without Na atom, respectively, and <i>E</i>(Na) represents the energy of an isolated Na atom.</p></section></section></section></section><section
