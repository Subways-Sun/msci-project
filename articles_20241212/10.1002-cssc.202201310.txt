However, designing carbon materials with a high nitrogen content and uniform distribution using conventional doping methods remains a challenge. In this study, a hollow carbon sphere with an ultrahigh nitrogen content of 9.58 wt % was successfully fabricated by rationally designing Schiff base chemistry (PTA‐NHCS‐700). Stable hierarchical pore structures, moderate defects, and large specific surface areas were formed during the carbonization process. Excellent electrochemical performance was observed in LIBs (204.2 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 7000 cycles at 5 A g<sup>−1</sup>) and SIBs (154.2 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 10000 cycles at 5 A g<sup>−1</sup>). This study not only promotes the development of efficient carbon anode materials for LIBs and SIBs, but also provides a novel idea for the doping of heteroatoms with special chemical structures.</p></abstract><abstract type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p><b>Needs more N</b>: Nitrogen‐doped hollow carbon spheres (PTA‐NHCS‐700) are synthesized by a rational Schiff base chemistry. The PTA‐NHCS‐700 anode displays excellent storage capability for both Li<sup>+</sup> and Na<sup>+</sup>. The strategy of nitrogen doping by Schiff base chemistry not only saves sample preparation time but, more importantly, solves the shortcomings of traditional doping strategies.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18645631:media:cssc202201310:cssc202201310-toc-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/cssc202201310-toc-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="cssc202201310-sec-0001" numbered="no"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>With the rapid development of power equipment and the large‐scale application of renewable energy, lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs), as one of the most important energy storage devices, have been widely used in mobile electronic devices, electric vehicles, power grids, and other fields.<link href="#cssc202201310-bib-0001"><sup>[1]</sup></link> Further development and application of LIBs will undoubtedly be hindered due to the shortage and uneven geographical distribution of lithium resources.<link href="#cssc202201310-bib-0006"><sup>[2]</sup></link> In contrast, sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) have attracted increasing attention because of their rich sodium resources and low costs. However, the sodium ion (Na<sup>+</sup>) radius is large (0.102 nm) and the direct utilization of graphite anodes in commercial LIBs suffers from severe volume changes and kinetic hysteresis during cycling, resulting in a significant reduction in the specific capacity.<link href="#cssc202201310-bib-0007"><sup>[3]</sup></link> It is therefore of great significance to explore stable mechanical properties and low‐cost anode materials to improve the electrochemical performance of LIBs and SIBs.<link href="#cssc202201310-bib-0011"><sup>[4]</sup></link></p><p>Carbon‐based anode materials are most likely to be commercialized owing to its advantages of being low cost, widely available, and having good cycling stability. However, carbon‐based anodes also face several challenges such as good rate performance, high reversible capacity, and so on.<link href="#cssc202201310-bib-0012"><sup>[5]</sup></link> Several strategies have been adopted to solve these problems. One method is to prepare materials with special morphologies to shorten the diffusion distance of lithium and sodium ions (Li<sup>+</sup> and Na<sup>+</sup>), primarily porous carbon,<link href="#cssc202201310-bib-0015"><sup>[6]</sup></link> hollow carbon spheres,<link href="#cssc202201310-bib-0016"><sup>[7]</sup></link> and carbon nanotubes.<link href="#cssc202201310-bib-0017"><sup>[8]</sup></link> Among them, hollow carbon spheres have great potential as anode materials for high‐rate LIBs and SIBs owing to their unique properties, such as high electrical conductivity and chemical inertness.<link href="#cssc202201310-bib-0018"><sup>[9]</sup></link> Another effective strategy involves heteroatomic doping. Nitrogen is a heteroatom commonly used to improve the electrochemical properties of carbon materials. Doping with nitrogen atoms is fairly straightforward, as heteroatoms of the same size may be incorporated into the framework of carbon atoms or attached as functional groups on surfaces and defect active sites. When nitrogen atoms are introduced into carbon materials, they induce changes in the distribution of electron density and energy band positions. The introduction of polarity enhances the adsorption properties and wetting ability of carbon materials, thereby enhancing the storage of Li<sup>+</sup> and Na<sup>+</sup>.<link href="#cssc202201310-bib-0019"><sup>[10]</sup></link> Huo et al. prepared nitrogen‐doped hollow carbon spheres using silica as a template and coated them with dopamine hydrochloride. The capacity remains at 485 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 1000 cycles at 0.5 A g<sup>−1</sup> in LIBs.<link href="#cssc202201310-bib-0022"><sup>[11]</sup></link> Ni et al. prepared nitrogen‐doped hollow carbon spheres using CaCO<sub>3</sub> nanoparticles as the template and coated them with dopamine hydrochloride. In SIBs, the capacity was 169 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.5 A g<sup>−1</sup> current density.<link href="#cssc202201310-bib-0023"><sup>[12]</sup></link> The prepared nitrogen‐doped hollow carbon spheres exhibited high reversible capacity and good cycling stability. However, most nitrogen doping in the process of preparation of hollow carbon spheres is very dependent on the silica template, the silicon template to be removed by HF, NaOH, and other dangerous chemicals, and inevitably on the environment caused by pollution.<link href="#cssc202201310-bib-0024"><sup>[13]</sup></link> In doping strategy, there are usually two ways to obtain nitrogen‐containing carbon materials. One is a post‐treatment method using ammonia, urea, and/or nitrogen plasma. However, this always involves a relatively inhomogeneous nitrogen distribution considering N‐functional groups are preferentially introduced at the edge of carbon materials. An alternative is the in‐situ synthesis approach using diverse nitrogen‐containing precursors, including ZIF‐8 and dopamine hydrochloride, which can introduce nitrogen atoms with a uniform distribution. Unfortunately, this method frequently results in nitrogen content lower than 5 % in the products, which limits deeper exploration of the N‐doping effect on the electrochemical properties.<link href="#cssc202201310-bib-0025"><sup>[14]</sup></link> Designing hollow carbon sphere with high nitrogen content, uniform distribution, and no pollution, therefore, remains a challenge.</p><p>In this study, melamine formaldehyde (MF) nanospheres and poly tannic acid (PTA) were selected as templates and carbon sources. PTA is inexpensive and can be coated on almost any material surface in an alkaline buffer. More importantly, its structure contains a large number of phenolic hydroxy groups, which provide more active sites for redox reactions. The nitrogen‐containing polymer MF sphere spontaneously decomposed during the carbonization process, and its nitrogen atoms were introduced into the carbon skeleton along with nitrogen‐rich gas during the decomposition process to activate the carbon shell. Conventional ammonia<link href="#cssc202201310-bib-0026"><sup>[15]</sup></link> or urea<link href="#cssc202201310-bib-0027"><sup>[16]</sup></link> was not used for post‐processing in the doping strategy. Instead, the special structures of PTA (carbonyl group) and diethylenetriamine (DETA, amine group) were rationally used for the classical Schiff base reaction to carbon materials with uniform distribution and high nitrogen content (PTA‐NHCS‐700). The classical and ingenious doping method exposes the active nitrogen‐doping sites of the electrode on the electrode surface, resulting in high activity. PTA‐NHCS‐700 as LIBs anode, at a current density of 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>, the reversible capacity is up to 635.7 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, and even at a high current density of 5 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 7000 cycles, the capacity is still 204.2 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>. For SIBs, at the current density of 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>, the reversible capacity is 351.9 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 100 cycles. When the current density is increased to 5 A g<sup>−1</sup>, the reversible capacity is still 154.2 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 10000 cycles. This anode material has broad prospects for applications in LIBs and SIBs.</p></section><section xml:id="cssc202201310-sec-0002" numbered="no"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p>Figure <link href="#cssc202201310-fig-0001">1</link> presents the preparation flow chart of PTA‐NHCS‐<i>T</i> (<i>T</i> indicates the carbonization temperature). The reaction of melamine with formaldehyde first leads to hydroxylation, and the hydrogen atoms in the NH<sub>2</sub> group of melamine are replaced by methyl groups (−CH<sub>2</sub>OH).<link href="#cssc202201310-bib-0028"><sup>[17]</sup></link> The melamine‐formaldehyde oligomeric derivatives are then produced by the formation of two different types of bridges between triaging rings, namely, an amethylene ether bridge (M−NHCH<sub>2</sub>OH+HOCH<sub>2</sub>HN−M⇄M−NHCH<sub>2</sub>OCH<sub>2</sub>NH−M+H<sub>2</sub>O)<link href="#cssc202201310-bib-0029"><sup>[18]</sup></link> and a methylene bridge (M−NHCH<sub>2</sub>OH+H<sub>2</sub>N−M⇄M−NHCH<sub>2</sub>NH−M+H<sub>2</sub>O),<link href="#cssc202201310-bib-0030"><sup>[19]</sup></link> which forms a sequential cross‐linking network. Formic acid acts as a catalyst to accelerate the reaction. The TA/DETA binary system was co‐deposited in an alkaline environment. During the deposition process, the phenolic hydroxyl group in TA is first oxidatively polymerized to form a highly active quinone (PTA),<link href="#cssc202201310-bib-0031"><sup>[20]</sup></link> and the amine group in DETA and the active carbonyl group in PTA are condensed (Schiff base reaction), resulting in an ingenious hollow carbon sphere doped with nitrogen atoms. In the carbonization process, MF spheres gradually decompose, releasing CO<sub>2</sub> and nitrogen‐rich alkaline gas, part of which nitrogen atoms can directly enter the carbon framework through nitrogen‐rich alkaline gases,<link href="#cssc202201310-bib-0028"><sup>[17]</sup></link> and CO<sub>2</sub> can react with graphite microcrystals, thereby resulting in pores in the carbon shell.<link href="#cssc202201310-bib-0024"><sup>[13]</sup></link> Fourier‐transform infrared (FTIR) spectroscopy was used to characterize the chemical structure of the PTA‐NS (Figure S1). Peaks at approximately 1547, 1160, 1000, and 811 cm<sup>−1</sup> are attributed to −NH<sub>2</sub>, C−N, C−O−C, and C−N−C bonds, respectively. The presence of these absorption peaks can prove the formation of MF resin.<link href="#cssc202201310-bib-0032"><sup>[21]</sup></link> PTA shows characteristic peaks at 3338 and 1200 cm<sup>−1</sup>, which are related to the stretching vibration of Ar−OH.<link href="#cssc202201310-bib-0033"><sup>[22]</sup></link> The peak near 1650 cm<sup>−1</sup> corresponds to imine groups (−C=N−), confirming the occurrence of the Schiff base reaction during the co‐deposition process of PTA and DETA onto the MF sphere surface.<link href="#cssc202201310-bib-0034"><sup>[23]</sup></link></p><figure xml:id="cssc202201310-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18645631:media:cssc202201310:cssc202201310-fig-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/cssc202201310-fig-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>PTA‐NHCS synthesis process diagram.</p></caption></figure><p>Figure <link href="#cssc202201310-fig-0002">2</link> presents scanning electron microscopy (SEM) and transmission electron microscopy (TEM) images of the template MF sphere and the carbon material PTA‐NHCS‐<i>T</i>. Figure <link href="#cssc202201310-fig-0002">2</link>a shows the template MF sphere prepared with F127 as the surfactant and formic acid as the catalyst, with a diameter of approximately 500 nm. The surface of the sphere is smooth, and the entire sphere is scattered. Figure <link href="#cssc202201310-fig-0002">2</link>b–d presents PTA‐NHCS‐<i>T</i> after carbonization (<i>T</i>=600, 700, and 800 °C). It can be found that the shape of the sphere remains the best at 700 °C, and the surface of the sphere is rough. Previous studies have found that spheres with rough surfaces exhibit better performance than spheres with smooth surfaces (particle size in Figure S2).<link href="#cssc202201310-bib-0037"><sup>[24]</sup></link> Similarly, coating thickness is an extremely important parameter. Figure S3a,b presents the SEM images of PTA‐NHCS‐700 stirred for 2 and 6 h, respectively. With the increase in stirring time, the coating became sufficiently thick to increase the ion diffusion distance. As the stirring time decreased, most of the spherical morphology collapsed, primarily because the coating became thin, and the degradation and carbonization of the frame further reduced the coating thickness, resulting in the carbon layer being unable to support the hollow structure.<link href="#cssc202201310-bib-0038"><sup>[25]</sup></link> A moderate thickness of the carbon layer thickness is therefore crucial for preserving the morphology. The TEM images of PTA‐NHCS‐700 are presented in Figure <link href="#cssc202201310-fig-0002">2</link>e,f. The thickness of the carbon layer is between 45–50 nm. Some carbon spheres were connected to each other and could be used as electron transfer channels. High‐resolution (HR)TEM (Figure <link href="#cssc202201310-fig-0002">2</link>g–i) demonstrates that the carbon wall of PTA‐NHCS had no obvious long‐range ordered structure, indicating that the material was amorphous.<link href="#cssc202201310-bib-0023"><sup>[12]</sup></link> With an increase in temperature, locally ordered structures appeared in some locations, especially in PTA‐NHCS‐800. It may be inferred that the carbonization temperature has a significant influence on the microstructure of materials.<link href="#cssc202201310-bib-0039"><sup>[26]</sup></link></p><figure xml:id="cssc202201310-fig-0002"><label>2</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18645631:media:cssc202201310:cssc202201310-fig-0002"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/cssc202201310-fig-0002-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>SEM images of (a) MF sphere template and (b–d) PTA‐NHCS‐<i>T</i> (<i>T</i>=600, 700, 800 °C). (e,f) TEM images of PTA‐NHCS‐700. (g–i) HRTEM images of PTA‐NHCS‐<i>T</i> (<i>T</i>=600, 700, 800 °C).</p></caption></figure><p>Figure <link href="#cssc202201310-fig-0003">3</link>a presents the X‐ray diffraction (XRD) patterns of the PTA‐NHCS‐600, PTA‐NHCS‐700, and PTA‐NHCS‐800. Two wide peaks appeared at 23 and 43°, corresponding to the plane expansion of the (002) and (100) crystal planes, and the deformation and dislocation of the internal lattice of carbon.<link href="#cssc202201310-bib-0040"><sup>[27]</sup></link> It can be seen that the peak value of (002) moves to the right with an increase in carbonization temperature, implying an increase in the graphitization content, in agreement with the results observed by HRTEM. The results demonstrated that the spacing of the crystal plane gradually decreases.<link href="#cssc202201310-bib-0041"><sup>[28]</sup></link> As presented in Figure <link href="#cssc202201310-fig-0003">3</link>b, the structure of PTA‐NHCS was studied using Raman spectroscopy. There were two peaks at approximately 1340 and 1580 cm<sup>−1</sup>, corresponding to disordered carbon (D band) and graphitic carbon (G band), respectively. Considering the average defect size <i>L</i><sub>a</sub> of PTA‐NHCS‐T is less than 3 nm, the Raman spectrum of PTA‐NHCS‐T is suitable for the three‐stage Raman model proposed by Ferrari and co‐workers.<link href="#cssc202201310-bib-0042"><sup>[29]</sup></link> This feature corresponds to the disordered carbon in the second stage instead of ordered graphite carbon in the first stage. With an increase in temperature, the peak strength ratio of <i>I</i><sub>D</sub>/<i>I</i><sub>G</sub> gradually increased, indicating that the defect concentration of the bent graphene layer of disordered carbon decreased and the degree of crystallinity or order increased.<link href="#cssc202201310-bib-0045"><sup>[30]</sup></link> These results are consistent with those obtained using HRTEM. PTA‐NHCS‐800 has more local short‐range graphite nanodomains than PTA‐NHCS‐600 and PTA‐NHCS‐700 but fewer defects. PTA‐NHCS‐600 has fewer local short‐range graphite nanodomains but more defects. The defects in PTA‐NHCS‐700 and short‐range graphite nanodomains were moderate. In the Raman model studies, if the disordered carbon <i>L</i><sub>a</sub> in stage 2 is very small (&lt;3 nm), the strength of the G‐peak may be considered constant considering it is only associated with sp<sup>2</sup> bond stretching, which is very rare in disordered carbon. The strength of the D band is proportional to the number of benzene stretches in the disordered carbon. Therefore, in this stage, the increase in the ratio of <i>I</i><sub>D</sub>/<i>I</i><sub>G</sub> is an increase in the benzene ring, which is essentially different from the ordered graphite carbon in the first stage. The formula for determining the average defect size (<i>L</i><sub>a</sub>) is shown in Equation <link href="#cssc202201310-disp-0001">1</link>:<link href="#cssc202201310-bib-0046"><sup>[31]</sup></link><displayedItem xml:id="cssc202201310-disp-0001" numbered="yes" type="mathematics"><label>1</label><mediaResource href="graphic/cssc202201310-math-0001.png" alt="urn:x-wiley:18645631:media:cssc202201310:cssc202201310-math-0001"/></displayedItem></p><figure xml:id="cssc202201310-fig-0003"><label>3</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18645631:media:cssc202201310:cssc202201310-fig-0003"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/cssc202201310-fig-0003-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Structural characteristics analysis of PTA‐NHCS‐T. (d–f) XPS spectra of PTA‐NHCS‐700: (d) C 1s, (e) N 1s, and (f) O 1s.</p></caption></figure><p>where <i>I</i><sub>D</sub> and <i>I</i><sub>G</sub> are the peak intensities of peak D and peak G, respectively, and <i>E</i><sub>L</sub> is the laser energy (when the wavelength is 532 nm, which is 2.33 eV).</p><p>Figures <link href="#cssc202201310-fig-0003">3</link>c and S4 present the N<sub>2</sub> adsorption/desorption isotherms and pore size distribution of PTA‐NHCS‐<i>T</i>. The specific surface areas of PTA‐NHCS‐600, PTA‐NHCS‐700, and PTA‐NHCS‐800 are 314.7, 438.0, and 211.3 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. It may be observed that PTA‐NHCS‐700 has the highest specific surface area, but when the carbonization temperature rose to 800 °C, the specific surface area decreased. This is probably because the morphology of hollow carbon spheres cannot be maintained at the carbonization temperature of 800 °C, leading to the destruction of some of the material's pores. Typical type‐IV N<sub>2</sub> adsorption/desorption hysteresis loops were be observed for PTA‐NHCS‐700.<link href="#cssc202201310-bib-0047"><sup>[32]</sup></link> Combined with the pore size distribution diagram, it may be confirmed that PTA‐NHCS‐700 has a hierarchical pore structure dominated by mesopores. The physical parameters of PTA‐NHCS‐T are listed in Table S1. To show the impact of the hierarchical pore structures more intuitively, we established a schematic diagram (Figure S5) to analyze the reasons for the excellent performance. The abundant hierarchical pore structures have two main advantages. (1) Compared with non‐pore or single‐pore structure carbons, the abundant hierarchical pore structures promote Li<sup>+</sup> and Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion and transport, which not only can create more active sites to storage alkali metal ions but can also offer a sturdy framework even after thousands of cycles. (2) The structure ensures large electrode/electrolyte contact area for electrochemical reactions. The micropores increase the contact area between the plate and electrolyte. The mesopores can boost the transport of electrolyte.<link href="#cssc202201310-bib-0048"><sup>[33]</sup></link></p><p>X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) was used to analyze the carbon material to determine its elemental composition and bonding state. The full XPS spectrum in Figure S6 clearly shows that the three samples contain C 1s, N 1s and O 1s peaks. The content of each element is listed in Table S2. Depending on the binding energy of the different bonds, the peaks of C 1s, O 1s, and N 1s may be deconvoluted into different peaks. Figures <link href="#cssc202201310-fig-0003">3</link>d–f and S7a–f are described in detail. Figure <link href="#cssc202201310-fig-0003">3</link>d decouples the PTA‐NHCS‐700 C 1s spectra into four peaks located at 284.6, 285.1, 286.4, and 289.2 eV, respectively. Each binding energy represents C=C, C−C, C−N, and C−O.<link href="#cssc202201310-bib-0049"><sup>[34]</sup></link> The C=C peak at 284.6 eV belongs to graphitic carbon, and its content is presented in Table S3. The graphitization content increases with an increase in carbonization temperature, which is consistent with the TEM and Raman spectroscopy observations. As presented in Figure <link href="#cssc202201310-fig-0003">3</link>e, the N 1s spectrum can be decoupled into peaks at 398.5, 400.9, and 402.9 eV.<link href="#cssc202201310-bib-0015"><sup>[6]</sup></link> These three peaks correspond to pyridinic nitrogen (N‐6), pyrrolic nitrogen (N‐5), and graphite nitrogen (N−Q).<link href="#cssc202201310-bib-0052"><sup>[35]</sup></link> In general, pyridinic nitrogen and pyrrolic nitrogen play an important role in the storage of Li<sup>+</sup>/Na<sup>+</sup> because they induce defects and produce more active sites, whereas graphite nitrogen improves the conductivity of the carbon skeleton.<link href="#cssc202201310-bib-0052"><sup>[35]</sup></link> The contents of PTA‐NHCS‐700 for pyridinic nitrogen, pyrrolic nitrogen, and graphite nitrogen are 35.9, 46.8, and 17.3 % (Table S4). It can be seen from the table that the content of PTA‐NHCS‐700 pyrrolic nitrogen is much higher than that at the other two temperatures, which further explains why the performance of PTA‐NHCS‐700 is better than that at the other two temperatures in subsequent electrochemical tests. Figure <link href="#cssc202201310-fig-0003">3</link>f presents the spectrum of O 1s, which can be divided into three peaks, namely C=O (531.1 eV), C−OH (532.5 eV), and COOH (533.8 eV).<link href="#cssc202201310-bib-0051"><sup>[34b]</sup></link> PTA is a carbon source with high oxygen content. However, to date, most published papers ignore the role of oxygen in carbon anodes, even though oxygen‐containing groups often exist in the carbon surface. Hydroxy groups (C−OH) can improve the wettability of carbon anode toward electrolyte. Carboxyl groups (COOH) can enhance the Li<sup>+</sup> and Na<sup>+</sup> intercalation by expanding <i>d</i>‐spacing of carbon material edge. Carbonyl groups (C=O) can react with Li<sup>+</sup> and Na<sup>+</sup> through a surface‐redox reaction, which are beneficial contribute to the enhanced Li<sup>+</sup> and Na<sup>+</sup> storage capacity in carbon anode.<link href="#cssc202201310-bib-0053"><sup>[36]</sup></link> In addition, recent research revealed that carbon materials in which the oxygen element coexists with other heteroatoms generally show superior structural/interfacial stability for enhanced long‐term cycling stability.<link href="#cssc202201310-bib-0056"><sup>[37]</sup></link> It is because of the synergistic effect of these oxygen‐containing groups that the electrochemical performance of the PTA‐NHCS‐700 anode is further improved (Table S5 describes the specific content). XPS analysis demonstrated that the formation of important functional groups in PTA‐NHCS‐700 contributed significantly to the storage of Li<sup>+</sup> and Na<sup>+</sup> and the improved electrochemical performance.</p><p>The Li<sup>+</sup> storage capacity of the PTA‐NHCS‐<i>T</i> electrode was studied in a half‐battery using lithium metal as the counter electrode. As presented in Figure <link href="#cssc202201310-fig-0004">4</link>a, the cyclic voltammetry (CV) of the PTA‐NHCS‐700 half‐battery is tested in the voltage range of 0.01–3.0 V at a sweep speed of 0.2 mV s<sup>−1</sup>. At 0.75 V, a wider cathode peak appears in the first cycle and disappears in the following two cycles. This may be due to the electrolyte decomposition and solid electrolyte interphase (SEI) film formation on the surface of the PTA‐NHCS‐700 electrode.<link href="#cssc202201310-bib-0057"><sup>[38]</sup></link> In addition, the steep cathode peak in the range of 0.25 to 0.01 V is related to the interaction between Li<sup>+</sup> and nitrogen atoms and Li<sup>+</sup> insertion, and the wide peak at approximately 1.2 V in the subsequent anodic peak scan corresponds to Li<sup>+</sup> de‐insertion.<link href="#cssc202201310-bib-0057"><sup>[38]</sup></link> Except for the first cycle, the CV curves of the PTA‐NHCS‐700 anode overlap, indicating that the PTA‐NHCS‐700 anode demonstrates excellent Li<sup>+</sup> insertion/extraction stability after the first cycle. Figures <link href="#cssc202201310-fig-0004">4</link>b and S8 show the constant‐current charge–discharge curves of the three samples. The initial discharge/charge capacities of PTA‐NHCS‐600, PTA‐NHCS‐700, and PTA‐NHCS‐800 were 1246.2/646.3, 1474.1/875.1, and 1214.7 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>/685.9 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. The initial coulombic efficiencies were 51.9, 59.4, and 56.5 %. The lower coulombic efficiencies were attributed to the formation of SEI layer and irreversible side reactions.<link href="#cssc202201310-bib-0058"><sup>[39]</sup></link> It can be seen that the charge–discharge curves of the three samples have no obvious platform area. Based on the adsorption–intercalation mechanism, the voltage in the slope region primarily corresponds to the adsorption of Li<sup>+</sup> on the surface active sites, and the platform region corresponds to interlayer carbon insertion. This indicates that the sample capacity was composed of Li<sup>+</sup> storage mechanisms controlled by surface capacitance behavior. Experiments have demonstrated that this capacitance‐based control mechanism is more suitable for Li<sup>+</sup> storage.<link href="#cssc202201310-bib-0051"><sup>[34b]</sup></link> Figure <link href="#cssc202201310-fig-0004">4</link>c shows the charge–discharge cycle performance of PTA‐NHCS‐<i>T</i> at a low current density. The retention capacities of PTA‐NHCS‐600, PTA‐NHCS‐700, and PTA‐NHCS‐800 were 518.5, 635.7, and 559.8 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively, after 150 cycles at a current density of 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>. The high capacity of PTA‐NHCS‐700 is attributed to the ingenious Schiff base reaction and the decomposition of MF spheres. The doping of heteroatoms (N) and the hollow carbon spheres provide abundant defects and hierarchical pore structures, shorten the diffusion distance of Li<sup>+</sup>, and enhance the storage of Li<sup>+</sup>. At the carbonization temperature of 700 °C, the performance comparison of stirring times of 2 and 6 h at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> is presented in Figure S9. It is not difficult to understand that when the stirring time was 2 h, the hollow carbon spheres collapsed during the carbonization process owing to the thin carbon shell, destroying the original defects and pore structure, thereby resulting in a large reduction in capacity. However, when the stirring time was 6 h, a large amount of PTA accumulated on the surface of the MF spheres during the stirring process and formed thicker carbon shells after carbonization, which increased the diffusion path of Li<sup>+</sup> and led to the degradation of the electrochemical performance. The rate of performance of PTA‐NHCS‐700 is presented in Figure <link href="#cssc202201310-fig-0004">4</link>d. At 0.1, 0.2, 0.5, 1, and 2 A g<sup>−1</sup>, the anodes of PTA‐NHCS‐700 were 719.6, 713.8, 540.7, 485.7, and 422.7 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. When the current density was restored to 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>, the reversible specific capacity was almost restored. The results showed that PTA‐NHCS‐700 demonstrated good reversibility for Li<sup>+</sup> storage. The excellent Li<sup>+</sup> storage performance of PTA‐NHCS‐700 was further reflected by its ultra‐long cycle stability under a high current density. Figures S10 and 4e present the cycle performance of PTA‐NHCS‐700 at 1 and 5 A g<sup>−1</sup>. The capacity was determined to be 451.6 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 3000 cycles at 1 A g<sup>−1</sup>. A reversible capacity of 204.2 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> can be maintained even after 7000 cycles at 5 A g<sup>−1</sup>. Compared with the mixing times of 2 and 6 h, the long cycle of 4 h (Figure S11) also demonstrated better performance advantages. Cycling performance of hollow carbon sphere without nitrogen‐doping (PTA‐HCS‐700) at 0.1 and 1 A g<sup>−1</sup> are presented in Figure S12. Under the same conditions, the cycle performance of the PTA‐NHCS‐700 electrode was higher than that of the PTA‐HCS‐700 electrode, indicating that the N element introduced into the material effectively improved the electrochemical performance of the material. Overall, the excellent electrochemical performance exhibited during the test stems from the combined effects of the unique structure of PTA‐NHCS‐700. The main features include moderate carbon layer thickness, in‐situ nitrogen doping by the Schiff base reaction, hierarchical pore structures, and moderate defects. By comparing different stirring times (Figure S14), it can also be seen that if the stirring time is too short, the morphology after carbonization cannot be maintained stably, while if the stirring time is too long, the thickness of the carbon layer is thicker. Therefore, either too short or too long stirring times will affect the electrochemical performance of the battery. This further indicates that good morphology and appropriate thickness of the carbon layer are very important for the preparation of nitrogen‐doped hollow carbon spheres. The Na<sup>+</sup> storage capacity of PTA‐NHCS‐700 was evaluated by magnification. As presented in Figure <link href="#cssc202201310-fig-0006">6</link>d, when the current density was 0.1, 0.2, 0.5, and 1 A g<sup>−1</sup>, the specific discharge capacity of PTA‐NHCS‐700 was 379.4, 302.7, 225.1, and 193.6 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. After 3000 cycles at the current density of 1 A g<sup>−1</sup>, the capacity remained at 209.7 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, and even at the current density of 5 A g<sup>−1</sup>, the capacity still remained at 154.2 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 10000 cycles. In addition, the good morphology of the hollow carbon spheres and the medium thickness of the carbon layer can buffer the expansion volume, which is key to the high reversible capacity and good cycle stability in the long cycle of the PTA‐NHCS‐700 anode. We believe that PTA‐NHCS‐700 is a promising anode for LIBs and SIBs. The strategy of nitrogen doping by Schiff base chemistry not only saves sample preparation time but, more importantly, solves the shortcomings of traditional doping strategies. The PTA‐NHCS‐700 anode displayed excellent storage capability for both Li<sup>+</sup> and Na<sup>+</sup>, which is attributed to the hollow structure design of PTA‐NHCS‐700 doped with nitrogen atoms, moderate defects, and hierarchical pore structure. The hollow structure with hierarchical pores can provide a fast transport channel for Li<sup>+</sup>, Na<sup>+</sup>, and electrons, and alleviate volume expansion during circulation. The doping of N atoms promoted the electron transfer kinetics and ion adsorption capacity of the PTA‐NHCS‐700 anode, thus improving its electrochemical performance. This study not only provides a strategy with commercial potential for the design of efficient and fast‐storage carbon hollow structures, but more importantly, the idea of heteroatomic doping through Schiff base chemistry may be used as a reference for several materials.</p></section><section xml:id="cssc202201310-sec-0004" numbered="no"><title type="main">Experimental Section</title><section xml:id="cssc202201310-sec-0005" numbered="no"><title type="main">Materials and reagents</title><p>Tannic acid (Chengdu Kelong Chemical Co., Ltd., China, 98 %), formaldehyde in water (Chengdu Kelong Chemical Co., Ltd., China, 37 %), melamine (Chengdu Kelong Chemical Co., Ltd., China, ≥98.5 %), formic acid (Chengdu Kelong Chemical Co., Ltd., China, ≥88.0 %), diethylenetriamine (Chengdu Kelong Chemical Co., Ltd., China, ≥99.0 %), triblock copolymer Pluronic F127 (Chengdu Kelong Chemical Co., Ltd., China). All the chemicals were used without further purification.</p></section><section xml:id="cssc202201310-sec-0006" numbered="no"><title type="main">Fabrication of the MF sphere template method</title><p>0.3 g F127 was added to 200 mL deionized water and stirred for 30 min. Subsequently, 7.5 mL formaldehyde and 2.0 g melamine were added to the mixed solution at 80 °C and stirred until the solution was clarified, followed by the addition of 0.12 mL formic acid, which was stirred for 90 min at 80 °C. The products were collected by centrifugation, washed several times with deionized water and ethanol, and freeze‐dried for 24 h.</p></section><section xml:id="cssc202201310-sec-0007" numbered="no"><title type="main">Fabrication of the PTA‐NHCS method</title><p>A uniformly dispersed white suspension was obtained by ultrasonically dispersing 0.3 g MF sphere into 300 mL Tris‐HCl buffer (pH=8) and sonicating for 15 min. Then 0.6 g of tannic acid and 3.0 g of diethylenetriamine (mass ratio 1 : 5) were added to the white suspension and stirred continuously at room temperature for 4 h. The products were collected via centrifugation and frozen overnight. After carbonization at 600, 700, and 800 °C for 2 h (2 °C min<sup>−1</sup>), nitrogen‐doped hollow carbon spheres were finally obtained, denoted as PTA‐NHCS‐<i>T</i>, where <i>T</i> is the carbonization temperature (600, 700, and 800 °C). Using the same stirring time, the hollow carbon spheres (PTA‐HCS‐700) were obtained through calcinating the PTA@MF precursor without DETA treatment at 700 °C.</p></section><section xml:id="cssc202201310-sec-0008" numbered="no"><title type="main">Material characterization</title><p>The phase structure of the material was analyzed by XRD (DX‐2700B, Cu K<sub>α</sub>). Field‐emission (FE)SEM (FEI Inspect F50) was used to observe the microstructure of the material. HRTEM (FEI Talos F200S Super) was used to analyze the microstructural images. The surface valence states and elemental composition were analyzed using XPS (Thermo Fischer, ESCALAB Xi+). A self‐executing gas adsorption analyzer (Micromeritics, Shanghai, China) was used to analyze the specific surface area and pore size distribution of the material.</p></section><section xml:id="cssc202201310-sec-0009" numbered="no"><title type="main">Electrochemical measurements</title><p>In this work, the electrochemical properties and behaviors of the electrode materials were studied by assembling a CR2032 battery in a glovebox filled with Ar. The active material (80 wt %), acetylene black (10 wt %), and polyvinylidene fluoride (PVDF, 10 wt %) were mixed with <i>N</i>‐methyl‐2‐pyrrolidone to form a uniform slurry, which was then coated on a copper foil. The working electrode was finished after drying at 60 °C for 12 h. For the lithium‐ion (Li<sup>+</sup>) half‐battery, 1 <sc>m</sc> LiPF<sub>6</sub> in dimethyl carbonate (DMC)/diethyl carbonate (DEC)/ethylene carbonate (EC) (1 : 1 : 1 by volume) was used as the electrolyte, a glass microfiber filter as the diaphragm, and lithium foil as the counter electrode. For the sodium‐ion (Na<sup>+</sup>) half‐cell, 1 <sc>m</sc> NaClO<sub>4</sub> in DMC/EC (1 : 1 by volume) was used as the electrolyte, a glass microfiber filter as the diaphragm, and sodium foil as the counter electrode. Constant‐current charge and discharge tests and rate performance tests were performed between 0.01 and 3.0 V using the NEWARE battery test system. CV and EIS were performed on all cells using an electrochemical workstation (CHI660e). The amplitude was 5 mV in the frequency range of 0.01 Hz to 10<sup>5</sup> Hz.</p></section></section><section xml:id="cssc202201310-sec-0010" numbered="no"><title type="main">Authorship Contribution
