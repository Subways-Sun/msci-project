Herein, a cube‐like core/shell composite of single MnS nanocubes (<b>≈</b>50 nm) encapsulated in N, S co‐doped carbon (MnS@NSC) with strong CSMn bond interactions is successfully prepared as outstanding anode material for SIBs. The carbon shell significantly restricts the expansion of the MnS volume in successive sodiation/desodiation processes, as demonstrated by in situ transmission electron microscopy (TEM) of one single MnS@NSC nanocube. Moreover, the in situ generated CSMn bonds between the MnS core and carbon shell play a significant role in improving the Na‐storage stability and reversibility of MnS@NSC, as revealed by in situ Raman and TEM. As a result, MnS@NSC exhibits a high reversible specific capacity of 594.2 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at a current density of 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup> and an excellent rate performance. It also achieves a remarkable cycling stability of 329.1 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 3000 charge/discharge cycles at 1 A g<sup>−1</sup> corresponding to a low capacity attenuation rate of 0.0068% per cycle, which is superior to that of pristine MnS and most of the reported Mn‐based anode materials in SIBs.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="smll202003001-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>A cube‐like core/shell composite of single MnS nanocubes encapsulated in N, S co‐doped carbon (MnS@NSC) with strong CSMn bond interactions is prepared successfully. As demonstrated by electrochemical tests and in situ studies, the MnS@NSC delivers high reversible capacity, excellent cycling stability, and rate capability as anode material in sodium‐ion batteries, which benefits from the sulfur‐bridged bonds and unique core/shell structure.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll202003001:smll202003001-gra-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll202003001-gra-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body xml:id="smll202003001-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="smll202003001-sec-0010"><label>1</label><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Limited lithium sources with uneven distribution on earth and rising costs of lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs) make the development of new advanced electrochemical energy storage systems for replacement of LIBs urgent.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202003001-bib-0001"><sup>1</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Sodium and lithium have similar physical and chemical properties, and the earth is rich in sodium mineral resources with wide distribution and low cost. Hence, sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) are considered as a potent option to supplement LIBs, especially for energy storage on a large scale. The radius of the sodium ion (1.06 Å) is larger than that of the lithium‐ion (0.76 Å), causing a higher volume effect and lower ion mobility. As a result, SIBs exhibit lower battery capacities and unsatisfactory electrochemical performances, which restricts the large‐scale commercial application of SIBs.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202003001-bib-0002"><sup>2</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In recent years, intensive research has been carried out to develop SIB anode materials with satisfactory high capacity, outstanding rate performance, and low cost, which is crucial to overcome the drawbacks of low performance and cost and promote commercial applications of SIBs.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202003001-bib-0003"><sup>3</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>Transition metal sulfides, such as tin sulfide,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202003001-bib-0004"><sup>4</sup></link><sup>]</sup> iron sulfide,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202003001-bib-0005"><sup>5</sup></link><sup>]</sup> cobalt sulfide,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202003001-bib-0006"><sup>6</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and molybdenum sulfide,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202003001-bib-0007"><sup>7</sup></link><sup>]</sup> have attracted much attention as promising new SIB anode materials with excellent electrochemical activity as well as thermal and mechanical stability.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202003001-bib-0008"><sup>8</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Among these metal sulfides, manganese sulfide (MnS) possesses various advantages, as it exhibits abundant reserves,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202003001-bib-0009"><sup>9</sup></link><sup>]</sup> low cost, and a high theoretical capacity of 616 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> for Na storage (electromotive force: 1.049 V; Gibbs free energy variation: −202.50 kJ mol<sup>−1</sup>).<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202003001-bib-0010"><sup>10</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, some drawbacks of MnS as anode material must be overcome for a practical application for Na storage. First, the complex and controversial Na storage processes limit the progress in improving steady performance. Second, the inevitable volume expansion during the successive sodiation/desodiation of MnS electrodes changes the morphology of the material and hence gradually attenuates the Na storage capacity.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202003001-bib-0011"><sup>11</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Third, the poor conductivity of MnS causes a sluggish electrochemical reaction and poor multiplier performance. Fourth, the high dissolvability and loss of polysulfide intermediates generated during electrode sodiation further accelerate the capacity degradation and hence decrease the Coulombic efficiency.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202003001-bib-0012"><sup>12</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In comparison with bulk materials, nano‐crystallized active materials are advantageous for electrochemical reactions due to their high specific surface area and may accommodate expanded volume in large void space. Therefore, such materials present an excellent option to overcome some drawbacks of MnS.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202003001-bib-0013"><sup>13</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Coating nanostructured metal sulfides with a carbon shell presents another effective measure to enhance the electrical conductivity, attenuate volume expansion, and impede the formation of nano sulfide aggregates.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202003001-bib-0014"><sup>14</sup></link><sup>]</sup> By co‐doping nitrogen and sulfur in carbonaceous materials, the oxidation can catalyze the direct generation of low‐order sulfides from polysulfides and further inhibit the dissolution of polysulfides in the reaction process.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202003001-bib-0015"><sup>15</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Although it has been reported that combining MnS nanostructures with carbon can effectively improve the storage performance for sodium, development of new MnS‐based nanocomposites as anode materials for SIBs remains a great challenge.</p><p>In this article, a microemulsion precipitation method was used to first prepare manganese carbonate (MnCO<sub>3</sub>) nanocubes with a side length of 70–80 nm (Figure S1, Supporting Information). Then, N, S co‐doped carbon coated MnS (MnS@NSC) nanocubes with core/shell structure and strong CSMn bonds between core and shell were successfully prepared by using the MnCO<sub>3</sub> nanocubes as templates. The unique structure of MnS@NSC not only allows direct observation of phase transformations in individual MnS nanocubes during sodiation/desodiation by in situ transmission electron microscopy (TEM) but also possesses abundant Na‐storage sites, short Na<sup>+</sup>‐diffusion distances, and fast electron transport and also adapts to volume changes during charging/discharging. Moreover, CSMn bonds in MnS@NSC and N‐doping in carbonaceous materials support the catalysis of the one‐step reaction between sodium polysulfides and manganese during the sodiation process. CSMn bonds also inhibit the aggregation of MnS during the charge/discharge cycles and stabilize the MnS nanostructure, favoring a reversible conversion and maintenance of the specific capacity. As a result, MnS@NSC exhibits significantly improved Na‐storage properties in terms of an excellent reversible capacity of 594.2 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at a current density of 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup> and approximately 120 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 5000 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. Even after 3000 cycles at 1 A g<sup>−1</sup>, an exceptionally stable capacity of 329.1 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> is retained.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202003001-sec-0020"><label>2</label><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p>Figure S2, Supporting Information, shows three kinds of MnCO<sub>3</sub> nanoparticles with different shapes and sizes synthesized by adjusting the amount of potassium bicarbonate (KHCO<sub>3</sub>) and ammonium bicarbonate (NH<sub>4</sub>HCO<sub>3</sub>). X‐ray diffraction (XRD) patterns display sharp peaks assigned to the standard diffraction of hexagonal MnCO<sub>3</sub> (JCPDS No 44–1472) and no impurity peak, indicating single‐phase and highly crystalline MnCO<sub>3</sub> samples, as shown in Figure S2A, E, and I, Supporting Information. Manganese carbonate nanocubes (MnCO<sub>3</sub> NCs) of very small particle size with a side length of ≈80 nm are obtained when both KHCO<sub>3</sub> and NH<sub>4</sub>HCO<sub>3</sub> were used as carbonic acid source (Figure S2B, Supporting Information). To the best of our knowledge, these MnCO<sub>3</sub> NCs have the smallest size among all reported cubic MnCO<sub>3</sub> particles to date (Table S1, Supporting Information). In contrast, MnCO<sub>3</sub> parallelepipeds (Figure S2F and S2J, Supporting Information) with side lengths of up to ≈200 nm were obtained when NH<sub>4</sub>HCO<sub>3</sub> or KHCO<sub>3</sub> was used as the only carbonic acid source. Clear lattice fringes with interplanar distances of 0.24, 0.20, and 0.24 nm are assigned to the (110), (202), and (110) planes of MnCO<sub>3</sub>, as shown in Figure S2D, H, and L, Supporting Information.</p><p>After heat sulfuration of MnCO<sub>3</sub> nanocube‐wrapped polydopamine (PDA), MnS nanocubes encapsulated in N, S co‐doped carbon (MnS@NSC) were obtained. Figure S3, Supporting Information, shows the XRD patterns of the prepared MnS@NSC, pristine MnS, N, S co‐doped carbon (NSC), as well as a ball‐milling mixture of MnS and NSC (MnS/NSC). MnS, MnS@NSC, and MnS/NSC only showed strong diffraction peaks that are characteristic of cubic MnS (JCPDS:65–2919) and no diffraction peaks of impurities, indicating the high crystallinity of these compounds. Prepared NSC only showed a wide characteristic amorphous peak.</p><p>SEM of MnS@NSC (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll202003001-fig-0001"><b>1</b>A</link>) reveals well‐dispersed MnS@NSC, which maintained a cubic morphology filled with holes. N<sub>2</sub> adsorption‐desorption analysis further confirmed that MnS@NSC possesses a characteristic porous structure with a Brunauer‐Emmett‐Teller (BET) surface area of 68.96 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup> (Figure S4, Supporting Information). The pore size distribution curve of MnS@NSC (inset in Figure S4, Supporting Information) reveals that the primary pore diameters are in a range of &lt;1 and 20–100 nm, indicating the hierarchical coexistence of micropores, mesopores, and macropores originating from the effects of generated CO<sub>2</sub> gas during the high‐temperature decomposition of MnCO<sub>3</sub>. The mesoporous and macroporous structures are a prerequisite for the efficient penetration of electrolyte for fast access of ions but also support buffering the MnS volume expansion during sodiation/desodiation cycling. TEM images of MnS@NSC (Figure <link href="#smll202003001-fig-0001">1B–D</link>) exhibit that a single MnS is coated with a uniform carbon shell (thickness: ≈10 nm). HRTEM (Figure <link href="#smll202003001-fig-0001">1E</link>) shows obvious periodic lattice fringes with an interlayer distance of 0.18 nm, which was assigned to the (220) plane of MnS, indicating that MnS in MnS@NSC is highly crystalline although the surrounding carbon shell shows a typical amorphous structure (Figure <link href="#smll202003001-fig-0001">1F</link>). Elemental mapping images (Figure <link href="#smll202003001-fig-0001">1G1–4</link>) show the elemental distributions of C, N, Mn, and S and indicate the integrity of the core‐shell structure of the MnS core and the carbon shell. Mn is located at the core of the particle and has a square shape, indicating that the obtained MnS retained its cubic structure. In addition to the localization of Mn in the core part of the particle, sulfur was also sparsely distributed at the edge due to successful doping of S in the carbon shell. The random distribution of nitrogen originating from dopamine indicates that N is still preserved after carbonization of dopamine and also doped in the carbon shell. Inductively coupled plasma‐atomic emission spectrometry (ICP) revealed that the MnS@NSC composite contains 85.1 wt% MnS, which was further confirmed by thermogravimetry (TG; Figure S5, Supporting Information). At stage (I), a small weight loss due to the loss of moisture was observed, followed by a gradual decrease of weight between 250 and 430 °C related to the formation of Mn<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> at stage (II). Then, a weight loss was detected between 430 and 650 °C, indicating that the carbon shell of MnS@NSC has been completely burned.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202003001-bib-0016"><sup>16</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In comparison, irregular small MnS particles with an average particle size of 50–150 nm were obtained when MnCO<sub>3</sub> nanocubes reacted with sublimated sulfur (Figure S6, Supporting Information). SEM/EDS analysis showed that Mn and S are co‐localized, and obvious lattice fringes of MnS were observed in high‐resolution TEM images. Figure S7, Supporting Information, displays NSC particles with irregular morphology, which were obtained by sintering the mixture of dopamine and sublimated sulfur, and SEM/EDS analysis indicated that N and S were successfully co‐doped in carbon. NSC is amorphous carbon, and no lattice streak appeared in the HRTEM image, which is consistent with the XRD results. In MnS/NSC, NSC particles are randomly covered with MnS particles, showing disordered contacts and low structural stability (Figure S8, Supporting Information). Pore structures of MnS, MnS/NSC, and NSC were also investigated by Brunauer‐Emmett‐Teller (BET) measurements. MnS possesses a mesoporous structure, as revealed by its pore size distribution, with dominant diameters of 3.6 and 35 nm and a BET surface area of 28.90 m<sup>2</sup> g <sup>−1</sup> (Figure S9, Supporting Information). The corresponding NSC have a hierarchical pore structure containing micropores, mesopores, and macropores with a BET surface area of 14.36 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup> (Figure S10, Supporting Information). MnS/NSC has a much smaller pore volume and surface area (23.78 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup>; Figure S11, Supporting Information).</p><figure xml:id="smll202003001-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll202003001:smll202003001-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll202003001-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>A) SEM image; B<b>–</b>G) TEM and HRTEM images of MnS@NSC; G1<b>–</b>G4) elemental mapping images of MnS@NSC.</caption></figure><p>Furthermore, the chemical composition of the surface of MnS@NSC, MnS, NSC, and MnS/NSC was studied by XPS. The XPS spectrum of MnS@NSC displays characteristic peaks of Mn 3p, Mn 2p, S 2p, S 2s, Mn 3s, C 1s, N 1s, and O 1s (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll202003001-fig-0002"><b>2</b></link> and Figures S12–S15, Supporting Information). The high‐resolution Mn 2p spectrum shows a peak centered at 643.0 eV, which is assigned to the CSMn bond between MnS and the carbon shell (Figure <link href="#smll202003001-fig-0002">2A</link>).<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202003001-bib-0017"><sup>17</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Peaks at binding energies of 641.4 and 652.9 eV correspond to Mn 2p<sub>3/2</sub> and Mn 2p<sub>1/2</sub> bonds, respectively.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202003001-bib-0017"><sup>17c</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Compared with MnS and MnS/NSC, these two peaks of MnS@NSC are redshifted about ≈1.45 eV, which can be explained by the decreased electron density at the Mn site due to electron transfer from Mn to the more electronegative S via the MnSC bond between MnS and the carbon shell. Additionally, a satellite peak is detected at 640.3 eV, which may correspond to MnO originating from MnO<i><sub>x</sub></i> generated on the partly oxidized surface of MnS in the samples. In contrast to MnS and MnS/NSC, MnS@NSC exhibited in its S 2p spectrum two obvious peaks at 160.4 and 161.6 eV, which were assigned to S 2p<sub>3/2</sub> and S 2p<sub>1/2</sub> of the CSMn bond (Figure <link href="#smll202003001-fig-0002">2B</link>). Moreover, the high‐resolution S 2p spectrum of MnS@NSC can be deconvoluted into two other peaks at 164.4 and 163.2 eV, corresponding to S 2p<sub>1/2</sub> and S 2p<sub>3/2</sub>, respectively.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202003001-bib-0018"><sup>18</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Figure <link href="#smll202003001-fig-0002">2C</link> shows the deconvolution of the high‐resolution C 1s spectrum into three peaks. The peak centered at 286.3 eV is characteristic of CC or CC bonds.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202003001-bib-0019"><sup>19</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Peaks at binding energies of 288.1 and 287.0 eV correspond to CN and CSMn/CS bonds, respectively. Compared with MnS/NSC, MnS@NSC shows a remarkable positive shift of its C 1s spectrum caused by the electron density shift from the Mn sites to C sites, which further confirms the existence of CSMn bonds. The high‐resolution N 1s peaks (Figure <link href="#smll202003001-fig-0002">2D</link>) were mainly deconvoluted into three peaks associated with pyridinic N (397.8 eV), pyrrolic N (399.8 eV), and graphitic N (401.9 eV), which enhance the composite material's dispersion kinetics and sodium ion conductivity and greatly promote the electrochemical properties.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202003001-bib-0020"><sup>20</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><figure xml:id="smll202003001-fig-0002"><label>2</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll202003001:smll202003001-fig-0002" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll202003001-fig-0002-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>High‐resolution XPS spectra of the A) Mn 2p, B) S 2p, C) C 1s, and D) N 1s peaks of MnS@NSC, MnS/NSC, and MnS.</caption></figure><p>The electrochemical performances of MnS@NSC, MnS, MnS/NSC, and NSC as materials for SIB anodes were tested in CR2032 half cells using sodium metal as a counter electrode. <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll202003001-fig-0003"><b>3</b>A</link> shows four cycles of cyclic voltammograms (CVs) of MnS@NSC in a voltage range of 0.01–2.8 V (vs. Na<sup>+</sup>/Na) at a scan rate of 0.1 mV s<sup>−1</sup>. The first cathodic scan exhibits two irreversible peaks at 1.2 and 0.2 V, resulting from irreversible electrolyte decomposition and the formation of SEI layers on the electrode surface.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202003001-bib-0021"><sup>21</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In the subsequent cycle, these two peaks were not detected any longer. A peak caused by the reduction of MnS@NSC with sodium appeared between 0.4–1.0 V. In the reverse scan, an oxidation peak appeared between 1.7–2.3 V due to sodium ion removal and oxidation of manganese to MnS. In the 2nd to 4th cycles, the CV curves were well matched, indicating that MnS@NSC has high cycle reversibility as SIB anode material. However, MnS and MnS/NSC showed unstable curves in the 2nd to 4th cycles (Figures S16–18). Figure <link href="#smll202003001-fig-0003">3B</link> shows selected galvanostatic charge/discharge curves (1st, 2nd, 100th, and 200th cycle) of the MnS@NSC electrode in the potential window of 0.01–2.8 V (vs. Na<sup>+</sup>/Na) at the applied current density of 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. These curves were in good agreement with the CV curves, showing a linear voltage response. Figure <link href="#smll202003001-fig-0003">3C</link> shows charge/discharge capacities and the corresponding coulomb efficiencies of the four samples at a current density of 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, revealing a constant current charge/discharge cycle performance. MnS@NSC has a reversible specific capacity of 594.2 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> and an initial coulombic efficiency of 65.1%. After 200 cycles, MnS@NSC only retained a specific capacity of 388.7 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>. This decay in the specific capacity is ascribed to a combination of different events, such as the reductive electrolyte decomposition, formation, and stabilization of the SEI film, as well as irreversible reactions between sodium ions and residual oxygen‐containing functional groups of PDA‐derived carbon. The reversible specific capacities of MnS, MnS/NSC, and NSC are 275.9, 304.9, and 120.7 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. MnS/NSC and MnS experienced sharp capacity decays during the charge/discharge cycles and exhibited specific capacities of only 147.8 and 173.8 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> in the 200th cycle, respectively. The rate capabilities of MnS@NSC, MnS, MnS/NSC, and NSC electrodes were evaluated at varying current densities ranging from 50 mA g<sup>−1</sup> to 5 A g<sup>−1</sup>, as shown in Figure <link href="#smll202003001-fig-0003">3D</link>. The specific capacities of MnS@NSC were 629.4, 551.9, 484.4, 356.4, and 109.2 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at current densities of 50 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, 500 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, 1 A g<sup>−1</sup>, and 5 A g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. When the MnS@NSC electrode was again subjected to a current rate of 50 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, it quickly resumed a capacity of 529.9 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, which remained very stable for extended cycling. In contrast, the specific capacities of the MnS/NSC, MnS, and NSC electrodes dropped dramatically with increasing current density. At a current density of 5 A g<sup>−1</sup>, MnS/NSC, MnS, and NSC only exhibited capacities of less than 50 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>. Figure <link href="#smll202003001-fig-0003">3E</link> shows the specific capacities of MnS@NSC, MnS, MnS/NSC, and NSC after long‐term cycling at 1 A g<sup>−1</sup>. After 3000 charge/discharge cycles, MnS@NSC showed a specific capacity of 329.1 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, corresponding to a capacity retention rate of 79.5% (calculated for an initial reversible capacity of 413.9 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>) and a capacity attenuation rate of 0.0068% per cycle. Moreover, MnS@NSC exhibited a superior reversible capacity and higher cycling stability compared with recently reported MnS‐based materials<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202003001-bib-0022"><sup>22</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and other metal sulfides<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202003001-bib-0023"><sup>23</sup></link><sup>]</sup> (Table S2, Supporting Information).</p><figure xml:id="smll202003001-fig-0003"><label>3</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll202003001:smll202003001-fig-0003" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll202003001-fig-0003-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>A) CV curves of MnS@NSC; B) charge/discharge curves of MnS@NSC at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>; C) charge/discharge capacities and corresponding coulomb efficiencies at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>; D) rate capacities for current densities from 0.05 to 5 A g<sup>−1</sup> between 0.01 and 2.8 V (vs. Na<sup>+</sup>/Na); E) discharge capacities of MnS@NSC, MnS, MnS/NSC, and NSC composites at 1 A g<sup>−1</sup>.</caption></figure><p>Figure S19, Supporting Information, shows the typical Nyquist plots of electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) of the four samples. The high‐frequency region shows a small semicircle reflecting the charge transfer resistance.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202003001-bib-0024"><sup>24</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Typically, the straight line in the middle frequency region indicating the double layer mechanism. Compared with MnS and MnS/NSC electrodes, the MnS@NSC electrode shows a semicircle with a smaller diameter and a straight line with a slightly steeper slope, indicating that the charge transfer efficiency and thus the electrode reaction kinetics were effectively improved due to the encapsulation of MnS in the N, S co‐doped carbon shell. The carbon shell on the surface of MnS can increase conductivity, reduce ohmic polarization, reduce the occurrence of side reactions, and improve electrochemical cycle stability. The MnS@NSC electrode displayed a higher reversible capacity and better cycling stability compared with the MnS/NSC, MnS, and NSC electrodes, indicating that the N, S co‐doped carbon shell critically promotes the Na‐ion storage capacity of MnS. To further examine the structural stability of MnS@NSC and MnS, TEM and SEM were performed after subjecting the electrodes to 200 cycles at a current density of 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> (Figures S20, S21, Supporting Information). Although a small number of narrow cracks appeared on MnS in MnS@NSC, MnS did not break apart, and MnS@NSC retained its core‐shell structure with an intact outer carbon structure. However, compared with MnS@NSC, MnS shows bigger cracks on the particles and many more shed and scattered small granules. The initial structure of MnS collapsed, which may result in some pulverization and capacity attenuation.</p><p>Morphology and microstructure evolution upon sodium insertion into MnS@NSC were investigated via in situ TEM, as shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll202003001-fig-0004"><b>4</b></link> and Supporting Information 2 (real‐time video). Figure <link href="#smll202003001-fig-0004">4B,C</link> shows the morphology of the original MnS@NSC before sodiation and its electron diffraction (SAED) mode with a clear diffraction ring typical for the (200), (220), and (400) planes of MnS, which is in agreement with the XRD analysis. After sodiation at a constant potential of +3 V for 9 s (Figure <link href="#smll202003001-fig-0004">4D</link>), the diffusion of sodium ions can be analyzed based on the color change of the MnS particles in MnS@NSC. In addition to MnS, Na<sub>2</sub>S shows diffraction rings, as a part of the MnS particles reacted with sodium and converted into Na<sub>2</sub>S. The SAED pattern recorded at 36 s is indexed to the lattice planes of Na<sub>2</sub>S and Mn. MnS was absent, indicating that MnS was successfully sodiated, which is comparable to the lithiation of MnS undergoing an α to β phase transition.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202003001-bib-0022"><sup>22b</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The cubic structure of MnS increased its side length from 50 to 52 nm, corresponding to an expansion rate of only ≈4% during the sodiation process, which indicates that the carbon shell significantly impedes the volume expansion of MnS.</p><figure xml:id="smll202003001-fig-0004"><label>4</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll202003001:smll202003001-fig-0004" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll202003001-fig-0004-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>A) Schematic of in situ TEM experiments during the sodiation process; B–G) TEM images and corresponding SAED patterns of MnS@NSC at different sodiation times: (B, C) 0 s, (D, E) 9 s, and (F, G) 36 s.</caption></figure><p>The Na‐storage processes in MnS@NSC were further investigated by in situ Raman spectroscopy (using a quartz window) during the initial discharge‐charge‐discharge sequence, as shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll202003001-fig-0005"><b>5</b></link>. At the open‐circuit voltage (≈2.5 V), the peak at about 654 cm<sup>−1</sup> is attributed to the MnS/MnSC vibration.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202003001-bib-0025"><sup>25</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The other two peaks (1403 and 1571 cm<sup>−1</sup>) are characteristic for the D and G bands of carbonaceous materials (i.e., the carbon shell).<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202003001-bib-0026"><sup>26</sup></link><sup>]</sup> When the battery was discharged to 0.6 V for complete breaking, the MnSC/MnS peaks disappeared, indicating that MnS perfectly reacted with Na and converted into manganese and Na<sub>2</sub>S. It is worth noting that the MnS peak gradually increased as the battery was subsequently charged to 1.6 V for the reformation of the MnSC/MnS bonds. During the second discharge cycle, the regenerated MnSC/MnS peaks decayed again and disappeared at 0.8 V (Figure <link href="#smll202003001-fig-0005">5C</link>), implying good repeatability of MnSC/MnS bond formation and breaking. This indicates good reversibility of the conversion of MnS into MnS@NSC, which confirms the CV results. Figure <link href="#smll202003001-fig-0005">5F</link> illustrates the sodiation/desodiation mechanisms for pristine MnS and MnS@NSC. During sodiation of MnS@NSC, the generated Mn was well protected, and no aggregation occurred during the formation of the MnSC bond. In contrast, the generated Mn particles in pristine MnS aggregated and lost their intrinsic reactivity to form MnS with Na<sub>2</sub>S. This explains the high reversibility of MnS@NSC in contrast to pristine MnS, which showed a remarkable decrease in capacity during sodiation/desodiation. The I<sub>D</sub>/I<sub>G</sub> intensity ratio obtained by Raman spectroscopy increased during battery discharging and decreased during charging. This finding can be explained by the intercalation and deintercalation of Na<sup>+</sup> in the carbon shell of MnS@NSC, which changes the interlayer spacings. Furthermore, this result suggests that the main transformation reaction accompanies the intercalation during Na<sup>+</sup> ion storage in MnS@NSC.</p><figure xml:id="smll202003001-fig-0005"><label>5</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll202003001:smll202003001-fig-0005" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll202003001-fig-0005-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>A–E) Stacked in situ Raman patterns of an MnS@NSC electrode during the initial discharge‐charge‐discharge processes at different voltages. F) Schematics comparing the discharge/charge process in MnS@NSC and pristine MnS.</caption></figure></section><section xml:id="smll202003001-sec-0030"><label>3</label><title type="main">Conclusion</title><p>In summary, we successfully prepared a nanocomposite of single MnS cubes encapsulated in N, S co‐doped carbon with strong CSMn bond interactions. Furthermore, in situ TEM showed that the carbon shell significantly limited the volume expansion of MnS during the sodiation process of a single MnS@NSC cube. The in situ generated CSMn bond between MnS and the carbon shell can support the reversible conversion of MnS for Na‐ion storage. In situ Raman and TEM revealed that MnS successfully undergoes reversible sodiation/desodiation in MnS@NSC, which contributes to an excellent specific capacity, remarkable rate performance, and ultra‐high cycling stability. As anode material for SIBs, MnS@NSC achieves a reversible capacity of 594.2 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at a current density of 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup> and an initial coulombic efficiency of 65.1%. MnS@NSC has a reversible specific capacity of 413.9 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at a current density of 1 A g<sup>−1</sup> and exhibits a capacity of 329.1 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 3000 charge/discharge cycles, corresponding to a capacity retention rate of 79.5% and a capacity attenuation rate of 0.0068% per cycle.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202003001-sec-0040" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>J.L.Z. and P.C.W. contributed equally to this work.
