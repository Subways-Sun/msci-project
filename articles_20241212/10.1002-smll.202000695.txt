Tungsten disulfide (WS<sub>2</sub>) nanosheets are confined, through synergistic anchoring, on the surface and inside of hollow beaded carbon nanofibers (HB CNFs) via a hydrothermal reaction method to construct the hierarchical structure HB WS<sub>2</sub>@CNFs. Benefiting from this unique advantage, HB WS<sub>2</sub>@CNFs exhibits remarkable lithium‐storage performance in terms of high rate capability (≈351 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 2 A g<sup>−1</sup>) and stable long‐term cycle (≈446 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 1 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 100 cycles). Moreover, as an anode material for SIBs, HB WS<sub>2</sub>@CNFs obtains excellent long cycle life and rate performance. During the charging/discharging process, the evolution of morphology and composition of the composite are analyzed by a set of ex situ methods. This synergistic anchoring effect between WS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets and HB CNFs is capable of effectively restraining volume expansion from the metal ions intercalation/deintercalation process and improving the cycling stability and rate performance in LIBs and SIBs.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="smll202000695-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>Tungsten disulfide (WS<sub>2</sub>) nanosheets are anchored on the surface and inside of hollow beaded carbon nanofibers (HB CNFs) via a confined growth strategy to construct hierarchical structure HB WS<sub>2</sub>@CNFs. xml:id="smll202000695-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="smll202000695-sec-0010"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Advanced energy storage technology is urgently required to meet the need of modern society.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000695-bib-0001 #smll202000695-bib-0002 #smll202000695-bib-0003"><sup>1–3</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Currently, 2D materials, especially transition metal dichalcogenide (TMD),<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000695-bib-0004 #smll202000695-bib-0005"><sup>4,5</sup></link><sup>]</sup> have a wide range of application in the field of sodium and lithium storage.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000695-bib-0006 #smll202000695-bib-0007"><sup>6,7</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Among the existing myriad of TMDs, tungsten disulfide (WS<sub>2</sub>) nanosheets have been explored for their typical 2D layered structure,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000695-bib-0008 #smll202000695-bib-0009"><sup>8,9</sup></link><sup>]</sup> which can provide many interfacial sites for lithium and sodium storage, and fast channels for ions and electrons transportation, owing to its large specific surface area and extremely small size.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000695-bib-0010"><sup>10</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The inner layer is bonded by a strong covalent bond (S–W–S), while the interlayers are linked through a weak van der Waals force with spacing of 0.6 nm between layers.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000695-bib-0011 #smll202000695-bib-0012"><sup>11,12</sup></link><sup>]</sup> WS<sub>2</sub> has gained tremendous attention because it possesses a higher theoretical specific capacity (433 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>) as anode for lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs) and facilitates the diffusion of smaller metal ions in the matrix.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000695-bib-0013"><sup>13</sup></link><sup>]</sup> This special structure can promote the further reaction of lithium ions and sodium ions with the matrix material.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000695-bib-0014 #smll202000695-bib-0015"><sup>14,15</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, the 2D layer structure can be easily damaged by volume expansion and leads to attenuation of capacity.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000695-bib-0016 #smll202000695-bib-0017"><sup>16,17</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>In order to maintain the stability of the layered 2D structure of WS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets and to provide space for free expansion, various carbon materials with hollow topography are introduced as substrates to confine WS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000695-bib-0018 #smll202000695-bib-0019 #smll202000695-bib-0020"><sup>18–20</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Based on the synergistic structure relationship between 2D WS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets and hollow‐structured carbon materials, the electrochemical property can be improved effectively. The stability and conductivity of the layer structure prevent the capacity attenuation caused by the volume changes during charging/discharging.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000695-bib-0021 #smll202000695-bib-0022 #smll202000695-bib-0023"><sup>21–23</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Generally, the various inorganic compounds have been integrated into different shapes of carbon frames with the aim of improving properties including electrical conductivity, cyclic performance, and stability for anode materials.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000695-bib-0024 #smll202000695-bib-0025 #smll202000695-bib-0026"><sup>24–26</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, in the reaction process, if the conditions of the growth of the material are not well‐controlled, then the 2D materials can easily pulverized and detached from the carbon structure in the subsequent battery reaction, resulting in bad rate performance and cycle performance.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000695-bib-0027 #smll202000695-bib-0028 #smll202000695-bib-0029"><sup>27–29</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Therefore, the design of new type material is aimed to provide the buffer space to mitigate the volume changes, thereby maintaining excellent electrochemical properties and structural stability.</p><p>In this study, to fabricate the HB WS<sub>2</sub>@CNFs, the confinement growth strategy is adopted to grow WS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets into hollow beaded structured carbon nanofibers (HB CNFs). The strain induced by volume changes during Li<sup>+</sup>/Na<sup>+</sup> ion charge/discharge process is proved to be relieved by the hollow confined structure and the synergistic anchoring effect between WS<sub>2</sub> and HB CNFs, which in turn can enhance structure stability and cyclic performance. The mechanism of material is examined by ex situ methods.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000695-bib-0030"><sup>30</sup></link><sup>]</sup> To the best of our knowledge, the use of synergistic anchoring effect in confinement growth of WS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets to HB CNFs is an innovative tactic, and this is the first study to report the application of HB WS<sub>2</sub>@CNFs for LIBs and sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs).</p></section><section xml:id="smll202000695-sec-0020"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><section xml:id="smll202000695-sec-0030"><title type="main">Characterization of Hollow Beaded Structure WS<sub>2</sub>@CNFs (HB WS<sub>2</sub>@CNFs)</title><p>The specific route for fabricating the hollow beaded WS<sub>2</sub>@CNFs (HB WS<sub>2</sub>@CNFs) is demonstrated in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll202000695-fig-0001"><b>1</b></link>. First, the core–shell structure SiO<sub>2</sub>@SiO<sub>2</sub>/RF nanoparticles (Figure S1, Supporting Information) are incorporated into polyacrylonitrile (PAN) by electrospinning method.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000695-bib-0031"><sup>31</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In Figure S2a,b, Supporting Information, the scanning electron microscopy (SEM) images of PAN nanofibers are shown as a comparison. Second, the HB CNFs are formed through a two‐step process. In the first step, the formed SiO<sub>2</sub>@SiO<sub>2</sub>/RF@PAN nanofibers are calcined and carbonized to obtain the beaded SiO<sub>2</sub>/C@CNFs (Figure S2c,d, Supporting Information). In the two steps, the HB CNFs are generated after SiO<sub>2</sub> is etched away by NaOH (Figure S2e,f, Supporting Information). Finally, WS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets preferentially nucleate inside of hollow carbon spheres and then grow on the outer wall of hollow carbon spheres to form HB WS<sub>2</sub>@CNFs through hydrothermal reaction.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000695-bib-0032"><sup>32</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><figure xml:id="smll202000695-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll202000695:smll202000695-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll202000695-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Mechanism for the development of the hollow beaded WS<sub>2</sub>@CNFs.</caption></figure><p>The field‐emission SEM and transmission electron microscopy (TEM) show different microscopic morphologies for materials during the synthesis. In <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll202000695-fig-0002"><b>2</b>a</link>, SiO<sub>2</sub>/C@CNFs display a homogeneous beaded structure. Figure <link href="#smll202000695-fig-0002">2b,c</link> shows HB CNFs after etching with NaOH. When HB CNFs are used as nanoreactors, WS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets are confined to grow on the surface and inside of HB CNFs (Figure <link href="#smll202000695-fig-0002">2d–f</link>) during the hydrothermal process based on the synergistic anchoring effect. The confined growth of HB WS<sub>2</sub>@CNFs can be controlled by varying the solution concentration and reaction time. The synergistic anchoring effect between porous carbon layer and WS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets keeps structure intact of HB WS<sub>2</sub>@CNFs (Figure <link href="#smll202000695-fig-0002">2d–f</link>). Figure <link href="#smll202000695-fig-0002">2g</link> displays a fewer layered structure of WS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets in the intrinsic lattice fringe of HB WS<sub>2</sub>@CNFs from high‐resolution TEM (HRTEM). At the (002) crystal plane, the spacing of lattice stripes is measured to be 0.62 nm. The selected area electron diffraction (SAED, Figure <link href="#smll202000695-fig-0002">2i</link>) pattern analysis of HB WS<sub>2</sub>@CNFs shows four typical diffraction ring patterns of (002), (101), (103), and (110), which belong to the standard hexagonal WS<sub>2</sub>. The distribution of the elements of C, N, S, and W (Figure <link href="#smll202000695-fig-0002">2k–n</link>) at hollow beaded structure is homogeneous, which are further confirmed using annular dark‐field scanning transmission electron microscopy (STEM) and energy‐dispersive X‐ray spectrometry (EDX) mappings. The weight fraction of WS<sub>2</sub> in the HB WS<sub>2</sub>@CNFs is determined to be 73.5% from thermogravimetric analysis (Figure S3, Supporting Information). A typical mesoporous structure (Figure S4, Supporting Information) is found, and the specific surface area is 212.5 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup> for HB WS<sub>2</sub>@CNFs from nitrogen adsorption/desorption analysis.</p><figure xml:id="smll202000695-fig-0002"><label>2</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll202000695:smll202000695-fig-0002" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll202000695-fig-0002-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) TEM image of the SiO<sub>2</sub>/C@CNFs; b,c) TEM images of HB CNFs; d–f) TEM images, g,h) HRTEM images, and i) SAED pattern of HB WS<sub>2</sub>@CNFs; j) annular dark‐field STEM image and the corresponding EDX elemental mappings of k–n) C, N, S, and W in a single HB WS<sub>2</sub>@CNFs composite.</caption></figure><p>Pure WS<sub>2</sub> (Figure S5, Supporting Information) and WS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets grown on the surface of carbon nanofiber (SG WS<sub>2</sub>@CNFs, Figures S6 and S7, Supporting Information) are synthesized by means of the same method as the synthesis of HB WS<sub>2</sub>@CNFs to serve as comparisons and to highlight the novelty of HB WS<sub>2</sub>@CNFs. Figure S6, Supporting Information, shows the synthetic mechanism of SG WS<sub>2</sub>@CNFs. As shown in Figure S7a,b, Supporting Information, WS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets are coated on the surface of carbon fibers. SG WS<sub>2</sub>@CNFs exhibits a structure with a mixture of mesoporous and microporous structures, and the specific surface area of SG WS<sub>2</sub>@CNFs is 108.9 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup> (Figure S8a,b, Supporting Information), which is smaller than that of HB WS<sub>2</sub>@CNF. The pore size of SG WS<sub>2</sub>@CNFs is too small to form synergistic anchoring effect between WS<sub>2</sub> and CNFs when compared with HB CNFs (Figure S8c,d, Supporting Information) and HB WS<sub>2</sub>@CNFs (Figure S4, Supporting Information).</p><p>The crystal structures of pure WS<sub>2</sub>, SG WS<sub>2</sub>@CNFs, and HB WS<sub>2</sub>@CNFs were measured by X‐ray diffraction (XRD, <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll202000695-fig-0003"><b>3</b>a</link>), which are well‐indexed with hexagonal crystal structure of WS<sub>2</sub> (JCPDS no. 08‐0237).<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000695-bib-0033"><sup>33</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Nevertheless, when the 2θ value of (002) plane is compared with those of pure WS<sub>2</sub> (14.2°) and SG WS<sub>2</sub>@CNFs (14.0°), the 2θ degree value of HB WS<sub>2</sub>@CNFs is the smallest, at 13.7° (details can be found in the inset of Figure <link href="#smll202000695-fig-0003">3a</link>), owing to the enlargement of interlayer spacing. Moreover, the Raman shifts exhibit stronger intensities of D and G peaks ascribing to 1340 and 1591 cm. In general, the ratio of intensity of D‐band and G‐band, the <i>I</i><sub>D</sub>/<i>I</i><sub>G</sub> values, is used as a reference for the degree of graphitization. The <i>I</i><sub>D</sub>/<i>I</i><sub>G</sub> values are determined to be 1.16 and 1.32 for HB WS<sub>2</sub>@CNFs and SG WS<sub>2</sub>@CNFs, respectively. A smaller <i>I</i><sub>D</sub>/<i>I</i><sub>G</sub> of HB WS<sub>2</sub>@CNFs than that of SG WS<sub>2</sub>@CNFs, which is close to the ratio of standard graphite materials,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000695-bib-0034"><sup>34</sup></link><sup>]</sup> suggests that carbon in HB WS<sub>2</sub>@CNFs is more orderly than that of SG WS<sub>2</sub>@CNFs and HB WS<sub>2</sub>@CNFs has a relatively higher degree of graphitization (Figure <link href="#smll202000695-fig-0003">3b</link>). The Raman shifts at 417.2 (<i>E</i><sup>1</sup><sub>2g</sub>) and 349.5 cm (A<sub>1g</sub>) indicate the formation of high crystallinity of WS<sub>2</sub>.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000695-bib-0035"><sup>35</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><figure xml:id="smll202000695-fig-0003"><label>3</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll202000695:smll202000695-fig-0003" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll202000695-fig-0003-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) XRD pattern, b) Raman spectra of pure WS<sub>2</sub>, SG WS<sub>2</sub>@CNFs, and HB WS<sub>2</sub>@CNFs. c–f) High‐resolution XPS spectra, C 1s, N 1s, W 4f, and S 2p of HB WS<sub>2</sub>@CNFs.</caption></figure><p>X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) analysis of HB WS<sub>2</sub>@CNFs clearly demonstrates the presence of all expected peaks of C, N, W, and S in the composite (Figure S9, Supporting Information). In the C 1s spectrum, the peaks at 286.2, 283.8, 287.9, and 285.2 eV (Figure <link href="#smll202000695-fig-0003">3c</link>) are assigned to CC, CO, CO and CN. The main forms of nitrogen‐doped (Figure <link href="#smll202000695-fig-0003">3d</link>) carbon structure are pyridine N, pyrrolic N, quaternary N, and N=O, which play a significant role in the electrochemical property of material. The synergistic anchoring effect between 1D HB CNFs and 2D WS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets alleviates the strain induced by volume changes during Li<sup>+</sup> and Na<sup>+</sup> insertion/extraction process. HB WS<sub>2</sub>@CNFs achieves excellent rate capability (≈351 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 2 A g<sup>−1</sup>) and outstanding cyclic performance (≈446 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 1 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 100 cycles) as an anode material for LIBs. The SiO<sub>2</sub>@SiO<sub>2</sub>/RF core–shell structured nanospheres were synthesized according to a previous report (the more details can be found in Supporting Information).<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202000695-bib-0048"><sup>48</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Then 0.3 g of SiO<sub>2</sub>@SiO<sub>2</sub>/RF nanospheres was added into the DMF solution of PAN. The abovementioned mixture solution was stirred for 10 h under room temperature. The obtained spinning solution was loaded into the syringe with a 23‐gauge needle tip. For the electrospinning process, the flow rate of the solution was 0.1 mL h<sup>−1</sup>, the voltage was 15 kV, and the distance between the tip and the collector was 15 cm. After the electrospinning process, the as‐prepared SiO<sub>2</sub>@SiO<sub>2</sub>/RF@PAN nanofibers were heated at 700 °C for 2 h at a heating rate of 2 °C min<sup>−1</sup> under Ar atmosphere. Finally, the HB CNFs were obtained by removing silica using sodium hydroxide (NaOH, 1 M).</p></section><section xml:id="smll202000695-sec-0110" numbered="no"><title type="main">Synthesis of Hollow Beaded Structural WS<sub>2</sub>@CNFs (HB WS<sub>2</sub>@CNFs)</title><p>A total of 0.5 g Na<sub>2</sub>WO<sub>4</sub>·2H<sub>2</sub>O, 0.5 g CS(NH<sub>2</sub>)<sub>2</sub>, and 0.15 g NaBH<sub>4</sub> were dissolved into 30 mL deionized water to form a transparent solution under ultrasonic dispersion for 30 min, followed by dispersing of 100 mg HB CNFs to the solution. The autoclave was then heated in an air‐flow electric oven at 200 °C for 12 h. After that, the autoclave was cooled to room temperature. The solution was transferred out for centrifugation. The final products were collected and washed six times with deionized water and ethanol before being dried at 60 °C in vacuum overnight. Then the product was calcined in a tube furnace at 800 °C for 2 h under Ar atmosphere to obtain hollow beaded structural WS<sub>2</sub>@CNFs.
