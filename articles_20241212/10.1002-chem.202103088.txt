In this article, owing to the large π‐conjugated structure, plenty of nitrogen heteroatoms and multiring aromatic system, polyazaacene analogue poly(1,6‐dihydropyrazino[2,3 g]quinoxaline‐2,3,8‐triyl‐7‐(2<i>H</i>)‐ylidene‐7,8‐dimethylidene) (PQL) was applied as the anode in sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs). PQL was almost insoluble in conventional liquid organic electrolyte (1 M NaClO<sub>4</sub> in ethylene carbonate (EC)/dimethyl carbonate (DMC) (v:v=1 : 1) with 5 % fluoroethylene carbonate (FEC)), which strongly improved its cycle stability. The initial discharge capacity was obtained to be 1825 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at the current density of 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup> and stabilized at 317 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 400 cycles with the coulombic efficiency as high as 97 %. It not only showed good rate capability at high current densities (202, 183 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 1 A g<sup>−1</sup> and 1.5 A g<sup>−1</sup>) but also had a superior energy density around 290 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup>.</p></abstract><abstract type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p><b>Organic SIB anode</b>: Using polyazaacene analogue PQL as the anode, SIBs displayed stable electrochemical performances and great rate capability. This work would provide insights into the application of organic polymer electrodes in rechargeable batteries.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:09476539:media:chem202103088:chem202103088-toc-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/chem202103088-toc-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="chem202103088-sec-0001" numbered="no"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Rechargeable lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs) have been widely employed in portable electronics, smart grids and new‐energy vehicles.<link href="#chem202103088-bib-0001"><sup>[1]</sup></link> However, due to the shortage of lithium resources, relatively high production costs and safety problems, LIBs have faced a big issue in large‐scale practical applications.<link href="#chem202103088-bib-0008"><sup>[2]</sup></link> Comparatively, sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) have received considerable attention owing to the abundant sodium reserves, exploit convenience and low cost.<link href="#chem202103088-bib-0014"><sup>[3]</sup></link> Additionally, sodium locates in the same main‐group as lithium and shares similar physicochemical properties. Therefore, SIBs have the similar fundamental electrochemical principle to LIBs and are considered as one of the most promising candidates.<link href="#chem202103088-bib-0019"><sup>[4]</sup></link> It is worth noting that the mass and radius of Na<sup>+</sup> are larger than that of Li<sup>+</sup>, which aggravates the difficulty of ion migration, resulting in the structure collapse and pulverization of inorganic electrode materials during the cycles. Hence, SIBs with inorganic electrodes usually have the issues of low capacity and unstable cycle performances.<link href="#chem202103088-bib-0025"><sup>[5]</sup></link> Exploring high‐capacity electrode materials with flexible structures is an essential strategy to further promote the development of SIBs.</p><p>Nowadays, organic materials are proposed to be the appropriate electrode materials for the new‐generation batteries benefitting from the strong structural designability, potentially high specific capacity, relatively inexpensive manufacturing cost and eco‐friendly.<link href="#chem202103088-bib-0026"><sup>[6]</sup></link> Numbers of organic electrode materials with redox activities have been involved in SIBs and exhibited excellent electrochemical properties.<link href="#chem202103088-bib-0033"><sup>[7]</sup></link> For instance, trisodium 1,2,4‐benzenetricarboxylate displayed a reversible capacity of 137 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 600 cycles when used as anode in SIBs.<link href="#chem202103088-bib-0040"><sup>[8]</sup></link> Applied as the cathode, calix[4]quinone obtained an initial discharge capacity of 438 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, very close to its theoretical capacity (<i>C</i><sub>theo</sub>=446 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>).<link href="#chem202103088-bib-0041"><sup>[9]</sup></link> However, high solubility in conventional organic liquid electrolytes and poor conductivity are nonnegligible problems existing in organic electrodes, which leads to fast capacity decay, low utilization of active sites and poor rate capability.<link href="#chem202103088-bib-0042"><sup>[10]</sup></link> Polymerizing organic small molecules into polymers with stable frameworks can solve this puzzler effectively.<link href="#chem202103088-bib-0046"><sup>[11]</sup></link> Radical polymers, conductive polymers, organometallic polymers, conjugated microporous polymers, and metal‐organic frameworks with insoluble characteristics are commonly used as organic polymer electrodes for rechargeable batteries.<link href="#chem202103088-bib-0047"><sup>[12]</sup></link> In addition, the introduction of heteroatoms (for example, N, S) into stable skeletons can help regulate the redox properties of organic electrodes to improve their electrochemical properties.<link href="#chem202103088-bib-0050"><sup>[13]</sup></link> For example, sodium salt of poly(2,5‐dihydroxyp‐benzoquinonyl sulfide)/RGO (Na<sub>2</sub>PDHBQS/RGO) composite has been investigated in ether‐based electrolyte and exhibited excellent rate capability (228, 147 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1 and 4 C, respectively).<link href="#chem202103088-bib-0053"><sup>[14]</sup></link> Poly(benzoquinonyl sulfide) (PBQS) was reported to have a high energy density of 557 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup> while used as cathode in SIBs.<link href="#chem202103088-bib-0054"><sup>[15]</sup></link> Based on these, polyazaacene analogue poly(1,6‐dihydropyrazino[2,3 g]quinoxaline‐2,3,8‐triyl‐7‐(<i>2H</i>)‐ylidene‐7,8‐dimethylidene) (PQL) with large π‐conjugated structure, plenty of nitrogen heteroatom and multiring aromatic system has been synthesized and investigated. It is proposed to have fourteen redox‐active sites for Li<sup>+</sup> to intercalate/deintercalate.<link href="#chem202103088-bib-0055"><sup>[16]</sup></link> Through the nanoscale treatment, the electrical conductivity of PQL can reach up to 2.1×10<sup>−3</sup> S cm<sup>−1</sup> and be proven to perform well as the anode in LIBs. At 50 °C, it displayed a reversible capacity of 1750 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.05 C, excellent cycle stability of 500 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 1000 cycles at 2.5 C and good rate capability of 303 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> even at a high current density of 5 C.<link href="#chem202103088-bib-0055"><sup>[16]</sup></link></p><p>Since PQL has a stable large conjugated ladder structure and good lithium storage performance, it is necessary to further study the electrochemical performances of PQL in SIBs. In this study, the SIBs were assembled with PQL anode, 1 M NaClO<sub>4</sub> in ethylene carbonate (EC)/dimethyl carbonate (DMC) (v:v=1 : 1) with 5 % fluoroethylene carbonate (FEC) electrolyte and sodium foil counter electrode. The resulting PQL SIBs demonstrated a superb energy density of 290 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup>, great cycle stability of 317 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 400 cycles, and good rate capability of 183 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at the high current density of 1.5 A g<sup>−1</sup>.</p></section><section xml:id="chem202103088-sec-0002" numbered="no"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p>Firstly, PQL was synthesized by the reaction between the carbonyl (−C=O), hydroxyl (−OH) groups of 2,5‐dihydroxy‐1,4‐benzoquinone and amino (−NH<sub>2</sub>) groups of 1,2,4,5‐tetraaminobenzene tetrahydrobromide (TAB ⋅ 4HBr) in polyphosphoric acid (PPA). The synthetic details are shown in the Supporting Information. Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy (FTIR) and X‐ray diffraction (XRD) tests were conducted to verify the characteristic functional groups and crystal structure of the as‐prepared PQL. The absorption peak caused by N−H stretching vibration appears at 3060 cm<sup>−1</sup>. The stretching vibration of C=N bonds locates in the range of 1612–1250 cm<sup>−1</sup>, while the C−N skeletal vibration of the phenazine ring is associated with the peak at 840 cm<sup>−1</sup>. The characteristic absorption peaks were consistent with the previous report<link href="#chem202103088-bib-0056"><sup>[17]</sup></link> and prove that PQL has been synthesized successfully (Figure S1). In the XRD patterns, there is a broad and low diffraction peak shown at 27°, indicating the low crystallinity of PQL (Figure S2).</p><p>Secondly, the morphological characteristics of pristine PQL, uncycled PQL electrode and PQL electrode after cycles were characterized by scanning electron microscope (SEM). From the SEM images (Figure <link href="#chem202103088-fig-0001">1</link>), since the pristine PQL presents an irregular blocky structure, the anode surface with PQL as the active substance is uneven and arranges relatively loosely (Figure <link href="#chem202103088-fig-0001">1</link>a–c). Moreover, due to the addition of carbon nanotubes, obvious tubular structures and cavities can be observed in Figure <link href="#chem202103088-fig-0001">1</link>(c), which would increase the anode conductivity, facilitate the ion exchange between the PQL anode and the electrolyte, and is of great significance for the intercalate/deintercalate of Na<sup>+</sup> during the discharge‐charge progress. After circulation, the electrode surface was much smoother, the macroporous structures were disappeared and replaced by many microporous structures (Figure <link href="#chem202103088-fig-0001">1</link>d). By comparing the SEM images of PQL anodes before and after cycles, it is inferred that the morphological changes were caused by the intercalate/deintercalate of Na<sup>+</sup>.<link href="#chem202103088-bib-0057"><sup>[18]</sup></link></p><figure xml:id="chem202103088-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:09476539:media:chem202103088:chem202103088-fig-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/chem202103088-fig-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>SEM images of a) pristine PQL, b, c) uncycled PQL electrodes at the resolution of 10 μm and 1 μm, respectively. d) PQL electrode after cycles.</p></caption></figure><p>To investigate the electrochemical redox changes of PQL anode, cyclic voltammetry (CV) test was carried out with a scan rate of 0.1 mV s<sup>−1</sup> and a voltage range of 0–3 V at 30 °C, the results are shown in Figure <link href="#chem202103088-fig-0002">2</link>. The initial CV curve was highly different from the later cycles, especially in the discharge process. There was a representative reduction peak at about 1.2 V, indicating that the embedding reaction of Na<sup>+</sup> occurs at this voltage. The voltage, dropped sharply from 0.8 V to 0 V, was attributed to the further intercalation of Na<sup>+</sup> in the C<sub>6</sub>‐aromatic ring of PQL. At the same time, the solid electrolyte interfacial (SEI) membrane was formed by the reaction between electrode and electrolyte, leading to the irreversible loss of partial discharge capacity and rapid capacity decay. However, from the second cycle, the CV curves almost overlap, indicating that SIBs with PQL as the anode could have good reversibility and cycle stability. It can be seen in Figure <link href="#chem202103088-fig-0002">2</link>(b) that the initial discharge curve is different from others because of an obvious plateau at about 1.2 V and a significant slope increase at about 0.8 V, which is the same as the CV curves. At the current density of 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, the initial discharge capacity of PQL SIBs was 1825 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, corresponding to the theoretical insertion reaction of fourteen Na<sup>+</sup> (Figure <link href="#chem202103088-fig-0002">2</link>c). However, due to the formation of SEI, it showed an obvious irreversible capacity decay, and the initial charge capacity was only 182 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>. All capacities of PQL anode provided here have already deducted the capacity of CNTs.</p><figure xml:id="chem202103088-fig-0002"><label>2</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:09476539:media:chem202103088:chem202103088-fig-0002"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/chem202103088-fig-0002-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a) CV curves of PQL‐SIBs at the scanning rate of 0.1 mV s<sup>−1</sup>, b) galvanostatic discharge‐charge curve of PQL‐SIBs in the initial cycle. c) Theoretical electrochemical mechanism of PQL anode in SIBs.</p></caption></figure><p>The galvanostatic discharge‐charge curves and cyclic performance of as‐assembled SIBs were conducted at the current density of 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup> within the voltage range of 0–3 V. The electrochemical performances are exhibited in Figure <link href="#chem202103088-fig-0003">3</link>. Different from most previously reported organic electrodes, the discharge specific capacity of PQL anode firstly decreased from 312 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> (2<sup>nd</sup> cycle) to 256 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> (5<sup>th</sup> cycle). However, the capacity has increased to a certain extent in the subsequent cycles, which is consistent with the performance of PQL in LIBs.<link href="#chem202103088-bib-0055"><sup>[16]</sup></link> After 400 cycles, the capacity was maintained at 317 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#chem202103088-fig-0003">3</link>b). The great increase in capacity can be attributed to the occurrence of the activation process.<link href="#chem202103088-bib-0058"><sup>[19]</sup></link> In this process, PQL exposed more redox‐active sites to participate in the discharge‐charge cycles. At the same time, the interfacial sodium storage of the PQL anode was gradually carried out due to the existence of the SEI. Under different current densities, it still showed stable reversible capacity and good rate capability (Figure <link href="#chem202103088-fig-0003">3</link>c and d). As the current densities gradually increased to 100, 200, 500, 1000, 1500 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, the discharge capacities of the 5<sup>th</sup> cycle were 300, 261, 231, 202 and 183 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> respectively, which is superior to most reported organic anode materials used in SIBs (Table <link href="#chem202103088-tbl-0001">1</link>). When it returned to 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, the discharge capacity increased and stabled at 308 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>. Electronic conductivity is one of the key factors that affect the specific capacity and rate capability of rechargeable batteries. The large π‐conjugated structure and multiring aromatic system are beneficial for electrons to rapidly transfer during the discharge‐charge process, effectively enhance the conductivity of PQL anode and enable PQL to exhibit good rate capability.</p><figure xml:id="chem202103088-fig-0003"><label>3</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" rechargeable batteries. Since PQL anode shows good electrochemical stability in SIBs, it is supposed to have poor solubility in the EC/DMC electrolyte. To study the dissolution degree of PQL anode, it was immersed in 2 mL 1 M NaClO<sub>4</sub> EC/DMC (v:v=1 : 1) with 5 % FEC electrolyte, while only 80 μL electrolyte was used to assemble per coin‐type SIB. The dissolution comparison experiment (Figure <link href="#chem202103088-fig-0004">4</link>) was performed by recording the color changes of electrolytes on different days. After immersed PQL anode, the color of the electrolytes was the same as that of the blank electrolyte, indicating that the PQL anode possesses good stability and is insoluble in EC/DMC electrolyte. Taking DMC as the baseline, blank electrolyte and electrolytes soaked for different days were measured by UV‐vis spectrophotometer. The same absorbances provide evidence that active substance PQL was insoluble in EC/DMC electrolyte, and could effectively improve the cycle stability when used as anode in SIBs.</p><figure xml:id="chem202103088-fig-0004"><label>4</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:09476539:media:chem202103088:chem202103088-fig-0004"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/chem202103088-fig-0004-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>The visual‐dissolution experiment (insert) for PQL anode by immersed in 1 M NaClO<sub>4</sub> EC/DMC with 5 % FEC electrolyte and the corresponding UV‐vis spectra of electrolytes after soaking for different days.</p></caption></figure><p>The charge transfer resistance of PQL anode circulated in SIBs was reflected by electrochemical impedance spectra (EIS) with the frequency from 10<sup>−2</sup> Hz to 10<sup>5</sup> Hz. From Figure <link href="#chem202103088-fig-0005">5</link>, compared with the original electrode (390 Ω), the impedance of the PQL anode has been significantly increased around 955 Ω after 400 cycles. Furthermore, as the cycle number increased, the impedance value of PQL‐SIB tends to rise first and then fall down. This result is attributed to the activation process of PQL in the electrochemical reaction. The larger the impedance value is, the more unfavorable the electrode reaction will be, and the fast the battery capacity attenuates. However, after the activation completed, the specific capacity of PQL SIBs would increase to a certain extent, which coincides with its cycling process again.</p><figure xml:id="chem202103088-fig-0005"><label>5</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:09476539:media:chem202103088:chem202103088-fig-0005"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/chem202103088-fig-0005-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>EIS and equivalent circuit diagram of PQL‐SIB at different cycles.</p></caption></figure></section><section xml:id="chem202103088-sec-0003" numbered="no"><title type="main">Conclusion</title><p>Overall consideration, polyazaacene analogue PQL not only has excellent lithium storage performance but also performs great electrochemical properties in SIBs. When used as the anode in SIBs, PQL displayed a high initial discharge capacity of 1825 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at the current density of 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. Due to the formation of SEI, the specific capacity was affected irreversibly, resulting in a capacity decay from the second cycle. However, thanks to the completion of the activation process, more active sites are involved in the electrochemical reaction, making the capacity of PQL SIBs higher than 317 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> during the whole cycle. The stable electrochemical performance of polyazaacene analogue PQL in SIBs verifies its general applicability to metal‐ion batteries and proves the significance for future exploring organic polymer electrodes.</p></section><section xml:id="chem202103088-sec-0004" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>This article acknowledges the financial support of the National Natural Science Foundation of China (Nos. 21875206, 21403187) and the Natural Science Foundation of Hebei Province (No. B2019203487).
