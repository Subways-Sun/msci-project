Information</caption></supportingInfoItem></supportingInformation><abstractGroup><abstract type="main" xml:lang="en"><title type="main">Abstract</title><p>Antimony is an attractive anode material for sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) owing to its high theoretical capacity and appropriate sodiation potential. However, its practical application is severely impeded by its poor cycling stability caused by dramatic volumetric variations during sodium uptake and release processes. Here, to circumvent this obstacle, Sb@C@TiO<sub>2</sub> triple‐shell nanoboxes (TSNBs) are synthesized through a template‐engaged galvanic replacement approach. The TSNB structure consists of an inner Sb hollow nanobox protected by a conductive carbon middle shell and a TiO<sub>2</sub>‐nanosheet‐constructed outer shell. This structure offers dual protection to the inner Sb and enough room to accommodate volume expansion, thus promoting the structural integrity of the electrode and the formation of a stable solid–electrolyte interface film. Benefiting from the rational structural design and synergistic effects of Sb, carbon, and TiO<sub>2</sub>, the Sb@C@TiO<sub>2</sub> electrode exhibits superior rate performance (212 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 10 A g<sup>−1</sup>) and outstanding long‐term cycling stability (193 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 1 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 4000 cycles). Moreover, a full cell assembled with a configuration of Sb@C@TiO<sub>2</sub>//Na<sub>3</sub>(VOPO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>F displays a high output voltage of 2.8 V and a high energy density of 179 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup>, revealing the great promise of Sb@C@TiO<sub>2</sub> TSNBs as the electrode in SIBs.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="smll202001976-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>Sb@C@TiO<sub>2</sub> triple‐shell nanoboxes (TSNBs) composed of an inner Sb hollow nanobox protected by a conductive carbon middle shell and a TiO<sub>2</sub>‐nanosheet‐constructed outer shell are synthesized through a template‐engaged galvanic replacement approach. xml:id="smll202001976-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="smll202001976-sec-0010"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>As intermittent renewable energy technologies rapidly develop, large‐scale electrochemical energy storage (LS‐EES) technologies are urgently needed to enhance the grid resiliency and maintain a stable power supply.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202001976-bib-0001"><sup>1</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In addition, the rise of wearable electronics is demanding flexible energy storage (FES) technologies.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202001976-bib-0002"><sup>2</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) are considered a promising candidate for LS‐EES and FES in view of the natural abundance, low cost, and environmental benignity of sodium resources.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202001976-bib-0002 #smll202001976-bib-0003"><sup>2,3</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Although SIBs share a similar chemical storage mechanism with lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs), finding suitable electrode materials that can effectively host Na<sup>+</sup> ions, which are larger than Li<sup>+</sup> ions, and are stable during cycling remains a considerable challenge.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202001976-bib-0004"><sup>4</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Over the past decade, extensive efforts have been devoted to developing appropriate anode materials with a high rate capability and good cycling stability for SIBs, such as carbonaceous materials,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202001976-bib-0005 #smll202001976-bib-0006 #smll202001976-bib-0007"><sup>5–7</sup></link><sup>]</sup> conversion‐type materials (e.g., metal oxides,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202001976-bib-0008"><sup>8</sup></link><sup>]</sup> chalcogenides,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202001976-bib-0009 #smll202001976-bib-0010 #smll202001976-bib-0011 #smll202001976-bib-0012 #smll202001976-bib-0013"><sup>9–13</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and phosphides<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202001976-bib-0014 #smll202001976-bib-0015"><sup>14,15</sup></link><sup>]</sup>), intercalation compounds,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202001976-bib-0016 #smll202001976-bib-0017 #smll202001976-bib-0018 #smll202001976-bib-0019 #smll202001976-bib-0020 #smll202001976-bib-0021"><sup>16–21</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and alloy‐type materials (e.g., Sb,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202001976-bib-0022 #smll202001976-bib-0023 #smll202001976-bib-0024 #smll202001976-bib-0025 #smll202001976-bib-0026 #smll202001976-bib-0027 #smll202001976-bib-0028"><sup>22–28</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Bi,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202001976-bib-0029 #smll202001976-bib-0030 #smll202001976-bib-0031 #smll202001976-bib-0032"><sup>29–32</sup></link><sup>]</sup> P,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202001976-bib-0033 #smll202001976-bib-0034 #smll202001976-bib-0035"><sup>33–35</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and Sn<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202001976-bib-0036 #smll202001976-bib-0037"><sup>36,37</sup></link><sup>]</sup>). Among them, metallic Sb is regarded as one of the most promising anode materials for SIBs because of its high theoretical capacity of around 660 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> (corresponding to Na<sub>3</sub>Sb) and appropriate redox potential (0.5–0.8 V vs Na/Na<sup>+</sup>).<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202001976-bib-0038"><sup>38</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Nevertheless, the large volume variations (about 290%) during sodiation/desodiation process is the “Achilles’ heel” of Sb‐based anode materials, as these variations cause the continuous pulverization of the active material, damage the solid‐electrolyte interface (SEI), and degrade the electric contact between Sb and the current collector. All of these effects rapidly deteriorate the capacity and cyclability of the electrode.</p><p>To address these issues, many strategies have been proposed, such as designing new nanostructures (e.g., Sb nanotubes,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202001976-bib-0039"><sup>39</sup></link><sup>]</sup> antimonene,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202001976-bib-0040 #smll202001976-bib-0041"><sup>40,41</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and nanoporous Sb<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202001976-bib-0042 #smll202001976-bib-0043 #smll202001976-bib-0044"><sup>42–44</sup></link><sup>]</sup>), forming Sb‐based intermetallics (e.g., SbSn,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202001976-bib-0045 #smll202001976-bib-0046 #smll202001976-bib-0047 #smll202001976-bib-0048"><sup>45–48</sup></link><sup>]</sup> SbNi,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202001976-bib-0049 #smll202001976-bib-0050"><sup>49,50</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and SbBi<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202001976-bib-0051 #smll202001976-bib-0052"><sup>51,52</sup></link><sup>]</sup>) and constructing protective layers (e.g., TiO<sub>2</sub>,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202001976-bib-0053 #smll202001976-bib-0054 #smll202001976-bib-0055"><sup>53–55</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Co(OH)<sub>2</sub>,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202001976-bib-0056"><sup>56</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and carbon<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202001976-bib-0057 #smll202001976-bib-0058 #smll202001976-bib-0059 #smll202001976-bib-0060 #smll202001976-bib-0061 #smll202001976-bib-0062 #smll202001976-bib-0063 #smll202001976-bib-0064 #smll202001976-bib-0065 #smll202001976-bib-0066 #smll202001976-bib-0067 #smll202001976-bib-0068 #smll202001976-bib-0069"><sup>57–69</sup></link><sup>]</sup>). Among these approaches, constructing a protective layer has been regarded as the most effective way to solve the volume expansion problems. In particular, TiO<sub>2</sub> is a promising choice as the protective layer for constructing high‐performance Sb‐based anodes owing to its very good structural stability during sodiation/desodiation.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202001976-bib-0020"><sup>20</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Moreover, TiO<sub>2</sub>, as a typical extrinsic pseudocapacitive material,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202001976-bib-0070 #smll202001976-bib-0071 #smll202001976-bib-0072"><sup>70–72</sup></link><sup>]</sup> can exhibit a large proportion of pseudocapacitive charge storage when it is sized down to the nanoscale,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202001976-bib-0073"><sup>73</sup></link><sup>]</sup> which is favorable for achieving a high rate capability. For instance, Wang et al. synthesized double‐walled Sb@TiO<sub>2−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i> nanotubes and demonstrated that introducing an TiO<sub>2−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i> interface layer could increase the cycling stability and rate capability of SIBs.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202001976-bib-0054"><sup>54</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Li et al. fabricated Sb@TiO<sub>2−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i> nanoplates via a template‐assisted method, and the Sb@TiO<sub>2−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i> nanoplates showed much better rate performance and cycling stability relative to those of the pure Sb electrode. Despite these exciting advances, the low intrinsic electrical conductivity of the TiO<sub>2</sub> protective layer hinders further improvements in the cell performance.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202001976-bib-0021 #smll202001976-bib-0074"><sup>21,74</sup></link><sup>]</sup> On the other hand, constructing a carbon protective layer can also effectively alleviate the volume expansion of Sb. Many carbon‐shell‐protected Sb‐based materials (e.g., Sb@C yolk–shell spheres,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202001976-bib-0062 #smll202001976-bib-0063 #smll202001976-bib-0064"><sup>62–64</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Sb nanorods/nanoparticles encapsulated in carbon NTs/nanofibers,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202001976-bib-0065 #smll202001976-bib-0066 #smll202001976-bib-0067 #smll202001976-bib-0068"><sup>65–68</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and Sb@C coaxial nanotubes<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202001976-bib-0069"><sup>69</sup></link><sup>]</sup>) have been developed, which exhibited much better electrochemical performance than pure Sb electrode. Nevertheless, the carbon shells of the above Sb‐based materials are composed of amorphous carbon with low mechanical strength, which could crack under high Sb loading,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202001976-bib-0075"><sup>75</sup></link><sup>]</sup> thus decreasing the structural stability of the composite materials. The combination of the TiO<sub>2</sub> and carbon protective layers can provide dual protection, which is believed to be an effective way to resolve the above drawbacks and to synergistically combine the advantages of both materials.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202001976-bib-0076 #smll202001976-bib-0077"><sup>76,77</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>In addition, constructing a hollow structure with functional thin shells, a high surface area, and internal void spaces could also promote the sodium storage performance of electrode materials.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202001976-bib-0078"><sup>78</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The hollow structure offers the advantages of a short length for charge transfer, a large contact area, and enough room to alleviate the strain induced by volume expansion.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202001976-bib-0079"><sup>79</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Recent studies have revealed that multi‐shelled hollow structures exhibit a much higher volumetric energy density and better durability than conventional simple hollow structures, owing to the optimized utilization of the inner voids, the increased number of electrochemical reaction sites, and the increased structural integrity of the active material.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202001976-bib-0080 #smll202001976-bib-0081 #smll202001976-bib-0082 #smll202001976-bib-0083 #smll202001976-bib-0084 #smll202001976-bib-0085"><sup>80–85</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Therefore, fabricating an Sb‐based multi‐shelled hollow structure with both TiO<sub>2</sub> and carbon protective shells can be highly anticipated to enhance electrochemical performance.</p><p>Herein, we demonstrate the rational design and construction of Sb@C@TiO<sub>2</sub> triple‐shell nanoboxes (TSNBs) with a TiO<sub>2</sub>‐nanosheet (NS)‐constructed outer shell and a carbon middle shell supported on an inner Sb nanobox (NB) for efficient sodium storage. Such a design has many advantages: i) the inner hollow Sb box offers enough room to accommodate volume expansion during cycling; ii) the middle carbon layer can not only increase the conductivity of the composites but also prevent direct contact between the electrolyte and the inner Sb NB, which is conducive to forming a stable SEI film; iii) the outer TiO<sub>2</sub> NS layer can increase the mechanical strength of the multi‐shell hollow NBs and facilitate fast ion transport. As expected, the as‐synthesized Sb@C@TiO<sub>2</sub> TSNBs exhibita superior rate capability (212 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 10 A g<sup>−1</sup>) and an ultrastable long‐term cycling performance (193 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 1 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 4000 cycles) in half cells. Moreover, electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS), galvanostatic intermittent titration technique (GITT), and cyclic voltammetry (CV) curves are performed to reveal the reaction kinetics and charge storage mechanism of the Sb@C@TiO<sub>2</sub> electrode upon cycling. Finally, by pairing Sb@C@TiO<sub>2</sub> electrodes with homemade Na<sub>3</sub>(VOPO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>F cathodes, prototype full cells are assembled, which provide a high energy density of 179 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup> and a high working voltage of 2.8 V. The above‐mentioned electrochemical performance makes the Sb@C@TiO<sub>2</sub> electrode very attractive for future practical applications.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202001976-sec-0020"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><section xml:id="smll202001976-sec-0030"><title type="main">Synthesis and Characterization</title><p>The synthesis procedure of the Sb@C@TiO<sub>2</sub> TSNBs is schematically illustrated in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll202001976-fig-0001"><b>1</b></link>. First, Cu<sub>2</sub>O nanocubes (NCs) were synthesized through a previously reported approach<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202001976-bib-0086"><sup>86</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and used as sacrificial templates for the subsequent synthetic processes. The crystalline structures of the samples were examined via powder X‐ray diffraction (XRD, Figure S1, Supporting Information), and all the diffraction peaks could be assigned to crystalline Cu<sub>2</sub>O (JSPDS no. 78‐2076). Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) image shows that the as‐prepared Cu<sub>2</sub>O NCs have a smooth surface and are quite uniform, with particle sizes ranging from 400–500 nm (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll202001976-fig-0002"><b>2</b>a</link>). Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) images demonstrate the solid nature of the Cu<sub>2</sub>O NCs. The as‐obtained Cu<sub>2</sub>O NCs were coated with a carbon‐rich polysaccharide (PS) layer through a hydrothermal treatment using glucose as a carbon source (step [i] in Figure <link href="#smll202001976-fig-0001">1</link>). The cubic morphology is well retained after this carbon‐coating process (Figure <link href="#smll202001976-fig-0002">2b</link>), and a PS layer with an average thickness of approximately 16 nm clearly appears on the surface of the NC (Figure <link href="#smll202001976-fig-0002">2f,j</link>; Figure S2, Supporting Information). The XRD pattern of the as‐derived sample can be indexed to Cu<sub>2</sub>O and metallic Cu (Figure S1, Supporting Information), suggesting that Cu<sub>2</sub>O was partially reduced to metallic Cu during the hydrothermal process. Therefore, the above sample is denoted by Cu<sub>2</sub>O&amp;Cu@PS NCs. After annealing at 500 °C under an H<sub>2</sub>/Ar (10/90) atmosphere, the Cu<sub>2</sub>O&amp;Cu@PS NCs were converted into Cu@C NBs (step [ii] in Figure <link href="#smll202001976-fig-0001">1</link>). The XRD analysis confirms that Cu<sub>2</sub>O was fully reduced to metallic Cu (Figure S1, Supporting Information), and the cubic structure was maintained after the annealing treatment (Figure <link href="#smll202001976-fig-0002">2c</link>). However, the interior of the cube became hollow (Figure <link href="#smll202001976-fig-0002">2g,k</link>), which can be attributed to the large volume shrinkage during the reduction of Cu<sub>2</sub>O to metallic Cu. Additionally, the outer carbon layer (about 11 nm) of the Cu@C NBs became slightly thinner than the PS layer of the Cu<sub>2</sub>O&amp;Cu@PS NCs (Figure <link href="#smll202001976-fig-0002">2k</link>; Figure S3, Supporting Information) owing to the volume contraction of PS during the carbonization process. Moreover, the existence of the carbon layer was further confirmed by the Raman spectrum (Figure S4, Supporting Information). The Cu@C@TiO<sub>2</sub> NBs were prepared by a solvothermal approach using TiCl<sub>3</sub> as a precursor and Cu@C NBs as templates (step [iii] in Figure <link href="#smll202001976-fig-0001">1</link>). A typical SEM image (Figure <link href="#smll202001976-fig-0002">2d</link>) of the as‐obtained sample shows that the cubic morphology was well preserved after the solvothermal treatment, but the NC surface became rough. When evaluated as anode materials for SIBs, the Sb@C@TiO<sub>2</sub> electrode presents a high capacity of 212 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at a high current density of 10 A g<sup>−1</sup> and ultrastable long‐term cycling performance at 1 A g<sup>−1</sup> for over 4000 cycles with a retained capacity of 193 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> in half cells. The outstanding electrochemical performance of the Sb@C@TiO<sub>2</sub> NBs can be attributed to the distinctive triple‐shell hollow structure, which not only effectively accommodates the mechanical strain during repeated cycling, thus promoting the structural integrity of the electrode, but also facilitates fast ion and electron transport. Therefore, this study not only develops a new high‐performance anode material for SIBs but also proposes a dual‐protection strategy to stabilize alloy‐type anode materials, which may pave the way for developing high‐performance anode materials for SIBs.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202001976-sec-0080"><title type="main">Experimental Section</title><section xml:id="smll202001976-sec-0090" numbered="no"><section xml:id="smll202001976-sec-0100" numbered="no"><section xml:id="smll202001976-sec-0110" numbered="no"><title type="main">Synthesis of Cu<sub>2</sub>O NCs</title><p>Cu<sub>2</sub>O NCs were synthesized via a previously reported method. Typically, sodium citrate dihydrate (0.5 mmol) and copper(II) sulfate pentahydrate (1.5 mmol) were dissolved in deionized water (80 mL). Then, 1.25 <sc>m</sc> sodium hydroxide solution (20 mL) was added into the above solution under stirring. After stirring for 15 min, 0.03 <sc>m</sc> ascorbic acid (50 mL) was added slowly into this mixture and then kept at room temperature for 1 h under continuous stirring. Afterward, the products were collected by centrifugation and washed with ethanol and water three times. Finally, the obtained precipitate was dried under vacuum for 6 h.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202001976-sec-0120" numbered="no"><title type="main">Synthesis of Cu<sub>2</sub>O&amp;Cu@PS NCs</title><p>In a typical synthesis, glucose (100 mg) was dissolved in deionized water (50 mL). Then, Cu<sub>2</sub>O NCs (50 mg) were dispersed in this solution under ultrasonication. The obtained suspension was transferred into a Teflon‐lined autoclave and held at 190 °C for 10 h. After naturally cooling down to room temperature, the precipitate was washed with ethanol and water three times and dried under vacuum for 6 h.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202001976-sec-0130" numbered="no"><title type="main">Synthesis of Cu@C NBs</title><p>In a typical synthesis, Cu<sub>2</sub>O&amp;Cu@PS NCs (50 mg) were evenly placed in a quartz boat and annealed at 500 °C for 3 h in a tube furnace, which was heated at a ramp rate of 5 °C min<sup>−1</sup> under an H<sub>2</sub>/Ar (10/90) atmosphere. The obtained powder was stored in an Ar‐filled glovebox for further use.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202001976-sec-0140" numbered="no"><title type="main">Synthesis of Cu@C@TiO<sub>2</sub> NBs</title><p>In a typical synthesis, titanium(III) trichloride solution (0.5 mL) and deionized water (1 mL) were sequentially added to ethylene glycol (30 mL) and stirred for 25 min. Then, Cu@C NBs (30 mg) were dispersed in this solution via ultrasonication to form a homogenous suspension, which was transferred into a Teflon‐lined autoclave and maintained at 150 °C for 6 h. After cooling to room temperature, the upper white precipitate was discarded, and the reddish precipitate was collected and washed with ethanol and water three times. The product was dried under vacuum for 6 h and annealed at 500 °C for 2 h under an H<sub>2</sub>/Ar (10/90) atmosphere. The obtained powder was stored in an Ar‐filled glovebox for further use.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202001976-sec-0150" numbered="no"><title type="main">Synthesis of Sb@C@TiO<sub>2</sub> TSNBs</title><p>Sb@C@TiO<sub>2</sub> NBs were synthesized through a galvanic replacement reaction. In detail, antimony(III) chloride (500 mg) and tetrabutylammonium tetrafluoroborate (7.2 g) were dissolved in dimethyl sulfoxide (220 mL). Then, Cu@C@TiO<sub>2</sub> NBs (70 mg) were added into this solution and stirred at 300 rpm for 3 h. The obtained mixture was aged for 96 h at room temperature. Finally, the precipitate was washed with ethanol three times and dried under vacuum for 6 h.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202001976-sec-0160" numbered="no"><title type="main">Synthesis of Sb@C NBs</title><p>Sb@C NBs were prepared under conditions similar to those of Sb@C@TiO<sub>2</sub> NBs except that the Cu@C@TiO<sub>2</sub> NBs were replaced by Cu@C NBs.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202001976-sec-0170" numbered="no"><title type="main">Synthesis of Na<sub>3</sub>(VOPO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>F</title><p>Na<sub>3</sub>(VOPO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>F was synthesized via a previously reported solvothermal method.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202001976-bib-0098"><sup>98</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Briefly, vanadyl acetylacetonate (5 mmol), phosphoric acid (7.6 mmol), and sodium fluoride (8.35 mmol) were dissolved into a mixed solvent containing ethanol (15 mL) and acetone (5 mL) via sonication. Then, the resulting mixture was sealed in a Teflon‐lined stainless‐steel autoclave and aged at 120 °C in a preheated oven for 10 h. After cooling to room temperature, the light‐green precipitate was collected by centrifugation. The obtained product was washed with ethanol and water several times and dried at 60 °C under vacuum.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202001976-sec-0180" numbered="no"><title type="main">Electrochemical Measurements</title><p>The electrochemical performance of the samples (Sb@C@TiO<sub>2</sub> TSNBs, Sb@C NBs, and TiO<sub>2</sub> NSs) was evaluated in CR2032 coin cells. Before cell assembly, a homogenous slurry was prepared by blending active material, carbon black, and sodium carboxymethyl cellulose with a mass ratio of 7:2:1. Then, the slurry was spread on a copper foil and dried in an oven at 50 °C. Afterward, the foil was roll pressed by a calendrer machine and then punched into discs with a diameter of 12 mm. The obtained discs were further dried under vacuum at 60 °C for 12 h and stored in an Ar‐filled glove box (H<sub>2</sub>O &lt; 0.1 ppm, O<sub>2</sub> &lt; 0.1 ppm) before use. The typical mass loading of active material in a disc was approximately 1 mg cm<sup>−2</sup>. The compacted density of the Sb@C@TiO<sub>2</sub> and Sb@C electrode was estimated to be 0.87 and 0.80 g cm<sup>−3</sup>, respectively. The coin cells were assembled in the Ar‐filled glove box using the pre‐prepared discs as the working electrodes, Na foils as the counter electrodes, glass fibers (Whatman) as the separators, and a solution of 1 <sc>m</sc> NaClO<sub>4</sub> dissolved in a mixture of ethyl carbonate, dimethyl carbonate, and fluoroethylene (45:45:10 by volume) as the electrolyte. As for the full cell, a precycled Sb@C@TiO<sub>2</sub> electrode was used as the anode, and a homemade Na<sub>3</sub>(VOPO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>F electrode was used as the cathode. The Na<sub>3</sub>(VOPO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>F cathode was prepared under procedures almost identical to those of the Sb@C@TiO<sub>2</sub> electrode, except that the copper foil was replaced by aluminum foil (discs with a diameter of 16 mm). The mass loading of Na<sub>3</sub>(VOPO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>F cathode was approximately 3.1 mg cm<sup>−2</sup>. The mass ratio of the anode to cathode was 1:5.5. The average thicknesses of the Sb@C@TiO<sub>2</sub> anode and Na<sub>3</sub>(VOPO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>F cathode were 11.7 and 36.7 µm, respectively (Figure S23, Supporting Information). The galvanostatic charge–discharge test was conducted on an LAND CT3001A battery tester. Cyclic voltammetry and electrochemical impedance spectroscopy were performed on an Autolab 302N electrochemical workstation. The GITT results were acquired on the same battery tester after 50 activation cycles with a pulse current of 1 A g<sup>−1</sup> for 1 min and rest intervals of 29 min.</p></section></section></section></section><section xml:id="smll202001976-sec-0190" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>M.K. and Y.L. contributed equally to this work.
