Information</caption></supportingInfoItem></supportingInformation><abstractGroup><abstract type="main" xml:lang="en"><title type="main">Abstract</title><p>The natural insulating property and notorious pulverization of volume variation‐induced materials during cycling pares the electrochemical activity of red phosphorous (RP) for lithium/sodium‐ion batteries (LIBs/SIBs). To work out these issues, a tailored trimodal porous carbon support comprising highly ordered macropores and micro‐mesoporous walls embedded with copper (Cu) nanoclusters (Cu‐OMC) is proposed to confine RP. The construction of highly conductive copper–carbon wall facilitates fast electrons and ions transportation, while the interconnected and ordered porous structure not only creates enough space to resist the expansion effect of RP but also minimizes the ion diffusion length and enhances ion accessibility (the ion migration coefficient is ten times that of disordered porous carbon). Consequently, the resulting Cu‐OMC@RP anode delivers a high reversible capacity (2498.7 mAh g<sup>–1</sup> at 0.3 C for LIBs; 2454.2 mAh g<sup>–1</sup> at 0.1 C for SIBs), superb rate properties (824.7 mAh g<sup>–1</sup> at 10 C for LIBs; 774.2 mAh g<sup>–1</sup> at 5 C for SIBs), and outstanding cycling stability (an ultralow decay rate of 0.057% per cycle after 1000 cycles at 10 C for LIBs and 0.048% per cycle at 5 C over 500 cycles for SIBs).</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="smll202106930-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>A highly ordered porous carbon framework embedded with copper nanoclusters is tailored, and serves as the red phosphorus immobilizer for superior lithium and sodium storage. The unique structure not only facilitates the rapid lithium/sodium ions diffusion and electron transport but also effectively confines phosphorus and alleviates the volume expansion during cycling.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll202106930:smll202106930-gra-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll202106930-gra-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body xml:id="smll202106930-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="smll202106930-sec-0010"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Red phosphorus (RP), one of the most perspective anodes for high‐capacity lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs)/sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs), delivers enormous theoretical capacities (2596 mAh g<sup>–1</sup>), ideal operating potential (≈0.7 V vs Li<sup>+</sup>/Li and ≈0.4 V vs Na<sup>+</sup>/Na), and earth‐abundance.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202106930-bib-0001 #smll202106930-bib-0002 #smll202106930-bib-0003 #smll202106930-bib-0004 #smll202106930-bib-0005 #smll202106930-bib-0006 #smll202106930-bib-0007"><sup>1–7</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Unfortunately, the practical utilization of RP is hampered by the two major deficiencies: (i) low intrinsic electrical conductivity (1 × 10<sup>–14</sup> S cm<sup>–1</sup>), which makes electrochemical redox reactions become difficult, limiting the high‐rate capability; and (ii) unconstrained volume expansion during Li<sup>+</sup>/Na<sup>+</sup> insertion and extraction process, which triggers the irreversible mastication of active materials, impairing the long‐cycling stability.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202106930-bib-0008 #smll202106930-bib-0009 #smll202106930-bib-0010 #smll202106930-bib-0011 #smll202106930-bib-0012 #smll202106930-bib-0013"><sup>8–13</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>Reasonable structural design and material engineering are the keys to solving the inherent obstacles of RP. However, most porous carbon families only possess a single pore‐type structure,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202106930-bib-0014 #smll202106930-bib-0015 #smll202106930-bib-0016 #smll202106930-bib-0017 #smll202106930-bib-0018"><sup>14–18</sup></link><sup>]</sup> which makes it difficult to meet the demands due to the blockage of the simplex porous channels caused by the infiltration of RP and unsatisfied RP loading, leading to poor cycling properties. Micro‐mesoporous nanostructure is beneficial to capture RP and increase the effective contact area.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202106930-bib-0019 #smll202106930-bib-0020 #smll202106930-bib-0021"><sup>19–21</sup></link><sup>]</sup> But, the uncontrollable and disordered distribution of pores causes the heterogeneous size and agglomeration of RP, hindering the ion diffusion, and low pore volume cannot alleviate the drastic volume changes. Thus, constructing interconnected and ordered porous carbon host to exclusively confine RP and affording adequate space to clamp the volume expansion, is an effective strategy to respond to critical requirements for designing ideal carbon scaffolds as well as infiltration technology.</p><p>Another hurdle that impedes the high‐rate capacity of RP/C composites is that the porous carbon usually provides inferior electrical conductivity because of the sp<sup>3–</sup> hybridized CC bonding and amorphous state, leading to sluggish solid‐state diffusion processes.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202106930-bib-0021"><sup>21</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The most popular tactic is the engineering of the carbonaceous nanomaterials through iodine (I), boron(B), and nitrogen (N) doping, which could improve the reactivity and conductivity of carbon by creating heteroatoms defects.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202106930-bib-0022 #smll202106930-bib-0023 #smll202106930-bib-0024"><sup>22–24</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Non‐metallic heteroatom doping may increase the active sites in the carbon skeleton but may also create an intrinsic hurdle against charge transfer. In contrast, doping metal into the carbon supports can greatly improve its electrical conductivity as well as generate more active sites. Among them, Cu, which naturally owns the second‐highest conductivity,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202106930-bib-0025"><sup>25</sup></link><sup>]</sup> only 6% lower than the Ag, is a good choice for non‐noble metal doping. Nevertheless, because metal tends to be located on the external surface of carbon in the form of aggregated particles, ions must pass through an intricate island‐like structure byway to react with the guest RP during the discharge process, which aggravates the ionic and electrical resistance inevitably. Therefore, it is wise to reduce the particle size of Cu and nanoconfine it into the inside carbon layer of the porous matrix.</p><p>Aiming to simultaneously utilize the excellent conductivity of Cu and the high porosity of porous carbon, an ideal RP immobilizer with dispersed Cu nanoclusters anchored to a trimodal porous carbon framework with periodic macropores (denoted as Cu‐OMC) came into being. In the trimodal‐porous structure, highly ordered macropores can facilitate reliable RP accommodation and promote long‐range ions migration, while the wall building of micro‐mesopores can enlarge internal contact area and enhance local accessibility. Especially, the in situ introduction of Cu clusters on the carbon wall significantly ameliorates the conductivity of the entire electrode, ensuring rapid electron transfer and efficient mass transfer. Meanwhile, the intrinsically coupled trimodal‐porous carbon support displays outstanding structural stability and compatibility, providing enough space for the expansion and exclusive restraint of RP. As a consequence, the Cu‐OMC@RP anode exhibits rapid, stable, and reversible storage for both Li<sup>+</sup> and Na<sup>+</sup> ions at high current rates.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202106930-sec-0020"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p><b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll202106930-fig-0001"><b>1</b></link> schematically illustrates the synthetic strategy of Cu‐OMC@RP composites. First, highly ordered SiO<sub>2</sub> templates (Figure <link href="#smll202106930-supitem-0001">S1a,b</link>, Supporting Information) were synthesized by the vertical self‐assembly (Figure <link href="#smll202106930-supitem-0001">S2</link>, Supporting Information). Then, the SiO<sub>2</sub> template was impregnated in a zeolitic imidazolate framework solution with a certain concentration of Cu<sup>2+</sup> and evaporated under vacuum to induce the formation of Cu‐ZIF‐8 crystalline phase. As shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll202106930-fig-0002"><b>2</b>a</link>, the Cu‐ZIF‐8 crystal particles were evenly wrapped on the surface of the silica microsphere and fully infiltrated into the interstitial voids of the SiO<sub>2</sub> template. Under high‐temperature pyrolysis, the Cu‐ZIF‐8 particles were directly carbonized into the porous carbon matrix supported on the SiO<sub>2</sub> template (Figure <link href="#smll202106930-fig-0002">2b</link>). Subsequently, the ordered porous Cu‐OMC framework was obtained by removing the template (Figure <link href="#smll202106930-supitem-0001">S1c,d</link>, Supporting Information). The scanning electron microscopy (SEM) (Figure <link href="#smll202106930-fig-0002">2c</link>) and transmission electron microscopy (TEM) (Figure <link href="#smll202106930-fig-0002">2d</link>; <link href="#smll202106930-supitem-0001">S3a</link>, Supporting Information) images vividly illustrate the Cu‐OMC possesses a periodic macroporous architecture with a pore size of ≈320 nm, indicating the accurate replicating of ordered macropore arrays from the SiO<sub>2</sub> template. The well‐ordered “air spheres” featuring interconnected copper–carbon (CuC) walls create a honeycomb‐like pore structure in 3D. In addition to the visible macropores in Figure <link href="#smll202106930-fig-0002">2d</link>, abundant micro‐mesopores distributing on the CuC networks were also induced by the direct pyrolysis of Cu‐ZIF‐8 (Figure <link href="#smll202106930-supitem-0001">S3b</link>, Supporting Information). Through the high‐resolution TEM (HRTEM) (Figure <link href="#smll202106930-fig-0002">2e</link>), atomically dispersed copper sites with a diameter of 1–6 nm were observed to be anchored on the carbon framework, which indicates that the spatial confinement from the hard template and interconnected carbon walls restrict the further growth and accumulation of copper dots during the thermal reduction process, resulting in the copper being encapsulated in the interconnected thin carbon matrix in the form of atomic clusters. The HRTEM in Figure <link href="#smll202106930-fig-0002">2f</link> also reflects that the metallic copper clusters were well distributed in the carbon shell with the crystal interplanar spacings being ≈0.185 and 0.213 nm, assignable to the (200) and (111) lattice planes of Cu. High‐angle annular dark‐field scanning‐TEM (HAADF‐STEM) image and the corresponding element mappings in Figure <link href="#smll202106930-fig-0002">2g,h</link> clearly demonstrate the presence and homogeneous distribution of C, N, O, and Cu components on the entire Cu‐OMC.</p><figure xml:id="smll202106930-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll202106930:smll202106930-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll202106930-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Schematic illustration of the synthesis process of Cu‐OMC@RP.</caption></figure><figure xml:id="smll202106930-fig-0002"><label>2</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll202106930:smll202106930-fig-0002" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll202106930-fig-0002-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a,b) SEM images of Cu‐ZIF‐8@SiO<sub>2</sub> before and after carbonization. c) SEM image, d) TEM image, and e,f) HRTEM images; inset in (f) shows corresponding Cu crystal facet intensity profiles. g,h) HAADF‐STEM image and corresponding elemental mappings of Cu‐OMC. i) SEM image of Cu‐OMC@RP composite and corresponding RP mapping image.</caption></figure><p>To investigate the properties of the porous skeleton, Cu‐OMC was used as the host to immobilize RP. As shown in Figure <link href="#smll202106930-fig-0002">2i</link>, the morphology of the Cu‐OMC@RP composite remains through the vaporization–condensation (V–C) treatment, except that the macropores are occupied by RP species. There is no exposed agglomeration of RP residue on the surface of the framework, revealing that most of the active materials have been encapsulated in the interior cavities. The related energy dispersive X‐ray spectroscopy (EDS) mappings of Cu‐OMC@RP composite further suggest the existence of C, N, Cu, and P components, demonstrating the homogeneous coverage by carbon and the uniform distribution of RP in the structure (the inset in Figure <link href="#smll202106930-fig-0002">2i</link>; Figure <link href="#smll202106930-supitem-0001">S4</link>, Supporting Information).</p><p>The crystallization properties of the Cu‐OMC were further obtained. The X‐ray diffraction (XRD) pattern in Figure <link href="#smll202106930-supitem-0001">S5</link>, Supporting Information, confirms the existence of carbon‐based support in this composite; another two peaks located at 43.34° and 50.44° can be assigned to the diffraction for the (111) and (200) lattice planes of metallic Cu (JCPDS 04–0836),<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202106930-bib-0025 #smll202106930-bib-0026"><sup>25,26</sup></link><sup>]</sup> consistent with the HRTEM and HAADF‐STEM results. No featured diffraction signals for Zn element can be distinguished, proving that the metal Zn had been completely volatilized in thermal annealing at 900 °C and the remains were etched away by subsequent HF. The element constitution and chemical valence of the nanocomposites were further investigated via X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS). From the survey spectra in Figure <link href="#smll202106930-supitem-0001">S6a</link>, Supporting Information, Cu‐OMC and OMC exhibited well‐defined C 1s, N 1s, and O 1s peaks at ≈284.6, 398.5 and 531.9 eV, respectively, whereas Cu 2p electrons could be detected at ≈934.84 eV for the former. The related C 1s core‐level XPS spectrum in Figure <link href="#smll202106930-supitem-0001">S6</link>b, Supporting Information, for two samples are highly asymmetric and can be deconvoluted into two dominant peaks for CC (284.7 eV) and CN (285.8 eV) bonds, along with one weaker band for the O‐CO bond at 288.3 eV,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202106930-bib-0027 #smll202106930-bib-0028"><sup>27,28</sup></link><sup>]</sup> which indicates abundant defects and that oxygen‐containing functional groups exist in the porous carbon skeleton. The appearance of CN suggests that the N element has actually been incorporated into the carbon–carbon bond. Generally, the trace amount of O element is believed to be born from the adsorbed O<sub>2</sub> and CO<sub>2</sub> of the air, or oxygen‐containing groups in uncarbonized initial precursors.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202106930-bib-0029"><sup>29</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, in the nearly symmetrical O 1s XPS spectrum of samples (Figure <link href="#smll202106930-supitem-0001">S6c</link>, Supporting Information), there is no characteristic peak at 531.9 eV that can be assigned to the O<sup>2–</sup> in the CuO or ZnO bond, revealing that the presence of O element mainly arises from the adsorbed air. As for the N 1s fine XPS spectrum in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll202106930-fig-0003"><b>3</b>a</link>, three typical peaks were deconvoluted at 398.5, 400.7, and 401.9 eV, which could be ascribed to the formation of pyridinic‐N, pyrrolic‐N, and graphitic‐N species,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202106930-bib-0030"><sup>30</sup></link><sup>]</sup> respectively, again, indicating the N atoms were successfully incorporated into the carbon molecular skeletons. High nitrogen doping could raise the conductivity and reactivity of carbon matrix. Additionally, an extra characteristic peak of the CuN bond (399.5 eV) was found in Cu‐OMC, indicating that the Cu may coordinate with N atoms in the carbon matrix. Further testimony relies on the Cu 2p fine XPS spectroscopy. As shown in Figure <link href="#smll202106930-fig-0003">3b</link>, the two peaks located at 934.8 and 954.5 eV are assigned to the featured 2p<sub>3/2</sub> and 2p<sub>1/2</sub> peaks for Cu<sup>2+</sup> species in CuN, respectively; the other signals at the binding energy of 932.5 and 952.4 eV are accredited to the characteristic 2p<sub>3/2</sub> and 2p<sub>1/2</sub> peaks for Cu<sup>0</sup> species, respectively.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202106930-bib-0031 #smll202106930-bib-0032 #smll202106930-bib-0033"><sup>31–33</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Calculated from the peak areas integral, the ratio of Cu<sup>0</sup> and Cu<sup>2+</sup> species in Cu‐OMC was identified to exceed 3:1, indicating the Cu ions distributed in ZIF‐8 tend to be reduced and agglomerated into the inorganic metallic Cu during the thermal decomposition process instead of being stabilized on the N‐doped dots in the state of ions. The detection of Cu<sup>0</sup> species agrees well with the observation of XRD peaks of metallic copper in Cu‐OMC. Accordingly, the Cu content in the sample can be evaluated to be 13.3 wt% by the thermogravimetric analysis (TGA), in accordance with the EDS mapping data (13.4 wt%) (Figure <link href="#smll202106930-supitem-0001">S7</link>, Supporting Information).</p><figure xml:id="smll202106930-fig-0003"><label>3</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll202106930:smll202106930-fig-0003" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll202106930-fig-0003-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) High‐resolution XPS spectra of N 1s peaks for Cu‐OMC and OMC. b) High‐resolution Cu 2p spectra of Cu‐OMC. c) N<sub>2</sub> adsorption–desorption isotherms and d) corresponding pore size distribution plots for Cu‐OMC, OMC, and Cu‐OMC@RP.</caption></figure><p>The Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) surface area and pore size distributions were then conducted by nitrogen sorption measurements. As shown in Figure <link href="#smll202106930-fig-0003">3c</link>, the prepared Cu‐OMC and OMC samples exhibit the typical type IV isothermal curves, suggesting that both micropores and mesopores exist in hierarchical pores.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202106930-bib-0005"><sup>5</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Among them, the Cu‐OMC gives a higher specific surface area of 1272.03 cm<sup>2</sup> g<sup>–1</sup> and a larger pore volume of 13.03 cm<sup>3</sup> g<sup>–1</sup> than OMC (974.74 cm<sup>2</sup> g<sup>–1</sup>, 12.38 cm<sup>3</sup> g<sup>–1</sup>). The pore size distribution splines in Figure <link href="#smll202106930-fig-0003">3d</link> reveal that Cu‐OMC possesses abundant mesoporosity with a size ranging from 2 to 10 nm, which contributes 774.64 cm<sup>2</sup> g<sup>–1</sup> of the predominant mesoporous surface area to Cu‐OMC, reflecting that the presence of Cu in ZIF‐8 matrix plays a positive effect on the channel characteristics. Attributable to the fact that the Cu ions uniformly distributed in the matrix cannot be completely controlled by the N‐doping sites but are mostly reduced to metallic Cu atoms during the pyrolysis process and gradually aggregate atomic clusters, the sacrifice of some micropores creates more mesopores. After encapsulating RP into the OM structure, the hysteresis loop vanishes and micro‐mesopore size distribution area decreases dramatically, revealing the RP successfully inhabits the mesopores of the ordered porous carbon matrix. The XRD pattern of Cu‐OMC@RP plots the characteristics of the combination of RP and the Cu‐OMC (Figure <link href="#smll202106930-supitem-0001">S8a</link>, Supporting Information). No characteristic peaks of Cu can be observed for the composite, which may be ascribed to the stronger signals of RP that would cover those of Cu. In addition, the intensity of RP in the XRD pattern and Raman spectrum (Figure <link href="#smll202106930-supitem-0001">S8b</link>, Supporting Information) of Cu‐OMC@RP composite are weaker than those of pure RP, further demonstrating that PR is confined into the Cu‐OMC network. The content of RP in the Cu‐OMC@RP composite is calculated to be 40.15 wt% through the TGA curves, which is higher than the other two RP‐carbon composites (Figure <link href="#smll202106930-supitem-0001">S9</link>, Supporting Information).</p><p>The cyclic voltammetry (CV) was measured at a sweep rate of 0.1 mV s<sup>–1</sup> with a voltage range of 0.01–3.0 V to investigate lithiation/delithiation behavior of Cu‐OMC@RP anode, as depicted in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll202106930-fig-0004"><b>4</b>a</link>. In the cathodic polarization scan, one broad irreversible hump at 1.47 V appeared in the initial cycle and disappeared in the subsequent cycles, which may be related to the activation process for the insertion of Li<sup>+</sup> into RP and the formation of solid electrolyte interface (SEI) film.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202106930-bib-0004"><sup>4</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Afterward, three reversible reductive peaks could be detected. For the Cu‐OMC@RP anode, the open and interconnected OM host promotes the penetration of electrolyte deep into the anode structure in any dimension, enlarges effective interface contact of electrode‐electrolyte, shortens ions transport distance and intensifies the Li<sup>+</sup>/Na<sup>+</sup> accessibility, as well as enhancing the host–guest interactions. Specifically, the ultra‐small size and uniform distribution of Cu particles embedded in the carbon layers endow the carbon wall with high conductivity properties, thus weakening the energy barrier of electron transfer. The reinforced ions transport speed and charge transfer dynamics are essential to improve the great rate performance of the anode. This also explains that introducing Cu does contribute a remarkable improvement in the specific capacity, which has the same framework as the OMC@RP anode. During the electrochemical process (P changes to Li<sub>3</sub>P/Na<sub>3</sub>P), the trimodal‐porous carbon buffering framework provides ample space to accommodate the huge volume expansion, contributing to extraordinary cycling stability. Benefiting from the orderly interconnected trimodal‐porous carbon support and the high electrical conductivity of the CuC wall, the unique anode not only facilitates Li<sup>+</sup>/Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion and electron transport but also offers remarkable structural compatibility and stability upon cycling. As expected, the Cu‐OMC@RP exhibits an ultrahigh reversible capacity (2498.7 and 2191.2 mAh g<sup>–1</sup> at 0.3 C for LIBs and SIBs, respectively), and excellent cycle stability over 1500 cycles for LIBs and 950 cycles for SIBs with the same attenuation rate of 0.032% per cycle at 2 C, comparable to the best literature results. This evidence powerfully proves that the Cu‐OMC@RP is a promising anode candidate for high‐performance energy storage. The design of confining conducive Cu in the OM carbon framework opens an avenue for encapsulating active material with poor intrinsic electronic conductivity and tremendous volume expansion.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202106930-sec-0040"><title type="main">Experimental Section</title><section xml:id="smll202106930-sec-0050" numbered="no"><section xml:id="smll202106930-sec-0060" numbered="no"><section xml:id="smll202106930-sec-0070" numbered="no"><title type="main">Preparation of Cu‐OMC</title><p>Monodispersed silica microspheres with 320 nm diameter were suspended in deionized (DI) water (10 wt%) and dispensed into 15 mL vials, then silica spheres were assembled on the vial wall at 50 °C via controlled vertical drying method to obtain the ordered SiO<sub>2</sub> template. The Cu‐ZIF‐8 precursor solution was facilely prepared from metal salts (2.4 g of Zn(NO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub>·6H<sub>2</sub>O, 0.5 g of Cu(NO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub>·3H<sub>2</sub>O), and 6.6 g 2‐methylimidazole being dissolved in the mixture of methanol (60 mL) and <i>N</i>, <i>N</i>‐dimethylformamide (180 mL) solution, followed by vigorous stirring for 3 h at room temperature. The resultant mixture was then made to infiltrate into the SiO<sub>2</sub> template overnight. After evaporating at 90 °C for 12 h, Cu‐ZIF‐8 embedded inside the SiO<sub>2</sub> monolith was formed (denoted as Cu‐ZIF‐8@SiO<sub>2</sub>).</p><p>The as‐prepared Cu‐ZIF‐8@SiO<sub>2</sub> was carbonized at 900 °C under Ar flow for 2 h in a tube furnace to obtain the composite of silicon and carbon doped with copper. Subsequently, the composite was soaked in a hydrofluoric (HF) solution to remove the silica templates and residual metal species. After being collected by centrifugation, washing with DI water, and finally drying in vacuum, the ordered porous carbon doped with Cu (Cu‐OMC) was finally achieved. Besides, the pure OMC without Cu loading and the Cu‐DPC composite without ordered macropores were prepared through the same procedure in the absence of copper salt or silica spheres, respectively.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202106930-sec-0080" numbered="no"><title type="main">Preparation of Cu‐OMC@RP</title><p>The Cu‐OMC@RP composites were prepared by a vaporization–condensation–conversion approach. In brief, the obtained Cu‐OMC and commercial red phosphorus with a molar weight ratio (1:2) were mixed and placed in a sealed quartz tube under vacuum, and then the quartz tube was heated to 550 °C and held for 3 h. After cooling down to 260 °C, the tube was kept at this temperature for 24 h to convert condensed white P to red P, where the cooling rate was 1 °C min<sup>–1</sup>. The sealed quartz tube was opened in the argon‐filled glove box after it naturally cooled down to room temperature. The as‐prepared Cu‐OMC@RP composites were then washed with carbon disulfide to remove the surviving white P generated during the condensation process and dried in a vacuum oven at 60 °C for 8 h. The OMC@RP and Cu‐DPC @RP samples were also prepared under the same condition.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202106930-sec-0090" numbered="no"><title type="main">Materials Characterization</title><p>The morphology of all samples was characterized by a field‐emission scanning electron microscope (FESEM) (TESCAN, MIRA3) equipped with energy‐dispersive X‐ray spectroscopy (EDX) (XFlash6160). The deep microstructure and morphologies were probed using a field emission transmission electron microscope (FETEM) (FEI Talos F200X). X‐ray diffraction (XRD) (Bruker, D8 ADVANCE Da Vinci) and X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) (Kratos, AXIS UltraDLD) were applied to characterize the phase and surface properties of materials. Raman spectra were recorded with a Senterra R200‐L spectrometer. Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) surfaces and pore size distribution (PSD) plots were carried out by using a nitrogen adsorption/desorption instrument (ASAP2460). Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) was conducted at a heating rate of 10 °C min<sup>–1</sup> under argon flow and air to confirm the red P and Cu content, respectively, using a NETZSCH STA 449F3 TG analyzer.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202106930-sec-0100" numbered="no"><title type="main">Electrochemical Measurements</title><p>The working electrodes were prepared by mixing samples, super C45, and carboxymethyl cellulose with a mass ratio of 70:15:15, and DI water was used as the solvent to form the uniform slurry. Then, the slurry was painted onto the copper foil and dried at 60 °C in the vacuum oven overnight. The areal loading mass of the electrodes was ≈1.5–2.0 mg cm<sup>–2</sup>. CR2032 coin‐type cells were used as the vehicles for all electrochemical tests, which were assembled in an Ar‐filed glovebox (both O<sub>2</sub> and H<sub>2</sub>O contents were below 0.1 ppm). For the LIBs, the cells were constructed with lithium metal as the counter electrode, Celgard 2400 as the separator, and a mixture of 1M LiPF<sub>6</sub> dissolved in ethylene carbonate (EC) and diethyl carbonate (DEC) (1:1, w/w) as the electrolyte. As for SIBs test, sodium sheet (homemade) was used as the counter and reference electrode, the electrolyte solution was 1 <sc>m</sc> NaClO<sub>4</sub> dispersed in EC and dimethyl carbonate (DMC) (1:1) plus 5 wt% fluoroethylene carbonate (FEC) additive, while glass fiber membrane (Whatman, GF/D) was selected as the separator. Galvanostatic charge–discharge performances and galvanostatic intermittent titration technique (GITT) analysis were conducted on a battery test system (Neware, BTS‐610) within the voltage range of 0.01–3.0 V versus Li<sup>+</sup>/Li or 0.01–2.0 V versus Na<sup>+</sup>/Na.
