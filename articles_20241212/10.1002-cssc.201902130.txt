(1 1 0)‐oriented and bridged‐nanoblocks self‐assembled VS<sub>4</sub> microspheres are controllably synthesized by a facile one‐step hydrothermal method. The (1 1 0)‐bridged structure sets up open pathways for Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion among nanoblocks, and the (1 1 0)‐oriented structure provides unobstructed pathways for Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion in the nanoblocks, which collectively constructs multidimensional Na<sup>+</sup> transfer channels in the VS<sub>4</sub> microspheres, promoting the electrochemical kinetics. xml:id="cssc201902130-sec-0001" numbered="no"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Rechargeable Na‐ion batteries (SIBs) are considered a compelling candidate for large‐scale sustainable electrochemical energy storage, because Na metal possesses several advantages, such as high natural abundance, decent energy densities, and similar properties to Li metal.<link href="#cssc201902130-bib-0001 #cssc201902130-bib-0002 #cssc201902130-bib-0003 #cssc201902130-bib-0004"/> Among all of the reported anode materials for SIBs, V‐based sulfides have attracted attention owing to their high reversible capacities resulting from the large Na<sup>+</sup> storage spacing and the polytropic valence of V ranging from +5 to 0.<link href="#cssc201902130-bib-0005 #cssc201902130-bib-0006 #cssc201902130-bib-0007 #cssc201902130-bib-0008 #cssc201902130-bib-0009"/> VS<sub>4</sub>, as a cheap and environmentally benign mineral (Patronite), is a potential electrode material for application in SIBs, owing to its unique structural characteristics of large interchain distance (5.83 Å) caused by parallel 1 D chains, weak interaction between neighboring chains caused by weak van der Waals forces, and high content of S<sup>−</sup> caused by S<sub>2</sub><sup>2−</sup> monomer.<link href="#cssc201902130-bib-0010 #cssc201902130-bib-0011 #cssc201902130-bib-0012 #cssc201902130-bib-0013 #cssc201902130-bib-0014"/> These characteristics can not only afford enough potential sites for Na<sup>+</sup> storage with the theoretical specific capacity of 1196 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> for VS<sub>4</sub>+8 Na<sup>+</sup>+8 e<sup>−</sup>⇄4 Na<sub>2</sub>S+V,<link href="#cssc201902130-bib-0008 #cssc201902130-bib-0010 #cssc201902130-bib-0015"/> but also contribute to Na<sup>+</sup> transfer in the chains.</p><p>In the past few years, VS<sub>4</sub> has been successfully applied to lithium‐ion batteries,<link href="#cssc201902130-bib-0010 #cssc201902130-bib-0016 #cssc201902130-bib-0017"/> SIBs,<link href="#cssc201902130-bib-0008 #cssc201902130-bib-0018 #cssc201902130-bib-0019 #cssc201902130-bib-0020"/> magnesium‐ion batteries,<link href="#cssc201902130-bib-0014"/> aluminum‐ion batteries,<link href="#cssc201902130-bib-0021"/> and supercapacitors,<link href="#cssc201902130-bib-0013 #cssc201902130-bib-0022"/> and its electrochemical performances as an anode material has been explored. However, poorly defined bulk VS<sub>4</sub> undergoes severe pulverization and sluggish kinetics induced by the anisotropy in diffusion and the insufficient electrode–electrolyte contact, which lead to the poor cycling stability and rate capability.<link href="#cssc201902130-bib-0014 #cssc201902130-bib-0023 #cssc201902130-bib-0024"/> Two common strategies for improving the electrochemical performances are combining VS<sub>4</sub> with carbon nanomaterials<link href="#cssc201902130-bib-0008 #cssc201902130-bib-0018 #cssc201902130-bib-0019 #cssc201902130-bib-0020 #cssc201902130-bib-0025"/> and developing 3 D self‐assembled VS<sub>4</sub> nanoarchitectures.<link href="#cssc201902130-bib-0007 #cssc201902130-bib-0014 #cssc201902130-bib-0015"/> Although the introduction of carbon nanomaterials can alleviate pulverization and promote electrochemical kinetics to some extent, the large surface area and volume increase the side reactions and lower the energy density.<link href="#cssc201902130-bib-0026"/></p><p>Benefiting from the high‐efficiency physical entrapment for the self‐expansion/self‐shrinkage of building blocks in the nanoarchitectures, the pure VS<sub>4</sub> exhibits excellent cycling stability. Unfortunately, the insufficient coupling among building blocks and the poor diffusion pathway in building blocks largely restrict the charge transfer in the nanoarchitectures and then lead to the sluggish electrochemical kinetics, eventually resulting in the unsatisfactory rate capabilities. To the best of our knowledge, the reports about the effect of the nanoarchitectures on their electrochemical performances mainly focus on the morphology of building blocks, assembly method and surface area,<link href="#cssc201902130-bib-0009 #cssc201902130-bib-0027 #cssc201902130-bib-0028 #cssc201902130-bib-0029 #cssc201902130-bib-0030"/> and little reports pay attention to the coupling and diffusion pathway of building blocks. It is generally believed that the crystal lattice is a crucial channel for charge storage and transfer, which thus plays a decisive role in the electrochemical performances of electrode materials. In previous reports, the phase types,<link href="#cssc201902130-bib-0031 #cssc201902130-bib-0032 #cssc201902130-bib-0033"/> oriented structure,<link href="#cssc201902130-bib-0007"/> and crystallinity structure<link href="#cssc201902130-bib-0034 #cssc201902130-bib-0035 #cssc201902130-bib-0036"/> have been used to improve the electrochemical performances. This inspired us to optimize the coupling and diffusion pathway and thus enhance the rate capability of the pure VS<sub>4</sub> by constructing nanoarchitectures with well‐defined crystal structure, but it is still a significant challenge.</p><p>In this work, we propose a unique (1 1 0)‐oriented and bridged‐nanoblocks self‐assembled VS<sub>4</sub> microspheres (SB‐VS<sub>4</sub>) to meet the above challenge. The (1 1 0)‐oriented and bridged structure sets up the multidimensional Na<sup>+</sup> transfer channels, which opens and facilitates Na<sup>+</sup> transfer in the VS<sub>4</sub> microspheres and then promotes the electrochemical kinetics. When employed as anode for SIBs, the SB‐VS<sub>4</sub> electrode exhibits excellent rate capability with a high capacity retention of 79 % (the capacity ratio at 0.1 and 2.0 A g<sup>−1</sup>) in the voltage window of 0.5–3.0 V, and a high rate capacity of 575 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 1.0 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 300 cycles in the voltage window of 0.05–3.0 V. Moreover, electrochemical impedance and kinetic analysis are performed to explore the nature of Na<sup>+</sup> transfer and storage processes.</p></section><section xml:id="cssc201902130-sec-0002" numbered="no"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><section xml:id="cssc201902130-sec-0003" numbered="no"><title type="main">Structure characterization</title><p>Three VS<sub>4</sub> samples exhibit 3 D self‐assembled spherical structures (Figure <link href="#cssc201902130-fig-0001"/> and Figure S1 in the Supporting Information). Specifically, SB‐VS<sub>4</sub> and PB‐VS<sub>4</sub> are composed of nanoblocks self‐assembled into clew‐like microspheres (i.e., irregular curved nanoblocks are wound into microspheres), and PP‐VS<sub>4</sub> consists of irregular nanoparticles self‐assembled into microspheres (Figure S1 e). Low‐magnification TEM images (Figure S1 a–c) further confirm the solid spherical shapes of three VS<sub>4</sub> samples. It is worth mentioning that these nanoblocks in SB‐VS<sub>4</sub> and PB‐VS<sub>4</sub> microspheres (Figure <link href="#cssc201902130-fig-0001"/> d, f) tightly blend together, reflecting the coupling among nanoblocks. Meanwhile, the surfaces of SB‐VS<sub>4</sub> nanoblocks are smooth (Figure <link href="#cssc201902130-fig-0001"/> d), whereas PB‐VS<sub>4</sub> nanoblocks consist of numerous ultrasmall nanoparticles (Figure <link href="#cssc201902130-fig-0001"/> f) that are possibly related to their single‐crystal or polycrystalline structure.</p><figure xml:id="cssc201902130-fig-0001"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18645631:media:cssc201902130:cssc201902130-fig-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/cssc201902130-fig-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>SEM images of (a, d) SB‐VS<sub>4</sub>, (b, e) PP‐VS<sub>4</sub>, and (c, f) PB‐VS<sub>4</sub>.</p></caption></figure><p>SB‐VS<sub>4</sub> nanoblocks shows very intact lattice fringes with only one type interplanar spacing of 5.60 Å corresponding to (1 1 0) planes of VS<sub>4</sub> (Figure <link href="#cssc201902130-fig-0002"/> a and Figure S1 d), implying single‐crystal characteristics, which can be directly confirmed by the corresponding discrete and patterned diffraction spots in the FFT (inset of Figure <link href="#cssc201902130-fig-0002"/> a) and SAED patterns (Figure <link href="#cssc201902130-fig-0002"/> d). The diffraction spots of (1 1 0), (2 2 0), (3 3 0), (4 4 0), and (5 5 0) planes corresponding to the 1 D chains of monoclinic VS<sub>4</sub> can be obviously detected in Figure <link href="#cssc201902130-fig-0002"/> d, which, combined with the only observed (1 1 0) planes in Figure <link href="#cssc201902130-fig-0002"/> a, demonstrates the dominated crystal growth along (1 1 0) crystallographic direction. It is important to highlight that the (1 1 0) lattice fringes and (1 1 0) diffraction spots show different orientations, indicating that the interaction of (1 1 0) planes originating from different nanoblocks appears in the SB‐VS<sub>4</sub> microspheres. The interconnection (defined as “bridging”) of the crystal lattices originating from different nanoblocks sets up the connect channels for Na<sup>+</sup>/e<sup>−</sup> transfer across the homogeneous interface of nanoblocks. A similar phenomenon has also been found in the hetero‐nanostructures of MoS<sub>2</sub>/TiNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub>.<link href="#cssc201902130-bib-0037"/> PP‐VS<sub>4</sub> nanoparticles exhibit lattice fringes with two interplanar spacings of 5.57 and 5.29 Å (Figure <link href="#cssc201902130-fig-0002"/> b), corresponding to (1 1 0) and (0 2 0) planes, and the interaction of (1 1 0) and (0 2 0) planes originating from different nanoparticles appears in the PP‐VS<sub>4</sub> microspheres. The diffraction ring pattern with disorder diffraction spots in the FFT (inset of Figure <link href="#cssc201902130-fig-0002"/> b) and SAED patterns (Figure <link href="#cssc201902130-fig-0002"/> e), suggests the polycrystalline characteristic of PP‐VS<sub>4</sub> nanoparticles. Abundant small domains show the lattice fringes of (2 2 4) and (1 1 4) planes with 1.90 and 2.50 Å interplanar spacings in the PB‐VS<sub>4</sub> nanoblocks (Figure <link href="#cssc201902130-fig-0002"/> c and Figure S1 f), which correspond well to the ultrasmall nanoparticles observed in Figure <link href="#cssc201902130-fig-0001"/> f. These small domains demonstrate the intrinsic polycrystallinity of PB‐VS<sub>4</sub> nanoblocks, which can be directly confirmed by the corresponding distinct diffraction ring patterns in the FFT (inset of Figure <link href="#cssc201902130-fig-0002"/> c) and SAED pattern (Figure <link href="#cssc201902130-fig-0002"/> f). Specifically, the interaction of (2 2 4) and (1 1 4) planes originating from different nanoblocks appears in the PB‐VS<sub>4</sub> microspheres (Figure <link href="#cssc201902130-fig-0002"/> c).</p><figure xml:id="cssc201902130-fig-0002"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18645631:media:cssc201902130:cssc201902130-fig-0002"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/cssc201902130-fig-0002-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>HRTEM images and corresponding FFT patterns of (a) SB‐VS<sub>4</sub>, (b) PP‐VS<sub>4</sub>, and (c) PB‐VS<sub>4</sub>. SAED patterns of (d) SB‐VS<sub>4</sub>, (e) PP‐VS<sub>4</sub>, and (f) PB‐VS<sub>4</sub>.</p></caption></figure><p>Based on the previous investigations, it is inferred that at high pH, the larger microreaction pressure induced by the as‐generated NH<sub>3</sub> of C<sub>2</sub>H<sub>5</sub>NS hydrolysis and the selective adsorption of NH<sub>4</sub><sup>+</sup> on the surface of (1 1 0) planes collectively drive the oriented growth of VS<sub>4</sub> crystal nucleus along (1 1 0) crystallographic direction and the interaction of (1 1 0) planes originating from different nanoblocks in SB‐VS<sub>4</sub>.<link href="#cssc201902130-bib-0009 #cssc201902130-bib-0038"/> For PB‐VS<sub>4</sub>, although VS<sub>4</sub> self‐assembled microspheres have formed after 18 h, the ultrasmall nanoparticles have not had enough time to merge into a whole subunit with regular lattice. For PP‐VS<sub>4</sub>, the very short reaction time leads to that the as‐formed nanoparticles have not had enough time to self‐assemble into the subunits, but directly self‐assemble into the microspheres. Notably, during the hydrothermal process of PP‐VS<sub>4</sub> and PB‐VS<sub>4</sub>, the short reaction time makes their nanoparticles merge to a certain degree, but do not totally ripen into a whole structure, which results in the appearance of a crystal lattice interaction.</p><p>All main peaks with strong intensity for each sample are well indexed to monoclinic VS<sub>4</sub> (PDF# 72‐1294), confirming their high phase purity (Figure <link href="#cssc201902130-fig-0003"/> a). Compared with PP‐VS<sub>4</sub> and PB‐VS<sub>4</sub>, SB‐VS<sub>4</sub> has a smoother XRD pattern and higher (1 1 0) diffraction peak at 15.78° relative to other diffraction peaks, better revealing its high‐crystallinity feature and dominated crystal growth along (1 1 0) crystallographic direction. All Raman spectra appear very similar with seven peaks located at 141, 191, 281, 406, 521, 688, and 992 cm<sup>−1</sup> (Figure S2 a), which are induced by the bending and stretching vibrations of V−S bonds or their combination.<link href="#cssc201902130-bib-0012 #cssc201902130-bib-0019"/> When compared to their FTIR database in Figure <link href="#cssc201902130-fig-0003"/> b, the <i>v</i> (V−S−V) doubly bonded S<sup>2−</sup> and the ν (V=S) terminal S stretches of VS<sub>4</sub> are obviously probed at 545 and 990 cm<sup>−1</sup>, respectively.<link href="#cssc201902130-bib-0011 #cssc201902130-bib-0039"/> XPS analysis suggests the coexistence and valence states of V and S elements (Figure <link href="#cssc201902130-fig-0003"/> c, d and Figure S2 b). The large V peaks at around 516.4 and 523.7 eV in the high‐resolution V 2p XPS spectra (Figure <link href="#cssc201902130-fig-0003"/> c), attributed to V 2p<sub>3/2</sub> and V 2p<sub>1/2</sub> levels of V<sup>4+</sup>, demonstrate the domination of V<sup>4+</sup> in three VS<sub>4</sub> samples. The high‐resolution XPS scanning at S 2p region demonstrates the existence of S<sub>2</sub><sup>2−</sup> dimers (Figure <link href="#cssc201902130-fig-0003"/> d) with two peaks located at around 162.3 and 163.4 eV that can be ascribed to S 2p<sub>3/2</sub> and S 2p<sub>1/2</sub> orbits, respectively. It should be pointed out that the appearance of V<sup>3+</sup> XPS peaks (Figure <link href="#cssc201902130-fig-0003"/> c) and the obvious alteration of V−S−V/V=S (Figure <link href="#cssc201902130-fig-0003"/> b) and S 2p<sub>3/2</sub>/S 2p<sub>1/2</sub> (Figure <link href="#cssc201902130-fig-0003"/> d), further reflect the existence of the plane interaction among different building blocks for three VS<sub>4</sub> samples.<link href="#cssc201902130-bib-0040 #cssc201902130-bib-0041"/> Particularly, stronger V<sup>3+</sup> peaks in the V 2p XPS spectrum of SB‐VS<sub>4</sub> demonstrates more sufficient plane interaction among SB‐VS<sub>4</sub> nanoblocks.</p><figure xml:id="cssc201902130-fig-0003"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18645631:media:cssc201902130:cssc201902130-fig-0003"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/cssc201902130-fig-0003-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>(a) XRD patterns, (b) FTIR spectra, and high‐resolution (c) V 2p and (d) S 2p XPS spectra of SB‐VS<sub>4</sub>, PP‐VS<sub>4</sub>, and PB‐VS<sub>4</sub>.</p></caption></figure></section><section xml:id="cssc201902130-sec-0004" numbered="no"><title type="main">Sodium storage performance</title><p>Na storage performances of three VS<sub>4</sub> electrodes in the voltage window of 0.5–3.0 V was firstly investigated. The SB‐VS<sub>4</sub> electrode exhibits the best rate performance and cycling capacity relative to PP‐VS<sub>4</sub> and PB‐VS<sub>4</sub> electrode (Figure <link href="#cssc201902130-fig-0004"/> a and Figure S3). Concretely, SB‐VS<sub>4</sub> electrode delivers the high specific capacities of 339, 315, 285, 276, and 270 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at a rate of 0.1, 0.2, 0.5, 1.0, and 2.0 A g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. When the current density is reduced back to 0.2 A g<sup>−1</sup>, the specific capacity of 441 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> is achieved for SB‐VS<sub>4</sub> electrode, whereas that is only 198 and 103 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> for PP‐VS<sub>4</sub> and PB‐VS<sub>4</sub>, respectively, suggesting the excellent rate reversibility of SB‐VS<sub>4</sub> electrode. Notably, a capacity retention (the capacity ratio at 0.1 and 2.0 A g<sup>−1</sup>) of 79 % can be reached for SB‐VS<sub>4</sub> electrode, far higher than that of PP‐VS<sub>4</sub> (62 %) and PB‐VS<sub>4</sub> (30 %) electrode, implying the excellent rate capability of SB‐VS<sub>4</sub> electrode. After the following cycling test of 60 cycles at 0.2 A g<sup>−1</sup>, the reversible capacity of SB‐VS<sub>4</sub> electrode arrives at 488 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, which is almost 2.5 and 3.0 times higher than that of PP‐VS<sub>4</sub> (190 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>) and PB‐VS<sub>4</sub> (159 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>) electrode, indicating the higher cycling capacity of SB‐VS<sub>4</sub> electrode. The representative charge–discharge profiles of SB‐VS<sub>4</sub> electrode at various current densities from 0.1 to 2.0 A g<sup>−1</sup> show very similar plateaus (Figure S3), whereas those of PP‐VS<sub>4</sub> and PB‐VS<sub>4</sub> electrode show serious shrinkage, demonstrating the excellent rate stability of SB‐VS<sub>4</sub> electrode. Compared with that of PP‐VS<sub>4</sub> and PB‐VS<sub>4</sub> electrode, the charge–discharge profiles of SB‐VS<sub>4</sub> electrode exhibit smaller potential hysteresis with the increase of current density, revealing its better kinetic feature at different rates. At a high current density of 1.0 A g<sup>−1</sup>, SB‐VS<sub>4</sub> electrode achieves an excellent recycling capability, illustrating a high specific capacity of 268 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 1000 cycles (Figure <link href="#cssc201902130-fig-0004"/> b) with a stable coulombic efficiency (CE) of ≈100.3. The increase of specific capacity during cycles is mainly attributed to the expansion of VS<sub>4</sub> interchain spacing induced by repeated Na<sup>+</sup> insertion/extraction in the interchain, which contributes to exposing more active sites for Na<sup>+</sup> storage and lowering the diffusion energy barrier of Na<sup>+</sup> among interchains. The stable capacity after a number of cycles indicates that the as‐expanded structure can sustain to some extent. Besides, the charge plateau capacities of PP‐VS<sub>4</sub> and PB‐VS<sub>4</sub> electrode are far less than their discharge plateau capacities, indicating their poor electrochemical reversibility.</p><p>To provide further insight for Na<sup>+</sup> transfer and storage process, EIS and CV at various scan rates from 0.1 to 10 mV s<sup>−1</sup> were analyzed. The charge‐transfer resistance (<i>R</i><sub>ct</sub>) of the SB‐VS<sub>4</sub> electrode is only 115.8 Ω (Figure <link href="#cssc201902130-fig-0005"/> a, b and Table S2), which is far less than those of PP‐VS<sub>4</sub> (195.2 Ω) and PB‐VS<sub>4</sub> (282.2 Ω) electrodes, suggesting its faster Na<sup>+</sup> mobility and better electrochemical kinetics. The Warburg coefficient (<i>Z</i><sub>w</sub>; obtained from the slope of the fit of <i>Z</i>′−<i>ω</i><sup>−1/2</sup> (<i>ω</i>=2π<i>f</i>, with <i>f</i> being the frequency) of the SB‐VS<sub>4</sub> electrode (1037 Ω s<sup>−1/2</sup>) is also much lower than that of PP‐VS<sub>4</sub> (1081 Ω s<sup>−1/2</sup>) and PB‐VS<sub>4</sub> (1147 Ω s<sup>−1/2</sup>) electrode, indicating the enhanced Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion in SB‐VS<sub>4</sub> electrode. Four significant cathodic/anodic peaks at ≈1.96/2.23 and 1.23/1.75 V (Figure <link href="#cssc201902130-fig-0005"/> c) correspond to the insertion reactions of Na<sub><i>x</i></sub>VS<sub>4</sub> as a function of Na content according to Equation (S1).<link href="#cssc201902130-bib-0019"/> Specifically, the CV curves (especially for the four obvious cathodic/anodic peaks) of SB‐VS<sub>4</sub> electrode are well preserved with the increase of the scan rate from 0.1 to 10 mV s<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#cssc201902130-fig-0005"/> c). In contrast, the CV curves appear in slight disorder for PB‐VS<sub>4</sub> electrode (Figure S5 a) and severe disorder for PP‐VS<sub>4</sub> electrode (Figure S5 b). Thus, the SB‐VS<sub>4</sub> electrode is capable of keeping a quick CV response to a fast potential scan in comparison with PB‐VS<sub>4</sub> and PP‐VS<sub>4</sub> electrode, revealing its better rate capability for Na<sup>+</sup> storage, which is well in accordance with the rate charge–discharge profiles analysis. On one hand, the bridging of (1 1 0) planes among nanoblocks sets up more open pathways for facile Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion among nanoblocks. On the other hand, the (1 1 0)‐oriented structure of nanoblocks with high‐crystallinity, as the channel of Na<sup>+</sup> insertion/extraction, presents unobstructed pathways for Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion and stable active sites for Na<sup>+</sup> storage in nanoblocks without a serious structural distortion. The two structures collectively construct the multidimensional Na<sup>+</sup> transfer channels in the VS<sub>4</sub> microspheres and then promote the electrochemical kinetics. Besides, the excellent recycling capability of SB‐VS<sub>4</sub> electrode mainly benefits from its unique physical entrapment for the expansion/shrinkage of nanoblocks and unobstructed Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion pathways.</p><p>The cycling performance of three VS<sub>4</sub> electrodes in the voltage window of 0.05–3.0 V was then investigated. Associating with the synergistic effect of (1 1 0)‐oriented and bridged structures that collectively construct the multidimensional Na<sup>+</sup> transfer channels in the VS<sub>4</sub> microspheres, SB‐VS<sub>4</sub> electrode shows the dramatically enhanced rate capability as an anode of Na‐ion batteries (SIBs). In the voltage window of 0.5–3.0 V, reversible capacities of 339 and 270 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> are delivered at 0.1 and 2.0 A g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively, with a high capacity retention of 79 %. After cycling testing of 60 cycles at 0.2 A g<sup>−1</sup>, a reversible capacity of 488 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> is obtained. Furthermore, the SB‐VS<sub>4</sub> electrode delivers long cycling life with excellent capacity retention even after 1000 cycles at 1.0 A g<sup>−1</sup>. In the voltage window of 0.05–3.0 V, SB‐VS<sub>4</sub> electrode exhibits differentiated recycling capability at different current density and the specific capacity can be stable at 575 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> during 300 cycles at 1.0 A g<sup>−1</sup>. Electrochemical kinetic analysis demonstrates that the Na<sup>+</sup> storage of SB‐VS<sub>4</sub> electrode mainly depends on pseudocapacitive behavior. The impressive electrochemical performance reveals that VS<sub>4</sub> has great potential for next‐generation large‐scale energy storage.</p></section><section xml:id="cssc201902130-sec-0006" numbered="no"><title type="main">Experimental Section</title><section xml:id="cssc201902130-sec-0007" numbered="no"><title type="main">Material synthesis</title><p><b>SB‐VS<sub>4</sub></b>: In a typical procedure, NaVO<sub>3</sub> (1.0 g, Aladdin, AR, 99.0 %) and C<sub>2</sub>H<sub>5</sub>NS (3.6 g, Sinopharm Chemical Reagent, AR, ≥99.0 %) were dissolved in 60 mL of distilled (DI) water. After magnetic stirring for 10 min, a pH value of 9.8 was adjusted by dropwise adding the diluted NaOH solution (3 mol L<sup>−1</sup>) accompanied by continuously magnetic stirring at room temperature. After that, the above solution was poured into a 100 mL Teflon‐lined stainless‐steel autoclave, which was then heated to 180 °C and maintained for 24 h at a rotating speed of 10 rpm. After naturally cooling to room temperature, the precipitate was washed and collected with DI water and ethanol in turn for three times by filtration. After being dried at 60 °C for 12 h under vacuum, the resulting single‐crystal nanoblocks self‐assembled VS<sub>4</sub> microspheres were obtained as a black powder and denoted as SB‐VS<sub>4</sub>.</p><p><b>PP‐VS<sub>4</sub> and PB‐VS<sub>4</sub></b>: For comparison, based on the synthetic procedure for SB‐VS<sub>4</sub>, the polycrystalline nanoparticles and nanoblocks self‐assembled VS<sub>4</sub> microspheres, abbreviated as PP‐VS<sub>4</sub> and PB‐VS<sub>4</sub>, were synthesized by decreasing the pH to 5 by using a diluted HCl solution (3 mol L<sup>−1</sup>) and shortening the reaction time to 4 or 12 h, respectively. Note that the original pH value of the precursor solution is 9.3.</p></section><section xml:id="cssc201902130-sec-0008" numbered="no"><title type="main">Materials characterization</title><p>The morphologies were investigated via a field emission scanning electron microscope (FESEM, S‐4800). The crystal structures were characterized by transmission electron microscopy (TEM), high‐resolution transmission electron microscopy (HRTEM), selected area electron diffraction (SAED), fast‐Fourier transform (FFT) images (TEM, JEM‐3010), and X‐ray diffraction patterns [XRD, D/max2200PC, Cu<sub>Kα</sub> radiation (<i>λ</i>=0.15406 nm)] with a scanning step of 8° min<sup>−1</sup>. The electrodes were prepared by coating the slurry onto a Cu foil, which was dried in a vacuum oven at 120 °C for 12 h. The slurry was obtained by mixing the as‐synthesized VS<sub>4</sub> active materials, acetylene black, and polyvinylidene fluoride (PVDF) binder in a weight ratio 8:1:1 in <i>N</i>‐methyl‐2‐pyrrolidone (NMP) solution. The loading content of active material was 0.6–1.0 mg cm<sup>−2</sup> and the diameter of the electrode roundels was 16 mm. Cyclic voltammetry (CV) at different scan rates, and electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) with an amplitude of 5 mV in the frequency range between 100 kHz and 0.01 Hz were tested using CHI660E electrochemical workstation.
