However, capacity decrease owing to the volume expansion of the active material during cycling is a problem. Herein, a rod‐like CoC<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>⋅2 H<sub>2</sub>O/rGO hybrid is fabricated through a novel multistep solvo/hydrothermal strategy. The structural characteristics of the CoC<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>⋅2 H<sub>2</sub>O microrod wrapped using rGO sheets not only inhibit the volume variation of the hybrid electrode during cycling, but also accelerate the transfer of electrons and ions in the 3 D graphene network, thereby improving the electrochemical properties of CoC<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>⋅2 H<sub>2</sub>O. The CoC<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>⋅2 H<sub>2</sub>O/rGO electrode delivers a specific capacity of 1011.5 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.2 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 200 cycles for lithium storage, and a high capacity of 221.1 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.2 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 100 cycles for sodium storage. Moreover, the full cell CoC<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>⋅2 H<sub>2</sub>O/rGO//LiCoO<sub>2</sub> consisting of the CoC<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>⋅2 H<sub>2</sub>O/rGO anode and LiCoO<sub>2</sub> cathode maintains 138.1 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> after 200 cycles at 0.2 A g<sup>−1</sup> and has superior long‐cycle stability. In addition, in situ Raman spectroscopy and in situ and ex situ X‐ray diffraction techniques provide a unique opportunity to understand fully the reaction mechanism of CoC<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>⋅2 H<sub>2</sub>O/rGO. This work also gives a new perspective and solid research basis for the application of metal oxalate materials in high‐performance lithium‐ion and sodium‐ion batteries.</p></abstract><abstract type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p><b>Metal oxalate nanorods</b>: CoC<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>⋅2 H<sub>2</sub>O/rGO hybrids with a nanorod structure have superior rate capacity retention and long cycling stability in lithium‐ion batteries. The in situ Raman analysis technique is very powerful for probing the structure and phase composition of the CoC<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>⋅2 H<sub>2</sub>O/rGO electrode during the charge/discharge process.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:09476539:media:chem202003309:chem202003309-toc-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/chem202003309-toc-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="chem202003309-sec-0001" numbered="no"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>With the rapid development of the global economy, the progress of human society and the improvement of people's living standards, the consumption of and demand for energy are increasing daily. For the past few decades, fossil fuels have been an important resource for energy provision. However, the non‐renewability of fossil fuels and their continuous exploitation by human beings have led to the depletion of fossil energy. Therefore, lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs) and sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) have become important and reliable alternatives as green and efficient clean energy sources.<link href="#chem202003309-bib-0001"><sup>[1]</sup></link> It is well known that LIBs have excellent overall performance, such as their high operating voltage, high energy density, long cycling life, and good safety performance.<link href="#chem202003309-bib-0006"><sup>[2]</sup></link> However, the drawbacks are the limited reserves and high price of lithium resources. However, SIBs have attracted wide attention because of the naturally abundant resources and low cost of sodium.<link href="#chem202003309-bib-0012"><sup>[3]</sup></link> In commercial batteries, graphite is usually used as a negative active material for LIBs.<link href="#chem202003309-bib-0016"><sup>[4]</sup></link> However, the low theoretical capacity (LiC<sub>6</sub> ≈372 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>) and poor Li<sup>+</sup> intercalation kinetics are considered as primary limiting factors.<link href="#chem202003309-bib-0019"><sup>[5]</sup></link> Therefore, there is urgent need to search for a new type of anode material with low price, good safety, large theoretical capacity, and long cycle life.</p><p>Nowadays, the negative materials of batteries mainly include carbon (graphite, hard carbon, soft carbon),<link href="#chem202003309-bib-0023"><sup>[6]</sup></link> silicon,<link href="#chem202003309-bib-0024"><sup>[7]</sup></link> tin,<link href="#chem202003309-bib-0025"><sup>[8]</sup></link> metal nitrides,<link href="#chem202003309-bib-0026"><sup>[9]</sup></link> metal oxides,<link href="#chem202003309-bib-0027"><sup>[10]</sup></link> metal phosphides,<link href="#chem202003309-bib-0031"><sup>[11]</sup></link> metal sulfides,<link href="#chem202003309-bib-0035"><sup>[12]</sup></link> and so on. Among these materials, metal oxides (MO) have attracted wide attention owing to their high capacity. Customarily, the reaction process of metal oxides can be expressed as follows: <inlineGraphic href="graphic/chem202003309-math-0001.png" alt="urn:x-wiley:09476539:media:chem202003309:chem202003309-math-0001"/><link href="#chem202003309-bib-0013 #chem202003309-bib-0028"><sup>[3a, 10a]</sup></link> Significantly, most metal oxide materials also suffer from volumetric expansion and pulverization during repeated cycles, which lead to poor rate performance and unsatisfactory cycle stability and reversibility.<link href="#chem202003309-bib-0039"><sup>[13]</sup></link> As a novel conversion storage electrode material, transition metal oxalate (MC<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>) has become a possible electrode material for LIBs and SIBs.<link href="#chem202003309-bib-0013 #chem202003309-bib-0042"><sup>[3a, 14]</sup></link> In some earlier studies, Tirado et al. proposed a possible mechanism during the energy storage process: <inlineGraphic href="graphic/chem202003309-math-0002.png" alt="urn:x-wiley:09476539:media:chem202003309:chem202003309-math-0002"/>(M: transition metal).<link href="#chem202003309-bib-0045"><sup>[15]</sup></link> This concept can also be applied to the Na‐ion system, and can be expressed as follows: <inlineGraphic href="graphic/chem202003309-math-0003.png" alt="urn:x-wiley:09476539:media:chem202003309:chem202003309-math-0003"/>.<link href="#chem202003309-bib-0013"><sup>[3a]</sup></link> Aragon et al. reported that FeC<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> and CoC<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> nanoribbons are promising anode materials for LIBs. Of these two metal oxalates, the reversible capacity of about 900 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> for the CoC<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> material is higher than that of FeC<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> (≈700 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>).<link href="#chem202003309-bib-0048"><sup>[16]</sup></link> Nevertheless, it is observed that the capacity decreases considerably in subsequent cycles. This phenomenon occurs because metal oxalate possesses poor electronic conduction and shows large volumetric change during repeated lithiation/delithiation processes. An effective strategy to ameliorate these problems is to modify them with carbon‐containing materials<link href="#chem202003309-bib-0049"><sup>[17]</sup></link> such as carbon nanotubes, carbon nanowires, or graphene. In particular, reduced graphene oxide (rGO) is considered to be a promising carbonaceous material owing to its unique chemical stability, high flexibility, and large surface area.<link href="#chem202003309-bib-0052"><sup>[18]</sup></link></p><p>Metal oxalates can be synthesized through the hydrothermal method,<link href="#chem202003309-bib-0057"><sup>[19]</sup></link> solvothermal reaction,<link href="#chem202003309-bib-0058"><sup>[20]</sup></link> microwave‐assisted solution approach,<link href="#chem202003309-bib-0059"><sup>[21]</sup></link> the sol‐gel method,<link href="#chem202003309-bib-0060"><sup>[22]</sup></link> and the reverse‐micellar route.<link href="#chem202003309-bib-0061"><sup>[23]</sup></link> In this work, CoC<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>⋅2 H<sub>2</sub>O is modified with graphene oxide, and CoC<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>⋅2 H<sub>2</sub>O and CoC<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>⋅2 H<sub>2</sub>O/rGO are prepared by a facile solvothermal and hydrothermal method, respectively. The unique structure of CoC<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>⋅2 H<sub>2</sub>O/rGO has two distinct advantages. First, the rod‐like structure can provide more active sites and shorten the ion diffusion pathway. Secondly, the graphene structure can also suppress the volume expansion of CoC<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>⋅2 H<sub>2</sub>O during lithium or sodium intercalation/de‐intercalation. In LIBs, the CoC<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>⋅2 H<sub>2</sub>O/rGO electrode delivers an excellent capacity of 434.5 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> after 2500 cycles even at 10 A g<sup>−1</sup>. To understand the electrochemical phenomenon of CoC<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>⋅2 H<sub>2</sub>O/rGO, we explore its electrochemical reaction mechanism in detail by employing several characterization techniques. More broadly, we give a novel mechanistic insight into the fast kinetics of CoC<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>⋅2 H<sub>2</sub>O/rGO hybrids in LIBs.</p></section><section xml:id="chem202003309-sec-0002" numbered="no"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p>The synthesis route of CoC<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>⋅2 H<sub>2</sub>O/rGO is illustrated in Figure <link href="#chem202003309-fig-0001">1</link>. The intensity ratio of the D/G bands of CoC<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>⋅2 H<sub>2</sub>O/rGO (<i>I</i><sub>D</sub>/<i>I</i><sub>G</sub>=1.14) is relatively higher than that of the pure rGO (<i>I</i><sub>D</sub>/<i>I</i><sub>G</sub>=1.03), indicating that the rGO in the CoC<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>⋅2 H<sub>2</sub>O/rGO hybrids has many defects after hydrothermal treatment.<link href="#chem202003309-bib-0044"><sup>[14b]</sup></link> The CoC<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>⋅2 H<sub>2</sub>O/rGO has a larger pore volume and higher BET specific surface area (15.81 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup>) (Figure S1, Supporting Information), which not only enhances the electrolyte/electrode contact, but also boosts the electrolyte penetration along the material, so that it exhibits excellent electrochemical performance. The valence of the surface of CoC<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>⋅2 H<sub>2</sub>O/rGO was studied by XPS. As illustrated in Figure S2 (Supporting Information), the survey spectrum results show that the CoC<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>⋅2 H<sub>2</sub>O/rGO contains C, O, and Co elements. The high‐resolution C 1s spectrum of CoC<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>⋅2 H<sub>2</sub>O/rGO can be divided into three peaks (Figure <link href="#chem202003309-fig-0002">2 d</link>), which confirms the existence of C−C (at 284.6 eV), C−O (at 285.4 eV), and C=O (at 289.4 eV).<link href="#chem202003309-bib-0062"><sup>[24]</sup></link> The O 1s spectrum indicates the presence of C=O (at 531.5 eV) and the other peak of H<sub>2</sub>O (at 533.1 eV),<link href="#chem202003309-bib-0065"><sup>[25]</sup></link> as shown in Figure <link href="#chem202003309-fig-0002">2 e</link>. The CoC<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>⋅2 H<sub>2</sub>O microrods with diameter 200–300 nm can be observed from Figure <link href="#chem202003309-fig-0003">3 a,b</link> and Figure S3 a,b (Supporting Information). After the CoC<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>⋅2 H<sub>2</sub>O is wrapped by rGO, the CoC<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>⋅2 H<sub>2</sub>O/rGO is composed of two different sizes and shapes of materials, that is, CoC<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>⋅2 H<sub>2</sub>O with the one‐dimensional rod‐structure and rGO with the silk‐folded ultrathin sheets, as shown in Figure <link href="#chem202003309-fig-0003">3 c,d</link> and Figure S3 c. The TEM images show the fine morphological details of the CoC<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>⋅2 H<sub>2</sub>O/rGO microstructure; a clear graphene image is shown in the high‐magnification images, which also demonstrates the uniform distribution of CoC<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>⋅2 H<sub>2</sub>O on graphene, as shown in Figure <link href="#chem202003309-fig-0003">3 e</link> and Figure S3 d. The unique structure provides efficient pathways for electrons/ions, and fast kinetics, and effectively inhibits the volume change in the cycle upon application as the anode for LIBs and SIBs. Benefiting from the synergistic effect of the microstructure and rGO, the CoC<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>⋅2 H<sub>2</sub>O/rGO electrode exhibits excellent reversible capacity, cycling stability, and rate performance for both LIBs (1011.5 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.2 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 200 cycles, 434.5 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 10 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 2500 cycles) and SIBs (221.1 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.2 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 100 cycles). In addition, the full cell of CoC<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>⋅2 H<sub>2</sub>O/rGO//LiCoO<sub>2</sub> was designed successfully, and delivered 138.1 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> after 200 cycles at 0.2 A g<sup>−1</sup>, showing fascinating application prospects in LIBs. More importantly, the mechanistic insights verified by in situ Raman spectroscopy and in situ and ex situ X‐ray diffraction techniques heighten our understanding of its conversion and catalysis of Co for lithiation/delithiation processes. Furthermore, this work proposes a general method for the preparation of metal oxalate hybrids with novel structures, which enhance the electrochemical properties for LIBs and SIBs, and provides a feasible research basis for other materials.</p></section><section xml:id="chem202003309-sec-0004" numbered="no"><title type="main">Experimental Section</title><section xml:id="chem202003309-sec-0005" numbered="no"><title type="main">Synthesis of CoC<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>⋅2 H<sub>2</sub>O microrods</title><p>The CoC<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>⋅2 H<sub>2</sub>O microrods were prepared through the following steps. First, H<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> (3 mmol) and Co(NO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub>⋅6 H<sub>2</sub>O (3 mmol) were dissolved in ethylene glycol (45 mL, solution A) and deionized water (15 mL, solution B), respectively. Then, solution B was added dropwise into solution A and the mixture was stirred for another 5 min. Finally, the mixture was poured into an 80 mL autoclave at 140 °C for 24 h. Eventually, the collected pink precipitate was washed and dried to obtain CoC<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>⋅2 H<sub>2</sub>O.</p></section><section xml:id="chem202003309-sec-0006" numbered="no"><title type="main">Synthesis of CoC<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>⋅2 H<sub>2</sub>O/rGO microrods</title><p>Graphene oxide (GO) was prepared according to a modified Hummer's method.<link href="#chem202003309-bib-0081"><sup>[33]</sup></link> GO (60 mg) was added into deionized water (40 mL) and treated ultrasonically for 3 h. Subsequently, CoC<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>⋅2 H<sub>2</sub>O (0.240 g) was dissolved in the GO dispersion solution (40 mL) and kept stirring overnight. The as‐obtained mixture was transferred into a 50 mL autoclave and maintained at 180 °C for 12 h. Finally, the CoC<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>⋅2 H<sub>2</sub>O<b>/</b>rGO hybrids were obtained after freeze‐drying overnight.</p></section><section xml:id="chem202003309-sec-0007" numbered="no"><title type="main">Material characterization</title><p>The crystal structures of the samples were confirmed by X‐ray diffraction (XRD, Bruker D8 Advance). The thermal properties of the samples were obtained by thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) (Instrument TGA‐409 PC) under a flowing air atmosphere. A LabRAM HR Evolution (HORIBA) instrument was used to record the Raman spectra. The specific surface area and pore‐size distribution were measured with a Micromeritics ASAP 2420 at 77 K. The scanning electron microscopy (SEM) and transmission electron microscopy (TEM) images were obtained with a JEOL JSM‐6700F field‐emission and JEOL JEM‐2010F transmission electron microscope, respectively. X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) measurements were characterized by Kratos Analytical Ltd. U.K.</p></section><section xml:id="chem202003309-sec-0008" numbered="no"><title type="main">Electrochemical measurements</title><p>The anode was formed by active materials (the weight is based on the CoC<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>⋅2 H<sub>2</sub>O<b>/</b>rGO), conductive carbon black, and polyvinylidene fluoride (PVDF) with a mass ratio of 80:10:10 in <i>N</i>‐methyl‐2‐pyrrolidinone (NMP).
