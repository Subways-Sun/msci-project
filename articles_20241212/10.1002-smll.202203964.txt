Herein, the “flower‐like” porous carbon anchored by Zn‐Mn binary selenides (ZMS@FC) composites are fabricated by selenizing the modified hierarchically metal‐organic frameworks. The 2D conductive hierarchically flakes’ abundant pore structure and multiple active sites shorten the ion diffusion length and promote conductivity, while the synergistic effect of the binary metals and intrinsic large pseudocapacitive contribution effectively improve capacity and rate performance. ZMS@FC composites exhibit impressive rate capability of 294.4 mA h g<sup>–1</sup> at 10 A g<sup>–1</sup> and excellent cyclic stability with 369.6 mA h g<sup>–1</sup> specific capacity retention at 2 A g<sup>–1</sup> after 1000 cycling in SIBs. It is noted that 156.9 mA h g<sup>–1</sup> can be retained at 5 A g<sup>–1</sup> and 227.0 mA h g<sup>–1</sup> is remained after 500 cycles at 2 A g<sup>–1</sup> in PIBs. The ex situ X‐ray diffraction patterns and transmission electron microscopy pictures are used to confirm the conversion reaction processes of the Zn‐Mn‐Se. Designing high‐performance energy storage materials may benefit greatly from the universal synthesis technology of bimetallic sulfide anodes for enhanced SIBs and PIBs.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="smll202203964-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>Flower‐like Zn‐Mn‐Se carbon nanoflakes (ZMS@FC) with excellent Na<sup>+</sup>/K<sup>+</sup> storage performance are synthesized via selenizing the modified hierarchically metal‐organic frameworks. The ZMS@FC display excellent reversibility and high Na<sup>+</sup>/K<sup>+</sup> diffusion kinetics.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll202203964:smll202203964-gra-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll202203964-gra-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body xml:id="smll202203964-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="smll202203964-sec-0010"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Facing the depletion of lithium resources, sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) and potassium‐ion batteries (PIBs) gained sufficient momentum owing to the low cost and high abundance as the powerful candidates of next‐generation rechargeable storage system.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202203964-bib-0001 #smll202203964-bib-0002 #smll202203964-bib-0003"><sup>1–3</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Still, the low electrochemical activity of anode materials severed limited the large‐scale commercial application of SIBs and PIBs. However, the large ion radius of Na<sup>+</sup> (1.02 Å) and K<sup>+</sup> (1.37 Å) severe limited the electrochemical activity of anode, while the development of the new kind of anode materials became the primary mission for commercial application of SIBs and PIBs. The transition‐metal selenides (TMSs) were regarded as the powerful candidates for SIBs and PIBs anode materials according to the advantages of extraordinary narrowband gap semiconductor properties and large Na<sup>+</sup>/K<sup>+</sup> insertion interstitial sizes.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202203964-bib-0004 #smll202203964-bib-0005 #smll202203964-bib-0006"><sup>4–6</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>Among various of TMSs, zinc selenide (ZnSe) exhibited remarkable ion storage potential, based on the multiple electron transfers per metal atom of alloying and conversion reactions.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202203964-bib-0007 #smll202203964-bib-0008"><sup>7,8</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, its sluggish kinetics and poor conductivity heavily restricted the rate capability and cycling life of ZnSe electrodes. Thus, it is necessary to further improve the performance of ZnSe as SIBs and PIBs anode materials.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202203964-bib-0009"><sup>9</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Assembling binary‐metal selenides were proposed as an effective way to enhance Na<sup>+</sup>/K<sup>+</sup> storage performance owning to multiple advantages include: 1) produced more selenium vacancies and disorders, resulting in additional redox sites and better Na<sup>+</sup>/K<sup>+</sup> diffusion kinetics; 2) introduced considerable pseudocapacitance from the modified interface, which will offer extra specific capacity and enhance the rate performance; 3) the combination of two metal selenides can offer a satisfying conductivity comparing to mono‐metal selenides.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202203964-bib-0010 #smll202203964-bib-0011 #smll202203964-bib-0012"><sup>10–12</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Therefore, comparing with unary ZnSe electrodes, bimetallic selenide consisting of ZnSe and another suitable metal was expected to provide extraordinary Na<sup>+</sup>/K<sup>+</sup> storage performance.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202203964-bib-0013"><sup>13</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Manganese selenide (MnSe) anode displayed high theoretical specific capacity, low operating voltage, and low toxicity.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202203964-bib-0014"><sup>14</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Moreover, the electrochemistry synergy effect of manganese and zinc has been well proved in transition‐metal oxides.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202203964-bib-0015"><sup>15</sup></link><sup>]</sup> By synthesizing zinc‐manganese binary selenides (Zn‐Mn‐Se), the synergistic effect may show an improved ion storage performance of materials substantially.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202203964-bib-0016 #smll202203964-bib-0017"><sup>16,17</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Unfortunately, suffering from serious volume change and afterward unstable solid electrolyte interphase (SEI) layer, Zn‐Mn‐Se with satisfying rate performance and stable cycling behavior have not been reported as SIBs or PIBs anode yet to our knowledge.</p><p>The hybridization of carbon matrix can alleviate the volume change and substantial increase the electron transfer rate, and then has been investigated as an available strategy.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202203964-bib-0018 #smll202203964-bib-0019 #smll202203964-bib-0020 #smll202203964-bib-0021 #smll202203964-bib-0022 #smll202203964-bib-0023"><sup>18–23</sup></link><sup>]</sup> As effective precursors, metal‐organic frameworks (MOFs) have been employed in fabrication of TMSs carbon compound.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202203964-bib-0024 #smll202203964-bib-0025"><sup>24,25</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The periodically connection by metal ions and organic ligands in nanoscale promoted the homogeneous distribution of TMSs nanoparticles, and the adjustable porous structure dedicates a satisfactory interface to ion transport between anode and electrolyte.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202203964-bib-0026"><sup>26</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Nevertheless, the micro‐structure of MOFs precursors generally presents as regular polyhedron without additional conductive skeleton, which are tended to agglomeration under repeated ion ionsertion/desertion processes.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202203964-bib-0027"><sup>27</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Therefore, diversely suitable low‐dimensional carbon blocks (for example, rGO, CNTs) were an attempt to mix in MOFs nanoarchitectures to assemble hierarchical structure.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202203964-bib-0025 #smll202203964-bib-0028"><sup>25,28</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Limited by the friable connection, the advantages of the low‐dimensional hierarchical structure could not fully develop.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202203964-bib-0029"><sup>29</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Customizing MOFs‐devised nanoparticles with the inherent hierarchical structure would be the highest desirable owing to the appealing structural stability contributed by the tight coupling between metal‐base and carbon.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202203964-bib-0015"><sup>15</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>Inspired by the above considerations, a general strategy was designed to synthesize “flower‐like” porous carbon anchored Zn‐Mn binary selenides (ZMS@FC) hierarchically hybrid materials. As expected, ZMS@FC shows “metal‐like conductivity” and excellent electrochemical activities. Besides, the 2D porous “petals” hierarchical structure play a key role to enhance rate performance and stabilize long cycling life, which not only effectively limited the large volume change but also promoted the electron transfer efficiency and electrolyte wettability. Advantaged from the high reversible conversion reaction and large pseudocapacitive contribution, the as‐synthesized ZMS@FC electrodes delivered an outstanding high specific capacity (541.8 mA h g<sup>–1</sup> at 0.2 A g<sup>–1</sup> in SIBs, 330.2 mA h g<sup>–1</sup> at 0.2 A g<sup>–1</sup> in PIBs), exceptional rate capability (294.4 mA h g<sup>–1</sup> at 10 A g<sup>–1</sup> in SIBs, 156.9 mA h g<sup>–1</sup> at 5 A g<sup>–1</sup> in PIBs) and superior cycling performance (369.6 mA h g<sup>–1</sup> at 2 A g<sup>–1</sup> after 1000 cycles in SIBs, 227.0 mA h g<sup>–1</sup> at 2 A g<sup>–1</sup> after 500 cycles in PIBs). The synthesis strategy of the hierarchical porous architecture and the inherent advantage of bimetal selenides make the ZMS@FC composites become a competitive candidate for anode materials of SIBs and PIBs, and enlighten a novel way to the design of electrode materials.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202203964-sec-0020"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p>The two‐step synthesis of ZMS@FC is a schematic illustration in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll202203964-fig-0001"><b>1</b>a</link>. With the structure controlling reagent (PVP) assisting, Zn<sup>2+</sup> and Mn<sup>2+</sup> ions are cross‐linked with trimesic acid to be self‐assembled into the flower‐like Zn‐Mn‐BTC through the solvothermal method. Then, the selenium power sublimates and permeates into flower‐like Zn‐Mn‐BTC to in suit generate the Zn‐Mn binary selenides nanoparticles after calcination. Meanwhile, the hierarchically porous carbon flakes from the pyrolysis of organics 1) restrict the particles size of Zn‐Mn binary selenides; 2) improve the electronic transfer‐efficiency on the plane; 3) shorten the ion diffusion path on the vertical direction 4) provide the buffer area for volume change of Zn‐Mn binary selenides during charge/discharge.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202203964-bib-0012 #smll202203964-bib-0017 #smll202203964-bib-0030"><sup>12,17,30</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><figure xml:id="smll202203964-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll202203964:smll202203964-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll202203964-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) Schematic representation of the synthesis process of ZMS@FC; b) SEM image of flower‐like Zn‐Mn‐BTC; Morphology characterization of ZMS@FC: c,d) SEM images; e) TEM image; f,g) HRTEM images and corresponding FFT pattern (inset) at different area; h–m) elemental mapping images.</caption></figure><p>The morphology of samples was revealed by SEM and TEM. As shown in Figure <link href="#smll202203964-fig-0001">1b</link>, the flower‐like Zn‐Mn‐BTC shows a hierarchical branched flower‐like 3D morphology consisting of multiple flakes. After subsequently calcining treatments, the ZMS@FC displayed in Figure <link href="#smll202203964-fig-0001">1c</link> generally maintains the flower‐like shape, while the Zn‐Mn‐Se particles are evenly anchored on the carbon flakes. It is worth noticing that the plentiful mesoporous are directly observed on the cross‐section of the “petal” in Figure <link href="#smll202203964-fig-0001">1d,e</link>. The porous carbon flakes are elastic and flexible, which can effectively accommodate volumetric expansion.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202203964-bib-0031"><sup>31</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, without the collaboration of the bimetal ions or PVP, the ZMS@C, MnSe@C, ZnSe@C, and corresponding precursors shown in Figure <link href="#smll202203964-supitem-0001">S1–S3</link>, Supporting Information exhibit reunited‐spherical and stick‐like structure, respectively. The lack of the hierarchical structure would result in unsatisfactory Na<sup>+</sup> and K<sup>+</sup> diffusion ability inside the MnSe@C and ZnSe@C.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202203964-bib-0032"><sup>32</sup></link><sup>]</sup> High‐resolution transmission electron microscopy (HRTEM)images were obtained to further analyze the crystals information of ZMS@FC. The lattice fringes of 3.31 Å in Figure <link href="#smll202203964-fig-0001">1f</link> is corresponding to the (111) plane of Zn<sub>0.697</sub>Mn<sub>0.303</sub>Se, corroborated by the fast Fourier transform (FFT) pattern (inset). The hexagonal Zn<sub>0.71</sub>Mn<sub>0.29</sub>Se phase is also determined by inter‐planar spacing (3.52 Å) and FFT pattern in Figure <link href="#smll202203964-fig-0001">1g</link>. The EDX analysis shown in Figure <link href="#smll202203964-supitem-0001">S4</link>, Supporting Information unveils the existence of the C, N, Se, Mn, and Zn element in ZMS@FC. The atomic rate of Zn:Mn (around 2:1) corresponds to the original ratio of precursor solution, which indicates the Zn<sup>2+</sup> and Mn<sup>2+</sup> ions were transformed into flower‐like Zn‐Mn‐BTC and well retained after calcination. Besides, the element mapping images (Figure <link href="#smll202203964-fig-0001">1h–m</link>) intuitively illustrate the elemental distribution of ZMS@FC. The C and N elements are average distributed on the flake, which affirmed the porous flakes are constituted by nitrogen‐doped carbon. The distribution of Mn, Zn, and Se is consistent with the nanoparticles in Figure <link href="#smll202203964-fig-0001">1h</link>, demonstrate the well‐anchored of Zn‐Mn‐Se particles on carbon flakes as we design.</p><p>Figure <link href="#smll202203964-supitem-0001">S5</link>, Supporting Information exhibit the XRD patterns and FTIR spectra of Zn‐Mn‐BTC. The multifarious peaks shown in Figure <link href="#smll202203964-supitem-0001">S5a</link>, Supporting Information indicate the exist of crystal structure in Zn‐Mn‐BTC. Moreover, the FTIR spectra further support the formation of Zn‐Mn‐BTC. The bands located at 1628 and 1373 cm<sup>–1</sup> are indicated to (COO<sup>–</sup>)<sub>asym</sub> and (COO<sup>–</sup>)<sub>sym</sub>, respectively.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202203964-bib-0033"><sup>33</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The 1577 cm<sup>–1</sup> band is caused by the CC skeletal vibration of benzene groups, while the two stretching vibration bands at around 1442 and 1264 cm<sup>–1</sup> are attributed to the CH<sub>2</sub> deformation and the C–N stretching from PVP, respectively.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202203964-bib-0015"><sup>15</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Moreover, the COZn or COMn stretching vibrations is corresponding to the band of 1104 cm<sup>–1</sup>, and the two vibrational bands at 758 and 715 cm<sup>–1</sup> are attributed to the metal substitution on benzene groups, which can be regarded as the characteristic bands of Zn‐Mn‐BTC.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202203964-bib-0015"><sup>15</sup></link><sup>]</sup> <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll202203964-fig-0002"><b>2</b>a</link> and Figure <link href="#smll202203964-supitem-0001">S6</link>, Supporting Information display the XRD patterns of samples. All the characteristic peaks of ZMS@FC and ZMS@C can be indexed to the phase of Zn<sub>0.697</sub>Mn<sub>0.303</sub>Se (JCPDS No. 79‐0019) and Zn<sub>0.71</sub>Mn<sub>0.29</sub>Se (JCPDS No. 88–2465), suggesting the well‐crystallized Zn‐Mn binary selenides were synthesized as design without by‐product. The XRD patterns of ZnSe@C and MnSe@C were corresponding to the ZnSe (JCPDS No. 37–1463) and α‐MnSe (JCPDS No. 65–6528), respectively. The wide peak located at 1324 cm<sup>–1</sup> in Raman spectra (Figure <link href="#smll202203964-fig-0002">2b</link>) is matching with the D band of carbon, and the peak at 1571 cm<sup>–1</sup> for G band.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202203964-bib-0034"><sup>34</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The <i>I</i><sub>D</sub>/<i>I</i><sub>G</sub> of ZMS@FC is 0.89, which demonstrates the pyrolytic carbon in ZMS@FC mainly exists as amorphous carbon.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202203964-bib-0035"><sup>35</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The sharp peaks located at around 650 cm<sup>–1</sup> and the series of small peaks located at 200–400 cm<sup>–1</sup> are identified as the characteristic peaks of Zn–Mn–Se.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202203964-bib-0007 #smll202203964-bib-0036"><sup>7,36</sup></link><sup>]</sup> To calculate the carbon content of ZMS@FC, TGA analysis was carried out and shown in Figure <link href="#smll202203964-supitem-0001">S7a</link>, Supporting Information. The slight mass lost before 250 °C is ascribed to evaporation of water adsorbed by mesoporous in ZMS@FC, and the subsequent mass increase is caused by the oxidation of Zn—Mn–Se. The rapid mass lost from 400 to 500 °C is corresponding to the oxidation of carbon and sublimation of SeO<sub>2</sub>. The content of carbon in ZMS@FC is calculated to be 46.4% when the rate of Zn and Mn was regarded as 2:1.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202203964-bib-0010"><sup>10</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The N<sub>2</sub> sorption experiment was also conducted to assess the porous features of ZMS@FC. As shown in Figure <link href="#smll202203964-fig-0002">2c</link>, the appearance of the hysteresis loop represents the existence of mesopores in ZMS@FC. The BET surface area is 108.3 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>–1</sup>, and according to BJH models (inset) the average pore size is concentrated in the range of 3–10 nm.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202203964-bib-0037"><sup>37</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Meanwhile, as shown in Figure <link href="#smll202203964-supitem-0001">S8</link>, Supporting Information, the ZnSe@C and MnSe@C displayed less surface area and the average pore size enlarged to 10–70 nm. The ZMS@C exhibit the same pore size distribution compared with ZMS@FC of 3–10 nm. But the surface area only maintained 59.4 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>–1</sup>, which may be caused by the lace of the hierarchically structures. With the high rich porosity, synergistic effect of binary metal selenides, and unique flower‐like carbon conductive network, the ZMS@FC electrodes delivered an excellent electrochemical performance in both SIBs and PIBs. When assemble in SIBs, the ZMS@FC displays an outstanding specific capacity of 541.8 mA h g<sup>–1</sup> at 0.2 A g<sup>–1</sup>, admirable rate performance of 294.4 mA h g<sup>–1</sup> at 10 A g<sup>–1</sup>, and prolonged stability of 369.6 mA h g<sup>–1</sup> after 1000 loops at 2 A g<sup>–1</sup>. When assemble in PIBs, the ZMS@FC displays a high reversible specific capacity of 330.2 mA h g<sup>–1</sup> at 0.2 A g<sup>–1</sup> and satisfied rate performance of 156.9 mA h g<sup>–1</sup> at 5 A g<sup>–1</sup>. The dynamic capability is mainly dedicated to the intercalation of pseudocapacitive behavior. Ex suit XRD and ex suit TEM characterization confirmed the Na storage and K storage mechanism and high reversibility of ZMS@FC. Overall, the ZMS@FC has exhibited supreme performance as a promising candidate for SIBs and PIBs anode, and the synthesis strategy in this study will open a new way to enlighten the application of bimetallic selenides in anode materials.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202203964-sec-0040"><title type="main">Experimental Section</title><section xml:id="smll202203964-sec-0050" numbered="no"><section xml:id="smll202203964-sec-0060" numbered="no"><section xml:id="smll202203964-sec-0070" numbered="no"><title type="main">Materials</title><p>Zinc nitrate hexahydrate (Zn(NO3)2·6H2O, AR, Aladdin), Manganese nitrate tetrahydrate (Mn(NO3)2·4H2O, AR, Aladdin), trimesic acid (H<sub>3</sub>BTC, AR, Aladdin), polyvinyl pyrrolidone (PVP, Mw = 30 000, Sinopharm), <i>N, N</i>‐dimethylformamide (DMF, AR, Sinopharm), and selenium powder (Se, 99.9%, Aladdin), Sodium hexacyanoferrate (Na<sub>4</sub>Fe(CN)<sub>6</sub>·10H<sub>2</sub>O, AR, Macklin).</p></section><section xml:id="smll202203964-sec-0080" numbered="no"><title type="main">Preparation of Flower‐Like Zn‐Mn‐BTC</title><p>Typically, 2 mmol H<sub>3</sub>BTC, 1 mmol Zn(NO3)2·6H2O, 0.5 mmol Mn(NO3)2·4H2O, and 3 g PVP were dissolved into 80 ml mixed solvents (ethanol/DMF, with a volume ratio of 1:1) under magnetic stirring. Then. the transparent solution was heated at 160 °C for 6 h in a 100 mL Teflon‐lined stainless‐steel autoclave. Subsequently, the solids were separated by centrifugation and washed with ethanol several times. The resultant product was dried at 80 °C overnight and named as Zn‐Mn‐BTC.</p><p>For comparison, the granular Zn‐Mn‐BTC, Mn‐BTC, and Zn‐BTC were synthesized in the same preparation process except without adding the PVP, Zn(NO3)2·6H2O, and Mn(NO3)2·4H2O, respectively.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202203964-sec-0090" numbered="no"><title type="main">Preparation of ZMS@FC</title><p>The as‐prepared flower‐like Zn‐Mn‐BTC and Se with a weight ratio of 1:2 were put in the two ends of the corundum porcelain boat, respectively. Then the porcelain boat was placed into a tubular furnace and annealed at 600 °C for 2 h in Ar atmosphere with ramping rate of 3 °C min<sup>–1</sup>. The ZMS@FC was obtained after natural cooling. The ZMS@C, MnSe@C, and ZnSe@C were assembled in the same selenization process.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202203964-sec-0100" numbered="no"><title type="main">Preparation of PB</title><p>6 mmol Na<sub>4</sub>Fe(CN)<sub>6</sub>·10H<sub>2</sub>O was dissolved in 300 ml dilute HCl solution (0.12 mol L<sup>−1</sup>) and heated at 60 °C for 4 h with magnetic stirring. The PB was collected by filtration and dried at 80 °C overnight.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202203964-sec-0110" numbered="no"><title type="main">Materials Characterization</title><p>The scanning electron microscopy (Helios Nanolab G3 UC, SEM) and transmission electron microscopy (Titan G2 60–300, TEM) were carried out to observe the morphology of samples. Fourier transform infrared (FTIR) spectra were measured by Thermo Scientific Nicolet iS20. The crystallographic information was obtained by X‐ray diffraction (Rigaku D/max 2550, XRD,). Thermogravimetric analysis (Netzsch STA 409 EP, heating rate 10°C min<sup>–1</sup>, TGA) and Raman spectroscopy (Renishaw, Via 2000) were performed to analyze the chemical states. The chemical bonds of samples were evaluated through X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (Thermo ESCALAB 250XI, XPS) analysis. N<sub>2</sub> adsorption/desorption isotherm was recorded from Brunauer–Emmett–Teller test (USA Tristar 3020, BET) while the Barrett–Joyner–Halenda (BJH) method was conducted to calculate the pore size distributions.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202203964-sec-0120" numbered="no"><title type="main">Electrochemical Characterization</title><p>To prepare the working electrodes, the slurry made by homogeneously mixing the active materials with carboxymethyl cellulose sodium and acetylene black (mass ratio: 8:1:1) into deionized water was coated on copper foils and dried at 60 °C for 8 h. The loading density of active materials in each electrode was around 0.8 mg cm<sup>–2</sup>. The CR2032 coin cell was assembled in Ar‐filled glove‐box with the glass microfiber (Whatman GF/D) as a separator. The electrolyte was 1.0 M NaCF<sub>3</sub>SO<sub>3</sub> dissolved in DEGDME for SIBs, and 1.0 M KPF<sub>6</sub> dissolved in EC and DEC (1:1 vol%) for PIBs. When assembled in half cell, the counter electrodes were sodium and potassium for SIBs and PIBs, respectively. For the full‐cells, the cathode was prepared at the similar process with PB as active materials and aluminum foils as collector. The galvanostatic charge and discharge and long cycling properties were operated within a voltage window of 0.15–3 V (vs Na/Na<sup>+</sup>) on the Land CT2001A battery testing system. The electrochemical impedance spectrum (frequency set from 100 kHz to 0.01 Hz, EIS) test and cyclic voltammetry (CV) were measured on electrochemical workstation (CHI 760E).</p></section></section></section></section><section xml:id="smll202203964-sec-0130" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>The authors acknowledge the support from the Natural Science Foundation of Hunan Province, China (2018JJ2513), the Key Project of Research Foundation of Education Bureau of Hunan Province, China (18A378), and National Natural Science Foundation of China (U19A2088).</p></section><section xml:id="smll202203964-sec-0140" type="conflictOfInterest" numbered="no"><title type="main">Conflict of Interest</title><p>The authors declare no conflict of interest.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202203964-sec-0150" type="openResearch" numbered="no"><section xml:id="smll202203964-sec-0160" type="dataAvailability" numbered="no"><title type="main">Data Availability Statement</title><p>Research data are not shared.</p></section></section><bibliography xml:id="smll202203964-bibl-0001" style="numbered"><bib xml:id="smll202203964-bib-0001"><citation type="journal" xml:id="smll202203964-cit-0001"><author><givenNames>D.
