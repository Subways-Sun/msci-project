numbered="no"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Sodium‐based dual‐ions batteries (SDIBs) have attracted increasing research interests as they hold tremendous potential in a variety of fields thanks to their high operating voltage (i.e., &gt;3.0 V), environmental benignity, and natural abundance of sodium source.<link href="#cssc202201200-bib-0001 #cssc202201200-bib-0005"><sup>[1,2]</sup></link> One of the key challenges for the development of high‐performance SDIBs is to enhance Na<sup>+</sup> storage capacity and reaction kinetics of the anode.<link href="#cssc202201200-bib-0006"><sup>[3]</sup></link> Owing to the relatively large size of Na<sup>+</sup>, the intercalation/deintercalation of Na<sup>+</sup> tends to cause sluggish solid‐state ion transport, and even severe structure collapse, resulting in unsatisfactory electrochemical performance.<link href="#cssc202201200-bib-0009"><sup>[4]</sup></link> Therefore, it is highly desirable to explore newly anode materials with electrochemical properties of high capacity, fast kinetic, and robust structure durability.</p><p>Two‐dimensional (2D) layered materials have been widely studied as anode materials of the alkali‐ions battery owing to the weak interlayer van der Waals forces, which would afford permeable slit‐shaped channels for the fast Na<sup>+</sup> motility.<link href="#cssc202201200-bib-0012"><sup>[5]</sup></link> Among them, tungsten disulfide (WS<sub>2</sub>) is of particular interest since it has a relatively wide interlaminar spacing (6.2 Å) and a theoretical capacity (670 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>).<link href="#cssc202201200-bib-0016"><sup>[6]</sup></link> Nevertheless, WS<sub>2</sub> as Na‐storage electrode faces several critical issues: 1) pure WS<sub>2</sub> layers are prone to aggregation and thus decrease the reactive sites; 2) the poor electronic conductivity likely slow sluggish reaction kinetics; 3) the large volumetric change upon (de‐)insertion of Na<sup>+</sup> leads to the pulverization, and finally the rapid capacity fading.<link href="#cssc202201200-bib-0019"><sup>[7]</sup></link></p><p>To develop ideal WS<sub>2</sub> anode, a diversity of strategies has been proposed to address the above issues. For instance, decreasing the number of layers and enlarging the interlaminar distance of WS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets were previously reported to be conducive to improve the associated electrochemical performance, because few‐layered and interlayer‐expanded WS<sub>2</sub> can reduce Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion resistance and facilitate the reaction kinetic.<link href="#cssc202201200-bib-0020"><sup>[7a]</sup></link> In addition, incorporating WS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets onto conductive substrate was also demonstrated to be another effective pathway to ameliorate its capability of Na<sup>+</sup> storage.<link href="#cssc202201200-bib-0022"><sup>[8]</sup></link> Despite great improvement made so far, there is still great room to improve the WS<sub>2</sub>/C based anode that confront problems regarding how to prevent the peeling off WS<sub>2</sub> from conductive substrate.<link href="#cssc202201200-bib-0026"><sup>[9]</sup></link> In order to reinforce the stability and integrity of electrodes, encapsulating active‐materials within conductive carbon is a feasible route,<link href="#cssc202201200-bib-0031"><sup>[10]</sup></link> because such “sandwich‐like” space‐confined architectures can not only effectively stabilize structure by suppressing aggregation and pulverization, but also is conducive to facilitate electron transfer and enhance the utilization efficiency of active materials.<link href="#cssc202201200-bib-0034"><sup>[11]</sup></link></p><p>Here, we reported a facile synthetic strategy to construct one‐dimensional (1D) tubular nanohybrids composed of building blocks of carbon‐film wrapped ultrathin WS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets onto carbon nanotubes (WS<sub>2</sub>/C@CNTs). The supporting carbon nanotubes and carbon‐film can provide a double‐carbon confined space for the immobilization of WS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets, which endow them with numerous desired features, such as improved structure stability, conductive network, and enlarged interlayer spacing. The WS<sub>2</sub>/C@CNTs nanohybrids manifest an impressively electrochemical performance with considerably high capacity, excellent cycling stability as well as high rate capability. We illustrate its application feasibility in sodium‐based dual ion batteries by employing WS<sub>2</sub>/C@CNTs as anode and expanded graphite (EG) as cathode, which shows high stability with a specific capacity of 130 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 500 cycles at 1 A g<sup>−1</sup>.</p></section><section xml:id="cssc202201200-sec-0002" numbered="no"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p>Figure <link href="#cssc202201200-fig-0001">1a</link> schematically illustrates the formation procedure of tubular structural WS<sub>2</sub>/C@CNTs composite. First, the polypyrrole nanotubes (PPy NTs) (Figure S1) as precursors were prepared, the in situ growth of carbonaceous‐film (C<sub>f</sub>) coating WS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets onto the PPy NTs can be implemented through a hydrothermal process, resulting in the formation of WS<sub>2</sub>/C<sub>f</sub>@PPy NTs precursors (Figure S2), which were then annealed under a flowing H<sub>2</sub>/Ar to obtain the WS<sub>2</sub>/C@CNTs products. For comparison, the sample without C (WS<sub>2</sub>@CNTs, Figure S3), and pure flower‐like WS<sub>2</sub> nanospheres (WS<sub>2</sub>NS, Figure S4) were also synthesized.</p><figure xml:id="cssc202201200-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18645631:media:cssc202201200:cssc202201200-fig-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/cssc202201200-fig-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Schematic illustration of the fabrication process for the WS<sub>2</sub>/C@CNTs composites (a). SEM images (b), TEM images (c, e), High‐resolution TEM image (e) and elemental mapping images (e, g) of the WS<sub>2</sub>/C@CNTs.</p></caption></figure><p>Field emission scanning electron microscopy (FESEM) and transmission electron microscopy (TEM) are employed to analyze the structural features of the WS<sub>2</sub>/C@CNTs composite. As shown in Figure <link href="#cssc202201200-fig-0001">1b</link>, one can observe that the WS<sub>2</sub>/C@CNTs present hollow tubular structure with a diameter of 500 nm and a length of around 5 μm in length. Figure <link href="#cssc202201200-fig-0001">1c</link> and <link href="#cssc202201200-fig-0001">d</link> present the TEM images in a relatively low magnification, one can observe the interconnected nanosheets are uniformly anchored onto the surface of PPy‐derived carbon nanotubes, forming an open and hierarchical architecture. The high‐resolution TEM (HRTEM) image in Figure <link href="#cssc202201200-fig-0001">1e</link> demonstrates the WS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets are few‐layer structures with an adjacent lattice fringes of approximately 0.68 nm corresponding to the (002) plane of WS<sub>2</sub>. Of note is that a thin layer of amorphous carbon, with a thickness less 3 nm, uniformly decorating on the surface of WS<sub>2</sub> nanosheet. Furthermore, as shown in the elemental mapping of the WS<sub>2</sub>/C@CNTs (Figure <link href="#cssc202201200-fig-0001">1f</link>,<link href="#cssc202201200-fig-0001">g</link> and S5), elemental signals of W, S, C, and N are uniformly distributed throughout the tubular structure, further confirming the hybrid nanosheets decorating tubular structure.</p><p>Figure <link href="#cssc202201200-fig-0002">2a</link> displays the X‐ray diffractometry (XRD) pattern of WS<sub>2</sub>‐contained materials. All samples display similar profiles and their reflection peaks are well accorded with the standard PDF of 2H‐WS<sub>2</sub> (JCPDS No. 08–0237).<link href="#cssc202201200-bib-0023"><sup>[8a]</sup></link> Comparing with the WS<sub>2</sub> NS and the WS<sub>2</sub>@CNTs, the (002) planes reflection of WS<sub>2</sub>/C@CNTs slightly shift negatively to lower angles (≈12.8°), implying the expansion in the interlayer spacings, which might be related to the intercalation of carbon layers into the WS<sub>2</sub> gallery.<link href="#cssc202201200-bib-0033"><sup>[10b]</sup></link> This result matches well with TEM observation. The Raman spectrum of the WS<sub>2</sub>/C@CNTs composites is presented in Figure <link href="#cssc202201200-fig-0002">2b</link>, There are two peaks located at approximately 353 and 420 cm<sup>−1</sup> in the WS<sub>2</sub>/C@CNTs, corresponding to the in‐plane E<sup>1</sup><sub>2g</sub> and out‐of‐plane A<sub>1g</sub> vibration modes of W−S bands, respectively.<link href="#cssc202201200-bib-0038"><sup>[12]</sup></link> In addition, the peaks at approximately 1347 and 1594 cm<sup>−1</sup> belong to the D‐band and G‐band of the carbon, respectively, and the I<sub>D</sub>/I<sub>G</sub> value of 0.92 for WS<sub>2</sub>/C@CNTs indicates the presence of defective structure due to N‐doping.<link href="#cssc202201200-bib-0039"><sup>[13]</sup></link></p><figure xml:id="cssc202201200-fig-0002"><label>2</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18645631:media:cssc202201200:cssc202201200-fig-0002"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/cssc202201200-fig-0002-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>XRD pattern (a), Raman spectra (b), and TGA results (c) for the WS<sub>2</sub>‐containing active materials. High‐resolution XPS spectrum (d–f) of W 4 f (d), S 2p (e), and C 1s (f) for the WS<sub>2</sub>/C@CNTs composites.</p></caption></figure><p>The surface chemical components of the samples were further investigated by X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS). The survey spectrum clearly displays the co‐existence of W, S, C, and N signals in the WS<sub>2</sub>/C@CNTs sample (Figure S6). For the high‐resolution W <i>4 f</i> region (Figure <link href="#cssc202201200-fig-0002">2c</link>), two distinct signal peaks located at approximately 35.1 and 33.0 eV can be observed, which are attributed to the W 4f<sub>5/2</sub> and W 4f<sub>7/2</sub> peaks of +4 valence state of Mo, respectively, the weak peak of W 4f<sub>3/2</sub> at 36.9 eV indicates the existence of W−O bond due to the partial surface oxidation of samples.<link href="#cssc202201200-bib-0017"><sup>[6a]</sup></link> Figure <link href="#cssc202201200-fig-0002">2d</link> is the high‐resolution spectrum of S 2p, where one can observe the splitting signals are positioned at approximately 163.9 and 165.2 eV, referring to the S 2p<sub>3/2</sub> and S 2p<sub>1/2</sub>, respectively.<link href="#cssc202201200-bib-0025"><sup>[8c]</sup></link> In addition, the N 1s spectra (Figure <link href="#cssc202201200-fig-0002">2e</link>) also reveals the existence of bonding interaction between C and N atoms, i.e., graphitic‐N (≈401.9 eV), pyrrolic‐N (≈400.8 eV), and pyridinic‐N (≈399.1 eV).<link href="#cssc202201200-bib-0040"><sup>[14]</sup></link> It is interesting to note that the major form of N‐doing in WS<sub>2</sub>/C@CNTs is pyrrolic and pyridinic N, which are considered to be more active for adsorption of Na<sup>+</sup>.<link href="#cssc202201200-bib-0041"><sup>[15]</sup></link> For the deconvoluted spectrum of C 1s, the bonding signals at 284.1, 285.3, 286.1 and 287.2 eV are identified as C−C, C−N, C−O and C=O, respectively (Figure <link href="#cssc202201200-fig-0002">2f</link>).<link href="#cssc202201200-bib-0042"><sup>[16]</sup></link> The weight content of carbon in WS<sub>2</sub>/C@CNTs is evaluated to be approximately 8.7 wt % according to the thermogravimetric analysis (Figure S7).</p><p>The electrochemical sodium storage behaviors of the set of samples were studied using 2032 half‐cell. Figure <link href="#cssc202201200-fig-0003">3a</link> describes the initial three cyclic voltammogram (CV) curves of WS<sub>2</sub>/C@CNTs at 0.1 mV s<sup>−1</sup>. During the first cathodic scan, one can observe that there are three peaks at around 0.8, 0.6, and 0.1 V, corresponding to the formation of nonstoichiometric compound Na<sub>x</sub>WS<sub>2</sub>,<link href="#cssc202201200-bib-0020"><sup>[7a]</sup></link> solid electrolyte interface (SEI) film, and the reduction from Na<sub>x</sub>WS<sub>2</sub> to metallic W and Na<sub>2</sub>S, respectively.<link href="#cssc202201200-bib-0017 #cssc202201200-bib-0043"><sup>[6a,17]</sup></link> The initial oxidation peak in the anodic scan at 1.8 V is indicative of the reverse reaction of Mo and Na<sub>2</sub>S to MoS<sub>2</sub>. The CV curves show a high‐degree overlap in the subsequent cycles, implying good reversibility and stability of WS<sub>2</sub>/C@CNTs electrode. The galvanostatic charge/discharge curves (GCD, Figure <link href="#cssc202201200-fig-0003">3b</link>) also agree well with the redox behavior in CV results.</p><figure xml:id="cssc202201200-fig-0003"><label>3</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18645631:media:cssc202201200:cssc202201200-fig-0003"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/cssc202201200-fig-0003-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>CV curves (a) at 0.1 mV s<sup>−1</sup> and (b) charge/discharge curves at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> for WS<sub>2</sub>/C@CNTs composites. As depicted in Figure <link href="#cssc202201200-fig-0003">3c</link>, the WS<sub>2</sub>/C@CNTs exhibit the discharging and charging capacities of 725 and 490 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively, corresponding to an initial Coulombic efficiency (ICE) of 67.2 %, which is higher than those of WS<sub>2</sub>@CNT (61.1 %) and WS<sub>2</sub>NS (57.2 %). The structural merits, including enlarged interlayer spacing, high electrical conductivity and mechanical stability arising from dual‐carbon confined structure, enabled the WS<sub>2</sub>/C@CNTs composites to present an improved electrochemical performance as anodes for SDIBs when pairing with EG cathode. The WS<sub>2</sub>/C@CNTs‖EG SDIB exhibited excellent rate behavior and durable long‐term cycling life with considerably high capacity. The design strategy based on a dual‐carbon confined construction could overcome the problems associated with conversion reactions and may inspire the development of a wide range of high‐performance electrode materials.</p></section><section xml:id="cssc202201200-sec-0004" numbered="no"><title type="main">Experimental Section</title><section xml:id="cssc202201200-sec-0005" numbered="no"><title type="main">Materials synthesis</title><section xml:id="cssc202201200-sec-0006" numbered="no"><title type="main">Preparation of polypyrrole (PPy) nanotubes</title><p>The polypyrrole (PPy) nanotubes were synthesized according to the previous report. Typically, 400 mg of FeCl<sub>3</sub> ⋅ 6H<sub>2</sub>O and 50 mg of methyl orange solution was dissolved into 30 mL DI‐water through vigorous stirring. Then, 150 μL of pyrrole monomer was added to the above mixture dropwise and reacted for 24 h at room temperature. Afterwards, the resulting black precipitate was collected, washed repeatedly with distilled water and ethanol for several times, and then dried at in a vacuum oven overnight.</p></section><section xml:id="cssc202201200-sec-0007" numbered="no"><title type="main">Preparation of WS<sub>2</sub>/C@CNTs</title><p>The as‐obtained PPy nanotubes were suppressed into the mixing solution of DMF (15 mL) and DI‐water (15 mL) under ultrasonic. Then, 150 mg of Na<sub>2</sub>WO<sub>4</sub> ⋅ 2H<sub>2</sub>O, 300 mg of thiourea, 100 mg <sc>d</sc>‐glucose and 100 mg of NaBH<sub>4</sub> were added into the above solution under stirring for 30 min. After that, the mixture was transferred into a 50 mL of Teflon‐lined stainless‐steel autoclave and keep at 230 °C for 20 h. The product (WS<sub>2</sub>/C<sub>f</sub>@PPy NTs) was collected by centrifugation followed by washing with deionized water and ethanol. Finally, the power was annealed at 700 °C for 2 h in Ar/H<sub>2</sub> atmosphere with a rate of 3 °C min<sup>−1</sup> to obtain the WS<sub>2</sub>/C@CNTs product. For comparison, the procedure for the preparation of WS<sub>2</sub>@CNTs were similar to that described above in the absence of the <sc>d</sc>‐glucose. The pure WS<sub>2</sub> nanospheres were also synthesized by the same hydrothermal method without adding PPy nanotubes and D‐glucose.</p></section></section><section xml:id="cssc202201200-sec-0008" numbered="no"><title type="main">Materials characterizations</title><p>The XRD patterns were recorded on a Miniflex600 powder X‐ray diffractometer using Cu Kα radiation in the 2<i>θ</i> range from 10° to 70° at a scan rate of 0.05° s<sup>−1</sup>. The morphologies of the samples were characterized by FESEM (Hitachi SU‐8020), TEM, and HRTEM (Tecnai F20). Energy dispersive X‐ray spectroscopy analysis was carried out in the TEM. Nitrogen adsorption−desorption isotherms and Brunauer‐Emmett‐Teller surface area measurements were performed with an automated gas sorption analyzer (Hiden IGA100B). The measurements were performed at 77 K, and the pore‐size distribution was derived using the density functional theory model. TGA of the sample was recorded with a thermogravimetric analyzer (Netzsch STA449F3) in air with a heating rate of 10 °C min<sup>−1</sup> from room temperature to 800 °C. The Raman spectrum was recorded on a Renishaw in via Raman microscope (532 nm). XPS (ESCALAB 250) was used to determine the components on the surface of the samples. The inductively coupled plasma atomic emission spectroscopy (ICP‐AES) measurements were tested on an Optima 7300 DV. The elemental analysis (EA) measurements were tested on elemental analysis mode (Vario EL Cube). The defect structure was measured by Electron paramagnetic resonance (EPR) spectra (Bruker‐E500).</p></section><section xml:id="cssc202201200-sec-0009" numbered="no"><title type="main">Electrochemical measurements</title><p>The electrode (WS<sub>2</sub>/C@CNTs anode or EG cathode) was fabricated by mixing the slurry consisted of 80 wt % active materials, 10 wt % acetylene black and 10 wt % Polyvinylidene fluoride binder dissolved in N‐Methyl pyrrolidone (NMP) solution. Then the slurry was coated onto a Cu foil for WS<sub>2</sub>/C@CNTs anode and Al foil for EG cathode, followed by drying in a vacuum at 80 °C for 24 h. The mass loading of the active material on each anode electrode (diameter: 12 mm) was approximately 1.2 mg cm<sup>−2</sup>; as for cathode, it was approximately 5 mg cm<sup>−2</sup>. For the full cells, the mass ratio of EG cathode/ WS<sub>2</sub>/C@CNTs anode was fixed at 4 : 1. All the cells were assembled in a glove box filled with argon atmosphere (H<sub>2</sub>O &lt;0.5 ppm, O<sub>2</sub> &lt;0.5 ppm). The electrolyte with composition of 3.0 <sc>m</sc> NaPF<sub>6</sub> in ethylene carbonate/ethyl methyl carbonate/dimethyl carbonate was used in dual‐ion cells.</p><p>The electrochemical performances of the batteries were tested on Land battery testing system (LANDTE Co., China) with the potential window of 0.01–3 V for SIBs and 1.5–4.7 V for SDIBs, and CV tests were measured on a CHI604E electrochemical station (CHI instrument Co., Shanghai, China), respectively. For the long‐term cycling performance at high specific currents, the SDIBs were first activated at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> for ten cycles and then was operated at a higher specific current for a long cycle test.
