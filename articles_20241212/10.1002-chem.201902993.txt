However, suitable anode materials with high‐rate and stable cycling performance are still needed to promote their practical application. Herein, three‐dimensional Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> nanowire arrays with enriched surface vacancies endowed by phosphorus doping are reported. As anodes for sodium‐ion batteries, they deliver a high specific capacity of 290 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>at 0.2 C, good rate capability (50 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>at 20 C), and stable cycling capability (98 % capacity retention over 3100 cycles at 20 C). The superior electrochemical performance is attributed to the synergistic effects of the nanowire arrays and phosphorus doping. The rational structure can provide convenient channels to facilitate ion/electron transport and improve the capacitive contributions. Moreover, the phosphorus‐doping‐induced surface vacancies not only provide more active sites but also improve the intrinsic electrical conductivity of Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub>, which will enable electrode materials with excellent sodium storage performance. This work may provide an effective strategy for the synthesis of other anode materials with fast electrochemical reaction kinetics and good sodium storage performance.</p></abstract><abstract type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p><b>Superior sodium storage</b>: Three‐dimensional Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> nanowire arrays with enriched surface vacancies endowed by phosphorus doping were synthesized by a controllable hydrothermal process and subsequent phosphidating annealing. As anode materials for sodium‐ion batteries, they deliver superior sodium storage performance in terms of high specific capacity, stable cycling, and good rate performance (see scheme).<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:09476539:media:chem201902993:chem201902993-toc-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/chem201902993-toc-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="chem201902993-sec-0001" numbered="no"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>With the consumption of fossil energy and environmental deterioration, society needs to develop more renewable energy.<link href="#chem201902993-bib-0001 #chem201902993-bib-0002 #chem201902993-bib-0003"/> For more convenient and efficient use of renewable energy, it is an effective strategy to develop energy storage devices,<link href="#chem201902993-bib-0002 #chem201902993-bib-0004"/> such as lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs),<link href="#chem201902993-bib-0004 #chem201902993-bib-0005 #chem201902993-bib-0006"/> sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs),<link href="#chem201902993-bib-0007 #chem201902993-bib-0008 #chem201902993-bib-0009"/> potassium‐ion batteries,<link href="#chem201902993-bib-0010 #chem201902993-bib-0011 #chem201902993-bib-0012"/> and zinc‐ion batteries.<link href="#chem201902993-bib-0013 #chem201902993-bib-0014 #chem201902993-bib-0015"/>. Among them, due to their advantageous energy density and cycling life, LIBs have been widely used in electric cars, laptops, cellular telephones, and other fields.<link href="#chem201902993-bib-0002 #chem201902993-bib-0016 #chem201902993-bib-0017"/> However, the relatively high cost and scarcity of lithium resources limit its application in large‐scale energy storage systems.<link href="#chem201902993-bib-0018 #chem201902993-bib-0019"/> As an alternative, SIBs are favored by researchers because of their similar properties to LIBs, and sodium resources are abundant and evenly distributed in the world.<link href="#chem201902993-bib-0008 #chem201902993-bib-0020"/> The graphite anode materials commonly used for LIBs show unsatisfactory sodium storage performance, which is attributed to the larger ionic radius of Na<sup>+</sup> (Na<sup>+</sup>: 0.102 nm; Li<sup>+</sup>: 0.076 nm), which results in in sluggish reaction kinetics and large volume expansion during cycling.<link href="#chem201902993-bib-0007 #chem201902993-bib-0020"/> Therefore, it is still of great interest to pursue suitable anode materials with high‐rate and stable cycling performance for SIBs.</p><p>Recently, Ti‐based materials have attracted much attention because of their environmental benignity, abundant resources, and low cost.<link href="#chem201902993-bib-0021 #chem201902993-bib-0022"/> Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> is a promising anode material for SIBs due to its open layered framework, which is capable of exhibiting high specific capacity and high ionic conductivity.<link href="#chem201902993-bib-0022"/> However, the low electrical conductivity of Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> leads to fast capacity fading and poor cycling stability.<link href="#chem201902993-bib-0023 #chem201902993-bib-0024"/> Turning the surface electron configuration of metal oxides by creating vacancies in the crystal lattice is a highly effective strategy to improve the electrical conductivity and sodium storage performance.<link href="#chem201902993-bib-0025 #chem201902993-bib-0026"/> For instance, Li et al. prepared self‐supported nanotube arrays of sulfur‐doped TiO<sub>2</sub> through electrochemical anodization and subsequent sulfurization, which showed a considerable sodium storage capacity of 136 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> after 4400 cycles at a current density of 3.35 A g<sup>−1</sup>.<link href="#chem201902993-bib-0027"/> Du and co‐workers synthesized a series of lanthanide‐doped Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> that exhibited high reversible capacity and good cycling stability.<link href="#chem201902993-bib-0025"/> The enhanced electrochemical performance is attributed to the introduction of vacancies that can provide more active sites to enhance the electronic conductivity and result in improved electrochemical reaction kinetics. In addition, self‐supported 3D Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> nanowire arrays grown on Ti foil have been reported to overcome the above‐mentioned drawback of Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub>.<link href="#chem201902993-bib-0026"/> The nanowire‐array structures with rapid electron/ion‐transport pathways enable Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> electrode materials to deliver superior electrochemical performance. In this regard, the introduction of surface vacancies into 3D Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> nanowire arrays will offer synergistic advantages, including the ability to improve the intrinsic electrical conductivity of Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> and shorten the distance for electron/ion transport.</p><p>Herein, self‐supporting 3D phosphorus‐doped Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> nanowire arrays (P‐NTO NWAs) were prepared by simple hydrothermal and annealing routes. As anodes for SIBs, P‐NTO NWAs electrodes deliver a high specific capacity of 290 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>at 0.2 C (1 C=177 mA g<sup>−1</sup>), superb rate capability (50 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>at 20 C), and ultrastable cycling performance (capacity retention of 98 % after 3100 cycles at 20 C), and thus surpass the electrochemical performance of pristine NTO NWAs anodes. The superior sodium storage performance can be attributed to the rational design of the 3D hybrid structure. The surface vacancies endowed by the introduction of phosphorus could effectively improve the intrinsic electrical conductivity of Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub>. Furthermore, the 3D nanowire arrays can provide convenient transport paths for electrons/ions, which are is favor of good electrochemical reaction kinetics. Thus, P‐NTO NWAs are a high‐potential anode material for SIBs.</p></section><section xml:id="chem201902993-sec-0002" numbered="no"><title type="main">Experimental Section</title><section xml:id="chem201902993-sec-0003" numbered="no"><title type="main">Synthesis of P‐NTO NWAs</title><p>In a typical procedure, commercial titanium sheets were successively ultrasonicated in acetone, ethanol, and deionized water for 10 min and then dried in air at 60 °C for 12 h. Subsequently, the washed titanium sheets and NaOH (40 mL of a 1 <sc>m</sc> aqueous solution) were transferred to a 50 mL Teflon‐lined autoclave and heated at 220 °C for 4 h. The obtained products and sodium hypophosphite (NaH<sub>2</sub>PO<sub>2</sub> H<sub>2</sub>O) were put at two separate positions in a porcelain boat and annealed at 350 <sup>o</sup>C for 2 h in N<sub>2</sub> atmosphere to result in P‐NTO NWAs. For comparison, the NTO NWAs were obtained by a similar route without using NaH<sub>2</sub>PO<sub>2</sub> H<sub>2</sub>O.</p></section><section xml:id="chem201902993-sec-0004" numbered="no"><title type="main">Material characterization</title><p>The crystal structures of NTO and P‐NTO NWAs were studied with a Micro Confocal Raman Microscope (Horiba Jobin Yvon) and Rigaku MiniFlex600 X‐ray diffractometer (current: 20 mA, voltage: 40 kV). The surface elemental composition of NTO and P‐NTO NWAs were analyzed with an X‐ray photoelectron spectrophotometer (Thermo Fisher Escalab 250Xi). The microscopic appearance and microstructure of NTO and P‐NTO NWAs studied by SEM (5 kV, Hitach SU8010 field‐emission electron microscope) and TEM (FEL‐Talos F200S, operated at 200 kV) with energy‐dispersive spectroscopy (EDS) for elemental analysis.</p></section><section xml:id="chem201902993-sec-0005" numbered="no"><title type="main">Electrochemical measurements</title><p>The P‐NTO and NTO NWAs were used directly as the working electrodes (10×10 mm<sup>2</sup>). The 2032‐type half‐batteries were assembled in an argon‐filled glovebox (MBRAUN). The electrolyte was a solution of NaClO<sub>4</sub> (1 <sc>m</sc>) in ethylene carbonate/diethyl carbonate (1/1 v/v) containing 5 wt % fluoroethylene carbonate . Cyclic voltammetry (CV) and electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) were carried out with a Gamry electrochemical station. The galvanostatic charge/discharge data were collected with NEWARE battery testing system at room temperature.</p></section><section xml:id="chem201902993-sec-0006" numbered="no"><title type="main">Computational methods</title><p>All DFT calculations were performed with SIESTA code,<link href="#chem201902993-bib-0028"/> and the exchange‐correlation energy was calculated by using a generalized gradient approximation (GGA) as parametrized by Perdew–Burke‐Ernzerhof (PBE).<link href="#chem201902993-bib-0029"/> A double‐zeta plus polarization basis set of atomic orbitals with an energy cutoff of 180 Ry was used to calculate the Hamiltonian element, with the cutoff radius of the atomic orbitals determined by an energy shift of 270 meV. All the atom positions and the cell parameters were fully relaxed by using conjugate gradient minimization until the Hellmann–Feynman force acting on each atom was less than 0.02 eV Å<sup>−1</sup>. In all cases, spin‐polarized considerations were taken into account. A Monkhorst–Pack grid with a 4×8×4 k‐point mesh was used for integration in the irreducible Brillouin zone for the relaxation of the unit cell of bulk NTO. A 2×2 supercell of NTO was used to model doping with P. The atomic structures were analyzed by using the VESTA code.<link href="#chem201902993-bib-0030"/></p></section></section><section xml:id="chem201902993-sec-0007" numbered="no"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p>The fabrication process of P‐NTO NWAs is schematically illustrated in Figure <link href="#chem201902993-fig-0001"/>. The first step is the synthesis of NTO NWAs on Ti foil through a simple hydrothermal process in alkaline solution. The second step is the fabrication of P‐doped NTO NWAs by low‐temperature phosphidation of as‐synthesized NTO NWAs at 350 °C under N<sub>2</sub> atmosphere in the presence of NaH<sub>2</sub>PO<sub>2</sub><b>⋅</b>H<sub>2</sub>O. For comparison, pristine NTO NWAs were synthesized by a similar route without NaH<sub>2</sub>PO<sub>2</sub><b>⋅</b>H<sub>2</sub>O (Figure S1, Supporting Information). SEM images of the NTO NWAs (Figure S2, Supporting Information) show that the entire surface of the Ti foil is coated with NTO NWAs.</p><figure xml:id="chem201902993-fig-0001"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:09476539:media:chem201902993:chem201902993-fig-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/chem201902993-fig-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Schematic illustration of the preparation of P‐NTO NWAs on Ti substrate.</p></caption></figure><p>To prove the successful synthesis of NTO NWAs, XRD was performed. As shown in Figure S3 (Supporting Information), the (002), (201), and (020) characteristic peaks are in accordance with the standard XRD data card of NTO (PDF No. 31‐1329),<link href="#chem201902993-bib-0026"/> except for the strong peaks originating from the Ti foil. After the phosphidating calcination process, the absence of any peaks from impurities indicated high purity of the as‐synthesized P‐NTO NWAs. In the Raman survey spectra of NTO NWAs and P‐NTO NWAs (Figure S4, Supporting Information), all the peaks clearly corresponded to the NTO framework.<link href="#chem201902993-bib-0031 #chem201902993-bib-0032"/> Compared with NTO NWAs, the survey spectrum of P‐NTO NWAs shows obvious band broadening, which is related to the formation of oxygen vacancies.<link href="#chem201902993-bib-0026 #chem201902993-bib-0033"/> Furthermore, the P‐NTO NWAs were characterized by SEM. As shown in Figure <link href="#chem201902993-fig-0002"/> a and b, the as‐prepared 3D P‐NTO NWAs are composed of nanowires with an average diameter of about 40–140 nm. TEM images of 3D P‐NTO NWAs (Figure <link href="#chem201902993-fig-0002"/> c further confirmed the 1D structure of the P‐NTO nanowires, which consistent with the SEM images. In Figure <link href="#chem201902993-fig-0002"/> d, which shows an HRTEM image of P‐NTO NWAs, the clear lattice fringe with interplanar spacing of 0.68 nm corresponds to the (<inlineGraphic href="graphic/chem201902993-math-0001.png" alt="urn:x-wiley:09476539:media:chem201902993:chem201902993-math-0001"/>01) plane of the NTO phase. The EDS elemental mapping images (Figure <link href="#chem201902993-fig-0002"/> e–i) reveal the Ti, O, Na, and P elements are uniformly distributed in the P‐NTO NWAs, in further confirmation of the successful formation of P‐NTO NWAs.</p><figure xml:id="chem201902993-fig-0002"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:09476539:media:chem201902993:chem201902993-fig-0002"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/chem201902993-fig-0002-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Morphological characterization of P‐NTO NWAs. a), b) SEM, c) TEM, and d) HRTEM images of P‐NTO NWAs. e) TEM image of P‐NTO NWAs and the corresponding EDS elemental mapping images of f) O, g) Ti, h) Na, and i) P.</p></caption></figure><p>To further investigate the electronic states and chemical composition of P‐NTO and NTO NWAs, X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) was employed (Figure <link href="#chem201902993-fig-0003"/> and Figure S5, Supporting Information). The XP survey spectrum (Figure S5, Supporting Information) confirmed the presence of Na, O, Ti, and P elements in P‐NTO NWAs and the absence of the P element in NTO. In Figure <link href="#chem201902993-fig-0003"/> a, the Ti 2p peaks at 458.3 and 464.0 eV are assigned to Ti 2P<sub>3/2</sub> (Ti<sup>IV</sup>) and Ti 2P<sub>1/2</sub> (Ti<sup>IV</sup>), respectively.<link href="#chem201902993-bib-0034 #chem201902993-bib-0035"/></p><figure xml:id="chem201902993-fig-0003"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:09476539:media:chem201902993:chem201902993-fig-0003"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/chem201902993-fig-0003-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>High‐resolution XP spectra. a) Ti 2p of NTO NWAs, b) Ti 2p of P‐NTO NWAs, c) O1s of P‐NTO and NTO NWAs, and d) P 2p of P‐NTO NWAs.</p></caption></figure><p>Interestingly, compared with that of NTO NWAs, the high‐resolution Ti 2p spectrum of P‐NTO NWAs shows two additional Ti<sup>III</sup> peaks (459.1 and 464.9 eV), and the peak position of Ti<sup>IV</sup> is shifted toward higher binding energy, which can be ascribed to the generation of oxygen vacancies by element doping, which results in partial reduction of Ti<sup>IV</sup> (Figure <link href="#chem201902993-fig-0003"/> b).<link href="#chem201902993-bib-0025 #chem201902993-bib-0036"/> The same phenomenon also occurs in the high‐resolution O 1s XP spectrum, as shown in Figure <link href="#chem201902993-fig-0003"/> c. Three peaks at 532.8, 530.7, and 531.3 eV can be assigned to HO−Ti, O‐Ti‐O, and Ti‐O‐P,<link href="#chem201902993-bib-0023 #chem201902993-bib-0026 #chem201902993-bib-0037 #chem201902993-bib-0038"/> respectively, and this further confirms the existence of oxygen vacancies in the P‐NTO NWAs. The vacancies are generated by annealing the NTO NWAs in a facile phosphating process, which leads to the reduction of Ti<sup>IV</sup> to Ti<sup>III</sup> and oxygen vacancies in the NTO structure. The formation of oxygen vacancies is accompanied by production of excess electrons, which can further enhance electrical conductivity in NTO. Moreover, the dopant P atoms also produce abundant vacancies in the NTO structure, which can provide more active sites for Na<sup>+</sup> storage and resulting in highly reversible Na<sup>+</sup> storage capacity. In addition, the peak of P 2p can be due to P<sup>V</sup> in P‐NTO NWAs,<link href="#chem201902993-bib-0039 #chem201902993-bib-0040"/> as shown in Figure <link href="#chem201902993-fig-0003"/> d.</p><p>The electrochemical performance of P‐NTO and NTO NWAs as anode materials in SIBs was evaluated in a half‐cell configuration. The cycling performance of P‐NTO and NTO NWAs electrodes were first tested in a voltage window of 0.01–2.5 V (vs. Na<sup>+</sup>/Na) at 0.6 C (Figure <link href="#chem201902993-fig-0004"/> a). The P‐NTO NWAs electrode could deliver excellent cyclic performance and a high reversible specific capacity of 282 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> after 190 cycles, which is much higher than that of NTO NWAs (131 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>). Electrochemical impedance tests (inset in Figure <link href="#chem201902993-fig-0004"/> a) suggested that the P‐NTO NWAs have much lower charge‐transfer resistance than NTO NWAs, which confirms that the introduction of P can improve the electronic conductivity of NTO. Figure <link href="#chem201902993-fig-0004"/> b shows the first three CV curves of P‐NTO NWAs at a scan rate of 0.1 mV s<sup>−1</sup> in the voltage range of 0.01–2.5 V. In the first cycle, two pairs of redox peaks at 0.19/0.59 and 1.13/1.21 V are observed. The peaks at 0.19/0.59 V originate from Na<sup>+</sup> insertion/extraction in the P‐NTO NWAs.<link href="#chem201902993-bib-0026"/> The cathodic peak at 1.13 V and corresponding anodic peak at 1.21 V are attributable to the existence of low‐valence Ti atoms in P‐NTO NWAs during Na<sup>+</sup> insertion/extraction.<link href="#chem201902993-bib-0035 #chem201902993-bib-0041"/> Compared with the first cycle, the second and third cycles nearly overlap, and this indicates superior stability and reversibility of P‐NTO NWAs electrodes during the charge/discharge process. The first, second, and third charge/discharge voltage profiles at 0.2 C are shown in Figure <link href="#chem201902993-fig-0004"/> c and Figure S6 (Supporting Information). In the first cycle, the charge and discharge specific capacities of the P‐NTO NWAs of 282 and 290 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> give a high initial Coulombic efficiency (CE) of 97 %. Compared with the P‐NTO NWAs, the NTO NWAs exhibit lower specific capacity, that is, the P‐NTO NWAs have better Na<sup>+</sup> storage capability. The rate performance of P‐NTO and NTO NWAs was evaluated at varied current densities ranging from 0.2 to 20 C. As shown in Figure <link href="#chem201902993-fig-0004"/> d, the average reversible specific capacities of P‐NTO NWAs are 292, 283, 224, 158, 105, and 50 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>at current densities of 0.2, 0.5, 1, 2, 5, 10, and 20 C, respectively. Clearly, the P‐NTO NWs have much better rate capability than the NTO NWs (Figure <link href="#chem201902993-fig-0004"/> d). Importantly, the specific capacity of P‐NTO NWAs can return to the initial value after high‐rate tests (Figure <link href="#chem201902993-fig-0004"/> d). To further evaluate the superior electrochemical performance of P‐NTO NWAs, long‐term cycling tests on the P‐NTO NWAs electrode were carried out at 20 C. The formation energies of P‐doped NTO, which are in the range of −1.53 to −1.69 eV, show that P doping can be easily realized. The Ti−P bond length are longer due to the increased atomic radius (Table S1, Supporting Information).</p><p>Insights into sodium‐ion batteries can guide us to improve the electrochemical behavior and design better electrode materials. Pristine NTO is a semiconducting material with a bandgap of 3.23 eV, as shown in Figure <link href="#chem201902993-fig-0006"/> b, but after substitution of the surface O atoms (O1–O4) by P atoms, all P‐doped NTOs show metallic characteristics, and more peaks appear near the Fermi level, as shown in Figure <link href="#chem201902993-fig-0007"/>. This indicates that the doping of P can enhance electron transport by NTO and thus improve the electrochemical behavior, in agreement with our experimental results.</p><figure xml:id="chem201902993-fig-0007"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:09476539:media:chem201902993:chem201902993-fig-0007"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/chem201902993-fig-0007-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>PDOS for substitution of a) O1, b) O2, c) O3, and d) O4 sites with P atoms. The region near the Fermi energy (−1 to 1 eV) is shown below.</p></caption></figure></section><section xml:id="chem201902993-sec-0008" numbered="no"><title type="main">Conclusion</title><p>We have synthesized 3D P‐NTO NWAs by a simple hydrothermal reaction and annealing treatment. As anodes for SIBs, the as‐prepared P‐NTO NWAs deliver a high specific capacity of 282 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.6 C for 190 cycles and an excellent rate capacity of 50 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 20 C for 3100 cycles. The synergetic effect of the 3D structure and surface vacancies results in superior sodium storage performance. The 3D structure of the NWAs reduces the diffusion length of sodium ions and electrons and thus results in improved electrochemical reaction kinetics. Importantly, the surface vacancies endow P‐NTO NWAs with high electrical conductivity. Kinetic analysis further revealed that the P‐NTO NWAs electrode is mainly pseudocapacitive in nature during the sodiation/desodiation process, and this contributes to good rate performance and stable cycling capability. In addition, DFT calculations indicate that the introduction of P effectively improves the intrinsic electrical conductivity of NTO. Combining high specific capacity, stable cycling performance and good rate capability, the as‐synthesized P‐NTO NWAs could be a promising anode material for SIBs.</p></section><section xml:id="chem201902993-sec-0009" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>This research work was financially supported by the National Natural Science Foundation of China (Grant.
