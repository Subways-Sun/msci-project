    </span><span class="bold italic">, Accepted 25th June 2018</span></div><p class="bold italic">First published on 26th June 2018</p><hr><div class="abstract"><h2>Abstract</h2><p>In this work, few-layer SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small> nanosheets confined in a nitrogen-doped graphene sheet composite (SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small>/NGS) are successfully synthesized <span class="italic">via</span> a facile thermal decomposition method. SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small>/NGS demonstrates sufficient nitrogen-doping and full graphene encapsulation. When evaluated as an anode material for lithium/sodium-ion batteries, it exhibits an excellent electrochemical performance.</p></div><hr>
    
      <span>Battery technology has been widely considered as essential to reduce the energy and environmental problems, because it can be coupled with renewable energy and can be readily utilized in numerous applications, such as portable electronics, and transportation and renewable energy storage systems.<a title="Select to navigate to reference" href="#cit1"><sup><span class="sup_ref">1,2</span></sup></a> Among the battery technologies, lithium-ion batteries (LIBs) have been used as the predominant power sources in people's daily lives, due to their high energy density and long cycling life.<a title="Select to navigate to reference" href="#cit3"><sup><span class="sup_ref">3–5</span></sup></a> However, the limited abundance of Li-salt results in the cost increase of LIBs and impedes their further development. Moreover, commercial graphite anodes have a low theoretical capacity of 372 mA h g<small><sup>−1</sup></small>, which is insufficient to satisfy the increasing demand for high power densities. Sodium-ion batteries (SIBs) have attracted tremendous attention and been studied for large-scale energy storage applications owing to the low cost and earth-abundance of Na resources. Nevertheless, due to the larger ionic radius of the Na<small><sup>+</sup></small>-ion (0.97 Å) than the Li<small><sup>+</sup></small>-ion (0.64 Å), the sluggish kinetics of Na<small><sup>+</sup></small> during the insertion/extraction process leads to poor electrochemical performance for SIBs.<a title="Select to navigate to reference" href="#cit6"><sup><span class="sup_ref">6–10</span></sup></a> Therefore, there is a quest for new anode materials to improve the battery technologies in both LIBs and SIBs.</span>
      <p class="otherpara">SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small> with a layered structure and large interlayer spacing is one of the promising anode substitutes for LIBs and SIBs, because of its high theoretical capacity, low toxicity and earth-rich elements.<a title="Select to navigate to reference" href="#cit11"><sup><span class="sup_ref">11,12</span></sup></a> However, SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small> generally suffers from low electronic conductivity, large volume expansion and aggregation during the alloying/dealloying process, which result in problematic cycling stability and rate capability. One effective approach is combining SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small> with a conductive carbon matrix to buffer the volume changes and improve the electronic conductivity. Graphene, one of the various functional carbon materials, has been extensively integrated with tin-based electrodes to improve their cycling performance due to its large surface area, excellent conductivity, chemical stability, and mechanical flexibility.<a title="Select to navigate to reference" href="#cit13"><sup><span class="sup_ref">13–16</span></sup></a> What's more, it is well known that the doping of the graphene matrix with nitrogen atoms can bring about additional favorable properties and improve the electrochemically active sites for Li<small><sup>+</sup></small> and Na<small><sup>+</sup></small>-ion storage.<a title="Select to navigate to reference" href="#cit17"><sup><span class="sup_ref">17,18</span></sup></a> Thus, the integration of nanostructured SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small> with nitrogen-doped graphene would provide an appealing strategy to enhance the electrochemical performance of LIBs and SIBs.</p>
      <p class="otherpara">Herein, we report a facile and unique strategy to fabricate ultrathin SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small> nanosheets with nitrogen-doped graphene networks (SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small>/NGS) <span class="italic">via</span> a simple thermal decomposition method for the first time. The nitrogen-doped graphene matrix is also achieved simultaneously employing thiourea and ultrathin SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small> nanosheets are well incorporated into the graphene matrix. Owing to the synergistic effect of the unique nanostructured SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small> and NGS architecture, the as-prepared hybrid composite exhibits superior rate performance and high reversible capacities when used as an anode for LIBs and SIBs. This work could be extensively applied to synthesize other transition metal sulfides and boost the electrochemical performance in the related fields of energy storage.</p>
      <p class="otherpara">The synthesis process of the SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small>/NGS hybrid composite is illustrated in <a title="Select to navigate to figure" href="#imgfig1">Fig. 1</a>. Graphene oxide (GO) powders are well dispersed in deionized water and the oxygenic groups can tightly absorb Sn<small><sup>4+</sup></small>-ions on the surface. Thiourea molecules dissolve into the solution around the anions as the S and N sources. The Sn<small><sup>4+</sup></small>–CH<small><sub>4</sub></small>N<small><sub>2</sub></small>S precursors can be formed after a freeze-drying process, and are uniformly distributed into the GO layers. After calcining at 400 °C in an Ar atmosphere, GO is expected to transform into a N-doped graphene network with ultrathin SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small> nanosheets embedded into it. The synthetic details are described in the experimental section.</p>
      <br><div class="image_table"><table><tbody><tr><td colspan="3" class="imgHolder" id="imgfig1"><a href="/image/article/2018/CC/c8cc04255a/c8cc04255a-f1_hi-res.gif" title="Select to open image in new window" onclick="open(this.href, &quot;_blank&quot;, &quot;toolbar=1,scrollbars=yes,resizable=1&quot;); return false;"><img alt="image file: c8cc04255a-f1.tif" src="/image/article/2018/CC/c8cc04255a/c8cc04255a-f1.gif"></a></td></tr><tr><td class="pushTitleRight"> </td><td class="image_title"><b>Fig. 1 </b> <span id="fig1"><span class="graphic_title">Schematic illustration of the synthesis process of SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small>/NGS.</span></span></td><td class="pushTitleLeft"> </td></tr></tbody></table></div>
      <p class="otherpara">
        <a title="Select to navigate to figure" href="#imgfig2">Fig. 2a</a> presents the XRD pattern of as-prepared SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small>/NGS, and all the characteristic peaks are indexed well with the 2T-type layer structure (JCPDS card No. 23-0677) and no obvious impurities are detected. The characteristic peak of GO is absent, suggesting the formation of a graphene network after calcining. Furthermore, Fig. S1 (ESI<a title="Select to navigate to footnote" href="#fn1">†</a>) also shows the XRD patterns of precursors annealed at different temperatures, demonstrating that Sn<small><sub>3</sub></small>S<small><sub>4</sub></small> and SnS are formed with the temperature increasing to 500 °C. Raman spectroscopy is employed to characterize the graphene of SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small>/NGS (Fig. S2, ESI<a title="Select to navigate to footnote" href="#fn1">†</a>). Two main peaks at 1328 and 1587 cm<small><sup>−1</sup></small> are observed, corresponding to the D-band and G-band, respectively. Obviously, the intensity ratio of the D-band to G-band (<span class="italic">I</span><small><sub>D</sub></small>/<span class="italic">I</span><small><sub>G</sub></small> = 1.64) of SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small>/NGS is higher than that of GO (0.92), indicating the reduction of GO during the annealing process and the uniform distribution of SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small> nanosheets into the graphene layers.<a title="Select to navigate to references" href="#cit15"><sup><span class="sup_ref">15</span></sup></a> The compositions of the as-prepared samples are determined <span class="italic">via</span> a TGA test, as shown in Fig. S3 (ESI<a title="Select to navigate to footnote" href="#fn1">†</a>). The amount of NGS can be calculated to be around 6.8% in the hybrid composition by comparing the weight loss of the bare SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small> and SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small>/NGS. Fig. S4 (ESI<a title="Select to navigate to footnote" href="#fn1">†</a>) shows the N<small><sub>2</sub></small> adsorption/desorption isotherms of the SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small>/NGS with a type-IV hysteresis loop, revealing the mesopores in the NGS matrix. The BET surface area of SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small>/NGS is about 58.6 m<small><sup>2</sup></small> g<small><sup>−1</sup></small>, and the average pore size distribution is about 11.2 nm. The relatively high surface area of the SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small>/NGS composite in association with its mesoporous structure benefits electrolyte soaking and rapid Li<small><sup>+</sup></small>/Na<small><sup>+</sup></small> diffusion.</p>
      <br><div class="image_table"><table><tbody><tr><td colspan="3" class="imgHolder" id="imgfig2"><a href="/image/article/2018/CC/c8cc04255a/c8cc04255a-f2_hi-res.gif" title="Select to open image in new window" onclick="open(this.href, &quot;_blank&quot;, &quot;toolbar=1,scrollbars=yes,resizable=1&quot;); return false;"><img alt="image file: c8cc04255a-f2.tif" src="/image/article/2018/CC/c8cc04255a/c8cc04255a-f2.gif"></a></td></tr><tr><td class="pushTitleRight"> </td><td class="image_title"><b>Fig. 2 </b> <span id="fig2"><span class="graphic_title">(a) XRD patterns of the SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small>/NGS nanosheets and bare SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small>. (b) SEM image of SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small>/NGS nanosheets. (c and d) TEM and HRTEM images of SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small>/NGS nanosheets. (e) EDX mapping of Sn, S, C and N in the SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small>/NGS nanosheets.</span></span></td><td class="pushTitleLeft"> </td></tr></tbody></table></div>
      <p class="otherpara">The SEM image (<a title="Select to navigate to figure" href="#imgfig2">Fig. 2b</a>) shows that the obtained hybrid composite presents a homogenous sheet-like structure with two-dimensional (2D) SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small> nanocrystals distributed into the graphene layers. Moreover, the NGS networks present porous structures, which favorable for active materials connecting with the electrolyte. The TEM images (<a title="Select to navigate to figure" href="#imgfig2">Fig. 2c and d</a>) reveal that the SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small> nanosheets are uniformly distributed in the NGS sheets. Also, the HRTEM image shows that the SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small> nanosheets are encapsulated in the 3DNG matrix with tight bonding onto the surface. The SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small> nanosheets in the hybrid composite are ultrathin, just a few layers (about 3.54 nm), and benefit the infiltration of the electrolyte and help to release the volume changes during the electrochemical process. The lattice fringe of 0.59 nm corresponds to the (001) plane of 2T-SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small>. The detailed morphology of SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small>/NGS can be further observed in Fig. S5 (ESI<a title="Select to navigate to footnote" href="#fn1">†</a>). In addition, the EDX elemental mapping demonstrates the uniform distribution of Sn, S, C and N in the hybrid composite (<a title="Select to navigate to figure" href="#imgfig2">Fig. 2e</a>), indicating that the SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small> nanosheets are fully encapsulated by the NGS matrix.</p>
      <p class="otherpara">The chemical states of the hybrid composite are examined using the XPS. From the survey spectrum (Fig. S6, ESI<a title="Select to navigate to footnote" href="#fn1">†</a>), it can be clearly observed that the Sn, S, C and N elements coexist in the SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small>/NGS composite, which is in agreement with the EDX result. The amount of nitrogen atoms in the composite is about 19.2%, and the high N-doped graphene endows SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small>/NGS with enhanced electron/ion conductivity. The C 1s spectrum in Fig. S7a (ESI<a title="Select to navigate to footnote" href="#fn1">†</a>) shows three peaks centered at 284.8, 285.6 and 286.5 eV, representing C–C, C<img class="charmap" src="https://www.rsc.org/images/entities/char_e001.gif" alt="[double bond, length as m-dash]">C and C–N in graphene, respectively.<a title="Select to navigate to references" href="#cit19"><sup><span class="sup_ref">19</span></sup></a> The result demonstrates that nitrogen is chemically bonded to the carbon atoms of the graphene matrix. Fig. S7b (ESI<a title="Select to navigate to footnote" href="#fn1">†</a>) shows that the high-resolution N 1s spectrum can be well fitted into three peaks, indicating the presence of pyridinic-N (399.3 eV), pyrrolic-N (400.7 eV), and graphitic-N (401.6 eV).<a title="Select to navigate to references" href="#cit20"><sup><span class="sup_ref">20</span></sup></a> In general, nitrogen-doping could not only improve the electronic conductivity, but also provide more active sites for alkali metal ion (Li<small><sup>+</sup></small> or Na<small><sup>+</sup></small>) diffusion. The S 2p spectrum (Fig. S7c, ESI<a title="Select to navigate to footnote" href="#fn1">†</a>) can be divided into two peaks at 162.8 and 161.8 eV, corresponding to S 2p<small><sub>1/2</sub></small> and S 2p<small><sub>3/2</sub></small>, respectively.<a title="Select to navigate to references" href="#cit21"><sup><span class="sup_ref">21</span></sup></a> Additionally, in the Sn 3d high-resolution spectrum (Fig. S7d, ESI<a title="Select to navigate to footnote" href="#fn1">†</a>), two peaks are observed at 486.6 and 495.0 eV, which can be assigned to Sn 3d<small><sub>5/2</sub></small> and Sn 3d<small><sub>3/2</sub></small>, suggesting the Sn<small><sup>4+</sup></small> ion.<a title="Select to navigate to references" href="#cit22"><sup><span class="sup_ref">22</span></sup></a></p>
      <p class="otherpara">The SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small>/NGS hybrid composite is investigated as an anode for LIBs; the cyclic voltammetry (CV) curves are shown in <a title="Select to navigate to figure" href="#imgfig3">Fig. 3a</a>. The reduction peak at 1.21 V appearing in the first cycle can be attributed to the SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small> decomposition into metallic Sn and Li<small><sub>2</sub></small>S as well as the formation of a solid electrolyte interface (SEI).<a title="Select to navigate to reference" href="#cit18"><sup><span class="sup_ref">18,23</span></sup></a> The peak at 1.80 V in the first cathodic sweep is assigned to the Li<small><sup>+</sup></small>-ion intercalation in layers of SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small> without any phase change. The peaks at 0.62 and 1.92 V in the first anodic process represent the reversible delithiation of metallic Sn and the oxygenation of Sn, respecively.<a title="Select to navigate to reference" href="#cit24"><sup><span class="sup_ref">24,25</span></sup></a> The subsequent sweep curves coincide with each other, indicating relatively good cycling stability.</p>
      <br><div class="image_table"><table><tbody><tr><td colspan="3" class="imgHolder" id="imgfig3"><a href="/image/article/2018/CC/c8cc04255a/c8cc04255a-f3_hi-res.gif" title="Select to open image in new window" onclick="open(this.href, &quot;_blank&quot;, &quot;toolbar=1,scrollbars=yes,resizable=1&quot;); return false;"><img alt="image file: c8cc04255a-f3.tif" src="/image/article/2018/CC/c8cc04255a/c8cc04255a-f3.gif"></a></td></tr><tr><td class="pushTitleRight"> </td><td class="image_title"><b>Fig. 3 </b> <span id="fig3"><span class="graphic_title">Electrochemical tests of the SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small>/NGS composite as an anode material for LIBs. (a) CV curves for the first 4 cycles at a scan sweep rate of 0.2 mV s<small><sup>−1</sup></small>. (b) Charge–discharge profiles at a current density of 0.1 A g<small><sup>−1</sup></small>. (c and d) Rate performance at different densities. (e) Cycle performance at a current density of 0.5 A g<small><sup>−1</sup></small>.</span></span></td><td class="pushTitleLeft"> </td></tr></tbody></table></div>
      <p class="otherpara">
        <a title="Select to navigate to figure" href="#imgfig3">Fig. 3b</a> presents the charge–discharge profiles of the SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small>/NGS electrode during the 1st, 3rd and 5th cycles at a current density of 100 mA g<small><sup>−1</sup></small>. We can see that the SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small>/NGS electrode can deliver a discharge capacity of 1718.8 mA h g<small><sup>−1</sup></small> and a charge capacity of 1476 mA h g<small><sup>−1</sup></small>, corresponding to a high Coulombic efficiency (CE) of 85.9%. The irreversible capacity loss originates from the formation of the SEI layer. After the initial cycles, the CE increases up to about 98% in the following cycles, and a stable reversible capacity of 1425 mA h g<small><sup>−1</sup></small> can be obtained, demonstrating the superior cyclability.</p>
      <p class="otherpara">The rate performance of SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small>/NGS is evaluated at various densities ranging from 0.2 to 8 A g<small><sup>−1</sup></small> (<a title="Select to navigate to figure" href="#imgfig3">Fig. 3c and d</a>). It can be seen that the electrode exhibits average specific capacities of 932, 825, 746, 638, 539 and 488 mA h g<small><sup>−1</sup></small> at 1.0, 2.0, 3.0, 5.0, 7.0 and 8.0 A g<small><sup>−1</sup></small>, respectively. Furthermore, when the current density recovers back to 0.2 mA g<small><sup>−1</sup></small>, the capacity can return to 962 mA h g<small><sup>−1</sup></small>, suggesting that the unique structure of the SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small>/NGS electrode retains robustness and the electronic conductivity has been significantly improved. To further demonstrate the superior cycling performance of the SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small>/NGS hybrid composite, we test the electrode at a current density of 0.5 A g<small><sup>−1</sup></small> for 200 cycles. As depicted in <a title="Select to navigate to figure" href="#imgfig4">Fig. 4e</a>, the SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small>/NGS electrode at 100 mA g<small><sup>−1</sup></small> for the initial 3 cycles and then cycled at 0.5 A g<small><sup>−1</sup></small> for 200 cyclesexhibits a stable specific capacity of 763 mA h g<small><sup>−1</sup></small> after 200 cycles, and the CE is close to 100%. However, the bare SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small> exhibits a rapid capacity fading, and only 156 mA h g<small><sup>−1</sup></small> can be obtained after 200 cycles.</p>
      <br><div class="image_table"><table><tbody><tr><td colspan="3" class="imgHolder" id="imgfig4"><a href="/image/article/2018/CC/c8cc04255a/c8cc04255a-f4_hi-res.gif" title="Select to open image in new window" onclick="open(this.href, &quot;_blank&quot;, &quot;toolbar=1,scrollbars=yes,resizable=1&quot;); return false;"><img alt="image file: c8cc04255a-f4.tif" src="/image/article/2018/CC/c8cc04255a/c8cc04255a-f4.gif"></a></td></tr><tr><td class="pushTitleRight"> </td><td class="image_title"><b>Fig. 4 </b> <span id="fig4"><span class="graphic_title">Electrochemical tests of SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small>/NGS as an anode material for SIBs. (a) CV curves for the first 4 cycles at a scan sweep rate of 0.2 mV s<small><sup>−1</sup></small>. (b) Charge–discharge profiles at a current density of 0.1 A g<small><sup>−1</sup></small>. (c and d) Rate performances at different densities. (e) Cycle performance at a current density of 0.5 A g<small><sup>−1</sup></small>.</span></span></td><td class="pushTitleLeft"> </td></tr></tbody></table></div>
      <p class="otherpara">The electrochemical properties of the as-prepared SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small>/NGS electrode are also investigated as an anode for SIBs. <a title="Select to navigate to figure" href="#imgfig4">Fig. 4a</a> shows the CV curves in the 0.01–3.0 V potential range at 0.2 mV s<small><sup>−1</sup></small>; an obvious broad peak at 1.2 V in the first cathodic sweep is ascribed to Na<small><sup>+</sup></small> intercalation in the layers of SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small> without phase decomposition.<a title="Select to navigate to references" href="#cit26"><sup><span class="sup_ref">26</span></sup></a> The distinct reduction peak at around 0.55 V corresponds to the alloy reaction of Sn and Na and the formation of SEI. In the anodic scan, the peaks at 0.45 V and 1.2 V can be attributed to the desodiation reaction of Na<small><sub><span class="italic">x</span></sub></small>Sn and the reformation of SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small>, respectively.<a title="Select to navigate to reference" href="#cit27"><sup><span class="sup_ref">27,28</span></sup></a> Importantly, the curves well overlapped with each other after the first cycle, indicating the good reversibility of SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small>/NGS for sodium storage.</p>
      <p class="otherpara">The charge–discharge profiles of SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small>/NGS are shown in <a title="Select to navigate to figure" href="#imgfig4">Fig. 4b</a> at the current density of 100 mA g<small><sup>−1</sup></small>. The initial discharge and charge capacities are 763 and 1147 mA h g<small><sup>−1</sup></small>, respectively. The capacity loss is mainly attributed to the formation of the SEI film (similar to the LIBs). After the first cycle, the voltage profiles in the 3rd and 5th cycles mostly overlapped with each other, suggesting excellent cycling stability. The rate capability of the SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small>/NGS electrode for SIBs is also measured at various current densities. As shown in <a title="Select to navigate to figure" href="#imgfig4">Fig. 4c and d</a>, the stepwise specific capacities of 678, 608, 518, 476, and 364 mA h g<small><sup>−1</sup></small> can be obtained at 0.5, 1.0, 2.0, 3.0 and 5.0 A g<small><sup>−1</sup></small>, respectively. Moreover, when the current density returns back to 0.2 A g<small><sup>−1</sup></small>, the capacity can fully recover. <a title="Select to navigate to figure" href="#imgfig4">Fig. 4e</a> shows the cycle stability of the SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small>/NGS electrode at 100 mA g<small><sup>−1</sup></small> for the initial 3 cycles, and when cycled at 0.5 A g<small><sup>−1</sup></small> for 200 cycles, it can deliver a reversible capacity of 453 mA h g<small><sup>−1</sup></small> even after 200 cycles, which is much higher than that of bare SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small>, corresponding to 87.4% capacity retention rate. Furthermore, the CE reaches up to 100%, indicating the excellent cyclability of SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small>/3DNG for SIBs. Compared with other previous literature reports about SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small>-based anode materials, this work shows excellent electrochemical performance (Table S2, ESI<a title="Select to navigate to footnote" href="#fn1">†</a>).</p>
      <p class="otherpara">In order to further explain the advantages of the as-prepared SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small>/NGS composite, electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) was carried out. Fig. S8a and b (ESI<a title="Select to navigate to footnote" href="#fn1">†</a>) show the Nyquist plots of the SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small>/NGS electrode after 1, 10 and 50 cycles for LIBs and SIBs. After fitting the impedance parameters (Table S1, ESI<a title="Select to navigate to footnote" href="#fn1">†</a>), it can be seen that the interfacial resistance (<span class="italic">R</span><small><sub>ct</sub></small> + <span class="italic">R</span><small><sub>int</sub></small>) decreases from 72.4 to 77.9 Ω after 50 cycles compared to 1 cycle for LIBs and 408.1 to 395.0 Ω for SIBs. This can be ascribed to the fact that the active sites of the internal structure increase and facilitate the charge transfer during cycling.<a title="Select to navigate to reference" href="#cit29"><sup><span class="sup_ref">29,30</span></sup></a><span class="italic">Ex situ</span> TEM measurements are performed on SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small>/NGS to investigate the structural evolution during the charge/discharge process (Fig. S9, ESI<a title="Select to navigate to footnote" href="#fn1">†</a>). After 50 cycles at 0.1 A g<small><sup>−1</sup></small>, it is clearly observed that the SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small> nanosheets are encapsulated by the NGS network and the morphologies are well maintained for both LIBs and SIBs. The crystal structure of SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small> can be detected after 50 cycles, indicating the high reversibility of the SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small>/NGS anode.</p>
      <p class="otherpara">In summary, we report a versatile and scalable method to synthesize a SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small>/NGS hybrid composite with ultrathin SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small> nanosheets embedded into N-doped graphene sheets. The as-prepared SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small>/NGS composite shows the desirable architectural advantages of the synergistic function between the confined SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small> nanosheets and NGS. When evaluated as an anode for LIBs and SIBs, the SnS<small><sub>2</sub></small>/NGS electrode exhibits excellent electrochemical performance in terms of reversible capacity, rate capability and cycle stability. We believe that the synthetic strategy can be extended to develop other advanced electrodes for next-generation alkali-ion batteries.</p>
